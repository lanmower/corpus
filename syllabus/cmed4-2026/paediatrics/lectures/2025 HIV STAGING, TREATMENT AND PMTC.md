## Lecture Notes

### WHO Classification and CD4 Staging in Paediatric HIV

The World Health Organization employs a 4-stage classification system to categorize paediatric HIV disease. This system classifies clinical status along a spectrum from asymptomatic to severely symptomatic presentation, based on documented clinical or immunological criteria in the presence of lab-confirmed HIV infection. Understanding this classification is essential for risk stratification and treatment planning in children with HIV.

A critical distinction between paediatric and adult HIV management lies in CD4 cell interpretation. CD4 counts in healthy uninfected infants are considerably higher than those observed in uninfected adults, and these values slowly decline to adult levels by approximately age 6 years. This age-related variation means that absolute CD4 count must be interpreted within age-specific context—a CD4 count of 1000 cells/mm³ in a 6-month-old infant represents severe immunosuppression, whereas the same count in a 7-year-old indicates relative preservation of immune function.

In children younger than 5 years, percentage CD4 (%CD4+) proves more valuable and reliable than absolute CD4 count, since absolute values fluctuate considerably within individual children in this young age group. This is particularly important because viral load thresholds and immunological criteria for treatment decisions often rely on these parameters. The CD4 threshold for AIDS diagnosis across all ages is less than 200 cells/mm³, and this criterion defines severe immunosuppression warranting urgent intervention.

### Natural History and Stages of HIV Infection

Acute HIV infection develops within 2 to 4 weeks after a person becomes infected. During this window, many patients experience flu-like symptoms including fever, headache, and rash. The virus directly attacks and destroys CD4 cells of the immune system. Critically, HIV transmission risk is greatest during acute HIV infection, although transmission can occur during any stage of infection.

Following the acute phase, HIV progresses to chronic infection, also termed asymptomatic HIV infection or clinical latency. During chronic HIV infection, the virus continues to replicate in the body but at very low levels. People may have no HIV-related symptoms yet can still transmit HIV to others. Without treatment with HIV medicines, chronic HIV infection usually advances to AIDS in 10 to 12 years.

AIDS represents the final stage of HIV disease. AIDS is diagnosed when a person with HIV has a CD4 count of less than 200 cells/mm³ and/or one or more opportunistic infections. At this stage, HIV has destroyed the immune system such that the body cannot adequately fight off opportunistic infections and malignancy. Without treatment, people with AIDS typically survive about 3 years.

### Early Clinical Manifestations in Paediatric HIV

The clinical presentation of HIV in children varies dramatically by age and degree of immunosuppression. In the first year of life, common early signs include oral candidiasis, failure to thrive (FTT), and developmental delay. Lymphadenopathy frequently occurs alongside hepatosplenomegaly, reflecting systemic viral replication and immune activation.

Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PJP) holds particular significance in paediatric HIV, accounting for approximately 50% of all AIDS-defining conditions in the first year of life, with a median age of presentation at 5 months. Clinically, PJP presents with low-grade fever, tachypnea, non-productive cough, and shortness of breath. A characteristic and diagnostically challenging feature is that auscultation on physical examination may be entirely normal despite severe disease and significant hypoxemia. Chest X-ray imaging may also appear normal; however, CT imaging can reveal extensive ground-glass opacities with patchy distribution affecting the central lung while sparing the periphery.

Wasting syndrome in HIV is defined as persistent weight loss greater than 10% of baseline, OR a downward crossing of 2 or more major percentile lines on the paediatric weight-for-age chart, OR weight-for-height less than the 5th percentile on two consecutive measurements taken at least 30 days apart, accompanied by chronic diarrhea or documented fever. Wasting can result from HIV infection itself, concurrent opportunistic infections, and increased metabolic demands, leading to loss of both total body mass and lean muscle.

HIV encephalopathy represents severe neurological involvement and is diagnosed by the constellation of: failure to attain developmental milestones or loss of cognitive ability, AND evidence of impaired brain growth with acquired microcephaly, brain atrophy on CT or MRI, AND acquired symmetric motor deficits (specifically, two or more of the following: paresis, pathologic reflexes, ataxia, or gait disturbances).

Cytomegalovirus (CMV) disease in advanced HIV can present in multiple forms including fever, pneumonia, colitis, encephalitis, or retinitis. CMV transmission occurs from mother to child perinatally, through breastfeeding, and during pregnancy, as well as through person-to-person contact with secretions.

### Epidemiology and Burden of Paediatric HIV Disease

The epidemiological impact of paediatric HIV remains substantial, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa. In 2014, the global HIV burden was estimated at 36.9 million people living with HIV (range 34.3 to 41.4 million), with 220,000 children under 15 years (range 190,000 to 260,000) living with HIV. That year saw 1.2 million AIDS deaths globally (range 980,000 to 1.6 million), including 150,000 deaths among children under 15 years (range 140,000 to 170,000).

Geographically, approximately 75% of new HIV infections in children in 2014 occurred in Sub-Saharan Africa. Similarly, approximately 70% of all children under 15 years living with HIV globally are found in Sub-Saharan Africa, reflecting both the high prevalence in the region and the profound impact on childhood mortality and morbidity.

Within South Africa specifically, approximately 12% of the national population is HIV infected. However, prevalence is highly heterogeneous by region. KwaZulu-Natal (KZN) has the highest HIV prevalence rate among persons age 15 to 49 in South Africa at approximately 35 to 40%.

The impact of antiretroviral therapy on opportunistic infection rates has been transformative. In a study of 364 HIV-infected children born between 1986 and 1998 and monitored until 2004, the incidence rate of opportunistic infections decreased dramatically from 14.4 cases per 100 person-years before 1997 to 1.1 cases per 100 person-years after 1997, demonstrating the profound effect of combination antiretroviral therapy introduction.

### Current Treatment Policy and ART Initiation Criteria

Current policy mandates that all children infected with HIV should receive antiretroviral therapy (ARVs), with no CD4-based thresholds for treatment eligibility. Two developments have had the greatest impact on improving paediatric HIV outcomes: the availability and use of highly effective combination antiretroviral therapy (ART), and the early initiation of ART in HIV-infected infants.

Fast-track ART initiation (defined as starting treatment within 7 days) is indicated for children meeting any of the following criteria: age less than 1 year, WHO clinical stage 4, active multidrug-resistant or extensively drug-resistant tuberculosis (MDR or XDR-TB), or CD4 count less than 200 cells/μL or less than 15% CD4.

### Baseline Assessment and Monitoring at ART Initiation

Before starting antiretroviral therapy, specific baseline assessments are required to identify contraindications and establish baseline parameters for monitoring. These include CD4 count (if none has been performed within the previous 6 months), hemoglobin or full blood count (with the understanding that ART can be started if hemoglobin is less than 8 g/dL, though this should be discussed with a specialist regarding concurrent management), cholesterol and triglyceride levels (particularly if the planned regimen includes protease inhibitors), ALT testing (if the child is jaundiced or concurrently on tuberculosis treatment), and renal function assessment for children weighing more than 35 kg who will be receiving tenofovir.

### Current First-Line Antiretroviral Regimens by Age

The first-line antiretroviral regimens have evolved to maximize efficacy while minimizing toxicity in different age groups. For neonates, the new first-line regimen is AZT (zidovudine) plus 3TC (lamivudine) plus NVP (nevirapine). For infants and children in the weight range of 3 to 20 kg, the new first-line regimen is ABC (abacavir) plus 3TC (lamivudine) plus DTG (dolutegravir). For adolescents and adults, the first-line regimen is TDF (tenofovir) plus 3TC (lamivudine) plus DTG (dolutegravir).

Paediatric dolutegravir is available as 10 mg dispersable scored tablets for standard first-line use in children older than 4 weeks to less than 10 years of age in the weight band of 3 to 20 kg. The benefits of this paediatric formulation include increased clinical efficacy compared to older regimens, a better side effect profile, ease of administration, and improved palatability, facilitating adherence in young children.

### Antiretroviral Drug Classes and Pharmacology

Seven major classes of antiretroviral medications are currently available. Nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTI) include abacavir (ABC), didanosine (ddI), emtricitabine (FTC), lamivudine (3TC), stavudine (d4T), tenofovir (TDF), and zidovudine (AZT or ZDV). Non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NNRTI) include delavirdine (DLV), efavirenz (EFV), etravirine (EVR), nevirapine (NVP), and rilpivirine. Protease inhibitors comprise atazanavir (ATV), darunavir (DRV), fosamprenavir (FPV), indinavir (IDV), lopinavir (LPV), nelfinavir (NFV), ritonavir (RTV), saquinavir (SQV), and tipranavir (TPV). Integrase inhibitors include dolutegravir (DTG), elvitegravir (EVG), and raltegravir (RAL). Additionally, enfuvirtide (ENF or T-20) serves as a fusion inhibitor, and maraviroc (MVC) acts as a CCR5 antagonist.

### Dolutegravir: Profile and Monitoring

Dolutegravir represents a cornerstone of modern paediatric HIV treatment. Side effects are typically mild and self-limiting, including insomnia, headache, various CNS effects, and gastrointestinal symptoms. Weight gain has emerged as a notable side effect in some patients. A unique pharmacological property is that DTG decreases tubular secretion of creatinine without affecting glomerular filtration rate. As a consequence, serum creatinine levels increase early in treatment by less than 15%, remain stable thereafter, and this rise alone is not an indication to discontinue DTG unless creatinine continues to rise, which could indicate concurrent tenofovir nephrotoxicity or other renal pathology.

An important consideration for women of childbearing age is that dolutegravir may increase the risk of neural tube defects. Resistance to dolutegravir is relatively slow to develop, with at least 2 mutations required to acquire resistance, demonstrating synergy with other NRTIs in combination regimens.

### Abacavir: Benefits and Toxicity

Abacavir's key resistance property is that it requires at least 2 mutations to acquire resistance, showing synergy with other NRTIs. However, abacavir hypersensitivity reaction is a critical safety concern. This reaction can be fatal and typically presents with fever, rash, abdominal pain, pharyngitis, and dyspnea. If abacavir hypersensitivity reaction occurs, rechallenge is absolutely contraindicated, as re-exposure may precipitate severe or fatal reaction.

### Lamivudine, Efavirenz, and Protease Inhibitors: Individual Profiles

Lamivudine (3TC) is a well-tolerated NRTI available as an easy-to-take syrup and scored tablets, with an excellent safety profile overall. Efavirenz carries the burden of CNS side effects including drowsiness, insomnia, abnormal dreams, hallucinations, nightmares, and poor concentration. These effects are often significant enough that bedtime dosing is specifically recommended to minimize daytime impact.

Lopinavir/ritonavir (marketed as Kaletra or Aluvia) has a distinctive side effect profile including a strange and strong taste, increased triglycerides and cholesterol, hepatitis, pancreatitis, lipodystrophy, and numerous drug interactions that complicate co-administration with other medications.

### Zidovudine and Hematologic Toxicity

Zidovudine carries important toxicological concerns. Adverse effects include risk of anemia, neutropenia, headache, and myalgia. Practical considerations include the fact that capsules are insoluble, and the syrup formulation must be kept in a brown (light-opaque) bottle to prevent degradation.

### Early and Long-Term Toxicities of HAART

Common early toxicities associated with HAART therapy include gastrointestinal intolerance, anemia, sleep disturbances, and hyperbilirubinemia. These effects often improve with time or dose adjustment.

Hematologic complications deserve particular attention. Anemia is seen most frequently with zidovudine (AZT/ZDV). Neutropenia, while less common, usually causes no clinical complications unless the absolute polymorphonuclear cell (PMN) count falls below 250 cells/μL. Neutropenia in HIV patients can result from non-ARV drugs including trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX), ganciclovir, rifabutin, and hydroxyurea, in addition to zidovudine which remains the ARV most associated with this complication. Thrombocytopenia, by contrast, is typically associated with untreated HIV rather than with antiretroviral drugs.

Long-term metabolic complications can develop during extended HAART. These include glucose disorders, insulin resistance, impaired glucose tolerance, and hyperglycemia progressing to diabetes mellitus. Dyslipidemia develops with decreased HDL cholesterol and increased triglycerides and total cholesterol. Morphologic changes reflecting lipodystrophy syndrome include fat accumulation in certain areas, abdominal obesity, buffalo hump appearance, and gynecomastia (breast tissue enlargement in males).

### Tuberculosis-HIV Coinfection Management

When tuberculosis is diagnosed before ART initiation, the recommended approach is to complete 2 to 8 weeks of tuberculosis therapy before commencing ART. However, this timeline is dramatically shortened for patients with severe immunosuppression: if CD4 count is less than 50 cells/mm³, ART should be initiated as soon as possible. ART should be started as soon as the patient is tolerating tuberculosis therapy.

The choice of antiretroviral regimen in children with active tuberculosis differs from the standard regimen. Efavirenz-based regimens are generally preferred in patients with active tuberculosis. If lopinavir/ritonavir is being used, dose adjustment is mandatory: ritonavir should be doubled in patients with active tuberculosis due to enzyme induction by rifampicin reducing drug levels.

When tuberculosis is diagnosed after ART initiation has already begun, the approach differs: ART should be continued throughout TB treatment rather than interrupted or restarted.

Isoniazid preventive therapy (IPT) is recommended for HIV-infected children. Specifically, post-exposure IPT at a dose of 10 mg/kg/day for 6 months is recommended for all HIV-infected children irrespective of age or ART status. In contrast, pre-exposure IPT (given to children without known TB exposure) is not recommended in any HIV-infected or HIV-uninfected child.

### Immunization in HIV-Infected and HIV-Exposed Children

HIV-infected and HIV-exposed children should be immunized according to the routine national immunization schedule. This is because routine immunizations are vital—HIV-infected children are more susceptible to infections due to their weakened immune systems, making vaccination even more critical than in non-infected children.

However, the schedule differs by vaccine type and disease severity. The BCG vaccine is recommended for asymptomatic HIV-infected children only and must not be given to symptomatic HIV infection due to risk of disseminated BCG disease. Optimal timing for BCG in asymptomatic children is at birth. Measles vaccine, by contrast, is recommended for both asymptomatic and symptomatic HIV-infected children, given at 6 and 9 months of age. Yellow fever vaccine, similar to BCG, is recommended for asymptomatic HIV-infected children only (not for symptomatic HIV infection), with optimal timing at 9 months of age.

### Treatment and Prophylaxis of Opportunistic Infections

Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia treatment follows established first-line protocols. TMP-SMX (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) is the first-line treatment agent. Experts recommend that glucocorticoids be included in the treatment regimen to reduce inflammation and improve oxygenation.

CMV disease transmission occurs through multiple routes: from mother to child perinatally, through breastfeeding, and during pregnancy, as well as through person-to-person contact with secretions.

### Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS)

Immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS), also colloquially termed "HAART attacks," represents a paradoxical clinical deterioration that typically occurs in the first 6 weeks after ART initiation. The mechanism involves the recovering immune system responding more vigorously to antigens from existing infections, resulting in an exaggerated inflammatory response. This complication can manifest with worsening of pre-existing opportunistic infections or inflammatory conditions, and recognition is essential to distinguish IRIS from treatment failure or drug toxicity.

### Public Health Context and Treatment Goals

The global treatment target known as the 90-90-90 goal has been proposed to accelerate HIV disease control: 90% of the population tested for HIV, 90% of those testing positive placed on antiretroviral treatment, and 90% of those on treatment virally suppressed. This ambitious target requires coordinated efforts across testing, linkage to care, and treatment adherence programs.

The impact of combination antiretroviral therapy introduction has been transformative in paediatric HIV care. Early recognition of the two most impactful interventions—the availability of highly effective combination ART and the early initiation of treatment in infants diagnosed with HIV—has fundamentally shifted the trajectory of the disease from one of inevitable decline to one of durable viral suppression and preserved immune function compatible with normal growth and development.
