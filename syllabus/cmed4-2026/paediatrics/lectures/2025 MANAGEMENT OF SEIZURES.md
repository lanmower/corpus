# 2025 MANAGEMENT OF SEIZURES

## Lecture Notes

### Definitions and Terminology

A **seizure** is an abnormal, excessive, or synchronous discharge of neurons that results in sudden, transient alterations in neurological function, manifested by clinical or EEG changes. The terms "convulsion" and "fit" are often used colloquially to describe seizures, with convulsion specifically referring to involuntary muscular contractions and relaxations or violent uncontrollable contraction of muscles.

**Epilepsy** differs from a single seizure; it is a tendency to have recurrent seizures—a chronic disorder characterized by a predisposition to generate epileptic seizures and by the neurobiological, cognitive, psychological, and social consequences of this condition. An **epilepsy syndrome** is a complex of signs and symptoms that define a unique epilepsy condition, typically with specific etiology, age of onset, seizure types, EEG pattern, prognosis, and response to treatment.

**Status epilepticus** represents a serious state of prolonged seizure activity or a series of seizures without full recovery of consciousness between attacks. It is formally defined as seizure activity lasting more than 30 minutes or two or more seizures without regaining consciousness. This condition has two main presentations: **convulsive status epilepticus** consists of prolonged or recurrent generalized tonic-clonic seizures with persistent alteration of consciousness and visible muscle contractions, while **non-convulsive status epilepticus** is a state of mental alteration without visible muscle contractions, characterized by absence or minimal motor signs despite ongoing epileptic activity on EEG.

### Evolution of Seizure Classification

The modern era of seizure classification began in **1964** when Gastaut and colleagues proposed the first modern classification system. This approach gained international adoption in **1970** when Gastaut's work was popularized worldwide. The widespread implementation of video electroencephalography prompted a major update to seizure classification in **1981**, which introduced four classification terms: simple partial, complex partial, generalized, and unclassifiable seizures. This 1981 classification became the standard in clinical practice.

Epilepsy classifications underwent partial updates in 1985 and 1989, followed by another revision in 2010, which served as an intermediate stage toward a final accepted epilepsy classification. The 2010 ILAE decision was to maintain the 1981 classification of seizures with minor changes, following extensive discussions at the beginning of the 21st century.

### ILAE 2017 Classification

The **1981 classification was superseded by the 2017 classification**, with careful mapping of prior 1981 terminology to the new 2017 terms. The **ILAE 2017 Classification of Epilepsies** represents the most current international standard for classifying epilepsy syndromes, reflecting advances in genetics, neuroimaging, and understanding of seizure semiology.

The **ILAE 2017 basic version** of seizure type classification stratifies seizures into three main categories: **generalized-onset**, **focal-onset**, and **unknown-onset** seizures. This simplified framework is practical for clinical use. The **expanded version** includes detailed subcategories, with generalized-onset seizures subdivided into tonic-clonic, absence, myoclonic, and other types; focal-onset seizures further stratified into focal aware, focal impaired awareness, and focal to bilateral tonic-clonic categories; and unknown-onset seizures representing cases where the origin cannot be determined.

The diagnostic approach to seizure and epilepsy classification incorporates three main diagnostic tools: **semiology** (clinical features and patient/witness descriptions), **EEG** (electroencephalography), and occasionally supplementary information from **neuroimaging**. For classification of epilepsy type and epilepsy syndrome, additional investigations including **genetics**, **laboratory findings**, and **neuro-imaging findings** are utilized while maintaining a major focus on etiology throughout the diagnostic process.

### Seizure Types and Semiology

**Automatisms** are involuntary, coordinated motor behaviors occurring during altered consciousness in seizures. Common examples include lip smacking, chewing, picking at clothes, and mumbling. These manifest as purposeless but organized movements that reflect the altered state of consciousness during a seizure episode.

**Epileptic spasms** (also called infantile spasms) are sudden, brief flexor or extensor contractions of muscles that typically occur in clusters and are often associated with developmental regression. They are particularly characteristic of West syndrome and warrant urgent evaluation and treatment.

**Tonic seizures** are characterized by sustained muscle rigidity or contraction lasting seconds to minutes, with increased tone throughout the body. These may be either focal or generalized in presentation.

**Absence seizures** are brief episodes of altered consciousness lasting 5 to 10 seconds that typically occur multiple times daily. They are characterized by sudden onset and sudden offset without postictal confusion. During absence seizures, patients may exhibit subtle automatisms or myoclonic jerks. These seizures are distinctive for their brief duration and the absence of confusion following termination.

### Mechanisms of Anti-Seizure Drug Action

Anti-seizure medications work through diverse mechanisms targeting different components of neuronal function. Understanding these mechanisms helps guide selection for individual patients and specific seizure types.

**Sodium channel blockade** occurs in two main forms: **phenytoin** and **carbamazepine** block repetitive sodium channel activation, while **lacosamide** enhances slow inactivation of sodium channels. Additional medications that block sodium channels include **lamotrigine**, **topiramate**, and others.

**Calcium channel modulation** represents another key target. **Ethosuximide** and **valproic acid** block T-type calcium channels, which is particularly relevant for absence seizures. **Zonisamide** blocks N- and L-type calcium channels. **Lamotrigine** adds another dimension by blocking both N- and L-type calcium channels in addition to its sodium channel effects and modulation of H-current.

**Topiramate** employs multiple mechanisms: blockage of voltage-gated sodium channels, blocking of AMPA (alpha-amino-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-isoxazole propionic acid) receptors, and inhibition of carbonic anhydrase. This multi-targeted approach contributes to its broad spectrum of efficacy.

**GABA-A receptor enhancement** is the primary mechanism of **phenobarbital** and **benzodiazepines**, which prolong the open state of these chloride channels to facilitate cellular hyperpolarization.

Other unique mechanisms include **NMDA receptor blockade** by **felbamate** and opening of neuronal potassium channels by **ezogabine**.

### Phenobarbital

Phenobarbital is a barbiturate used for the treatment of **generalized and focal seizures**. Its mechanism of action involves binding to the GABA-A receptor and prolonging its open state, allowing for increased chloride influx and consequent cellular hyperpolarization.

Despite its long history of efficacy, phenobarbital is difficult to use in modern clinical practice due to its **significant sedating properties**, which compromise quality of life and cognitive function. A major clinical consequence of phenobarbital's interaction with the cytochrome P450 system is that it is a **potent inducer of the CYP system** and thus can substantially **decrease serum levels of other drugs metabolized by this pathway**, necessitating careful monitoring of drug interactions.

### Carbamazepine

**Carbamazepine (CBZ)** is a first-line agent used for **focal and generalized seizures**. Beyond seizure management, it is also used for patients with bipolar disorder and trigeminal neuralgia.

The mechanism of action involves **inhibition of voltage-gated sodium channels**, which reduces neuronal firing and prevents seizure propagation. Carbamazepine is **metabolized by the cytochrome P450 system and is a strong inducer of hepatic enzymes**, leading to decreased levels of other drugs metabolized by this pathway. This enzyme-inducing property necessitates careful management of drug interactions and potential dose adjustments of concomitant medications.

Common side effects of carbamazepine include **gastrointestinal upset, hyponatremia, rash, itching, drowsiness, dizziness, blurred vision, and headache**. These are generally manageable and often improve with dose titration or time.

More serious and rare but dangerous side effects include **Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, leukopenia, and aplastic anemia (pancytopenia)**. These serious hematologic and dermatologic reactions require immediate medical attention and discontinuation of the medication.

### Valproate

**Valproate (VPA)** is a **broad-spectrum anti-seizure medication** used for both focal and generalized seizures. Its effectiveness arises from multiple mechanisms of action: **voltage-gated sodium channel inhibition**, **augmentation of GABA concentrations**, and **mild inhibition of T-type calcium currents** (though not to the extent of ethosuximide).

Valproate is **metabolized by the liver and is an inhibitor of CYP enzymes**, so it must be used with caution in hepatic insufficiency. An important contraindication is its use in **patients with urea cycle disorders**, due to the high risk of **hyperammonemia**. When combining topiramate with valproate, clinicians must be aware that this combination can lead to **increased levels of ammonia**.

General side effects of valproate include **nausea and vomiting, tremor, easy bruising, weight gain, insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, and subclinical hypothyroidism**. These metabolic and gastrointestinal effects should be monitored and discussed with patients.

Serious adverse effects of valproate include **acute hepatocellular injury with jaundice and acute pancreatitis**. Notably, valproate is **contraindicated during pregnancy** due to **teratogenic neurotoxicity** that has been observed with in-utero exposure, making it unsuitable for women of childbearing potential who may become pregnant.

### Phenytoin

**Phenytoin** is widely used for **focal and generalized seizures** and is also administered for status epilepticus and as a second-line agent for patients with mixed seizure types such as tonic-clonic and myoclonic seizures.

The **primary mechanism of action** involves **blocking voltage-gated sodium channels**. Other possible mechanisms include decreased synaptic transmission, smaller changes in ionic gradients involving the sodium-potassium ATPase pump, and inhibition of calcium-calmodulin phosphorylation.

Phenytoin is a **broad-spectrum inducer of the CYP system**, leading to **decreased levels of other drugs metabolized by this pathway**, similar to phenobarbital and carbamazepine. This enzyme-inducing effect is a significant concern for polypharmacy.

**Major adverse effects** of phenytoin include **gingival hyperplasia** (enlargement of gum tissue), **increased body hair**, **folic acid depletion**, **rash**, and **worsening bone density**. Patients on long-term therapy should be counselled regarding the possible development of **confusion, ataxia, double vision, and neuropathy**, which can emerge insidiously with prolonged use.

### Levetiracetam

**Levetiracetam** has a broad spectrum of efficacy and represents a modern anti-seizure agent with favorable properties. Its **precise mechanism is not fully understood**, but studies have linked its effectiveness to **binding of synaptic vesicle protein 2A (SV2A)**.

The spectrum of levetiracetam efficacy is **very broad**, and it is used as **adjunctive therapy for focal-onset seizures in children and adults**, **myoclonic seizures in patients with juvenile myoclonic epilepsy (ages 12 and up)**, and **primary generalized tonic-clonic seizures**. This broad applicability makes it a versatile option across many seizure types.

A major advantage of levetiracetam regarding drug interactions is that it is **uniquely metabolized independent of the hepatic CYP system and does not induce CYP enzymes** like phenytoin, phenobarbital, and carbamazepine. This dramatically simplifies polypharmacy and eliminates concerns about enzyme induction.

Levetiracetam **does not necessitate a prolonged titration period** like lamotrigine, which carries a risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome. This allows for more rapid achievement of therapeutic doses. The medication is **extremely well-tolerated**, with common adverse effects limited to **behavioral disturbances, drowsiness, dizziness, and upper respiratory infections**—a notably favorable safety profile compared to many other anti-seizure medications.

### Ethosuximide

**Ethosuximide** represents an exception in anti-seizure medication use, with **specific and limited use for absence seizures**. Its narrow spectrum makes it ideally suited for patients with absence-type seizures, where its selective mechanisms provide excellent efficacy with fewer off-target effects than broad-spectrum agents.

### Topiramate

**Topiramate** is a broad-spectrum anti-seizure medication with multiple mechanisms of action: **blockage of voltage-gated sodium channels**, **enhancement of GABA transmission**, **antagonization of NMDA receptors**, and **modest carbonic anhydrase inhibition**. This multi-modal approach accounts for its broad effectiveness.

Topiramate can be used as **monotherapy in both pediatric and adult medicine for focal-onset seizures and primary generalized tonic-clonic seizures (ages 10 and up)**. It can also be used as **adjunctive therapy for focal seizures or in patients with Lennox-Gastaut syndrome (ages 2 and up)**, making it flexible for various clinical scenarios and age groups.

Common side effects of topiramate include **weight loss, impaired cognition, difficulties with expressive language, fatigue, depression, headache, and paresthesias**. The cognitive effects warrant discussion with patients, particularly students and those in cognitively demanding professions.

Serious metabolic complications can occur, including **metabolic acidosis with tachypnea** and **calcium phosphate nephrolithiasis (kidney stones)**. These complications require monitoring through laboratory assessment and patient education about hydration and warning signs.

### Lamotrigine

**Lamotrigine** is a broad-spectrum anti-seizure medication used across multiple seizure types. It is also used off-label as a mood stabilizer in psychiatric practice.

A critical consideration with lamotrigine is that it requires **prolonged and careful titration** due to a significant risk of **Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS)**, particularly at the **initiation of therapy or with rapid dose increases**. This serious dermatologic reaction necessitates slow titration protocols and patient education regarding warning signs such as unusual rashes.

Lamotrigine's mechanisms of action include **blocking sodium channels**, **blocking N- and L-type calcium channels**, and **modulating H-current**. This multi-targeted approach contributes to its broad spectrum of effectiveness across seizure types.

### Off-Label Uses of Anti-Seizure Medications

Anti-seizure medications are increasingly used for indications beyond epilepsy, leveraging their neurobiological mechanisms for other conditions. **Pregabalin, clonazepam, and clobazam** are used off-label as anxiolytics. **Zonisamide and valproic acid** are used off-label for migraine relief. **Valproic acid, lamotrigine, and carbamazepine** serve off-label as mood stabilizers in psychiatric management. **Pregabalin, gabapentin, and carbamazepine** are utilized off-label for neuropathic pain relief. **Zonisamide and topiramate** are used off-label for weight loss. Additionally, **zonisamide** has been used off-label as an antiparkinsonian agent.

### Febrile Seizures

**Simple febrile seizures** are defined as a short generalized seizure of duration less than 15 minutes, not recurring within 24 hours, occurring during a febrile episode not caused by an acute disease of the nervous system, in a child aged 6 months to 5 years with no neurologic deficits (no pre-, peri-, or postnatal brain damage, normal psychomotor development, and no previous afebrile seizures). Fever may not be detected before the seizure, but it must be present at least in the immediate post-acute period and be the symptom of a pediatric disease.

**Complex febrile seizures** are defined as focal or generalized prolonged seizure of duration greater than 15 minutes, recurring more than once in 24 hours, associated with post-ictal neurologic abnormalities (more frequently post-ictal palsy or Todd's palsy), or with previous neurologic deficits. If duration is greater than 30 minutes or there is no regaining of consciousness, this constitutes febrile status epilepticus.

#### Admission and Investigation of Febrile Seizures

Admission to hospital is indicated for a simple febrile seizure in the **first episode or for known diagnosis of simple febrile seizures when without reliable familiar context**. For complex febrile seizures, **admission is indicated for complex febrile seizures or febrile seizure without reliable familial context**.

For simple febrile seizures, **routine laboratory tests, EEG, and neuro-imaging are not recommended**, as these are low-yield in uncomplicated cases. However, **lumbar puncture is indicated with meningeal signs**, should be done before antibiotic treatment, is particularly useful in children less than 18 months old, and in children greater than 18 months should not be done routinely but rather based on the clinical assessment of the individual patient.

For complex febrile seizures, investigation is more thorough: **search for aetiology using bloods, EEG is recommended** (particularly for viral encephalitis), **neuro-imaging is recommended**, and **lumbar puncture is necessary for all patients with suspected CNS infections**.

#### Treatment of Febrile Seizures

For simple febrile seizures lasting 2 to 3 minutes, **no treatment is required**, as these brief seizures typically self-resolve without complication. For **prolonged febrile seizures**, management can occur in a **hospital or home setting**, with focus on prevention and management of recurrence risk. For **complex febrile seizures**, treatment is **variable, depending on aetiology, semiology, and prognosis**, and must be **tailored to the specific underlying cause** rather than following a standardized protocol.

### Assessment and Diagnostic Approach to Seizures and Epilepsy

A **comprehensive history** in a patient with seizures or epilepsy includes detailed seizure history encompassing onset, frequency, trigger factors, duration, semiology, post-ictal features, and impact on daily function. This detailed characterization is essential for accurate seizure classification.

**Physical examination** should include general medical examination with vital signs, full neurological examination, assessment of neurodevelopmental status, and evaluation for stigmata of underlying syndromes. These findings may reveal clues to syndromic diagnoses or structural etiologies.

**Bedside investigations** include fingerstick glucose and electrolytes assessment via simple bedside testing, along with other point-of-care investigations as indicated. These rapid assessments can identify reversible causes such as hypoglycemia.

**Laboratory investigations** should include complete blood count, serum electrolytes, renal function, liver function tests, glucose, and other tests as clinically indicated based on seizure type and suspected etiology. These baseline investigations help identify systemic causes or establish baseline values for medication monitoring.

**Neuroimaging**, including CT or MRI, should be considered based on seizure type, age, clinical presentation, and suspected etiology to identify structural abnormalities that may be causative or relevant to prognosis.

**Electroencephalography (EEG)** is essential for seizure classification, syndrome identification, and prognostic evaluation. EEG may include routine, prolonged, or ambulatory recordings depending on clinical context and diagnostic yield required.

### Treatment Principles

**Medication is the mainstay treatment strategy for seizures**. When selecting anti-seizure medications, clinicians must balance multiple factors: **efficacy** for the specific seizure type, **adverse events** profile, **cost** of the medication, potential **drug interactions** with other patient medications, **patient preference** regarding administration schedules and side effect profiles, and **availability** of the medication in local settings.

**Monotherapy is the ideal pathway for the treatment of seizures**, as it minimizes adverse effects and drug interactions. Patients should be started on a single appropriate medication at a low dose and titrated to efficacy, adding additional agents only when monotherapy fails to achieve seizure control.

#### Broad-Spectrum vs. Narrow-Spectrum Anti-Seizure Medications

**Broad-spectrum anti-seizure medications** are those with **efficacy across multiple seizure types and epilepsy syndromes**. The list of broad-spectrum medications includes: **levetiracetam, lamotrigine, zonisamide, topiramate, valproic acid, clonazepam, perampanel, clobazam, and rufinamide**. These medications provide flexibility in treating patients with multiple seizure types or where seizure type classification remains uncertain.

**Narrow-spectrum anti-seizure medications** have **more limited efficacy, typically effective for specific seizure types or syndromes**. The list of narrow-spectrum medications includes: **lacosamide, pregabalin, gabapentin, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, ezogabine, phenytoin, and vigabatrin**. These agents are reserved for specific indications where their particular mechanisms provide optimal efficacy with fewer off-target effects.

#### Factors in Anti-Epileptic Agent Selection

When choosing anti-epileptic agents, clinicians should consider the **age of the patient** (as some medications are contraindicated or less effective in specific age groups), the **seizure semiology** (the specific type and characteristics of seizures), and the **clinical setting and contraindications** (such as pregnancy status, hepatic insufficiency, or psychiatric comorbidities).

### Special Considerations

The modern approach to seizure management emphasizes individualized treatment selection based on seizure type, epilepsy syndrome, patient age, comorbidities, and medication side effect profiles. Understanding the mechanisms of action, metabolism, and adverse effects of each medication allows clinicians to make informed decisions that optimize seizure control while minimizing harm and maximizing quality of life for their pediatric patients.
