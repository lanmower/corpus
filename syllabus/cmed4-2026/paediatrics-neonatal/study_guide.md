## 2022 NEONATAL RESUS

### Lecture Notes

#### 2022 Neonatal Resuscitation

Effective neonatal resuscitation begins long before the delivery suite. Anticipation requires careful assessment of maternal factors including age, gestation and parity, and comorbidities or pre-existing illnesses. Similarly, fetal factors must be evaluated, particularly congenital anomalies, whether the pregnancy is singleton or multiple, and estimated fetal weight. All equipment necessary for resuscitation must be checked before delivery to ensure readiness when needed. Temperature control is critical from the moment of birth, as the fetus has been in the warm environment of amniotic fluid. After delivery, warmth must be maintained using either a Servicrib with heater or clean dry towels, with careful attention to remove wet towels from under the baby after use since they paradoxically cool the infant rather than warm it.

Initial assessment of a newborn requiring resuscitation depends on three key questions: Is the baby breathing adequately? Is the heart rate above 100 beats per minute? Is the baby centrally pink? If the answer to all three questions is yes, the baby does not need resuscitation and can proceed to routine care. If any answer is negative, resuscitation must begin immediately. The critical first action is to call for help, as resuscitation is fundamentally a team effort requiring coordinated care from multiple providers.

Understanding neonatal airway anatomy is essential for successful intervention. The airway extends from the nose to the lung tissue, and the head must be positioned neutrally to maintain patency. Suction should only be applied if obvious secretions such as meconium are blocking the nostrils or mouth. However, the ILCOR 2015 guideline provides important guidance regarding meconium exposure: neonates should not be routinely suctioned below the vocal cords even if they are not vigorous. Instead, intubation should be performed if needed to provide oxygenation as soon as possible. Certain congenital abnormalities, particularly choanal atresia, may cause upper airway obstruction and can be life-threatening; in such cases, an oropharyngeal airway may be life-saving.

Management of breathing and oxygenation follows a stepwise approach based on the baby's clinical presentation. A peripherally cyanosed neonate with spontaneous breathing and a heart rate greater than 100 beats per minute requires only supplemental oxygen. The normal respiratory rate in a neonate is 40 to 60 breaths per minute, and deviations from this range should prompt intervention. If a neonate is cyanotic with a heart rate less than 100 or is experiencing apnoea, breaths should be provided using bag-mask ventilation or neopuff for 30 seconds before reassessing the clinical status. If the baby is still not breathing after this initial intervention, ventilation must be continued manually. When providing breaths during resuscitation, it is critical to provide an adequate number—neither hyperventilating nor hypoventilating. A helpful technique is to use a counting rhythm such as "breath and 2 and 3 and breathe and 2 and 3 and breathe," which helps maintain appropriate ventilation rate. Bag-mask ventilation is effective in most neonates, though providers must take care not to hold down the pop-up valve on an ambu-bag, as increased pressures significantly increase the risk of air leak syndromes. If resuscitation must continue for an extended period, intubation may be considered to provide more definitive airway control.

The circulatory assessment and management of bradycardia form another critical component of neonatal resuscitation. The normal heart rate in a neonate is 120 to 160 beats per minute. During resuscitation, the heart rate should be assessed by palpating either the brachial or femoral pulse, and reassessment should occur after every 30 seconds of intervention. Chest compressions are indicated when the heart rate falls below 60 beats per minute. The proper technique for neonatal chest compressions involves using both hands encircling the chest with the thumbs positioned in front, specifically on the sternum in the midline along the nipple line. The compression depth must be at least one-third of the chest circumference. The compression-to-breath ratio is 3:1, meaning three chest compressions are delivered for every one breath. The rate of compression should match the normal heart rate as closely as possible, with compressions coordinated with the assisted breaths. If the heart rate remains below 60 beats per minute after 30 seconds of chest compressions, the next step is to administer necessary medications, beginning with adrenaline.

Vascular access is essential during neonatal resuscitation for both fluid administration and medication delivery. While peripheral intravenous access is preferred, if it is not easily available, an umbilical venous catheter should be inserted promptly to ensure reliable vascular access.

Adrenaline is the primary medication used in neonatal resuscitation and is indicated if the baby remains bradycardic (heart rate below 60 beats per minute) after 30 seconds of chest compressions. The concentration used is 1:10,000 solution, which is prepared by mixing 1 millilitre of adrenaline with 9 millilitres of saline. The dose is 0.01 milligrams per kilogram administered intravenously, and this dose may be repeated every 3 to 5 minutes as needed. Dextrose should be administered if the baby is hypoglycaemic in order to correct the hypoglycaemia. Sodium bicarbonate, by contrast, is not used routinely in neonatal resuscitation and should not be given as part of standard protocol.

The outcome and post-resuscitation management depend on the baby's response to these interventions. If the baby responds well at any stage during resuscitation, admission for observations for at least 24 hours is still necessary. If the baby shows some response with signs of life but remains critically ill, admission to ICU or high care may be needed for ongoing support including intubation, mechanical ventilation, and inotropic support. If the baby shows no response to resuscitation, the outcome is death. The decision to discontinue resuscitation should never be made unilaterally; a senior clinician must be consulted before stopping any resuscitation attempt. Resuscitation should be stopped after 10 minutes if no heart rate is present, or after 20 minutes if there has been no spontaneous breathing. It is important to differentiate gasping, which is usually a terminal sign, from actual breathing when making these assessments.

## 2024 APNEA

### Lecture Notes

#### 2024 Apnoea of Prematurity

Apnoea in the neonate is defined as cessation of breathing or respiratory airflow and is classified by duration and associated signs. Episodes lasting twenty seconds or longer, whether accompanied by cyanosis, pallor, or bradycardia below 100 beats per minute, constitute significant apnoea. Episodes shorter than twenty seconds are recorded as apnoea only if they occur in association with cyanosis, pallor, or bradycardia. Periodic breathing, predominantly seen between two and four weeks of age and resolving by six months, represents brief episodes of apnoea occurring in short cycles of five to ten seconds and is distinct from true pathological apnoea.

Apnoea of prematurity demonstrates a clear inverse relationship with gestational age. Almost every infant born before twenty-eight weeks gestation will experience at least one apnoeic episode, and at least eighty-five percent of infants born before thirty-four weeks will have apnoeic episodes. This contrasts sharply with term infants, in whom apnoea occurs in only one per one thousand births and typically indicates serious underlying disease.

The physiological basis for breathing comprises two interdependent systems. Control of respiration involves the cerebral motor cortex for voluntary breathing and the brainstem for involuntary control, particularly the medulla oblongata as the primary respiratory control centre. The dorsal respiratory group of the medulla stimulates inspiratory movements while the ventral respiratory group initiates expiratory movements; the medulla also controls non-respiratory air movements such as coughing and sneezing. The pons above the medulla regulates the rate of involuntary respiration through the apneustic centre, which sends signals for long and deep breaths increasing tidal volume, and the pneumotaxic centre, which inhibits inspiration and decreases tidal volume by inhibiting both the phrenic nerve and the apneustic centre. The Hering-Breuer reflex provides feedback through stretch receptors in pulmonary muscles at maximum inspiration, inhibiting the apneustic centre. Afferent signals reach the brainstem from the glossopharyngeal nerve (carrying signals from the carotid body) and the vagus nerve (bringing information from aortic arch chemoreceptors, lungs, and irritant receptors). Central chemoreceptors on the surface of the medulla sense hydrogen ion concentration and carbon dioxide, with increased carbon dioxide leading to decreased pH through production of hydrogen ions from carbonic acid. Peripheral chemoreceptors in the aortic arch and carotid bodies measure arterial oxygen tension. Efferent control passes via the phrenic nerve at spinal levels C3, C4, and C5 (remembered by the phrase "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive") to the diaphragm and through thoracic nerves to accessory muscles of respiration. The ventilation system itself comprises the upper airways (nostril, nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses, pharynx, larynx), lower airways (trachea, bronchi, bronchioles), gas exchange units (alveoli), respiratory muscles (diaphragm as primary muscle, with intercostal, sternocleidomastoid, and abdominal wall muscles as accessory muscles), and supporting structures of bone, cartilage, and connective tissue.

The pathogenesis of apnoea of prematurity reflects multiple interacting mechanisms. Developmental immaturity of the central respiratory drive represents the foundational factor, as preterm infants have incompletely mature brainstem respiratory centres. Neonates paradoxically respond to hypoxia and hypercapnoea with apnoea rather than increased breathing due to foetal and neonatal transition physiology in which hypoxia and hypercapnoea initially activate protective apneic responses. Reflexive depression of breathing occurs through vagally mediated pathways, particularly the laryngeal chemical reflex that depresses breathing during feeding. Preterm infants show increased sensitivity to stressors including toxins, hypoglycaemia, hypothermia, and anaemia. The respiratory muscles and chest wall are more easily fatigable in preterm infants, and the thoracic wall itself is more pliable, greatly increasing the work of breathing and prone these infants are to respiratory muscle fatigue. Finally, the obligatory nasal breathing pattern of neonates, combined with anatomical susceptibility to airway obstruction, creates inherent vulnerability to apnoeic episodes.

Apnoea is classified into four distinct types. Central apnoea comprises approximately twenty percent of cases and occurs when inspiratory effort is absent, reflecting failure of the central respiratory drive. Obstructive apnoea comprises approximately twenty percent of cases and occurs when inspiratory efforts persist but the airway becomes obstructed, preventing airflow despite continuing respiratory effort. Reflex apnoea is vagally mediated apnoea triggered by specific stimuli. Mixed apnoea comprises approximately sixty percent of cases and involves airway obstruction with inspiratory effort preceding or following a central apnoea episode, combining elements of both central and obstructive mechanisms.

Central apnoea in preterm infants results from failure of respiratory drive and carries numerous causes. Prematurity itself is the baseline risk factor, while hypoxia depresses the central respiratory centres. Acidosis directly depresses brainstem respiratory control centres and reduces respiratory drive. Central nervous system lesions including intraventricular hemorrhage, seizures, and meningitis disrupt the anatomical structures responsible for respiratory control. Necrotising enterocolitis, as a serious gastrointestinal pathology, contributes to systemic disturbance triggering apnoea. Ambient temperature fluctuations stress immature thermoregulatory systems and increase metabolic demands, destabilising respiratory control. Metabolic derangements including hypoglycaemia and electrolyte abnormalities including hypokalaemia and hyponatraemia depress respiratory centres. Anaemia reduces oxygen-carrying capacity and tissue oxygenation, increasing chemoreceptor sensitivity. Certain drugs including phenobarbitone and sedatives depress respiratory drive. Pain and excessive handling stress preterm infants and increase metabolic demands. Cardiac abnormalities including patent ductus arteriosus and heart failure impair cardiac output and perfusion, triggering apnoea.

Obstructive apnoea occurs because neonates are obligatory nose breathers and cannot switch to oral breathing when nasal obstruction occurs. Congenital causes include choanal atresia or stenosis, which directly blocks the nasal airway, macroglossia with physical obstruction of the nasopharynx, and micrognathia with reduced oral airway space and posterior tongue displacement. Positional factors matter significantly; excessive neck flexion or extension compresses the nasopharynx or airway structures, while secretions can physically obstruct the small neonatal airway.

Reflex apnoea is triggered by specific neonatal conditions and procedures. Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease causes acid aspiration and irritation of laryngeal chemoreceptors, triggering vagally mediated reflex apnoea. Overfeeding causes gastric distension and oesophageal irritation, activating the laryngeal chemoreflex and vagal pathways. Deep suctioning irritates laryngeal chemoreceptors and vagal afferents, triggering the protective laryngeal chemoreflex that depresses breathing. Intubation irritates the larynx and upper airway, activating vagal reflexes that suppress spontaneous breathing.

Mixed apnoea reflects a combination of central, reflex, and obstructive mechanisms and may occur as airway obstruction with inspiratory effort precedes or follows central apnoea episodes.

Assessment of apnoea begins with careful history to differentiate true apnoea from periodic breathing or breath holding, gathering antenatal, perinatal, postnatal, and feeding history. Family history should explore seizures, sudden infant deaths, serious family illnesses, and consanguinity. Social history identifies toxins, drugs, medications, tobacco smoke, and carbon monoxide exposure. Associated symptoms including sleep issues, snoring, and mouth breathing should be documented. Examination assesses for congenital anomalies and genetic syndromes, signs of congenital infection, inborn error of metabolism stigmata, fever or hypothermia, tachypnoea, and stridor.

Monitoring of apnoea employs multiple complementary techniques. Pulse oximetry measuring SpO2 is readily available but is limited by movement-related false alarms, though newer pulse oximetry models show greater resistance to motion artefact. Abdominal pressure monitoring tapes a diaphragm sensor to the abdominal wall to detect respiratory effort. Transthoracic impedance pneumography places electrodes above and below the diaphragm on the infant's chest but cannot identify obstructive apnoea well because it measures electrical impedance changes rather than airflow. Nasal thermistry attaches a device above the upper lip to detect temperature changes caused by airflow; cessation of airflow eliminates temperature changes and signals apnoea. Respiratory inductance plethysmography positions coil bands around the infant's abdomen and chest to estimate tidal volume in addition to detecting breathing effort.

Management of apnoea begins with identifying and treating the underlying cause. Respiratory support options include nasal oxygen at fractional oxygen concentrations, warm and humidified high-flow nasal cannula which maintains airway patency and reduces airway resistance, and nasal continuous positive airway pressure or synchronized intermittent positive airway pressure which provide positive end-expiratory pressure to prevent airway collapse; mechanical ventilation is reserved for severe cases. The positive end-expiratory pressure provided by non-invasive support prevents obstructive apnoea by maintaining positive pressure throughout the airway to prevent pharyngeal collapse.

Methylxanthines, particularly caffeine and aminophylline, serve as central respiratory stimulants and represent the pharmacological mainstay of apnoea management. Theophylline is not used despite being a methylxanthine due to unfavourable pharmacokinetics, unpredictable metabolism, and a narrow therapeutic window in preterm infants. Caffeine is formulated as either caffeine citrate or caffeine base. The mechanism of methylxanthines involves central blockade of GABA-ergic neurons through adenosine A2a receptor antagonism, resulting in decreased release of gamma-aminobutyric acid, the main inhibitory neurotransmitter. Methylxanthines preserve brain ATP levels during hypoxia and ischaemia, maintaining cellular energy homeostasis and protecting neurons from injury. They improve respiratory muscle function and enhance central and peripheral chemoreceptor responsiveness to carbon dioxide and oxygen changes, improving overall respiratory control. The Caffeine Therapy for Apnea of Prematurity trial, published by Schmidt and colleagues in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2006, demonstrated the efficacy of caffeine in reducing apnoea episodes and showed in eighteen-month follow-up that neonates treated with caffeine had significantly improved survival without neurodevelopmental disability. Side effects of methylxanthines include increased energy expenditure and oxygen consumption, which may decrease growth due to calories redirected from growth, and potential behavioural problems in later childhood, though evidence for long-term neurobehavioural effects remains uncertain.

Short-term consequences of apnoea episodes include hypoxaemia, bradycardia below 100 beats per minute, impaired perfusion with drop in blood pressure, decreased cerebral perfusion followed by reflex hyperperfusion. Apnoea is associated with poor neurodevelopmental outcome overall, although long-term effects are controversial and establishing a causal relationship is difficult in patients with co-existing medical problems. Infants born before thirty-two weeks gestation have a three-times-higher risk of sudden infant death syndrome compared to term infants.

Sudden Infant Death Syndrome is defined as the sudden unexplained death of a child less than one year of age and is most commonly seen between two and four months of age, becoming almost unheard of after six months. Peak risk occurs at this age range when protective arousal reflexes are immature and respiratory control is transitioning. Primary prevention focuses on sleeping position with infants placed on the back, avoiding blankets and pillows which can obstruct the nose and mouth and increase suffocation and carbon dioxide rebreathing risk, using a firm mattress to prevent the infant from sinking into soft surfaces, avoiding parental smoking which impairs respiratory chemoreceptor function and arousal responses and may increase airway inflammation, avoiding overheating which reduces arousal responses and impairs thermoregulatory responses, and avoiding bed-sharing which increases risk of accidental suffocation, entrapment, and reduced infant arousal. Protective factors that reduce SIDS risk include breastfeeding which improves infant arousal responses, enhances immune function, and reduces respiratory infections, and pacifier use which enhances arousal responses, maintains oral airway patency, and may improve protective airway reflexes during sleep. Infants born before thirty-two weeks gestation have immature arousal reflexes, chemoreceptor dysfunction, and altered sleep architecture predisposing them to SIDS.

## 2024 HYPOGLACEMIA IN NEONATES

### Lecture Notes

#### 2024 Neonatal Hypoglycaemia

Neonatal hypoglycaemia is defined as a whole blood glucose concentration below 2.6 mmol/ℓ. Severity is stratified into three categories: severe hypoglycaemia is diagnosed when whole blood glucose is 1.7 mmol/ℓ or lower, or when any symptomatic baby presents with low blood glucose regardless of the exact value; mild hypoglycaemia spans the range 1.8 to 2.5 mmol/ℓ in asymptomatic well babies; and normal glucose is 2.6 mmol/ℓ or above. Whole blood glucose measurement using glucometers and blood gas analysers is the standard bedside approach, with whole blood readings typically 15% or approximately 0.5 mmol/ℓ lower than serum glucose measured in the laboratory. Abnormal whole blood readings must be acted upon immediately without waiting for laboratory confirmation, though persistent hypoglycaemia should be confirmed with a serum glucose sample.

Glucose requirements differ by maturity: term babies require 6 to 8 mg/kg/minute to maintain euglycaemia, while preterm infants require 4 to 6 mg/kg/minute. The formula for calculating dextrose delivery rate in mg/kg/minute is (ml/kg/24 hours) × (% dextrose) × 0.007. Symptomatic or severe hypoglycaemia requires immediate treatment. The first intervention is administration of milk by nasogastric tube if the baby has not been fed within the previous two hours, given whilst preparing IV access. If milk feeds are contraindicated, give 2 to 5 ml/kg of 10% dextrose water as an intravenous bolus. If peripheral IV access cannot be established urgently, glucagon is given intramuscularly at a dose of 0.2 mg/kg, with a maximum single dose of 1 mg; this can be repeated after 20 minutes if needed. An emergency umbilical venous catheter should be inserted if peripheral IV access cannot be secured.

Once initial blood glucose rises above 2.5 mmol/ℓ, IV maintenance is started with 10% dextrose at 60 to 120 ml/kg/24 hours depending on gestational age and day of life, taking care not to exceed 120 ml/kg/day on day one. If hypoglycaemia persists despite IV dextrose, the volume should be increased or the dextrose concentration escalated sequentially through 12.5%, 15%, and 20% if no improvement occurs at each concentration. Glucose concentrations exceeding 12.5% must be infused through a central line; however, an emergency UVC can be used for short-term control during transfer to a referral centre. 50% glucose must never be administered intravenously or orally due to its hypertonicity. Recheck blood glucose every 30 minutes after intervention until normal, then 2-hourly and/or before feeds until the baby achieves 2.6 mmol/ℓ or higher for two consecutive readings.

Neonatal hypoglycaemia most commonly affects infants with established risk factors. These include prematurity and post-maturity, small for gestational age and large for gestational age infants, intrauterine growth restriction, and infants of diabetic mothers who often develop transient hyperinsulinism. Perinatal stress represents a major category of risk, encompassing hypoxia, meconium aspiration syndrome, haemolytic disease, hypothermia, fluid restriction, and polycythaemia. Family history of genetic hypoglycaemia disorders and congenital syndromes or midline brain abnormalities such as Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome and hypopituitarism carry specific risk. Healthy term infants without risk factors who are establishing normal breast or formula feeding do not require blood glucose monitoring.

Symptomatic hypoglycaemia presents with non-specific and frequently absent signs. When symptoms do occur, they include jitteriness, sweating, tachypnoea, tachycardia, pallor, cyanosis, poor suck and feeding difficulty, weak or high-pitched cry with irritability, decreased level of consciousness, seizures, hypotonia, and apnoeas. Because many hypoglycaemic babies remain asymptomatic, routine screening is essential for all at-risk infants during the first 24 hours: perform testing after the first feed, then before subsequent feeds or at 6 to 8 hourly intervals if the baby is not being fed.

For asymptomatic well babies with glucose readings in the mild range of 1.8 to 2.5 mmol/ℓ, the first-line management is to give milk immediately or start IV fluids if feeding is contraindicated, ensure normothermia, and recheck blood glucose after 30 minutes. If glucose rises to 2.6 mmol/ℓ or above, continue monitoring hourly for three hours, then 3-hourly for 24 hours. If glucose is still below 2.6 mmol/ℓ but increasing, recheck after another 30 minutes. If glucose is neither increasing nor decreasing, increase feed frequency to every two hours and increase daily feed volume by 20 to 30 ml/kg/day, rechecking in 30 minutes. If glucose is decreasing or remains below 2.6 mmol/ℓ, manage according to the severe hypoglycaemia protocol.

The commonest cause of persistent neonatal hypoglycaemia—defined as persistently low blood glucose beyond 48 hours of life or requirement of parenteral glucose infusions beyond 48 hours—is hyperinsulinism, in which elevated insulin levels are detected despite low blood glucose. Hyperinsulinism is frequently transient and typically resolves within the first few weeks of life in association with infants of diabetic mothers, intrauterine growth restriction, and intrapartum stress, though persistent forms require specialist referral. At the time of any documented hypoglycaemia episode, a critical sample of blood must be drawn to measure serum glucose, insulin, and cortisol for diagnostic purposes. Hydrocortisone is given at a dose of 1 to 2 mg/kg IV or orally every 6 hours for hypoglycaemia secondary to cortisol deficiency and for persistent hypoglycaemia not responding to maximum IV glucose infusion.

Diazoxide is the first-line pharmacological agent for neonatal hyperinsulinism and is given at a dose of 2 to 6 mg/kg/day intravenously or orally in three divided doses (every 8 hours). Hydrochlorothiazide is used as an adjunct at a dose of 1 to 3 mg/kg/dose given orally every 6 to 12 hours; it potentiates the effect of diazoxide and enhances glucose homeostasis in hyperinsulinism. Both agents are typically continued under specialist guidance until hyperinsulinism resolves.

Neonatal hyperglycaemia, defined as whole blood glucose above 7.0 mmol/ℓ or plasma (laboratory) glucose above 8.3 mmol/ℓ, is frequently observed particularly in extremely low birth weight infants and does not always require intervention. However, when plasma glucose exceeds 11 mmol/ℓ, osmotic diuresis and polyuria may develop. Glycosuria is not a reliable marker for hyperglycaemia. Blood glucose monitoring is required 6 to 8 hourly in the first 24 to 48 hours (and more frequently if abnormal) for babies receiving IV glucose infusions, extremely low birth weight infants, septic babies, intubated babies, babies with intraventricular haemorrhages, and babies receiving inotropes, corticosteroids, or insulin.

Hyperglycaemia above 10 mmol/ℓ is managed with a stepwise approach. The first step is to decrease the parenteral glucose infusion rate to 4 to 6 mg/kg/minute by reducing the dextrose concentration from 10% to 5% (not below 5%) and/or by decreasing the volume. Underlying causes must be actively investigated and treated, including performing a septic screen with blood cultures and initiating broad-spectrum antibiotics, obtaining cranial ultrasound to exclude intraventricular haemorrhage, and consulting a paediatric endocrinologist if neonatal diabetes mellitus is suspected. When persistent hyperglycaemia at 12 mmol/ℓ or above develops despite reduction of glucose infusion rate—always confirmed with a laboratory or blood gas analyser reading before starting—insulin therapy is considered. Insulin is never given as a bolus; specialist consultation and referral to the neonatal unit per local pathways is mandatory before initiation.

Neonatal insulin infusion requires specific preparation and monitoring. The solution is prepared by adding 2 ml (100 units) of Actrapid to 10 ml of normal saline, then adding 1 ml/kg of this solution to 50 ml of 0.9% normal saline to make a total volume of 50 ml; at this concentration, 0.1 ml/hour delivers 0.02 units/kg/hour. Plastic tubing must be primed with insulin before use because insulin binds to tubing, resulting in decreased availability to the baby. The insulin infusion rate ranges from 0.02 to 0.5 units/kg/hour. Blood glucose is monitored hourly during insulin infusion, and the infusion is immediately stopped when blood glucose falls below 8 mmol/ℓ.

The insulin infusion rate is titrated according to blood glucose level using the following scale: for blood glucose above 25 mmol/ℓ, infuse at 1 ml/hour; for 20 to 25 mmol/ℓ, infuse at 0.5 ml/hour; for 18 to 19 mmol/ℓ, infuse at 0.4 ml/hour; for 15 to 17 mmol/ℓ, infuse at 0.3 ml/hour; for 12 to 14 mmol/ℓ, infuse at 0.2 ml/hour; for 8 to 11 mmol/ℓ, infuse at 0.1 ml/hour; and for blood glucose below 8 mmol/ℓ, stop the insulin infusion immediately.

## 2024 KMC

### Lecture Notes

#### 2024 Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC)

Kangaroo Mother Care, defined by the World Health Organization as skin-to-skin contact between a mother and her baby along with exclusive breastfeeding, represents a fundamental shift in how preterm and low birth weight infants are managed in contemporary paediatric practice. The care approach recognizes that when biological mothers are unavailable, any family member or caregiver can adopt the kangaroo care role, ensuring continuity of this critical intervention. Contact should begin as early as possible after birth and continue for as many hours as possible each day, with an ideal duration of 8 to 24 hours daily for preterm and low birth weight babies. This extended contact duration distinguishes kangaroo care from passive holding and forms the foundation of its therapeutic effectiveness.

Kangaroo care is implemented across three distinct clinical presentations. Intermittent kangaroo care is appropriate for infants not yet on full oral feeds who still require oxygen therapy, ranging from several hours daily to once every few days depending on the baby's clinical condition and maternal availability while the infant remains in the nursery setting. Continuous kangaroo care is provided day and night, interrupted only when the mother attends to personal needs, and can be practiced both in hospital and home environments, making it particularly suited to stable low birth weight infants. Immediate kangaroo care is initiated directly after birth in hospital settings for preterm and low birth weight infants requiring close monitoring, enabling early establishment of the therapeutic relationship.

The correct kangaroo position is essential for safety and efficacy. The baby is placed upright against the caregiver's chest with the neck turned to the side and slightly extended, ensuring clear airway positioning. The infant wears a nappy and woolen cap to maintain thermoregulation, and the mother or caregiver is covered together with the baby using clothing, sheet, wrap, or blanket, creating a contained microenvironment that preserves warmth and promotes bonding.

Kangaroo nutrition builds upon exclusive breastmilk feeding with particular emphasis on colostrum provision in the early postnatal period. Initially, tube feeding may be necessary for infants too immature to coordinate suckling, but the rooting reflex improves progressively as the baby matures. Non-nutritive sucking is encouraged throughout the care period, promoting oral motor development and facilitating the transition to direct breastfeeding.

Support within the kangaroo care model is multifaceted and shared. Healthcare workers provide emotional support, education, empowerment of mothers to perform care competently, and structured follow-up post-discharge. Family members assist in continuing the practice at home and step in when the biological mother is unable to provide care, ensuring uninterrupted contact despite changing circumstances.

Discharge from kangaroo care follows clear clinical criteria: the baby must be well, actively breastfeeding, and gaining weight steadily, with continuation until the baby chooses not to be in the kangaroo position or once weight exceeds 2500 grams. Maternal discharge criteria require that the mother be confident in handling and caring for her baby with available support to continue kangaroo care at home, recognizing the critical importance of sustained engagement after hospital discharge.

The benefits of kangaroo care extend across all stakeholders in the care dyad and system. For mothers, kangaroo care empowers them as primary care providers, builds confidence in caregiving abilities, strengthens maternal-infant attachment and bonding, and reduces the risk of moderate to severe postpartum depression by 24 percent. For infants, the benefits are equally compelling: decreased hypothermia, increased breastmilk production promoting exclusive breastfeeding, decreased hypoglycaemic episodes, and enhanced immune function through protective microbiota acquired from the mother's skin. The approach stimulates oxytocin secretion in both mother and infant, triggering cascading physiological benefits including cardiovascular stability, weight gain, improved sleep architecture, strengthened maternal-fetal bonding, and enhanced brain and cognitive development. Healthcare facilities benefit substantially through significant cost savings achieved via decreased hospital length of stay and improved infant survival rates through reduced overcrowding and nosocomial infection.

This comprehensive approach to perinatal care reflects an evolving vision of maternal-newborn care in which mothers, newborns, parents, and families form an inseparable centre around which the entire maternal-newborn service delivery system is organized, underpinned by close collaboration between health care providers at all levels. Kangaroo care stands alongside four essential interventions for decreasing complications of prematurity—antenatal steroids, continuous positive airway pressure, exogenous surfactant, and kangaroo care itself—each addressing a distinct mechanism of prematurity-related morbidity. Together, these integrated approaches represent the contemporary evidence-based standard for optimizing outcomes in the preterm and low birth weight population.

## 2024 NEONATAL INFECTIONS

### Lecture Notes

#### 2024 Neonatal Infections

Neonatal sepsis is defined as a clinical syndrome of bacteremia with systemic signs and symptoms of infection occurring within the first 28 days of life. Globally, sepsis and meningitis account for approximately 4% of all neonatal deaths, making this a critical area of focus for both prevention and acute management. Understanding the temporal classification of neonatal infections is fundamental to clinical decision-making, as early and late onset infections differ markedly in their epidemiology, causative organisms, and risk factors.

Early neonatal infections occur during the period from birth to 7 days of life and are acquired primarily through vertical transmission from the mother via the birth canal or through ascending infection during prolonged rupture of membranes. The key risk factors for early onset disease include prematurity, maternal chorioamnionitis, prolonged rupture of membranes exceeding 18 hours, and maternal colonization with Group B Streptococcus. In contrast, late neonatal infections develop from day 8 through day 28 of life and are typically acquired in the hospital, home, or community environment. These infections are associated with nosocomial risk factors including prematurity, invasive procedures such as central lines, indwelling devices, mechanical ventilation, and ventricular peritoneal shunts.

The causative organisms of acquired neonatal sepsis fall into distinct microbiological categories. Gram-positive organisms include Group B Streptococcus, which remains the most common cause of early onset sepsis, along with other Streptococcus species, Staphylococcus species (particularly coagulase-negative Staphylococcus in late onset disease), and Listeria monocytogenes. Gram-negative organisms that frequently cause neonatal infection include Escherichia coli, which is particularly prevalent as a cause of both early and late onset disease and meningitis, and Klebsiella pneumoniae. These organisms are acquired through maternal colonization or environmental exposure and vary in their prevalence depending on the timing of infection and the clinical setting.

The clinical presentation of neonatal sepsis is notoriously nonspecific and requires a high index of suspicion for accurate diagnosis. Affected neonates may present with temperature instability, which can manifest as either fever or dangerous hypothermia. Respiratory manifestations include distress and apnea, while feeding difficulties, poor suck reflex, and vomiting are common. Neurological signs may include lethargy and refusal to suckle, and systemic signs include pallor, petechiae, mottling, jaundice, poor perfusion, and unstable blood glucose levels. The vagueness of these presentations underscores why clinical suspicion and a low threshold for investigation remain essential even when individual signs are subtle.

The septic screen forms the cornerstone of investigation in suspected neonatal sepsis and consists of both specific and nonspecific components. Specific investigations include cultures from blood, cerebrospinal fluid, urine, and superficial swabs, along with serology for congenital infections and relevant radiology such as chest X-rays when indicated. Nonspecific investigations provide supportive evidence and include a full blood count, where particular attention is paid to the immature-to-total neutrophil ratio, with a value greater than 0.2 being predictive of infection. Thrombocytopenia may indicate serious infection, white cell count may be either elevated or reduced (normal range 5,000 to 20,000 cells per microliter), and C-reactive protein elevation supports the diagnosis of infection. However, these markers must be interpreted in the clinical context, as no single test is definitively diagnostic.

First-line antimicrobial therapy for suspected neonatal sepsis consists of penicillin, which may be used alone or in combination with an aminoglycoside depending on local protocols and institutional preferences. The duration of antimicrobial treatment differs significantly based on the site of infection and culture results. For proven septicemia confirmed by positive blood cultures, a course of 7 to 10 days is standard. However, when bacterial meningitis is confirmed, either by positive cerebrospinal fluid culture or by compatible clinical and laboratory findings, the duration of therapy extends to 21 days to ensure adequate central nervous system penetration and prevention of relapse. Treatment decisions regarding continuation, modification, or cessation of antibiotics should be guided by culture and sensitivity results once available.

Congenital infections, which are acquired either transplacentally, through ascending infection, or from the birth canal, represent a distinct category with different etiological agents and prevention strategies. The TORCH acronym (Toxoplasma, Other organisms, Rubella, Cytomegalovirus, Herpes, HIV, Syphilis) encompasses the major congenital pathogens, though in the South African context, additional organisms including tuberculosis, hepatitis B, malaria, Listeria monocytogenes, and COVID-19 are also critically important.

Congenital tuberculosis, while rare, carries a gravely high mortality rate of 43.48% in affected South African neonates. The source of infection is primary tuberculosis in the mother, transmitted through the placenta via the umbilical vein just before or during delivery, or through aspiration of infected amniotic fluid. Following placental transmission, tubercle bacilli first reach the liver, causing periportal lymph node involvement with subsequent spread through the main fetal circulation to multiple organs. Diagnosis relies on specimens including gastric aspirate (which yields positive smear or culture in 8 of 9 specimens), endotracheal aspirate (positive in 7 of 7 specimens), lymph node biopsy (positive smear or culture in 7 of 8 specimens with granulomatous changes in all), and liver biopsy (positive in 4 of 6 specimens). TB prophylaxis with isoniazid is the cornerstone of management for TB-exposed neonates, administered for 6 months at approximately 10 mg/kg daily, with dosing adjusted by weight: half a 100 mg tablet for infants weighing less than 5 kg, one tablet for 5.0 to 7.9 kg, one and a half tablets for 8.0 to 10.9 kg, two tablets for 11.0 to 13.9 kg, two and a half tablets for 14.0 to 19.9 kg, three tablets for 20.0 to 24.9 kg, and one 300 mg tablet for those weighing 25 kg or greater. BCG vaccination must be deferred until TB prophylaxis is complete, and EPI vaccinations should continue as per the normal schedule. If TB infection is confirmed during follow-up, treatment proceeds according to pediatric tuberculosis guidelines.

Early congenital syphilis presents with snuffles (nasal congestion), purulent nasal discharge, hepatosplenomegaly, desquamative skin rash particularly on palms and soles, bone changes visible on radiography, pseudoparalysis, pallor, and lymphadenopathy. Late congenital syphilis, manifesting after 2 years of age, presents with characteristic dental changes (Hutchinson's teeth), ocular involvement (interstitial keratitis), eighth nerve involvement (deafness), facial features (frontal bossing, saddle nose, and rhagades), central nervous system manifestations (mental retardation and hydrocephalus), and skeletal changes (sabre shins and Clutton's joints). Prevention of congenital syphilis depends on antenatal screening, which should occur at the first antenatal care visit and again at 32 to 34 weeks gestation if the initial test is negative. All pregnant women with positive syphilis screening tests, irrespective of titer, should receive treatment. For neonates born to mothers with syphilis, management depends on maternal treatment status. Asymptomatic neonates whose mothers received less than three doses of benzathine benzylpenicillin or who deliver within 4 weeks of treatment initiation should receive benzathine penicillin at a dose of 50,000 units per kilogram intramuscularly as a single stat dose. Symptomatic neonates with evidence of disease, including hepatosplenomegaly, pseudoparalysis, snuffles, edema, jaundice, anemia, purpura, or desquamative rash, require more intensive therapy with either procaine penicillin at 50,000 units per kilogram intramuscularly daily for 10 days or penicillin G at 50,000 units per kilogram per dose intravenously 12-hourly for the same 10-day period. Congenital syphilis is a notifiable condition in many jurisdictions.

HIV prevention through mother-to-child transmission (PMTCT) represents one of the most successful public health interventions in modern medicine. The principle of current PMTCT strategy is that all HIV-positive pregnant women should be initiated on antiretroviral therapy as soon as possible, regardless of gestational age, CD4 count, or clinical stage (Option B+ from 2015 onwards), and therapy must continue for life. The preferred first-line regimen for newly diagnosed pregnant women consists of tenofovir, lamivudine, and dolutegravir as a fixed-dose combination, though an alternative of tenofovir, lamivudine, and efavirenz is available when dolutegravir is not suitable; however, careful individualized counseling regarding neural tube defect risk is required when initiating dolutegravir. HIV testing should occur at the first antenatal care visit and at every subsequent visit ideally up to 8 visits total. Viral load monitoring is essential: the first viral load is checked at 3 months on ART in newly diagnosed women and at the booking visit in those already on therapy; if the viral load is below 50 copies per milliliter, repeat testing occurs at delivery. The target for successful PMTCT is a viral load below 50 copies per milliliter. Co-trimoxazole prophylaxis is commenced after 14 weeks gestation, regardless of CD4 count or clinical stage. For HIV-exposed neonates, all infants should receive a birth HIV-PCR to identify transmission occurring in utero. The minimum duration of nevirapine post-exposure prophylaxis is 6 weeks for standard-risk exposed infants. High-risk infants, defined as those born to mothers with viral load at delivery of 1,000 copies per milliliter or greater or where no viral load measurement exists within 12 weeks of antenatal care, those whose mothers initiated ART less than 12 weeks before delivery, or those with treatment interruptions of 4 weeks or more during pregnancy, require additional prophylaxis including zidovudine for the first 6 weeks of life and nevirapine for at least 12 weeks in breastfed infants; in formula-fed high-risk infants, nevirapine may be stopped earlier than 12 weeks. The dosing for ARV medications in neonates aged 0 to 28 days is based on birth weight rather than current weight and includes lamivudine at 4 mg per kilogram twice daily (10 mg/ml formulation), zidovudine at 4 mg per kilogram twice daily (10 mg/ml formulation), and nevirapine at 6 mg per kilogram twice daily (10 mg/ml formulation). For infants weighing 2.5 to 3 kg, this translates to lamivudine 1.0 ml morning and evening, zidovudine 1.0 ml morning and evening, and nevirapine 2.0 ml morning and evening; for 3.1 to 4 kg, lamivudine 1.5 ml, zidovudine 1.5 ml, and nevirapine 2.5 ml; and for 4.1 to 5 kg, lamivudine 2 ml, zidovudine 2 ml, and nevirapine 3.5 ml. Caregivers should be supplied with calibrated syringes for each medication and instructed on proper preparation and administration, with color-coding used when helpful. For neonates identified as HIV-positive by birth-PCR, ART initiation should occur within 7 days of birth, ideally as soon as feeding tolerance is established, through daily contact at primary health care facilities. Baseline assessment before ART initiation includes clinical evaluation and laboratory studies of HIV-PCR, CD4 count, full blood count and hemoglobin, and aspartate aminotransferase. At follow-up visits in the first month, children are reviewed at 1 week to check results and adjust dosing, at 2 weeks to monitor tolerability and compliance, and at 1 month to assess compliance and repeat blood work including full blood count, hemoglobin, and lipid profile before transitioning to a regimen of abacavir, lamivudine, and lopinavir/ritonavir. The peak mortality period for HIV-infected infants in South Africa occurs between 2 and 4 months of age, underscoring the critical importance of rapid diagnosis and early treatment. Exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months is recommended for HIV-exposed infants, with continuation thereafter guided by infant diagnosis status and other factors.

Cytomegalovirus causes both asymptomatic and symptomatic congenital infection. Diagnosis is achieved through blood and urine PCR testing, with IgM being the most sensitive test in infants younger than 3 weeks of age. Symptomatic congenital CMV presents with chorioretinitis, neurological abnormalities including microcephaly, sensorineural hearing impairment, hepatitis, thrombocytopenia, intrauterine growth restriction, jaundice, and sepsis-like presentations. Treatment of symptomatic congenital CMV consists of intravenous ganciclovir at 6 mg per kilogram per dose administered every 12 hours for 6 weeks, followed by oral valganciclovir at 16 mg per kilogram per dose administered every 12 hours to complete a total of 6 months of therapy.

Rubella infection in the neonate presents primarily through serology with IgM antibodies, which are positive in the first month of life. PCR detection of the virus can be accomplished from urine, throat swab, blood, or cerebrospinal fluid and remains positive for approximately 6 months. The classic manifestations of congenital rubella include sensorineural deafness, cataracts, cardiac lesions such as patent ductus arteriosus or peripheral pulmonary stenosis, thrombocytopenia, hepatosplenomegaly, microcephaly, growth failure, and the distinctive blueberry muffin baby appearance caused by extramedullary hematopoiesis. Unfortunately, there is no specific treatment for congenital rubella; infants remain infectious and require appropriate infection control precautions. Management is primarily supportive with essential ophthalmology and hearing screening and long-term neurodevelopmental follow-up.

Herpes simplex virus infection in the neonate is rarely congenital, with most infections acquired postnatally. Manifestations include characteristic vesicular skin lesions and disseminated disease with severe pneumonitis or encephalitis, which carries a mortality rate of 50 to 60 percent without treatment. Management combines supportive care including parenteral nutrition and mechanical ventilation when necessary with specific therapy using intravenous aciclovir. Investigation includes HSV-PCR from skin lesions, blood, and cerebrospinal fluid. For HSV encephalitis, high-dose aciclovir is administered at 20 mg per kilogram every 8 hours for 3 weeks, while for disease limited to skin, eyes, and mouth (SEM disease), the same dose is continued for 2 weeks. Throughout treatment, disease activity should be monitored clinically and through repeat PCR testing as appropriate.

Toxoplasma gondii congenital infection typically presents as intrauterine growth restriction, intrauterine fetal death, or stillbirth. Clinical manifestations in surviving infants include encephalitis, hydrocephalus, cataracts, and chorioretinitis. Diagnosis depends on serology detecting IgM and IgG antibodies, along with molecular testing including CMV, EBV, and Rubella PCR. Critically, all infants exposed to Toxoplasma at any stage of pregnancy may experience lifelong infection, making diagnosis and initiation of appropriate therapy essential. Prevention in pregnant women focuses on dietary measures including avoiding raw or undercooked meat (lamb, pork, beef, poultry), avoiding unpasteurized milk products and soft cheeses, and thoroughly washing or peeling raw fruits and vegetables with careful hand hygiene after handling uncooked food.

Infection prevention and control measures form the foundation of neonatal infection prevention. Early skin-to-skin contact between neonate and mother, known as kangaroo mother care, promotes immune transfer and protective bonding. Exclusive breastfeeding provides passive immunological protection through maternal antibodies, particularly IgA, and should be promoted as a standard practice. Strict hand washing before and after contact with the neonate, along with aseptic technique during all invasive procedures, significantly reduces nosocomial infection risk. Duration of mechanical ventilation should be actively managed with regular weaning assessments and discontinuation as soon as clinically safe, as prolonged ventilation increases bacterial colonization and infection risk.

Prevention of congenital infections operates through four interconnected pillars established in current South African Department of Health guidelines. Pillar 1 focuses on primary prevention of HIV, sexually transmitted infections, and tuberculosis, particularly among women of childbearing age. Pillar 2 addresses prevention of unintended pregnancies among women already diagnosed with these conditions. Pillar 3 encompasses preventing transmission of maternal HIV, syphilis, and tuberculosis to the infant through appropriate antenatal screening, treatment, and delivery management. Pillar 4 ensures appropriate treatment, care, and support to the entire family unit including mothers, children, partners, and extended family. These measures work synergistically: antenatal syphilis testing at the first care visit and again at 32 to 34 weeks allows identification and treatment of maternal infection; PMTCT programs ensure viral suppression at delivery; TB screening and preventive therapy protect exposed neonates; education about respiratory and viral pathogen exposure helps pregnant women reduce risk of infection; condom use and barrier precautions during sexual contact prevent acquisition; blood and needle precautions prevent bloodborne pathogen transmission; dietary counseling on food safety and handwashing with running water reduce risk of toxoplasmosis and other foodborne infections; and use of insecticide-treated bed nets protects against malaria in endemic areas. Additionally, pregnant women must take special precautions with children in their household who have respiratory or rash symptoms, using careful hand washing, avoiding close contact such as mouth kissing, and avoiding shared towels. Effective management of maternal infections and prevention of prolonged rupture of membranes through appropriate intrapartum care complete the comprehensive approach to reducing neonatal infection risk. The overarching key message is that most congenital infections are preventable and treatable, particularly when detected early, with excellent outcomes achievable through prevention and cure where treatment is available.

## 2025 BIRTH DEFECTS

### Lecture Notes

#### 2025 Birth Defects

Birth defects represent a spectrum of structural and functional abnormalities present at birth, arising from diverse genetic and environmental causes. Approximately two to five percent of all live-born infants have at least one congenital abnormality, while one to two percent carry chromosome abnormalities or single gene disorders. By adulthood, five to ten percent of the population manifests some form of genetic disorder. The etiology of birth defects follows a predictable distribution: approximately forty percent result from preconceptual genetic factors (chromosomal, single gene, or multifactorial), ten percent from post-conception non-genetic causes such as teratogens or mechanical constraint, and fifty percent remain of unknown aetiology. Malformations, the most common form of birth defect, develop during the first trimester when the embryo fails to develop normally, affecting one or more organ systems. Birth prevalence measures the frequency of a defect per one thousand live births, while population prevalence reflects the proportion within the general population.

The foundation for understanding birth defects begins with cytogenetics and basic inheritance principles. The human cell nucleus contains chromosomes, which package and organize the genetic material DNA into functional units called genes. Normal humans possess a diploid number of forty-six chromosomes—twenty-two pairs of autosomes plus one pair of sex chromosomes—inherited one from each parent. Each chromosome contains genetic material derived fifty-fifty from maternal and paternal contributions. During meiosis, the process by which gametes are formed, chromosome pairs normally divide and separate equally so that each gamete receives the haploid number of twenty-three chromosomes. The ovum thus contains twenty-two autosomes plus one X chromosome, while sperm carry either twenty-two autosomes plus X or twenty-two autosomes plus Y, determining offspring sex. Non-disjunction, the abnormal failure of parent chromosomes to separate during gamete formation, generates abnormal gametes: one with twenty-four chromosomes and one with twenty-two. When an abnormal gamete fertilizes a normal one, the resulting zygote may carry forty-seven chromosomes (trisomy) or forty-five chromosomes (monosomy). A trisomy results from the fusion of a gamete with twenty-four chromosomes (carrying an extra chromosome) with a normal gamete carrying twenty-three. Monosomy arises conversely when a gamete lacking a chromosome (twenty-two) combines with a normal gamete. Turner syndrome (45,X) stands as the only monosomy viable to postnatal life; all other autosomal monosomies are incompatible with survival beyond the first trimester.

Mosaicism occurs when an individual contains two or more genetically distinct cell lines derived from the same fertilized zygote. This typically arises when an aneuploid cell (such as a monosomy cell) dies during early development, and the remaining trisomy cells continue dividing and populating tissues, creating a mixed population of normal and aneuploid cells. Chromosomal structural aberrations extend beyond numerical abnormalities. Translocation involves the transfer of a chromosome segment to a different chromosome: balanced translocations involve no net loss or gain of genetic material, leaving carriers clinically unaffected but at risk of transmitting unbalanced translocations to offspring—a mechanism responsible for recurrent miscarriage in some families. Unbalanced translocations result in genetic material being lost or gained. Deletion refers to the loss of a chromosomal segment of variable length. Duplication occurs when a chromosomal segment copies itself, resulting in a chromosome with two identical pieces. Ring chromosomes form when both ends of a chromosome break and the broken ends fuse together, creating a ring structure.

The most frequently occurring chromosomal abnormalities are the autosmal trisomies. Trisomy 21, or Down syndrome, affects approximately one in seven hundred live-born infants and represents the most common chromosomal disorder compatible with prolonged life. The karyotype is 47,XX,+21 in females or 47,XY,+21 in males. Down syndrome manifests a distinctive constellation of features across multiple organ systems. Intellectual disability ranges from mild to severe, accompanied by generalized hypotonia in infancy. The characteristic facial appearance includes a flat facial profile, upslanting palpebral fissures, low nasal bridge, and micrognathia, features that may be subtle in early infancy but become more pronounced with age. A single transverse palmar crease, present in approximately fifty percent of individuals with Down syndrome, serves as a helpful clinical sign. Cardiac involvement occurs in forty to sixty percent of infants with Down syndrome, with atrioventricular canal defects (endocardial cushion defects) being particularly characteristic, though ventricular septal defects and other lesions also occur. Down syndrome patients have significantly elevated risk for acute leukemia and thyroid disorders. Growth retardation and increased susceptibility to infections further characterize the syndrome.

Trisomy 18, known as Edwards syndrome, occurs in approximately one of every six thousand live births and presents a much more severe phenotype than Down syndrome. The karyotype is 47,XX,+18 or 47,XY,+18. Edwards syndrome is characterized by severe intrauterine and postnatal growth restriction, profound intellectual disability, and multiple structural malformations affecting nearly every organ system. A distinctive physical sign is the characteristic clenched fist with overlapping fingers (second and fifth fingers overlap the third and fourth), present in a high proportion of cases. Rocker-bottom feet, microcephaly, micrognathia, and low-set ears are consistent findings. Congenital heart defects occur in ninety percent of cases, including ventricular septal defects, patent ductus arteriosus, and atrial septal defects. Renal anomalies, cleft lip and palate, and a variety of other malformations are common. The prognosis is grave: most infants with Edwards syndrome die within the first year of life, and survivors often have severe developmental impairment. Trisomy 13, or Patau syndrome, represents the third common autosomal trisomy, occurring in approximately one of every ten thousand live births. The karyotype is 47,XX,+13 or 47,XY,+13. Like Edwards syndrome, Patau syndrome causes severe growth restriction and profound developmental abnormalities. A hallmark feature is holoprosencephaly, a failure of the embryonic forebrain to divide into two hemispheres, ranging in severity from alobar (single ventricle, most severe with cyclopia) through semilobar (partial division) to lobar (nearly complete separation but with abnormal olfactory bulbs and tracts), and invariably associated with severe facial midline defects. Cleft lip and palate, often bilateral, are present in many cases. Polydactyly, characteristically postaxial (extra digit on the lateral side), occurs frequently. Additional findings include rocker-bottom feet, microcephaly, micrognathia, renal anomalies, cryptorchidism (failure of testicular descent), and scalp defects. Congenital heart defects are nearly universal. Most infants with Patau syndrome die within the first year of life.

Sex chromosome aneuploidies present a different phenotypic spectrum. Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY) is the most common sex chromosome disorder in males, occurring in approximately one of every five hundred to one thousand male births. Many cases remain undiagnosed throughout life because affected males may be asymptomatic in childhood. The characteristic features include tall stature with disproportionately long legs relative to the trunk, primary hypogonadism with small and soft testes, infertility due to azoospermia or severe oligozoospermia, reduced testosterone levels leading to decreased facial and body hair growth, development of gynecomastia (breast tissue development), and increased risk of mild developmental delay and learning difficulties. Some individuals manifest behavioral or emotional difficulties. Turner syndrome (45,X), in contrast, is the only viable monosomy and occurs in approximately one of every twenty-five hundred female live births. The clinical presentation varies considerably depending on whether complete monosomy or mosaicism is present. Classic features include short stature—often the most obvious finding—and primary ovarian dysgenesis with resultant infertility. Characteristic facial features include a broad, flat face, micrognathia, and low-set ears. Many individuals develop a webbed neck and shield chest. Cardiovascular malformations occur in approximately thirty to forty percent and include bicuspid aortic valve, aortic stenosis, coarctation of the aorta, and hypoplastic left heart syndrome. Renal anomalies, including renal agenesis, hypoplasia, and duplications, occur in a substantial minority. Intelligence is typically normal, though some individuals have learning difficulties or spatial reasoning deficits. Additional features may include lymphedema (particularly of the hands and feet), hearing loss (both conductive and sensorineural), and increased risk of hypertension.

Among autosomal dominant conditions, Marfan syndrome stands as a prototype. Caused by mutations in the FBN1 gene located on chromosome fifteen, Marfan syndrome has a prevalence of approximately one in five thousand. The disorder manifests across skeletal, ocular, and cardiovascular systems. Skeletal features include tall stature with characteristic body proportions—notably, the arm span typically exceeds height. Arachnodactyly (abnormally long, slender fingers and toes) is a hallmark sign, best assessed by the Walker-Murdoch wrist sign (when the thumb and middle finger of the same hand are wrapped around the contralateral wrist, the distal phalanx of the thumb extends well beyond the ulnar border in Marfan syndrome) and the Steinberg thumb sign (when the thumb is opposed across a closed fist, it extends beyond the ulnar border). Additional skeletal features include long, slender feet, pectus excavatum or pectus carinatum (anterior chest deformity), scoliosis, and lumbar lordosis. The ocular manifestation of greatest clinical significance is ectopia lentis—subluxation or dislocation of the lens—which occurs in approximately half of patients and can lead to vision loss if untreated. The most life-threatening feature is cardiovascular: aortic root dilatation with progressive aortic aneurysm occurs in a substantial minority and carries risk of aortic dissection and sudden death. This complication necessitates regular echocardiographic surveillance and often beta-blocker or angiotensin receptor blocker therapy to slow aortic root progression.

Waardenburg syndrome, an autosomal dominant condition with a prevalence of one in thirty thousand, demonstrates the phenomenon of genetic heterogeneity, with approximately two-thirds of cases inherited from an affected parent and one-third arising from de novo mutations (notably associated with advanced paternal age). The clinical hallmark is heterochromia iridis—eyes of different colors—though blue eyes are common in all variants. Sensorineural hearing loss, often congenital, occurs in a substantial proportion and may be unilateral or bilateral and of variable severity. A white forelock (tuft of white hair on the forehead) and early greying of the hair and eyebrows are distinctive features. Partial albinism may also be present. Abnormalities of tear drainage and recurrent eye infections can complicate the condition. The most significant clinical impact often stems from deafness: the associated hearing loss can lead to speech development difficulties, communication barriers, school performance problems, and psychosocial consequences. Management focuses on early diagnosis of hearing loss, prompt intervention with hearing aids or other auditory devices, speech therapy, and educational support in schools for the hearing-impaired.

Myotonic dystrophy, the most common muscular dystrophy in adults, is an autosomal dominant disorder caused by trinucleotide repeat expansions (CTG repeats) in the DMPK gene. Its most distinctive feature is the phenomenon of anticipation—the disorder tends to present with earlier onset and increased severity in successive generations. Congenital myotonic dystrophy represents the most severe manifestation, occurring when the affected parent is the mother. Clinical features in adults include frontal balding (receding hairline pattern), characteristic "hatchet facies" resulting from selective atrophy of the temporalis muscles, cataracts (both subcapsular and cortical), ptosis (drooping eyelids), and a drooping mouth appearance. Sternocleidomastoid muscle wasting contributes to a thin neck appearance. Gynecomastia may develop. Beyond the facial and skeletal features, myotonic dystrophy affects multiple organ systems, including the cardiovascular system (conduction abnormalities, arrhythmias) and endocrine system (testicular atrophy, insulin resistance).

Achondroplasia represents the most common form of skeletal dysplasia and dwarfism, typically arising from dominant mutations in the FGFR3 gene. Most cases result from de novo mutations, particularly associated with advanced paternal age. The characteristic skeletal features include relative macrocephaly (the head appears disproportionately large relative to the body), frontal bossing (prominent forehead), short proximal (rhizomelic) limbs—the upper arms and thighs being most strikingly shortened—short trident hands (the fingers remain extended rather than forming a normal fist, with characteristic spacing), and lumbar lordosis (exaggerated forward lumbar curvature). Affected individuals have short stature throughout life, but most achieve independent ambulation and relatively normal function in everyday activities.

Neurofibromatosis type 1 (NF-1), inherited as an autosomal dominant trait with nearly complete penetrance, affects approximately one in three thousand to five thousand individuals. The skin manifestations are prominent and distinctive: café-au-lait spots (light-brown macules with well-demarcated borders) appear in early childhood in virtually all cases, and Crowe's sign—dense small brown freckles concentrated in the axillae (armpits) and other intertriginous areas—is pathognomonic of NF-1. Neurofibromas, benign tumors arising from peripheral nerves, develop over time and can occur superficially (cutaneous) or deeper in tissues (subcutaneous and plexiform). Lisch nodules—tan or orange pigmented hamartomas of the iris—are present in the majority of adults and constitute another pathognomonic finding. Ophthalmic involvement can be extensive, including optic nerve gliomas (which may be asymptomatic or cause vision loss), visual impairment, and nystagmus. Skeletal complications include scoliosis (which may be severe), osseous dysplasias, and pseudarthrosis (false joints). Central nervous system manifestations are significant: brain tumors (gliomas, particularly optic nerve gliomas), seizures, headaches, macrocephaly, learning disabilities, and intellectual disability occur with increased frequency. Vascular complications include hypertension from renal artery stenosis or other vasculopathy and cerebrovascular abnormalities. Patients also have substantially elevated cancer risk, with increased incidence of both benign and malignant neoplasms including pheochromocytoma, optic pathway gliomas, and malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors. Speech impairment, gastrointestinal symptoms (resulting from neurofibromas within the bowel), and endocrine abnormalities (precocious or delayed puberty, short stature in some) further characterize the condition.

Osteogenesis imperfecta, caused by mutations affecting type I collagen production, presents with the cardinal features of skeletal fragility (recurrent pathologic fractures with minimal trauma), brittle and easily fractured bones, and characteristic blue sclerae (the sclera appears blue due to the translucency of the thin sclera allowing the choroid to be visible). The severity ranges from lethal in utero forms to milder presentations with relatively few fractures.

Ectodermal dysplasia encompasses a heterogeneous group of conditions characterized by primary dysplasia of tissues derived from the ectoderm. The hallmark features include hypohidrosis (decreased sweating ability), resulting in heat intolerance and risk of overheating, transparent or translucent skin that may be unusually fragile, abnormal or completely absent teeth (oligodontia), and variable abdominal abnormalities. Patients may suffer from chronic respiratory infections due to abnormal mucous membranes and inadequate respiratory secretions.

Apert syndrome, caused by mutations in the FGFR2 gene, is an autosomal dominant condition characterized by craniosynostosis (premature fusion of the skull sutures, visible as a peaked or tower-shaped head and potentially limiting brain growth), distinctive symmetric syndactyly (fusion) of both hands and feet that is often complex with bone fusion in addition to soft tissue webbing, midfacial hypoplasia (underdevelopment of the midface), and high-arched palate. Congenital heart defects occur in approximately twenty percent. Intelligence ranges from normal to mildly reduced, with learning difficulties present in some individuals. Hearing loss due to middle ear abnormalities is common and may require intervention.

Oculocutaneous albinism, inherited in an autosomal recessive pattern, has a prevalence of approximately one in four thousand in populations of African descent, with higher frequencies in communities practicing consanguinity. The condition results from deficiency in melanin production. Cutaneous findings include pale, corn-colored hair (ranging from pale blonde to light brown), lighter-than-normal skin tone, and characteristic freckles. Ocular findings reflect absent or severely reduced melanin in the eye: pale or light-colored irides, photophobia (increased light sensitivity), nystagmus (involuntary oscillating eye movements), significantly reduced visual acuity, astigmatism, and absent binocular vision. Complications are substantial: sunburned skin develops easily and rapidly, the skin undergoes accelerated aging, and there is dramatically increased risk of squamous cell and basal cell carcinoma. Ocular complications include sun-induced damage to the eyes leading to cataracts and further vision loss, and the visual disability impairs school performance and educational achievement. Beyond physical complications, albinism carries significant social and psychological burden in many cultures.

Cystic fibrosis, caused by mutations in the CFTR gene (cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator) on chromosome seven, is inherited in an autosomal recessive pattern and has a prevalence of approximately one in twenty-five hundred to one in thirty-five hundred live births, with higher frequencies in populations of Northern European ancestry. The sweat chloride test, showing chloride concentration exceeding sixty millimoles per liter, remains the diagnostic gold standard. The pathophysiology involves defective ion transport across epithelial membranes, resulting in abnormally thick, inspissated secretions in multiple organ systems. Pancreatic insufficiency develops due to ductal obstruction and damage, leading to malabsorption, failure to thrive, and steatorrhea. Chronic pulmonary infections result from thick airway secretions and impaired mucociliary clearance, with colonization by organisms such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Burkholderia cepacia. Progressive pulmonary disease remains the primary cause of morbidity and mortality. Liver involvement, including cirrhosis and portal hypertension, occurs in a minority. Diabetes mellitus, resulting from pancreatic beta-cell dysfunction, develops in a substantial proportion of surviving adults.

Spinal muscular atrophy type 1 (SMA type 1), also called Werdnig-Hoffmann disease, is inherited as an autosomal recessive trait caused by mutations in the SMN1 (survival motor neuron 1) gene on chromosome five. The prevalence is approximately one in ten thousand live births. This represents the most severe form of SMA, with onset of symptoms before six months of age, typically within the first three months. Affected infants develop profound hypotonia (generalized muscle weakness and poor muscle tone), often assuming a characteristic "frog-legged" posture. Paradoxical (see-saw) breathing develops due to intercostal muscle weakness, with the infant demonstrating prominent abdominal movement with respiration and inadequate chest wall expansion. Deep tendon reflexes are absent or severely diminished. Tongue fasciculations (fine, involuntary muscle twitches) may be visible. Feeding difficulties and respiratory distress become apparent as the infant's ability to swallow and breathe weakens. Without ventilatory support, median survival without intervention is less than two years. With modern supportive care, including non-invasive ventilation and nutritional support, survival and quality of life have improved substantially. Recent introduction of disease-modifying therapies, including antisense oligonucleotide (nusinersen) and gene therapy approaches, offers promise for altering the natural history.

Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD), caused by mutations in the DMD gene on the X chromosome (Xp21), is inherited as an X-linked recessive disorder. The prevalence is approximately one in thirty-five hundred male live births. Affected males typically develop normally in the first year or two of life, with symptom onset between ages three and five. Progressive proximal muscle weakness begins in the lower extremities, evidenced by difficulty rising from the floor (characteristically using Gower's maneuver—using the arms to climb up the legs—to achieve standing), frequent falling, and difficulty climbing stairs. Calf pseudohypertrophy (apparent enlargement of the calf muscles due to fat and connective tissue infiltration) is characteristic. Progressive weakness continues through childhood, and most affected males become wheelchair-dependent by adolescence. Cardiac involvement, including dilated cardiomyopathy, occurs in the majority and represents a leading cause of death in the second and third decades. Intellectual disability affects a proportion of individuals. Heterozygous carrier females are typically unaffected but may manifest mild symptoms due to random X-inactivation (lyonization); approximately ten percent of carriers show some clinical manifestations, particularly those with skewed X-inactivation, Turner syndrome, or homozygous genotypes (extremely rare).

Hemophilia A, caused by Factor VIII deficiency, and hemophilia B, caused by Factor IX deficiency, are both inherited as X-linked recessive disorders with identical inheritance patterns and clinical presentations, differentiated only by the specific clotting factor deficiency. Hemophilia A has a prevalence of approximately one in five thousand males, while hemophilia B is less common at approximately one in twenty-five thousand to thirty thousand males. Affected males (hemizygous for the gene mutation) present with prolonged bleeding from minor trauma, spontaneous bleeding into joints and muscles, epistaxis, hematuria, and other hemorrhagic manifestations. Female carriers (heterozygous) are typically clinically unaffected but are capable of transmitting the condition to offspring. Of new cases, approximately eighty percent are inherited from carrier mothers and twenty percent represent de novo mutations. The inheritance pattern is characteristic: an affected male mated with a normal female produces no affected sons but all daughters become carriers. A carrier female mated with a normal male produces a fifty percent chance of affected sons and fifty percent chance of carrier daughters.

Prader-Willi syndrome and Angelman syndrome, though caused by the same chromosomal deletion on chromosome fifteen, produce strikingly different phenotypes due to the phenomenon of genomic imprinting—selective silencing of one parental allele by epigenetic DNA methylation. In Prader-Willi syndrome, the paternal chromosome fifteen is inherited normally while the maternal chromosome fifteen is imprinted (silenced); if the paternal copy is deleted, Prader-Willi syndrome results. In contrast, Angelman syndrome arises from maternal chromosome deletion with paternal imprinting, causing loss of maternal-derived gene expression. Prader-Willi syndrome manifests as neonatal hypotonia with poor feeding and failure to thrive in infancy, followed by development of hyperphagia (excessive appetite and food-seeking behavior) after one to three years, resulting in severe obesity. Short stature is characteristic. Intellectual disability of mild to moderate degree occurs. Primary hypogonadism develops with small penis and testes, and cryptorchidism may be present. Distinctive behavioral features include stubbornness, tantrums, and obsessive-compulsive tendencies. Angelman syndrome, by contrast, is characterized by severe intellectual disability, movement disorders including ataxia and tremors, a distinctive "happy demeanor" with frequent smiling and laughter, seizures (present in sixty to eighty percent), and characteristically inappropriate laughter and excitability. Hyperphagia does not occur; growth and feeding are typically normal in infancy.

Cat Eye Syndrome results from partial trisomy of chromosome twenty-two (duplication of a segment of chromosome twenty-two). The condition is named for the ocular colobomas—iris clefts—that give a cat-like appearance to the eyes. Other features include preauricular pits (small indentations anterior to the ears) and preauricular tags (small skin appendages), conductive hearing loss, cardiac defects, cleft palate, anal atresia, renal anomalies, and generally normal intelligence. The prognosis is typically favorable with supportive care and surgical intervention as needed.

Cri-du-chat syndrome results from deletion of the short arm of chromosome five. The syndrome is named for the characteristic high-pitched, cat-like cry heard in infants, resulting from laryngeal abnormalities. Intellectual disability is severe in most cases, and multiple congenital malformations are common. Prognosis is generally poor.

Understanding the language of dysmorphology—the field describing abnormal physical development—is essential for clinical recognition of birth defects. Frontal bossing refers to prominent, forward protrusion of the forehead, visible in conditions including achondroplasia, Down syndrome, and congenital hydrocephalus. Macrocephaly describes an abnormally large head, which may result from hydrocephalus, neurofibromatosis, or overgrowth syndromes. Microcephaly denotes an abnormally small head with reduced brain volume, typically associated with intellectual disability, congenital infections, or neural tube defects. Syndactyly refers to webbing or fusion of digits, which may be simple (involving skin only) or complex (involving bone fusion and structural abnormality); it occurs characteristically in Apert syndrome. Polydactyly describes the presence of extra digits (fingers or toes), which may be preaxial (on the thumb or great toe side) or postaxial (on the little finger or toe side); postaxial polydactyly is characteristic of Trisomy thirteen. Micrognathia denotes an abnormally small chin or mandible, seen in Trisomy eighteen and Pierre Robin sequence. Holoprosencephaly represents failure of the embryonic forebrain to divide completely into two hemispheres, classified into three grades of severity: alobar (most severe, with a single ventricle and complete midline fusion), semilobar (intermediate, with partial division), and lobar (least severe, with nearly complete separation but abnormal olfactory bulbs and tracts). Holoprosencephaly is strongly associated with severe facial midline defects and intellectual disability; the spectrum ranges from cyclopia (single central eye) at the most severe end to mild hypotelorism (closely-spaced eyes). Arachnodactyly refers to abnormally long, slender fingers and toes characteristic of Marfan syndrome. Ectopia lentis denotes subluxation or dislocation of the crystalline lens of the eye, also characteristic of Marfan syndrome. Café-au-lait spots are light-brown, well-demarcated macules on the skin; their presence in multiple locations (six or more spots greater than 1.5 centimeters) is a diagnostic criterion for neurofibromatosis type 1. Lisch nodules are tan or orange pigmented hamartomas of the iris that are pathognomonic (characteristic) of neurofibromatosis type 1. Crowe's sign refers to dense brown freckling in the axillae and other intertriginous areas, also pathognomonic of NF-1. Cryptorchidism, the failure of one or both testes to descend into the scrotum, represents both a malformation and a developmental delay; it occurs with increased frequency in prematurity, low birth weight, and genetic syndromes such as Trisomy thirteen. If not corrected, cryptorchidism significantly increases the risk of testicular malignancy and compromises fertility. While many cases resolve spontaneously by one year of age, surgical intervention is typically recommended if descent has not occurred by six months.

Neural tube defects represent a major category of structural malformations resulting from incomplete closure of the neural tube during the third to fourth week of gestation. The classification includes a spectrum of severity. Spina bifida occulta, the mildest form, involves failure of the vertebral arches to fuse posteriorly while the spinal cord and meninges remain within the spinal canal. Most cases are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on imaging performed for other reasons. Physical examination may reveal a skin dimple, tuft of hair, or lipoma over the affected spinal segment. Meningocoele occurs when the meninges alone herniate through the spinal defect; the spinal cord remains in normal anatomic position. While the spinal cord is not involved, patients may nonetheless have some degree of neurologic deficit, though the prognosis is generally better than in meningomyelocele. Meningomyelocele, the most common and most severe form of spina bifida, involves herniation of both the spinal cord and its surrounding meninges through the unfused vertebral arches. The degree of neurologic impairment depends on the level and extent of the lesion: lumbar lesions typically result in paraplegia (paralysis of the legs), loss of bowel and bladder control, and inability to ambulate without assistance, while thoracic and higher lesions produce more extensive paralysis. Management is urgent: surgical closure should be performed within twenty-four to forty-eight hours of birth to prevent infection, progressive CSF leakage, and further neurologic deterioration. A ventriculoperitoneal shunt is often required simultaneously or shortly thereafter if associated hydrocephalus is present, as the Arnold-Chiari malformation (caudal displacement of brainstem and cerebellum into the foramen magnum) frequently coexists with meningomyelocele and obstructs normal CSF flow.

Encephalocoele refers to protrusion of meninges, and often brain tissue, through a defect in the skull vault, most commonly in the midline between the eyes or in the occipital region. The defect results from incomplete neural tube closure, typically during the fourth week of gestation. Associated findings commonly include hydrocephalus, seizures, and developmental delay. Definitive management requires neurosurgical repair, typically performed within weeks to months of birth, with the precise timing dependent on the size of the defect and the amount of herniated brain tissue. A ventriculoperitoneal shunt may be required if hydrocephalus is present.

Anencephaly represents the most severe end of the neural tube defect spectrum: complete absence of the cerebral hemispheres, meninges, and scalp due to failure of the anterior neural tube to close. This condition is incompatible with life; most affected fetuses are stillborn, and those rarely born alive die within hours to days. Prenatal diagnosis is achieved through elevated maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein (a marker for neural tube defects and abdominal wall defects) and ultrasound examination, which reveals absence of the fetal skull vault. Anencephaly can be detected as early as eleven to fourteen weeks of gestation on transvaginal ultrasound.

Prevention of neural tube defects is achievable through periconceptual folic acid supplementation. The recommended regimen consists of either five milligrams daily (considered high-dose) or the standard dosage of four hundred micrograms daily, initiated before conception and continued throughout the first trimester of pregnancy. This intervention is especially important for women who have previously delivered a child with a neural tube defect, as they have substantially elevated recurrence risk (approximately three to five percent). Prenatal screening for neural tube defects involves measurement of maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) between fifteen and twenty weeks of gestation; levels exceeding the ninety-fifth percentile for gestational age are considered abnormal. Ultrasound examination performed between eighteen and twenty-three weeks can directly visualize most neural tube defects and should be offered to all pregnancies. When maternal serum AFP is elevated but ultrasound examination shows no apparent defect, amniocentesis is indicated to measure both amniotic fluid AFP and acetylcholinesterase. Acetylcholinesterase, an enzyme present in cerebrospinal fluid and amniotic fluid when there is exposure of neural tissue (as occurs with neural tube defects), serves as a highly specific confirmatory marker when combined with elevated AFP.

Congenital heart defects constitute the most common category of structural birth defects, affecting approximately eight per one thousand live-born infants. The pathophysiology of specific lesions drives clinical presentation and management. Ventricular septal defects, the most common congenital heart defect overall (accounting for twenty-five to thirty percent of all congenital heart disease), result from incomplete closure of the interventricular septum. Small VSDs, particularly muscular defects, often close spontaneously during childhood—many by age two to five years. Small VSDs are typically asymptomatic, discovered incidentally on auscultation of a systolic murmur, and require only periodic clinical assessment and echocardiography. Large VSDs, by contrast, create substantial left-to-right shunting of blood from the systemic to pulmonary circulation. As pulmonary vascular resistance declines in the first weeks of life (a normal physiologic transition from fetal to neonatal circulation), left-to-right shunting increases, precipitating congestive heart failure. Affected infants typically present at two to eight weeks of age with signs of heart failure including feeding difficulty (due to increased work of breathing and fatigue), poor weight gain, tachypnea, and hepatomegaly. Unless the defect is associated with concurrent pulmonary stenosis (which limits right ventricular flow), cyanosis is minimal. Small defects follow expectant management; larger defects with hemodynamic consequences require surgical closure, typically performed at three to six months of age. Emergency surgical intervention is indicated for severe heart failure with pulmonary edema unresponsive to medical management.

Atrial septal defects result from incomplete closure of the interatrial septum, most commonly the secundum type (ostium secundum ASD). Small to moderate ASDs often remain asymptomatic throughout childhood, and diagnosis is frequently delayed until adolescence or adulthood when the patient presents with dyspnea or fatigue related to the left-to-right shunt and resulting right heart volume overload. Atrial arrhythmias and right heart failure may develop in adulthood. The hemodynamic significance of an ASD is quantified by the pulmonary-to-systemic flow ratio (Qp:Qs): surgical or catheter-based device closure is indicated when the Qp:Qs exceeds 1.5:1. Timing of closure is typically three to four years of age in asymptomatic children, allowing normal growth; earlier intervention is warranted if significant symptoms develop. Device closure has become the preferred approach for many secundum ASDs in recent years, accomplished via cardiac catheterization without the need for open surgery.

Patent ductus arteriosus, failure of the ductus arteriosus to close after birth, is particularly common in premature infants. The condition is promoted by prematurity, maternal rubella infection during pregnancy, and various degrees of neonatal hypoxia. The ductus arteriosus normally closes within hours to days after birth as oxygen tension rises and prostaglandins decline. In susceptible infants, the ductus remains patent, creating a left-to-right shunt from the aorta to the pulmonary artery. Clinical presentation typically occurs within the first week of life and is characterized by a distinctive continuous "machinery" murmur, wide pulse pressure (reflecting the combination of elevated systolic and decreased diastolic pressures), bounding pulses, and hyperactive precordium. Feeding intolerance, poor growth, and respiratory distress from pulmonary edema may be evident. Medical management is attempted first using indomethacin or ibuprofen, both of which inhibit prostaglandin synthesis and promote ductal closure; these agents are successful in closing the ductus in approximately seventy to eighty percent of cases. Surgical ligation is reserved for cases where medical management fails, is contraindicated (as in patients with necrotizing enterocolitis or active infection), or when the infant becomes hemodynamically unstable. If surgery is required, it is typically performed at three to six weeks of age; earlier closure is considered for infants with severe symptoms or hemodynamic compromise.

Tetralogy of Fallot, the most common cyanotic congenital heart defect, consists of four anatomic components: a ventricular septal defect, right ventricular hypertrophy (secondary to obstruction to right ventricular outflow), right ventricular outflow tract obstruction (pulmonary stenosis), and rightward deviation of the aorta so that it overrides the VSD and receives blood from both ventricles. The result is a right-to-left shunt with deoxygenated blood bypassing the lungs and reaching the systemic circulation, producing cyanosis. Affected infants typically present with progressive cyanosis (often more noticeable with agitation or crying) and characteristic "tet spells"—acute episodes of severe hypercyanosis accompanied by dyspnea, rapid deep respirations (hyperpnea), loss of consciousness, and potential seizures or death if prolonged. The mechanism of tet spells involves sudden increase in right ventricular outflow tract obstruction (from spasm of the right ventricular infundibulum), increasing right-to-left shunting. Over time, chronic hypoxia leads to clubbing of the fingertips. Early surgical repair in infancy (typically between three and six months of age) is the standard approach, markedly improving survival compared to historical palliative procedures such as the Blalock-Taussig shunt. Prostaglandin E1 (PGE1) is administered preoperatively to maintain patency of the ductus arteriosus, which can provide an alternative source of pulmonary blood flow and improve oxygenation while awaiting surgery.

Transposition of the great arteries, where the aorta arises from the right ventricle and the pulmonary artery from the left ventricle (the reverse of normal anatomy), results in two separate, non-communicating circulations and is incompatible with life unless some form of mixing occurs between the systemic and pulmonary circulations. Affected infants are profoundly cyanotic within the first hours to days of life (the "blue baby" presentation). Survival depends on mixing of blood through a patent foramen ovale, atrial septal defect, or patent ductus arteriosus. Emergency intervention is required within hours of birth: balloon atrial septostomy (the Rashkind procedure), performed via cardiac catheterization, creates or enlarges an atrial septal defect to allow mixing at the atrial level. This procedure is a temporizing measure, followed by definitive surgical repair—the arterial switch operation (Jatene operation)—performed within the first weeks of life. This operation involves transecting the aorta and pulmonary artery and reattaching them in the correct positions, with reimplantation of the coronary arteries. PGE1 is administered preoperatively to maintain ductus arteriosus patency and allow continued pulmonary blood flow via this route, improving systemic oxygenation while awaiting surgery.

Atrioventricular canal defect (AVCD), also called an endocardial cushion defect, results from abnormal development of the endocardial cushion tissue and involves an ostium primum atrial septal defect, an inlet ventricular septal defect, and cleft mitral and tricuspid valves. A complete AVCD creates a single atrioventricular valve with a central regurgitant jet. AVCD occurs with markedly increased frequency in Down syndrome (Trisomy 21) and other chromosomal abnormalities, and karyotyping should be performed in all infants with this defect. Affected infants present with signs of congestive heart failure and cyanosis. Surgical repair is indicated for all complete AVCDs, typically performed at three to six months of age (before irreversible pulmonary vascular disease develops from chronic excessive pulmonary blood flow). The procedure involves closure of the septal defects and reconstruction of the atrioventricular valves. Partial AVCDs may be managed conservatively if asymptomatic, with observation for hemodynamic deterioration.

Coarctation of the aorta, a localized narrowing of the descending thoracic aorta usually occurring just distal to the left subclavian artery, impedes blood flow to the lower body. Clinical presentation varies with severity. Infants with severe coarctation may present with signs of heart failure (poor feeding, respiratory distress, hepatomegaly) or shock in the first days to weeks of life, particularly as the ductus arteriosus closes. Older children or adults with milder lesions may be asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on blood pressure screening, with hypertension in the upper extremities noted. Physical examination reveals weak or delayed femoral pulses compared to upper extremity pulses, lower blood pressure in the legs compared to the arms, and possibly a systolic murmur. Coarctation is associated with Turner syndrome, bicuspid aortic valve, and other cardiac anomalies. Diagnosis is confirmed by echocardiography or computed tomography. Surgical or catheter-based intervention is indicated when the pressure gradient across the coarctation exceeds twenty millimeters of mercury or when hemodynamic consequences are evident. In symptomatic infants, intervention is performed at three to six months of age. Asymptomatic children with good collateral circulation may have intervention delayed until three to four years of age. The standard surgical approach involves resection of the narrowed segment with end-to-end anastomosis or placement of a graft.

Ebstein anomaly, a displacement of the tricuspid valve leaflets (particularly the septal and posterior leaflets) apically into the right ventricle, results in a redundant, aneurysmal atrial segment and tricuspid regurgitation. The right atrium becomes massively enlarged. Right-to-left shunting occurs through an associated atrial septal defect or patent foramen ovale, producing cyanosis. Arrhythmias, including accessory pathways (as seen in Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome), are common. Ebstein anomaly is notably associated with maternal lithium exposure during the first trimester; pregnant women taking lithium for bipolar disorder require careful risk-benefit counseling and monitoring, as the teratogenic risk, while elevated, is not sufficiently high to mandate discontinuation in all cases.

The use of various medications during pregnancy carries substantial teratogenic risk. Fetal alcohol spectrum disorder results from prenatal ethanol exposure; while the critical window for morphologic teratogenesis is the first trimester, all three trimesters carry risk, with the first trimester associated with the greatest risk for central nervous system and facial features and the third trimester associated with growth restriction. No safe threshold has been established, and complete avoidance is recommended. Valproate (Depakote) used as an anticonvulsant or mood stabilizer carries a one to two percent risk of neural tube defects (particularly spina bifida), along with cardiac defects, craniofacial abnormalities, digit anomalies, growth restriction, and developmental delay. Women of reproductive age taking valproate require careful consideration of the risk-benefit ratio and discussion of alternative medications when possible. Lithium, used in the treatment of bipolar disorder, particularly increases the risk of Ebstein anomaly (tricuspid valve displacement into the right ventricle with resulting regurgitation and cyanosis) when exposure occurs in the first trimester; cardiac arrhythmias may develop with third trimester exposure. The teratogenic risk is real but must be weighed against the serious consequences of untreated maternal bipolar disorder.

Warfarin, an anticoagulant used in the treatment of thromboembolism, carries teratogenic risk, particularly in the first trimester: fetal warfarin syndrome includes nasal hypoplasia, skeletal abnormalities with characteristic stippled epiphyses, central nervous system malformations (microcephaly, hydrocephalus, agenesis of the corpus callosum), eye abnormalities, and growth restriction. Warfarin is generally avoided in the first trimester in favor of heparin, which does not cross the placenta. Retinoic acid and other retinoids (including isotretinoin, commonly known as Accutane) are highly teratogenic and are contraindicated in pregnancy. First trimester exposure causes multiple organ system malformations including central nervous system abnormalities (hydrocephalus, microcephaly, cleft palate), cardiac defects, facial clefting, thymic hypoplasia (underdevelopment of the thymus, compromising immune function), growth restriction, and intellectual disability. The FDA pregnancy category for isotretinoin is X (absolutely contraindicated); women of childbearing age taking isotretinoin must use strict contraception and participate in the iPLEDGE program mandating monthly pregnancy tests.

Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, while safe in the first trimester, carry significant teratogenic risk when used in the second and third trimesters: fetal and neonatal exposure results in renal dysgenesis (abnormal kidney development), oligohydramnios (reduced amniotic fluid from reduced fetal urine output), intrauterine growth restriction, fetal and neonatal acute kidney injury with oliguria, hypotension, and fetal death. Angiotensin II receptor blockers carry similar risks. Women of reproductive potential taking ACE inhibitors or ARBs for hypertension or heart failure require counseling regarding these risks and transition to safer alternatives during pregnancy.

Inheritance patterns determine recurrence risk and guide genetic counseling. Autosomal dominant disorders require only one abnormal gene copy (allele) for phenotypic expression. Affected × normal matings produce approximately fifty percent affected offspring, regardless of the sex of the affected parent. The condition may be inherited from a parent or arise as a de novo mutation in the proband. All autosomal dominant conditions show male-to-male transmission (affected fathers can pass the condition to sons), which is impossible in X-linked conditions. Autosomal recessive disorders require both alleles to be abnormal (homozygous state) for expression. Heterozygous carriers are phenotypically normal. Two heterozygous parents produce twenty-five percent affected offspring, fifty percent carrier offspring, and twenty-five percent phenotypically normal homozygotes per pregnancy. Affected individuals typically appear in a single generation unless consanguinity (marriage between blood relatives) is present, which increases the likelihood of both parents being carriers. Consanguinity substantially increases the risk of rare autosomal recessive disorders in offspring. X-linked recessive conditions affect males (hemizygous, carrying a single X chromosome) predominantly, while females are usually unaffected carriers (heterozygous). Affected males crossed with normal females produce no affected sons (sons receive the Y chromosome from fathers) but all daughters become carriers. Carrier females crossed with normal males produce fifty percent affected sons and fifty percent carrier daughters. Affected females (extremely rare, occurring only if the father is affected and the mother is a carrier or affected) crossed with normal males produce one hundred percent affected sons and one hundred percent carrier daughters.

Multifactorial (polygenic) inheritance, seen in neural tube defects and cleft palate, involves the interaction of multiple genes plus environmental factors. The recurrence risk to first-degree relatives (siblings and offspring) of an affected individual is approximately three to five percent, higher than population risk but substantially lower than mendelian single-gene conditions. The recurrence risk increases if multiple family members are affected. Notably, for some multifactorial conditions like neural tube defects, the recurrence risk is higher if the affected individual is female, reflecting a higher genetic threshold required for females to manifest the phenotype.

Genetic counseling for families affected by birth defects serves multiple purposes: providing accurate information regarding diagnosis, inheritance pattern, recurrence risks, and available management and prevention strategies; addressing parental guilt and emotional responses; and facilitating informed decision-making regarding future pregnancies. Counseling should be non-directive, respecting parental autonomy and cultural values while ensuring understanding of the medical facts. Ethnic or consanguineous background increases risk for specific autosomal recessive conditions and should prompt targeted counseling and carrier screening. Advanced maternal age (thirty-five years and older at delivery) significantly increases chromosomal abnormality risk: the risk of Down syndrome, for example, increases from approximately one in two hundred fifty at age thirty-five to approximately one in thirty at age forty-five. Advanced paternal age (forty to fifty years and older) increases risk of de novo dominant mutations, particularly in conditions like achondroplasia and some cases of Waardenburg syndrome. Prenatal diagnosis options, including ultrasound, maternal serum screening (first trimester biochemical markers combined with nuchal translucency ultrasound, second trimester quad screen, or cell-free fetal DNA testing), chorionic villus sampling, amniocentesis, and now non-invasive prenatal testing, should be discussed. Preimplantation genetic diagnosis may be considered by families with a known genetic condition planning assisted reproductive techniques. Families should be informed of support resources, including disease-specific organizations, genetic support groups, and counseling services. Neonatal screening programs, now performed universally in many developed countries, provide early identification of treatable genetic and metabolic conditions, enabling prompt intervention. Follow-up counseling following diagnosis of a birth defect in a newborn or child should address the specific condition, its natural history, management and treatment options, anticipated complications, educational and developmental considerations, and family implications.

Birth defects remain a substantial cause of childhood morbidity and mortality, but understanding their underlying biology, recognizing their clinical manifestations, and implementing preventive and therapeutic strategies has substantially improved outcomes. The integration of genetic information into clinical practice, combined with advances in prenatal diagnosis, neonatal screening, and early intervention, offers improved prospects for affected children and families.

## 2025 MANAGEMENT OF HYPOGLYCAMIA

### Lecture Notes

#### 2025 Management of Neonatal Hypoglycaemia

Neonatal hypoglycaemia is defined by the most widely used criterion as a whole blood glucose concentration below 2.6 mmol/litre. Severe neonatal hypoglycaemia occurs when whole blood glucose falls to 1.7 mmol/litre or lower, or when any symptomatic baby presents with documented low blood glucose. It is important to note that whole blood glucose is typically about 15 percent lower than serum glucose, a difference of approximately 0.5 mmol/litre. Clinically, any abnormal whole blood glucose measurement should prompt immediate action rather than waiting for laboratory confirmation, even if persistent hypoglycaemia requires confirmation through laboratory samples. Both blood glucometers with reagent strips and blood gas analysers are used to measure whole blood glucose in neonates.

The physiological drop in blood glucose that occurs after birth represents a normal adaptation, and a key goal of management is to avoid unnecessary treatment of this transitional low blood glucose while ensuring symptomatic hypoglycaemia is always corrected. Six main management goals guide clinical practice: preventing and correcting symptomatic hypoglycaemia in at-risk babies through feeds or fluids within the first hour depending on gestational age, avoiding hypothermia, avoiding unnecessary treatment of transitional glucose changes, identifying infants with underlying hypoglycaemic disorders, preventing long-term neurological sequelae, and using a stepwise treatment approach. The exact blood glucose levels and duration that cause brain injury in neonates have not been definitively established, though persistent and prolonged hypoglycaemia is recognised as pathological and potentially brain-damaging.

Glucose requirements differ by maturity: term babies require 6 to 8 milligrams per kilogram per minute, while preterm babies require 4 to 6 milligrams per kilogram per minute. Screening is indicated in the first 24 hours of life for infants with multiple risk factors including prematurity, post-maturity, small or large for gestational age, intrauterine growth restriction, infants of diabetic mothers, perinatal stress such as intrapartum hypoxia, meconium aspiration, haemolytic disease, hypothermia, fluid restriction or polycythaemia, family history of genetic hypoglycaemia, and syndromes like Beckwith-Wiedemann or midline brain abnormalities causing hypopituitarism. In at-risk babies, blood glucose monitoring is performed after the first feed, then before subsequent feeds or every 6 to 8 hours if feeding is not yet established. Healthy term infants without risk factors who are breast or formula feeding normally do not require routine blood glucose monitoring.

Clinical features of neonatal hypoglycaemia are non-specific and include jitteriness, sweating, tachypnoea, tachycardia, pallor, cyanosis, poor sucking or feeding difficulty, weak or high-pitched cry and irritability, decreased level of consciousness, seizures, hypotonia, and apnoeas. Importantly, many babies remain asymptomatic despite documented hypoglycaemia.

Management of asymptomatic babies with blood glucose in the range 1.8 to 2.5 mmol/litre begins with giving a milk feed immediately or starting intravenous fluids if contraindications exist, keeping the baby warm to ensure normothermia, and rechecking blood glucose in 30 minutes. If the rechecked glucose reaches 2.6 mmol/litre or above, monitoring continues hourly for 3 hours then 3-hourly for 24 hours. If glucose remains below 2.6 mmol/litre but is increasing, a repeat check is performed in 30 minutes. If glucose is neither increasing nor decreasing, feed frequency is increased to 2-hourly with daily volume increased by 20 to 30 millilitres per kilogram per day, and a recheck is performed in 30 minutes. Escalation to severe hypoglycaemia management is triggered when glucose is decreasing or remains below 2.6 mmol/litre despite these interventions.

For symptomatic or severe hypoglycaemia at or below 1.7 mmol/litre, the first intervention involves giving milk by nasogastric tube if the baby has not been fed in the preceding 2 hours, whilst simultaneously preparing to insert an intravenous line. If an intravenous line can be established, 2 to 5 millilitres per kilogram of 10 percent dextrose water is given as an intravenous bolus. Should peripheral intravenous access not be achievable within 30 minutes, glucagon is given intramuscularly at 0.2 milligrams per kilogram per dose, which may be repeated after 20 minutes up to a maximum of 1 milligram total, with discussion recommended with the referral centre. If peripheral intravenous access cannot be established, an emergency umbilical venous catheter should be inserted.

For hypoglycaemia unresponsive to maximum intravenous glucose while awaiting critical samples, hydrocortisone is given at 1 to 2 milligrams per kilogram per dose intravenously or orally every 6 hours. Once glucose exceeds 2.5 mmol/litre, a starting intravenous dextrose infusion rate of 10 percent dextrose at 60 to 120 millilitres per kilogram per 24 hours is used depending on gestational age and day of life, with careful attention to fluid volumes as those exceeding 120 millilitres per kilogram per day on day 1 should be avoided. If hypoglycaemia persists, dextrose concentration is escalated sequentially to 12.5 percent, then 15 percent, then 20 percent. Concentrations exceeding 12.5 percent require a central line for infusion, though an emergency umbilical venous catheter may be used temporarily during transfer. The potent hypertonic 50 percent glucose must never be given intravenously or orally in neonates due to its hypertonicity.

Blood glucose monitoring frequency after intervention is performed every 30 minutes until normal, then 2-hourly before feeds until glucose reaches 2.6 mmol/litre or above for two consecutive readings. Once glucose is normal with no clinical contraindications, milk feeds are added gradually while decreasing intravenous fluids provided blood glucose remains at or above 2.6 mmol/litre. Dextrose-containing intravenous fluids should not be stopped until feeds are well established.

The dextrose delivery rate is calculated using the formula: dextrose delivery rate in milligrams per kilogram per minute equals the volume in millilitres per kilogram per 24 hours multiplied by the percentage of dextrose multiplied by 0.007.

Persistent neonatal hypoglycaemia is defined as persistently low blood glucose beyond the first 48 hours of life or the requirement for parenteral glucose infusions to treat hypoglycaemia beyond 48 hours. All babies with persistent hypoglycaemia require investigation to determine the underlying cause and should be discussed with the referral centre for advice and admission if management at the place of birth or presentation is not possible.

Investigations for persistent hypoglycaemia include consideration of sepsis with blood culture and initiation of broad-spectrum antibiotics, obtaining a critical sample at the time of hypoglycaemia for serum glucose, insulin, and cortisol measurements, and always confirming hypoglycaemia with a laboratory sample. Further investigation is directed by the paediatrician, neonatologist, or paediatric endocrinologist.

The commonest cause of persistent neonatal hypoglycaemia is hyperinsulinism, defined by elevated insulin levels despite documented hypoglycaemia. Hyperinsulinism occurs in infants of diabetic mothers, those with intrauterine growth restriction, and babies with intrapartum stress. In most cases hyperinsulinism is transient, resolving within the first few weeks of life, though persistent hyperinsulinism requires specialist referral. For low cortisol in persistent hypoglycaemia, hydrocortisone is given at 1 to 2 milligrams per kilogram per dose intravenously or orally every 6 hours. For hyperinsulinism, diazoxide is used at 2 to 6 milligrams per kilogram per day given intravenously or orally every 8 hours, and hydrochlorothiazide at 1 to 3 milligrams per kilogram per dose orally every 6 to 12 hours, both requiring specialist guidance.

Discharge criteria for babies with hypoglycaemia require two normal pre-prandial blood glucose readings consecutively obtained on full 8-hourly feeds. All babies experiencing severe hypoglycaemia require neurodevelopmental follow-up at 20 weeks corrected gestational age, with consideration given to brain magnetic resonance imaging.

Neonatal hyperglycaemia is defined as whole blood glucose exceeding 7.0 mmol/litre or plasma glucose exceeding 8.3 mmol/litre. Hyperglycaemia is frequent in extremely low birth weight and very low birth weight infants but does not always require intervention. When plasma glucose exceeds 11 mmol/litre, osmotic diuresis and polyuria occur as a physiological consequence. Glycosuria does not serve as a reliable marker for hyperglycaemia in neonates as it correlates with plasma glucose exceeding 11 mmol/litre rather than lower thresholds.

Monitoring frequency for hyperglycaemia in the first 24 to 48 hours is every 6 to 8 hours in babies receiving intravenous glucose, extremely low birth weight infants, septic babies, intubated infants, those with intraventricular haemorrhage, or those receiving inotropes, steroids, or insulin. The causes of neonatal hyperglycaemia include intravenous glucose administration, extremely low or very low birth weight status, stress response, medications such as steroids or inotropes, and the rare condition of neonatal diabetes mellitus.

Treatment of hyperglycaemia is considered using a stepwise approach in babies with glucose exceeding 10 mmol/litre. The first-line approach involves decreasing parenteral glucose to 4 to 6 milligrams per kilogram per minute by reducing dextrose concentration from 10 percent to 5 percent (not below 5 percent) or reducing volume. Insulin therapy is considered if hyperglycaemia persists at 12 millimoles per litre or above despite reduction in dextrose, using specialist guidance and confirming hyperglycaemia with laboratory blood gas analysis before initiation. Insulin must never be bolused in neonatal management; continuous infusion only is used.

Investigations for persistent hyperglycaemia include a septic screen and antibiotics, cranial ultrasound to exclude intraventricular haemorrhage, and endocrinology referral if neonatal diabetes mellitus is being considered.

Insulin infusion preparation for neonatal use involves adding 2 millilitres (100 units) of Actrapid to 10 millilitres of normal saline, then adding 1 millilitre per kilogram (equal to 10 units per kilogram) to 0.9 percent saline to a final volume of 50 millilitres. At this concentration, 0.1 millilitres per hour equals 0.02 units per kilogram per hour. Plastic tubing must be primed with insulin before use as insulin binds to tubing. The infusion dose range for neonatal hyperglycaemia is 0.02 to 0.5 units per kilogram per hour. Blood glucose monitoring during insulin infusion is performed hourly. When blood glucose falls below 8 millimoles per litre, insulin infusion is stopped.

The insulin infusion rate is adjusted according to blood glucose levels. For blood glucose above 25 mmol/litre, the rate is 1 millilitre per hour. For glucose 20 to 25 mmol/litre, 0.5 millilitres per hour is used. For glucose 18 to 19 mmol/litre, 0.4 millilitres per hour is used. For glucose 15 to 17 mmol/litre, 0.3 millilitres per hour is used. For glucose 12 to 14 mmol/litre, 0.2 millilitres per hour is used. For glucose 8 to 11 mmol/litre, 0.1 millilitres per hour is used. When glucose falls below 8 millimoles per litre, the insulin infusion is stopped.

## Assess feeding and Counselling

### Lecture Notes

#### Assess Feeding and Counselling

Feeding assessment and maternal counselling form the cornerstone of neonatal care, requiring systematic evaluation of infant feeding capability, milk transfer efficiency, and maternal confidence. The clinical approach integrates visual observation, hands-on examination, and compassionate communication to identify feeding difficulties early and support families in achieving optimal nutrition.

**Positioning and Attachment**

Successful breastfeeding depends critically on correct positioning and attachment. Proper positioning ensures that the infant's head and body are in a straight line without twisting, the baby's face is held close to the breast at nipple level, and the entire body is supported—not just the head. The mother must be positioned comfortably with her back supported, and skin-to-skin contact should be encouraged when possible. The baby's mouth is brought to the breast (rather than the breast to the baby), and the infant's chest turns toward the mother to achieve chest-to-chest contact. Four holding positions facilitate breastfeeding: the cradle hold, cross-cradle hold, football hold, and side-lying position, each suited to different maternal preferences and clinical situations.

Signs of correct infant attachment at the breast are readily identifiable on examination. The infant's mouth should cover the areola, not just the nipple. The lips are flanged or everted outward, creating a seal with the breast tissue. The chin touches the breast during suckling, and the cheeks appear full and rounded rather than drawn inward, indicating that the breast tissue is correctly positioned in the infant's mouth. These observations confirm that milk transfer will be efficient and that nipple trauma is unlikely to occur.

**Effective Suckling and Milk Transfer**

During observation of feeding, several signs indicate that milk is transferring effectively to the infant. Audible swallows occur regularly at a rate of one or two swallows per suck, signalling that milk is entering the infant's mouth. Milk may be visible at the corners of the infant's mouth during feeding, particularly in the early feeds when letdown is strong. The suckling pattern is characterized by slow, deep sucks with visible jaw movement, demonstrating that the infant is compressing the lactiferous sinuses and drawing milk effectively. A regular breathing pattern maintained throughout feeding (without gasping) indicates comfort and proper airway positioning. At the end of a feed, the infant appears relaxed and satisfied, often spontaneously releasing from the breast.

**Feed Volumes and Growth Patterns**

In replacement-fed infants and those requiring supplementation, precise volume prescriptions guide adequate nutrition. On day one of life, neonates typically receive 10 ml per feed. This volume increases progressively: 20 ml on day two, 30 ml on day three, 40 ml on day four, and 60–80 ml per feed from day five onward. Alternative expressions of requirement state a total daily intake of 150–200 ml per kilogram of body weight per day. Following initial physiological weight loss, term infants should gain a minimum of 20 grams per kilogram per day, while preterm infants require 10 grams per kilogram per day.

Growth charts are essential tools for monitoring feeding adequacy. Weight should be plotted daily or weekly, with careful interpretation of trends. Weight loss exceeding 10% of birth weight indicates inadequate milk intake and requires intervention. Weight loss of less than 10% is expected and normal; the infant should regain birth weight within 7–14 days. Once birth weight is recovered, the daily weight gain should be tracked against the minimum expected rates. Downward crossing of percentile lines signals faltering growth and mandates investigation of underlying causes, which may include incorrect feeding technique, insufficient volume, infection, inadequate temperature control, or maternal education gaps.

**Growth Chart Interpretation**

A newborn infant is weighed daily and the weight recorded on a growth chart alongside the daily weight gain calculation. The first week of life typically sees weight loss of up to 10% of birth weight; this is physiological and requires reassurance rather than intervention. After this initial loss, the infant should regain birth weight within 7–14 days. Assessment of preterm infants requires particular attention to corrected age, and weight gain should be monitored closely, with minimum expected gains of 10 g/kg/day in preterm infants and 20 g/kg/day in term infants after birth weight recovery. Any crossing of percentile lines downward should prompt investigation for illness, insufficient feeding frequency, inadequate temperature control, incorrect feeding method, or deficient parental education regarding feeding practices.

**Counselling Framework**

Effective counselling in infant feeding differs fundamentally from instruction-giving. Counselling is not about imposing directives; rather, it aims to help families reach appropriate decisions based on understanding and through collaborative discussion. The process respects the mother's perspective, acknowledges her efforts, and builds her confidence in her ability to feed her infant.

The APAC counselling framework provides a structured yet flexible approach. ASK involves listening carefully to the mother's concerns, questions, and lived experience with feeding, understanding her perspective and any challenges she perceives. PRAISE acknowledges her existing efforts, strengths, and successes, however small, reinforcing her confidence and commitment. ADVISE provides evidence-based information and recommendations tailored to her situation, avoiding overwhelming amounts of guidance and focusing on the most relevant points. CHECK confirms that the mother has understood the advice, asks her to summarize the plan in her own words, and invites her to identify any barriers to carrying it out. This final step ensures that the plan is realistic, acceptable, and likely to be implemented successfully.

The three foundational counselling skills support this framework: communication skills enable clear, non-judgmental exchange of information; listening and learning skills ensure the mother feels heard and respected; and confidence-building skills affirm her capabilities and strengthen her resolve to continue feeding.

**Assessment of Feeding Problems**

The LOOK, LISTEN, FEEL approach provides a systematic framework for identifying feeding difficulties. LOOK encompasses assessment of positioning (correct alignment of infant head, body, and breast), attachment (areolar coverage, lip flange, chin position, cheek fullness), and any obvious problems such as facial cleft, blocked nose, mouth ulcers, or oral thrush. LISTEN for audible swallows and the infant's feeding sounds, distinguishing effective feeding sounds from signs of stress or ineffective milk transfer. FEEL involves palpating the breast for engorged or swollen tissue, noting any areas of tenderness, and assessing whether milk flow can be felt or expressed.

In infants receiving replacement milk, assessment similarly focuses on feeding technique (how the bottle or cup is held, the infant's suckling pattern, signs of milk aspiration), observation for localizing problems such as thrush or mouth ulcers, and careful measurement of weight and weight gain trends to detect inadequate intake.

**Common Breastfeeding Problems and Management**

Breast engorgement occurs when milk accumulates and breast tissue becomes swollen, making the areola firm and attachment difficult. Management includes frequent feeding at least eight times daily, warm compresses applied before feeds to promote milk flow, gentle hand expression or massage to soften the areola, cold compresses between feeds to reduce swelling, and ensuring correct positioning and complete breast emptying. Analgesia may be offered for pain relief.

Mastitis presents as breast infection with inflammation, pain, and systemic symptoms. Mothers should be reassured that continued breastfeeding on the affected breast is safe for the infant and essential for recovery. Frequent feeds empty the breast completely, reducing milk stasis. Warm compresses and gentle massage before feeds improve comfort and drainage. Antibiotics appropriate to breastfeeding—such as flucloxacillin or cephalexin—are indicated. Analgesics (paracetamol or ibuprofen), bed rest, and adequate hydration support recovery. Correct positioning and attachment at future feeds prevent recurrence.

Inverted or flat nipples may be managed through several techniques. Nipple shields provide temporary assistance in enabling direct breastfeeding. Hand expression before feeds or brief use of a breast pump can evert the nipple, facilitating latch. The football hold or other positional variations may improve the infant's ability to attach. If direct breastfeeding proves impossible despite support, expressing and cup-feeding preserves breast milk provision while resting the nipples.

Cracked or sore nipples usually result from poor latch. Management prioritizes correcting attachment, as improved positioning immediately reduces trauma. Varying feeding positions distributes pressure across different areas. Manual expression of milk before feeds softens the areola, easing infant positioning. Lanolin or expressed breast milk applied to nipples after feeds aids healing. Air-drying and breast shells prevent friction from clothing. If pain is severe, expressing milk and cup-feeding allows complete healing before returning to direct breastfeeding. Most mothers find that pain diminishes as the nipples toughen and healing progresses.

Insufficient milk supply is addressed through increased feeding frequency (8–12 times daily) and ensuring complete breast emptying through correct attachment, full drainage at each feed, alternating breasts multiple times during a session, and hand expression after feeds. The clinician assesses the infant for tongue tie, which may impair milk transfer, and investigates maternal factors such as infection, thyroid disorders, or excessive stress. Galactagogues (fenugreek, blessed thistle) may support milk production with appropriate guidance. Maternal nutrition, hydration, and overall health directly influence milk supply, warranting discussion and support.

Tongue tie occurs when the frenulum restricts tongue movement, impairing the infant's ability to compress the lactiferous sinuses. Assessment determines whether the restriction actually prevents effective feeding. Positioning techniques, particularly the football hold, may improve latch despite the tie. Hand expression provides milk to the infant if direct breastfeeding is ineffective. Referral for frenulotomy (division of the frenulum) is indicated if breastfeeding remains unsuccessful despite support, if the infant cannot feed effectively, or if the mother experiences pain or tissue damage. Following frenulotomy, reassessment of latch and continued support often result in immediate improvement, though some infants require repositioning assistance.

**Hand Expression Technique**

Mothers benefit from instruction in hand expression of breast milk. The technique begins with thorough hand washing and application of warmth via compress or warm shower to stimulate the letdown reflex. The mother cups the breast in one hand with fingers below and thumb above at the edge of the areola. She gently presses the breast tissue against the chest wall, then rolls the thumb and fingers forward in a coordinated compress-and-express motion, releases, and repeats in a rhythmic pattern. By rotating around the breast quadrants, she ensures complete emptying. Expression typically continues for 5–10 minutes until milk flow slows. Milk is collected in a sterile cup or bottle for storage or direct feeding.

**Danger Signs and Urgent Referral**

Three danger signs indicate that a baby cannot feed and demand urgent evaluation: the infant is not feeding at all, not attaching to the breast, or not sucking. These signs necessitate immediate investigation for infection (a leading cause in the first weeks of life) or asphyxia-related encephalopathy (in infants under three days old with no infection risk factors). Observation includes checks for discharging eyes, umbilical sepsis, septic skin lesions, and jaundice, all requiring prompt return to the primary healthcare clinic. Signs mandating immediate hospital referral include poor feeding or suckling, convulsions, rapid breathing, diarrhoea, and bleeding.

**Follow-up Scheduling**

All infants should be reviewed at three days of age, six weeks, and then according to standard developmental review schedules. HIV-exposed infants require follow-up at six weeks and monthly during the first year. Low-birth-weight infants under two kilograms need review at three days, weekly until two and a half kilograms is reached, then at six weeks. High-risk infants require the most intensive follow-up: at three days, weekly until two and a half kilograms, then at six weeks, four months, and nine months.

## Complications of Prematurity LBW and RDS

### Lecture Notes

#### Complications of Prematurity, Low Birth Weight, and Respiratory Distress Syndrome

Prematurity is defined as birth occurring before 37 completed weeks of gestation, or before 259 days. The severity of prematurity is stratified by gestational age into distinct categories that carry progressively increasing morbidity and mortality risk. Late preterm infants are born between 34 and less than 37 weeks of gestation and often transition well to postnatal life with minimal intervention. Very preterm (VPT) infants, born before 32 weeks of gestation, face significantly greater challenges across multiple organ systems. Extremely preterm (EPT) infants, born at or before 28 weeks of gestation, represent the most fragile population with profound immaturity of virtually all physiological systems and requiring intensive supportive care.

Birth weight classification provides a complementary stratification to gestational age. Low birth weight (LBW) is defined as birth weight less than 2500 grams and encompasses infants who are premature, growth-restricted, or both. Very low birth weight (VLBW) infants weigh less than 1500 grams at birth and require specialized neonatal intensive care. Extremely low birth weight (ELBW) infants, weighing less than 1000 grams, represent the most vulnerable subset with the highest risk for complications and long-term neurodevelopmental sequelae. These weight classifications often overlap significantly with gestational age categories, though some term or near-term infants may fall into the LBW category due to intrauterine growth restriction.

The etiology of preterm birth is multifactorial and involves maternal, fetal, obstetric, and environmental factors. Socioeconomic and psychosocial risk factors include absence of a partner, low socioeconomic level, anxiety, stress, depression with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor use, major life events such as divorce or bereavement, and low educational achievement. Occupational exposures that increase risk include prolonged upright posture, use of industrial machinery, sustained physical exertion, and mental stress related to work or environmental conditions. Maternal factors include substance abuse, smoking, maternal age less than 18 or greater than 40 years, African-American ethnicity, poor nutrition with low body mass index, inadequate prenatal care, anaemia with hemoglobin less than 10 grams per deciliter, and maternal or family history of spontaneous preterm birth. Abdominal surgery during pregnancy increases the risk of preterm delivery. Obstetric factors are numerous and include multiple gestation, polyhydramnios, uterine anomalies including diethylstilbestrol-induced structural changes and leiomyomas, preterm premature rupture of membranes, history of second-trimester abortion, prior cervical surgery, premature cervical dilatation or effacement with shortened cervical length, placenta previa, placental abruption, vaginal bleeding particularly when occurring across multiple trimesters, previous preterm delivery, and excessive uterine contractility. Infectious risk factors include sexually transmitted infections, systemic maternal infection such as pyelonephritis or pneumonia, bacteriuria, and periodontal disease. Fetal and environmental factors contributing to preterm birth include fetal anomalies, fetal growth restriction, fetal demise, positive fetal fibronectin testing in vaginal secretions, and environmental exposures to heat or air pollution.

The immediate causes of preterm birth vary considerably in their frequency. Spontaneous preterm labor accounts for 30 to 50 percent of cases. Preterm premature rupture of membranes (PPROM) occurs in 5 to 40 percent of preterm births. Multiple gestation accounts for 10 to 30 percent. Preeclampsia or eclampsia is responsible for approximately 12 percent of preterm births. Antepartum bleeding, frequently from placental abruption or previa, causes 6 to 9 percent of cases. Fetal growth restriction necessitates preterm delivery in 2 to 4 percent. Other etiologies, including infection, structural anomalies, and iatrogenic reasons, account for 8 to 9 percent.

Short-term complications of prematurity affect virtually every organ system. Respiratory complications are particularly prominent and include respiratory distress syndrome (also called hyaline membrane disease or HMD), apnea of prematurity, chronic lung disease (bronchopulmonary dysplasia), pulmonary hemorrhage, and air leak syndromes such as pneumothorax. Neurological complications include intraventricular hemorrhage, periventricular leukomalacia, and neurodevelopmental delay. Gastrointestinal complications encompass necrotizing enterocolitis, feed intolerance, and severe hyperbilirubinemia. Hematological complications include anemia of prematurity, thrombocytopenia, and coagulopathy. Circulatory complications feature patent ductus arteriosus, hypotension, and early-onset sepsis. Renal complications include acute kidney injury and electrolyte derangement. Additional complications comprise hypothermia, hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia, nosocomial infection, and retinopathy of prematurity.

Respiratory distress syndrome, caused by surfactant deficiency, is the hallmark complication of prematurity and is synonymous with hyaline membrane disease. The pathogenesis unfolds through a characteristic cascade. Prematurity leads to markedly reduced synthesis, storage, and release of pulmonary surfactant, the critical lipoprotein that reduces surface tension at the alveolar air-liquid interface. With decreased alveolar surfactant, surface tension increases dramatically, promoting widespread alveolar collapse or atelectasis. Collapsed alveoli create severe ventilation-perfusion mismatch and hypoventilation, resulting in hypoxemia and carbon dioxide retention. The hypoxemia and hypercarbia induce systemic and pulmonary acidosis. Acidosis provokes pulmonary vasoconstriction, leading to pulmonary hypoperfusion. The combination of hypoperfusion and hypoxemia damages both the pulmonary endothelium and the alveolar epithelium. As a consequence of this vascular and epithelial injury, plasma leaks into the alveolar spaces. The leaked protein-rich fluid combines with fibrin and necrotic cellular debris to form hyaline membranes that line the alveoli, creating the pathological hallmark of the disease. These membranes dramatically increase the alveolar-capillary diffusion gradient, perpetuating hypoxemia and hypercarbia in a self-reinforcing cycle. Chest radiographs in HMD characteristically demonstrate low lung volumes with a diffuse reticulogranular ground-glass appearance and prominent air bronchograms that reflect the contrast between patent airways and collapsed alveoli. Moderate and severe cases show more extensive radiographic involvement. Management of HMD rests on two pillars: respiratory support via continuous positive airway pressure or intermittent positive pressure ventilation to mechanically inflate collapsed alveoli, and exogenous surfactant therapy to restore the surface-tension-lowering properties necessary for alveolar stability.

Necrotizing enterocolitis represents one of the most serious gastrointestinal emergencies in preterm infants. It is characterized by ischemic necrosis of the intestinal mucosa, accompanied by severe inflammation and invasion of enteric gas-forming organisms. The pathogenesis involves a triad of mechanisms: intestinal mucosal ischemia, intense inflammatory response, and bacterial translocation with gas production. The severity and progression of NEC are systematically classified using the Modified Bell staging criteria, which includes six stages with clinical, abdominal, and radiographic components. Stage IA (suspected NEC) presents with nonspecific systemic signs including temperature instability, apnea, bradycardia, and lethargy, together with gastrointestinal signs of gastric retention, abdominal distention, emesis, and heme-positive stool, while radiographs show normal or mildly dilated bowel with mild ileus. Stage IB (suspected NEC) shares the same systemic findings and radiographic appearance but is distinguished by the presence of grossly bloody stool. Stage IIA (definite, mildly ill NEC) includes similar systemic and gastrointestinal signs but adds absent bowel sounds with or without abdominal tenderness, and radiographs demonstrate intestinal dilation, ileus, and the critical finding of pneumatosis intestinalis, which represents gas bubbles within the bowel wall resulting from invasion by gas-forming organisms. Stage IIB (definite, moderately ill NEC) escalates the systemic severity to include mild metabolic acidosis and thrombocytopenia, abdominal findings progress to definite tenderness with or without cellulitis or a right lower quadrant mass, and radiographs show intestinal dilation, ileus, pneumatosis intestinalis, and ascites. Stage IIIA (advanced, severely ill NEC with intact bowel) represents critical illness with hypotension, severe apnea, combined respiratory and metabolic acidosis, disseminated intravascular coagulation, neutropenia, marked abdominal tenderness and peritoneal signs, and radiographic evidence of intestinal dilation, ileus, pneumatosis intestinalis, and ascites. Stage IIIB (advanced, severely ill NEC with perforated bowel) carries identical systemic and clinical features but distinguishes itself by the radiographic finding of pneumoperitoneum, which indicates perforation of the necrotic intestinal wall and free intraperitoneal air, typically necessitating urgent surgical intervention. Abdominal radiographs may also reveal portal venous gas, seen as branching lucencies within the liver, which results from dissection of gas into the portal venous system through areas of mucosal necrosis. Management of NEC is stage-dependent and includes keeping the infant nil per os (NPO), initiating broad-spectrum antibiotics, providing nutritional support via total parenteral nutrition, and proceeding to surgical exploration and bowel resection when indicated by perforation or clinical deterioration despite maximal medical therapy.

Long-term complications of prematurity extend well beyond the neonatal period. The major long-term morbidity in preterm-born children is neurodevelopmental disability, reflecting the vulnerability of the developing brain during the critical period of intrauterine and early extrauterine development. Chronic medical problems frequently persist, including respiratory abnormalities related to previous lung disease, poor growth trajectory despite improved nutrition, and the need for frequent hospitalizations in early childhood due to infection susceptibility and residual organ dysfunction. Preterm-born individuals who reach adulthood demonstrate increased insulin resistance and elevated blood pressure compared to term-born peers, suggesting that prematurity imparts a lasting metabolic phenotype. Additionally, decreased reproductive success has been documented in some cohorts of preterm-born adults, indicating that the effects of prematurity may extend to the next generation through altered fecundity. These long-term findings underscore the profound systemic impact of preterm birth and emphasize the importance of comprehensive follow-up and developmental monitoring during infancy, childhood, and into adulthood.

## Hypoxic Ischaemic Encephalopathy

### Lecture Notes

#### Hypoxic Ischaemic Encephalopathy

Hypoxic ischaemic encephalopathy (HIE) represents one of the most serious complications of the perinatal period, arising from insufficient oxygen delivery or blood flow to the neonatal brain. Understanding the pathophysiology, clinical presentation, and evidence-based management is essential for neonatal care. The condition must be differentiated from related but distinct entities: hypoxia describes reduced oxygen delivery to tissue, while ischaemia refers to reduced blood flow; asphyxia encompasses progressive hypoxia, hypercarbia, and acidosis together. Neonatal encephalopathy is a broader clinical syndrome characterized by disturbed neurological function—altered level of consciousness, tone disturbance with or without seizures—that may result from multiple causes (meningitis, cerebral malformations, intracranial haemorrhage, perinatal stroke, metabolic derangements, inborn errors of metabolism, or neonatal abstinence syndrome) and does not inherently imply irreversible neurological injury. HIE specifically denotes an abnormal neurobehavioural state combining altered consciousness, tone disturbance, and/or seizures with objective evidence of a hypoxic-ischaemic insult during the perinatal period.

Diagnosis of HIE requires integration of clinical suspicion with laboratory evidence. All four diagnostic criteria must be present: metabolic or mixed acidaemia documented by umbilical artery blood gas with pH less than 7.2 and base excess greater than 15 mmol/L; an Apgar score less than 7 at 5 minutes of life; clinical evidence of neonatal neurological disturbance such as seizures, coma, or hypotonia; and objective multi-organ involvement affecting kidney, lungs, liver, heart, and intestines. Importantly, no single confirmatory test for HIE exists; diagnosis rests upon historical context, physical examination findings, detailed neurological assessment, and laboratory evidence of metabolic disturbance. Laboratory investigations should assess serum electrolytes (which may show marked hyponatremia, hypokalaemia, and hypochloraemia in the setting of reduced urine flow and excessive weight gain, suggesting acute tubular necrosis or SIADH), renal function including serum creatinine and blood urea nitrogen, cardiac and liver enzyme patterns reflecting hypoxic-ischaemic injury to distant organs, coagulation parameters (prothrombin time, partial thromboplastin time, fibrinogen), and serial arterial blood gases to monitor acid-base status and avoid both hyperoxia/hypoxia and hypercapnia/hypocapnia.

The pathophysiology of HIE proceeds through distinct temporal phases. The primary or acute phase occurs within minutes of the hypoxic-ischaemic insult and involves rapid depletion of cerebral energy metabolites, forcing the brain into anaerobic metabolism. This triggers progressive cellular depolarization, cytotoxic oedema, excessive intracellular calcium and sodium accumulation, increased extracellular glutamate release, and progressive acidosis, culminating in cell injury and potential necrosis. Remarkably, the immature brain demonstrates relative resistance to hypoxic-ischaemic events through three mechanisms: a lower cerebral metabolic rate requiring less oxygen substrate, immaturity in the balance of neurotransmitter systems conferring greater tolerance, and inherent plasticity of the immature central nervous system permitting functional reorganization. The therapeutic window—the interval from onset of hypoxia to initiation of neuroprotective intervention—extends approximately 6 hours, during which cerebral blood flow gradually reperfuses and cerebral oxidative metabolism and acid-base status slowly recover, representing the critical opportunity for hypothermia or other neuroprotective strategies. Beyond this window, the secondary or latent phase develops over hours to days, characterized by secondary cytotoxic oedema with progressive cell death, a robust inflammatory cascade with elevation of interleukin-1, interleukin-6, and tumour necrosis factor-alpha, cerebral oedema and seizure activity, increased free radical generation, further calcium overload, and accumulation of excitatory amino acids that perpetuate neuronal death through both necrotic and apoptotic pathways.

The pattern and location of CNS injury correlate with gestational age at the time of insult. Insults occurring before 20 weeks gestation result in neuronal heterotopia or polymicrogyria (abnormal cortical development). Between 26 and 36 weeks gestation, the vulnerable target is white matter, producing periventricular leukomalacia characterized by focal necrosis and gliosis around the lateral ventricles. At term gestation, grey matter—particularly the basal ganglia, thalamus, and cerebral cortex—bears the brunt of hypoxic-ischaemic injury.

Multiple aetiological pathways converge on perinatal asphyxia. Maternal causes include sudden loss of maternal consciousness or perfusion such as cardiac arrest, asphyxiation from airway obstruction or aspiration, severe anaphylaxis, status epilepticus, and hypovolaemic shock from massive haemorrhage. Uteroplacental causes interrupt the fetal-placental circulation: placental abruption with separation of the placental surface, umbilical cord prolapse compromising cord flow, uterine rupture with loss of uterine containment, and hyperstimulation with oxytocic agents causing tetanic contractions and placental insufficiency. Fetal causes originate within the fetus itself and include fetomaternal haemorrhage with fetal blood loss into the maternal circulation, twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome, severe isoimmune haemolytic disease (such as Rh or ABO incompatibility) with profound anaemia, and fetal cardiac arrhythmias such as complete heart block or severe supraventricular tachycardia.

Clinical assessment of HIE severity employs the Thompson score, a validated neurological examination tool that captures nine discrete components, each scored on a graded scale and summed to a maximum of 34 points, with higher scores indicating more severe encephalopathy. Tone is graded as normal (0), hypertonia (1), hypotonia (2), or flaccidity (3). Level of consciousness ranges from normal (0), through hyperalert with fixed stare (1), lethargy (2), to deep coma (3). The frequency of clinical seizures is classified as none (0), fewer than three per day (1), or three or more per day (2). Posture is assessed as normal (0), fisting and cycling movements (1), strong distal flexion (2), or decerebrate posturing (3). The Moro reflex is graded as normal (0), partial (1), or absent (2). Grasp reflex assessment yields normal (0), poor (1), or absent (2). Sucking is scored as normal (0), poor (1), or absent with biting (2). Respiratory pattern is classified as normal (0), hyperventilation (1), brief apnoeal episodes (2), or requirement for intermittent positive pressure ventilation (3). Finally, the anterior fontanel is palpated as normal (0), full but not tense (1), or tense (2).

Sarnat staging provides a clinical framework linking neonatal presentation to prognosis and guides decisions regarding neuroprotective interventions. Mild HIE (Sarnat Stage 1) is characterized by hyperalertness, no need for mechanical ventilation, mild feeding difficulty, jittery tone without seizures, and carries a long-term prognosis of essentially zero mortality and zero disability in term infants, with less than 1 percent risk of severe handicap or death. Moderate HIE (Sarnat Stage 2) presents with lethargy, spontaneous respiration without ventilator support, moderate feeding problems, hypertonic tone with the presence of clinical seizures, and confers a 25 percent probability of severe handicap or death; long-term outcome in term infants shows 6 percent mortality and 30 percent disability. The moderate-to-severe category (Sarnat Stage 2–3) is reserved for infants requiring mechanical ventilation but not displaying flaccidity and deep coma, with lethargy, moderate feeding problems, hypertonic tone, seizures, and approximately 50 percent risk of severe handicap. Severe HIE (Sarnat Stage 3) manifests as deep coma, requirement for intermittent positive pressure ventilation, severe feeding intolerance, flaccid tone, early and frequent seizures, and carries a grim prognosis with 75 percent risk of severe handicap or death; long-term outcome demonstrates 60 percent mortality and 100 percent disability in term infants.

Seizure management in HIE follows a stepwise pharmacological approach. Phenobarbitone is the first-line anticonvulsant, administered as an initial loading dose of 20 mg/kg intravenously over 20 minutes. If seizures persist, two additional doses of 10 mg/kg each may be given intravenously at 15-minute intervals, permitting a cumulative total of 40 mg/kg. Anticonvulsant drug levels require monitoring to maintain therapeutic concentrations. For convulsions refractory to phenobarbitone, phenytoin is the second-line agent, given as 20 mg/kg intravenously slowly over 20 minutes. Lorazepam, a benzodiazepine, may be administered transiently at 0.05 to 0.1 mg/kg per dose intravenously for acute seizure control. When status epilepticus develops and does not respond to phenobarbitone and phenytoin, midazolam infusion provides continuous seizure suppression for refractory seizure activity.

Hypothermia therapy, supported by evidence from the Cool Cap trial and the NICHD Neonatal Research Network trial, has become the standard neuroprotective strategy for moderate-to-severe HIE. The target core body temperature (measured rectally) is maintained between 33 and 35 degrees Celsius for 72 hours. Two cooling modalities are available: whole-body cooling systems and selective head-cooling via the Cool Cap technique. The neuroprotective mechanism of hypothermia operates through multiple pathways: reduction of cerebral metabolic rate and prevention of cerebral oedema by decreasing energy utilization; suppression of cytotoxic amino acid accumulation and nitric oxide release; inhibition of the platelet-activating factor and downstream inflammatory cascade; suppression of free radical generation and oxidative stress; and attenuation of secondary neuronal damage through inhibition of both necrotic and apoptotic cell death pathways. The clinical outcome benefit is substantial: death or severe disability at 18 months of age is significantly reduced compared to normothermic controls.

Long-term follow-up is essential for all neonates with moderate and severe asphyxia, particularly those meeting Sarnat Stage II and III criteria. High Risk Clinic attendance provides structured neurological surveillance with complete neurological assessment at regular intervals and early developmental intervention if neurological abnormalities or developmental delays are identified. Formal psychometric assessment should be performed at 18 months of age to quantify neurodevelopmental outcome, cognitive development, and motor function as indicators of long-term functional ability and disability.

## KINC 5th Year Presentation

### Lecture Notes

#### KINC — 5th Year Presentation

KINC, the KZN Initiative on Newborn Care, is a comprehensive framework developed by the KZN Department of Health for managing newborns in resource-limited settings such as district and regional hospitals. This initiative provides essential guidance for routine neonatal care while placing particular emphasis on the management of sick and small newborns, enabling clinicians to deliver high-quality care even in settings with constrained resources. The OOSHI mnemonic encodes the five core principles of newborn care: Observation, Oxygen, Sugar, Hypo/hyperthermia, and Infection. These principles form the foundation of KINC management and represent the essential domains that must be systematically addressed in every newborn. The five core principles themselves are to maintain normal body temperature, provide safe oxygen therapy, maintain normal glucose levels, ensure adequate feeds and fluids for sick and small babies, and implement robust infection prevention and control practices.

Systematic observation begins in the delivery room, where preparation must include ensuring adequate ambient temperature and having resuscitation equipment ready according to Helping Babies Breathe and Golden Minute protocols to enable prompt intervention if required. Post-delivery assessment follows a structured approach: rapid assessment immediately after birth focuses on the APGAR score and detection of any signs of distress, followed by a full postnatal ward assessment that includes head-to-toe examination, ongoing observation for signs of distress, and identification of any risk factors requiring special management. The APGAR score comprises five key components assessed at one and five minutes: colour, tone, heart rate, cry or respiratory effort, and activity level. The full assessment in the postnatal ward must include vital signs (pulse and respiratory rate) alongside SpO2 measurement at birth and at six hours post-delivery, with SpO2 saturation being the most important vital sign in guiding oxygen therapy decisions.

Oxygen management is guided by the fundamental principle that oxygen is a drug and excessive administration is harmful, requiring careful titration and continuous monitoring to maintain appropriate saturation while minimizing complications. Target oxygen saturations differ by gestational age: term neonates should maintain SpO2 above 92%, while preterm neonates should target 90 to 94% saturation. During resuscitation, term babies are started at 21% FiO2 and increased as required, whereas preterm babies begin at 30% FiO2 and are titrated upward based on response. Oxygen therapy is indicated in several clinical scenarios including severe hypothermia, respiratory failure, circulatory failure, and severe respiratory distress syndrome characterized by respiratory rate above 80 breaths per minute, severe chest indrawing or grunting, SpO2 below 90%, or central cyanosis. Multiple delivery routes are available depending on severity and setting: nasal prongs oxygen (NPO2) and face mask oxygen (FMO2) represent standard approaches, with headbox oxygen as a backup option, while CPAP is considered for severe RDS when available, and ventilation is employed with a view toward transfer to a tertiary facility.

Blood glucose monitoring follows a tiered schedule based on risk: all newborns should have glucose checked within one hour of delivery, small and sick babies require measurement every three hours for the first 24 hours and until glucose remains normal for a full 24 hours, and large-for-gestational-age infants plus infants of diabetic mothers require hourly testing for the first six hours. Additional groups requiring glucose monitoring include hypothermic babies and those who have not yet fed. Prevention of hypoglycaemia is paramount and involves feeding preterm babies immediately after birth, providing nasogastric feeds with expressed breast milk or cup feeds if sucking is inadequate, using a neonatal nasogastric line immediately for babies who cannot take oral feeds, and maintaining thermal neutrality. When blood glucose falls in the mild hypoglycaemia range of 1.4 to 2.5 mmol/litre, feed immediately and repeat glucose measurement in 15 minutes; if glucose remains low, treat as severe hypoglycaemia, but if normal, check three-hourly until sustained normoglycaemia is confirmed over 24 hours. Severe hypoglycaemia (glucose below 1.4 mmol/litre) demands urgent intervention: administer a 10% dextrose bolus of 2 millilitres per kilogram via nasogastric line, continue dextrose infusion at the recommended rate for the baby's age and gestation, and recheck glucose in 15 minutes; if glucose remains low, increase dextrose concentration or seek specialist consultation. Hyperglycaemia occurs commonly in small, sick, and septic babies and is managed by checking hydration status and correcting any deficits, switching intravenous fluids to 5% dextrose if already on infusions, and considering insulin therapy if glucose remains above 14 mmol/litre, in which case specialist advice should be sought.

Thermoregulation is critical for preventing hypothermia-related complications and begins immediately at birth by thoroughly drying the newborn and providing skin-to-skin contact if possible; if skin-to-skin is not feasible, the baby should be covered and clothed with only necessary observation areas left uncovered. Early feeding supports thermoregulation by providing metabolic fuel for heat generation. The newborn unit environment must be maintained at 24 to 25 degrees Celsius, kept free of draughts, with the wall thermometer checked at least four times daily and readings recorded to ensure consistent environmental thermal support. Temperature monitoring of the baby itself varies by weight and clinical status: infants below 1.2 kilograms with serious infection require hourly temperature measurement, babies weighing 1.2 to 1.5 kilograms require three-hourly monitoring, and stable babies above 1.5 kilograms require six-hourly monitoring.

Infection prevention and control encompasses multiple interconnected practices that must be consistently applied throughout the neonatal unit. Hand hygiene is fundamental and must be performed on entering the unit and between patients using either soap and water or alcohol-based hand sanitizer. Comprehensive IPC measures also include isolation protocols and appropriate admission management for sick neonates or those suspected of infection, consistent application of clean nursing practices, documentation of staff compliance, and proper cleaning and decontamination of all equipment between patient uses. Antibiotics warrant consideration in several risk categories: babies weighing less than 1.5 kilograms, those born in potentially infected environments such as following prolonged rupture of membranes, infants with obvious clinical signs of infection, babies born at less than 37 weeks gestation without an apparent reason for preterm delivery, and those presenting with respiratory distress. First-line empiric therapy for suspected neonatal infection consists of penicillin or ampicillin, with gentamycin added as required based on clinical judgment for gram-negative coverage. For suspected meningitis, ampicillin combined with cefotaxime at high dose is employed for broader central nervous system penetration. Initial investigations on admission include full blood count, C-reactive protein, and blood culture; follow-up investigations should be obtained after at least 48 hours. Antibiotics may be ceased if CRP is normal and blood culture is negative in a clinically stable baby, allowing discontinuation of unnecessary prolonged therapy.

## NEONATAL JAUNDICE

### Lecture Notes

#### Neonatal Jaundice and Metabolic Disturbances

Neonatal jaundice—visible yellowing of skin and sclera—appears when bilirubin exceeds 85–120 μmol/ℓ and demands systematic investigation and urgent management to prevent kernicterus, the devastating neurotoxic sequela of bilirubin encephalopathy. Understanding bilirubin metabolism, distinguishing physiological from pathological jaundice, recognising dangerous signs, and mastering metabolic complication management are core competencies for neonatal care.

Bilirubin metabolism begins with breakdown of haemoglobin into globin and heme. Heme oxygenase catalyses the next critical step, converting heme to biliverdin while releasing carbon monoxide (a reaction inhibited by metalloporphyrins, which can suppress hyperbilirubinaemia experimentally). Biliverdin reductase then converts biliverdin to unconjugated bilirubin, the fat-soluble form that circulates bound to albumin. In the liver, unconjugated bilirubin is taken up via ligandin protein and conjugated with glucuronic acid to become water-soluble conjugated bilirubin, which is then excreted into bile, concentrated in the gallbladder, and delivered to the intestines. In the neonatal gut, bacterial enzymes deconjugate bilirubin, producing urobilinogen (excreted in urine) and stercobilin (excreted in faeces). Crucially, a significant fraction of unconjugated bilirubin is reabsorbed from the intestine and recycled back to the liver—the enterohepatic circulation—which prolongs jaundice in early life, particularly with poor feeding or meconium retention.

Pathological jaundice is defined by three criteria: total serum bilirubin exceeding 220 μmol/ℓ in term infants or 204 μmol/ℓ in preterm infants within the first 24 hours, a rise exceeding 85 μmol/ℓ per day, direct (conjugated) bilirubin above 34 μmol/ℓ, or persistence beyond one week in term infants or two weeks in preterm infants. Danger signs demanding immediate evaluation include family history of significant haemolytic disease, onset in the first 24 hours, onset after day 3, vomiting, lethargy, poor feeding, fever, high-pitched cry, dark urine, or pale stool.

Physiological jaundice—unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia below 220 μmol/ℓ in the first week of life—results from four mechanisms: increased red blood cell breakdown in the postnatal period, defective hepatic uptake, conjugation, and excretion of bilirubin due to neonatal enzyme immaturity, inadequate hepatic perfusion, and increased enterohepatic circulation from poor feeding and delayed meconium passage. In the first week (days 3–5), jaundice is most commonly physiological, secondary to blood group incompatibility, bleeding and bruising, infection (intrauterine or acquired), immaturity, or maternal diabetes. By days 5–7, causes shift toward infections (urinary tract infection, gram-negative sepsis), neonatal hepatitis syndrome, metabolic disturbances such as galactosaemia, endocrine disorders like hypothyroidism, miscellaneous syndromes, and biliary atresia.

Kernicterus—yellow staining of basal ganglia and hippocampus—occurs when high concentrations of unconjugated, free bilirubin cross the blood-brain barrier, particularly in areas of high blood flow and high metabolic demand. Risk factors include asphyxia, sepsis, acidosis, hypoglycaemia, and respiratory distress syndrome, all of which compromise the integrity of the blood-brain barrier or impair hepatic clearance. Early clinical signs—poor feeding, irritability, temperature instability, and cycling movements—appear within the first week. Progressive signs develop as encephalopathy worsens: opisthotonus (backward arching of the body), crossed extension of the legs, high-pitched cry, and setting sun eyes (downward deviation of eyes), reflecting basal ganglia involvement. Terminal signs—gastric and pulmonary haemorrhage—herald multi-organ failure. Survivors suffer devastating long-term sequelae: hypotonia progressing to hypertonia with spastic cerebral palsy, choreoathetosis (involuntary writhing movements), developmental delay, intellectual impairment, high-tone sensorineural hearing loss, dental dysplasia, and clumsiness. In preterm infants, kernicterus presents subtly as jaundice with fisting, increased tone, and apnoea.

Rh disease develops when an Rh-negative mother carrying an Rh-positive fetus experiences fetal-maternal haemorrhage, triggering maternal antibody production against the fetal D antigen. Subsequent pregnancies are at risk as maternal anti-D antibodies cross the placenta and haemolyse fetal red blood cells. Disease severity depends on parity and the gestational age at which antibodies cross the placenta; earlier crossing produces more severe disease. Clinical manifestations include early onset jaundice, anaemia, hepatosplenomegaly, generalised oedema, hypoproteinaemia, cardiac failure, and ascites (hydrops fetalis), the most severe form. Amniocentesis, abortion, and external cephalic version are obstetric procedures that increase fetal-maternal bleeding and Rh sensitisation risk. Prevention begins with screening Rh-negative women for anti-D antibodies; following sensitising events, anti-D (Rh-hyperimmune gammaglobulin) must be administered within 48 hours. Anti-D binds to fetal cells bearing the D antigen and facilitates their rapid removal from maternal circulation before sensitisation can occur, providing passive immunisation that protects subsequent pregnancies.

ABO incompatibility—group O mother with group A or B infant—is more common than Rh disease but typically milder because naturally occurring anti-A and anti-B antibodies are predominantly IgM, which cross the placenta poorly, or are weak reactants, and the Coombs test is frequently negative despite ongoing haemolysis. Anaemia and hepatosplenomegaly are unusual; factors that aggravate hyperbilirubinaemia include low birth weight, haemorrhage, and infection.

Management of neonatal jaundice prioritises early detection through daily clinical examination and prevention of kernicterus via aggressive treatment. The first step is daily examination to detect jaundice early; the second is investigating causes according to the differential diagnosis outlined above. Four parameters must always be checked: formal total serum bilirubin (TSB), group and cross-match (G&C) to identify blood group incompatibility, fluid balance to ensure adequate intake preventing enterohepatic circulation, and feeds to optimise nutrition and gut motility. Maintenance fluids must be correct—60–150 ml/kg/day depending on postnatal age and prematurity—and increased fluid losses from open incubators, phototherapy lights, or radiant heaters demand compensation. In severe jaundice, feeding should be encouraged under phototherapy lights or via nasogastric tube to maximise bilirubin excretion and limit interruption of phototherapy. Twenty percent albumin enhances bilirubin binding, preventing free bilirubin toxicity, and is administered at 5 ml/kg IV. Phototherapy—blue or white light at wavelengths 460–490 nm—converts unconjugated bilirubin to isomers that are water-soluble and can be excreted without hepatic conjugation; the principle is to maximise exposure time. Exchange transfusion, the final intervention for severe hyperbilirubinaemia unresponsive to phototherapy, replaces the infant's blood with fresh donor blood, simultaneously removing bilirubin and correcting anaemia if present.

Fluid and electrolyte disturbances are endemic in the neonatal period and closely intertwined with jaundice management. Neonatal fluid requirements of 60–150 ml/kg/day are higher than in older children due to greater insensible losses through immature skin, particularly in preterm infants. Osmotic diuresis, excess gut losses, and diuretic therapy increase requirements further; conversely, excessive parenteral fluids, repeated arterial line flushing, and bolus drug doses cause fluid overload, precipitating cardiac failure, pulmonary oedema, patent ductus arteriosus, necrotising enterocolitis, and electrolyte disturbances. Asphyxiated infants, those with poor renal function, and those with leaky capillaries are particularly prone to fluid retention and demand careful monitoring every 6–12 hours.

Hypoglycaemia (blood glucose <2.5 mmol/ℓ confirmed on a second sample) affects neonates at high risk: those with decreased glucose intake (poor feeding), depleted glycogen and fat stores (prematurity, growth restriction), hyperinsulinism (infants of diabetic mothers), or increased consumption during hypothermia, asphyxia, respiratory distress, or infection. Clinical signs—jitteriness, lethargy, poor sucking, convulsions, hypothermia, and apnoea—are non-specific and often attributed to other causes, necessitating high clinical suspicion and rapid glucose screening within one hour of birth in at-risk infants, then hourly until glucose stabilises above 3 mmol/ℓ. Management begins with feeding all infants within 30 minutes of delivery and frequent breastfeeds (1–2 hourly); if poor sucking is present, expressed breast milk is given via nasogastric tube. Glucose is checked 30 minutes after each feed; if it remains below 2.5 mmol/ℓ, an intravenous drip is established and 10% dextrose in neonatal normal saline is given as a 3–5 ml/kg bolus, followed by maintenance intravenous fluids while breastfeeds continue. Advanced management for persistent hypoglycaemia includes urine dipstix testing for glucosuria, 15% glucose via central line (avoiding extravasation injury), and pharmacological agents (hydrocortisone, glucagon); the underlying aetiology must be investigated to guide definitive treatment.

Hyperglycaemia (glucose >10 mmol/ℓ) occurs in extreme prematurity, stress states (intraventricular haemorrhage), iatrogenic causes (poorly controlled intravenous fluid concentration), sepsis, and asphyxia. Management begins with ensuring correct drip rate and monitoring for glucosuria; intraventricular haemorrhage must be screened for. If hyperglycaemia persists, the infusion is changed to a 5% dextrose cocktail with hourly glucose monitoring; if still elevated, it is further reduced to half-saline. Blood tests for insulin, cortisol, growth hormone, liver and thyroid function are obtained. If insulin is required, 2 iu of Actrapid is mixed in 20 ml of half-saline solution; the initial bolus is 0.05 iu/kg stat, followed by continuous infusion at 0.05 iu/kg. Once glucose falls below 12 mmol/ℓ, the infusion is changed back to 5% dextrose cocktail; when glucose reaches below 10 mmol/ℓ, insulin is weaned and the infusion is changed to neonatal normal saline.

Hypocalcaemia (serum calcium <2 mmol/ℓ in term infants or <1.8 mmol/ℓ in preterm infants) affects approximately one-third of low birth weight infants and 50% of infants of diabetic mothers within the first three days of life. Relative hypoparathyroidism, caused by suppression of the fetal parathyroid gland by transplacental maternal parathyroid hormone, is the primary mechanism in early hypocalcaemia and improves within days as neonatal parathyroid function matures. Preterm infants are calcium-deficient due to inadequate intrauterine mineral accretion. Asphyxia, correction of acidosis, and endotracheal feeding can precipitate hypocalcaemia. Clinical manifestations include asymptomatic disease detected only by screening, twitching, jitteriness, seizures, high-pitched cry, and a prolonged QT segment on electrocardiography. Treatment varies by severity: asymptomatic infants receive 1–3 g of calcium gluconate or lactate orally daily; those with symptoms receive 3 ml of 10% calcium gluconate per 100 ml neonatal normal saline added to feeds. For acute symptomatic hypocalcaemia, 2 ml/kg of 10% calcium gluconate is diluted and infused slowly intravenously.

Hypomagnesaemia (serum magnesium <0.62 mmol/ℓ) occurs in infants of diabetic mothers, small-for-gestational-age infants, and after exchange transfusion. Clinical signs mirror hypocalcaemia. Treatment is 0.1 ml of 50% magnesium sulphate administered intravenously or intramuscularly, repeated 12-hourly for three doses.

Hypernatraemia (sodium >150 mmol/ℓ) results from insufficient fluid intake or dehydration secondary to excessive insensible water loss from phototherapy lights and radiant heaters. The pathophysiology involves osmotic shift of water from brain tissue, causing brain cell shrinkage and pressure differentials that lead to capillary dilatation and rupture, precipitating cerebral venous thrombosis and intracranial haemorrhage. Clinical signs include lethargy, irritability, and convulsions. Serum sodium must be lowered slowly over 24–48 hours to prevent cerebral oedema.

Hyponatraemia (sodium <125 mmol/ℓ) reflects water being drawn into brain cells, causing central nervous system injury. Very low birth weight infants less than 32 weeks gestation cannot conserve sodium efficiently and suffer high urinary sodium losses. Respiratory distress syndrome combined with asphyxia triggers inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion, leading to sodium loss in urine, water intoxication, and diarrhoea. Clinical manifestations include lethargy, poor feeding, apnoea, and shock. Treatment depends on severity and rate of onset, with fluid restriction being the primary intervention.

Hypokalaemia (potassium <3 mmol/ℓ) results from poor intake, alkalosis, diarrhoea, or diuretic therapy. Hyperkalaemia (potassium >7 mmol/ℓ) is far more dangerous and occurs in severe catabolic states, acute renal failure, hypoxia, shock, acidosis, and congenital adrenal hyperplasia. Electrocardiographic signs of hyperkalaemia—peaked T waves, prolonged PR interval, absent P waves, and arrhythmias—demand immediate recognition and aggressive treatment. Management follows a stepwise cascade monitored continuously by electrocardiography. First, calcium gluconate 10% at 1 ml/kg is given slowly to stabilise myocardial membrane potential and prevent arrhythmias. Second, sodium bicarbonate (Sodabic) 2 mmol/kg is administered to correct acidosis, which worsens hyperkalaemia. Third, salbutamol 4 mcg/kg IV bolus drives potassium intracellularly via beta-adrenergic stimulation. Fourth, glucose and insulin shift potassium into cells: 2 iu of Actrapid is mixed in 20 ml of half-saline solution, with an initial bolus of 0.05 iu/kg stat, followed by continuous infusion at 0.05 iu/kg. Fifth, kayexalate 1 g/kg/day is given orally or per rectum to bind potassium in the intestinal lumen for faecal excretion. This multi-agent approach addresses the life-threatening arrhythmia risk while working toward sustained potassium normalisation through renal excretion and gastrointestinal elimination.

The neonatal period presents a compressed timeline for metabolic derangements to cause permanent harm; vigilant screening, accurate diagnosis, and decisive intervention prevent kernicterus, seizures, cerebral injury, and death.

## PMTCT updated 2020

### Lecture Notes

#### PMTCT — Elimination of Mother-to-Child Transmission (EMTCT)

Mother-to-child transmission (MTCT) of HIV remains the primary mode of infection in children, yet it is the most preventable. Without any intervention, transmission risk ranges from 15 to 45 percent globally, but effective prevention strategies can reduce this below 5 percent. Understanding the mechanisms, risk stratification, and evolving clinical guidelines—especially the shift from 2015 to 2019 recommendations—is essential for paediatric HIV care and the delivery of EMTCT services from preconception through 18 months of age (or six weeks after cessation of breastfeeding if continued beyond 18 months).

**Transmission Routes and Rates**

HIV reaches infants through three distinct perinatal pathways, each contributing different proportions to overall transmission. Intrauterine transmission accounts for 25 to 40 percent of perinatal infections and occurs predominantly in the third trimester when placental integrity breaks down, allowing microtransfusions of viraemic maternal blood to cross into the fetal circulation. The majority of transmission—60 to 75 percent—occurs during the intrapartum period, when the infant's mucosal membranes contact maternal blood and secretions, with additional passage of infected cells across the placenta during labour contractions. Ruptured membranes exceeding four hours duration significantly increase intrapartum risk. Post-natally, breastfeeding contributes an additional 5 to 28 percent transmission risk if the mother remains viraemic; women who seroconvert after delivery have higher transmission rates than those infected before pregnancy, and mastitis—with elevated breast milk HIV RNA—further increases this risk. Exclusive breastfeeding is safer than mixed feeding. Cumulative perinatal transmission without intervention breaks down as intrauterine five percent, peripartum 13 to 18 percent, and breastfeeding five to 28 percent, summing to 13 to 39 percent overall.

The primary maternal determinant of transmission is plasma and breastmilk viral load. Transmission risk scales dramatically with viral burden: at less than 400 copies per millilitre, transmission risk is zero percent (95% confidence interval 0–11%), rising to 8.6 percent at 400–3500 copies/ml, 19.4 percent at 3500–10,000 copies/ml, and 34.8 percent above 30,000 copies/ml. However, transmission is possible at all maternal viral load levels. Plasma and cervico-vaginal secretion viral loads do not always correlate, emphasising the importance of direct testing. Secondary maternal risk factors include low CD4 lymphocyte count, clinical stage of disease, genital tract infections, placental inflammation, hepatitis C and CMV co-infection, bacterial vaginosis, maternal drug use, and inadequate AZT coverage during pregnancy. Obstetrical factors—vaginal delivery, prolonged rupture of membranes, chorioamnionitis, scalp electrodes, and birth of the first twin—increase risk. Maternal cigarette smoking and low CD4 counts further compound transmission risk. Infant prematurity is an independent risk factor.

**Clinical Manifestations and Outcomes**

At birth, infected and uninfected infants are clinically indistinguishable. Slow-progression disease emerges with oral thrush, diffuse lymphadenopathy, hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, and recurrent diarrhoea. Rapid-onset presentations include life-threatening opportunistic infections—notably Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia at four to six months of age—as well as obscure manifestations such as thrombocytopenia, HIV encephalopathy, nephrotic syndrome, cardiac failure, and failure to thrive. Early complications include growth failure and central nervous system involvement. Among AIDS-defining conditions in children under 13 years, Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia is most common (35%), followed by lymphoid interstitial pneumonitis (23%), recurrent bacterial infections (21%), HIV wasting syndrome (19%), and HIV encephalopathy (17%). Post-HAART, life expectancy for perinatally infected children now exceeds 95 percent survival to 16 years of age.

**Laboratory Diagnosis and Testing Limitations**

Maternal and infant HIV antibody assays are unreliable in young children because transplacentally acquired maternal antibodies persist, causing false-positive results. Antibody tests may also be falsely negative in the first weeks after acute maternal infection. HIV DNA polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is the diagnostic tool for neonates and young infants. Qualitative HIV-1 DNA PCR detects proviral DNA within peripheral blood mononuclear cells, using either whole blood or dried blood samples, and remains positive even in patients on antiretroviral therapy. DNA PCR cannot detect very recent infection reliably—two-thirds of MTCT occurs perinatally, so sensitivity at birth is only 55 percent. However, sensitivity rises to greater than 90 percent by two to four weeks and reaches 100 percent at one, three, and six months of age. Complementary tests include HIV RNA viral load quantitation (assessing prognosis and therapy response), genotype and phenotype resistance testing, and therapeutic drug monitoring to optimise antiretroviral regimens.

**2015 versus 2019 Guideline Changes**

The evolution of PMTCT guidance reflects improved evidence and operational simplification. In 2015, pregnant mothers underwent HIV testing at first visit and every three months thereafter; by 2019, testing was integrated with routine antenatal care visits (eight visits total). Maternal viral load testing was newly formalised in 2019 as mandatory at delivery and again at six months postpartum (or six-monthly if breastfeeding). For infants, the 2015 algorithm included PCR at birth, 10 weeks, and 14 weeks for high-risk cases, with 18 months rapid testing for confirmation. The 2019 guidelines eliminated the 14-week PCR test, added a new 6-month PCR for all exposed infants, and changed the 18-month test to universal rapid HIV testing for all exposed uninfected children, reserving PCR as the confirmatory test up to two years of age (rather than rapid confirmation). The definition of high-risk infants shifted from a binary "maternal VL >1000 c/ml or ART <4 weeks prior to delivery" to the more nuanced 2019 criteria: maternal VL greater than 1000 c/ml at delivery, most recent VL during the final 12 weeks of antenatal care, or absence of a VL result in the last 12 weeks of antenatal care.

**Infant Post-Exposure Prophylaxis and Management**

High-risk infants receive post-exposure prophylaxis with AZT for six weeks and nevirapine (NVP) for a minimum of 12 weeks. NVP should be administered as soon as possible after birth—ideally before but no later than after birth PCR collection, as any delay in testing must not delay NVP initiation. The first dose is given as soon as possible; subsequent doses are one per 24-hour period, with repeat dosing once only if the infant vomits. The decision to continue NVP depends on maternal viral load: if the mother's VL remains less than 1000 copies per millilitre after more than 12 weeks of NVP, the drug can be stopped; if greater than 1000, NVP must continue until one week after breastfeeding cessation. If maternal VL elevates during the monitoring period, high-risk prophylaxis is restarted with AZT 12-hourly (six weeks) and NVP daily (minimum 12 weeks), with dosages adjusted to the infant's weight. When NVP-positive and a repeat confirmatory HIV PCR is obtained, breastfeeding may continue. Cotrimoxazole prophylaxis can be stopped once the infant is confirmed HIV-negative six weeks after breastfeeding cessation, or after a negative 10-week PCR if not breastfeeding. Infants diagnosed as HIV-positive on PCR should immediately stop NVP and AZT, undergo confirmatory testing with a second HIV PCR, and initiate antiretroviral therapy without waiting for confirmatory results.

For abandoned infants with unknown HIV exposure, assume high-risk status: perform birth HIV PCR and initiate high-risk prophylaxis with both AZT and NVP. If the initial PCR is positive, confirm with viral load, start ART, and discontinue both NVP and AZT. If initial PCR is negative, repeat PCR at 10 weeks of age and continue the testing algorithm. Indeterminate PCR results require checking prior PCR and viral load history. If PCR is positive or indeterminate but viral load is detectable, treat as infected and initiate ART. If PCR is negative or indeterminate with undetectable viral load and no prior results, repeat both PCR and viral load urgently. Once repeat results are negative, follow the standard testing algorithm for exposed infants.

**Testing Algorithm and Age-Based Confirmation**

Children younger than 18 months require PCR screening with PCR confirmatory testing. Between 18 months and two years, rapid screening tests are followed by PCR confirmation. Children older than two years use rapid tests for both screening and confirmation. For any positive or indeterminate PCR or rapid result in a child under two years, confirmation with a new-sample HIV PCR is mandatory, but antiretroviral therapy initiation must not be delayed pending confirmatory results. The routine testing schedule across infancy includes PCR at birth (55% sensitivity), PCR at 10 weeks (>90% sensitivity), PCR at six months (100% sensitivity for all exposed), and universal rapid HIV testing at 18 months. Testing is also performed six weeks after cessation of breastfeeding if breastfeeding extends beyond the standard schedule.

**High-Risk Infant Definition**

Under 2019 guidelines, an infant is classified as high-risk at birth if the mother's viral load exceeds 1000 copies per millilitre at delivery, if her most recent viral load during the final 12 weeks of antenatal care was greater than 1000 copies/ml, or if no viral load was documented in the last 12 weeks of antenatal care. The 2015 definition was more restrictive (VL >1000 c/ml or ART <4 weeks prior to delivery), but 2019 recognised that missing maternal VL results during this critical window itself constitutes sufficient risk to warrant high-risk prophylaxis.

**TB Co-Exposure and Preventive Care**

When an exposed neonate has been exposed to tuberculosis, clinical examination is the first step. If the neonate is asymptomatic, isoniazid prophylaxis at 10 milligrams per kilogram daily is given for six months; BCG vaccination is delayed and administered two weeks after isoniazid completion. If clinical TB is suspected, referral for evaluation is mandatory. The typical three kilogram neonate receives 30 milligrams daily; a four kilogram infant receives 40 milligrams daily.

**Breastfeeding Recommendations**

For mothers receiving ART, antiretroviral therapy should be continued for a minimum of 24 months or longer, in line with recommendations for the general population. While breastfeeding increases transmission risk by five to 28 percent if the mother is viraemic, the protective effect of maternal ART and exclusive breastfeeding (safer than mixed feeding) makes breastfeeding appropriate in resource-limited settings where safe formula is unavailable. Maternal HIV counselling and testing is repeated every three months during pregnancy, at labour and delivery, at the six-week postpartum EPI visit, and every six months throughout breastfeeding.

**Antiretroviral Safety in Neonates**

Zidovudine (AZT) is generally safe for neonatal prophylaxis but may cause transient anemia. The combination of 3TC and ZDV may increase hematologic toxicity. Nevirapine is associated with rare but severe rash and hepatotoxicity; resistance may emerge in infants who become infected. Protease inhibitors are not recommended for neonates due to lack of pharmacokinetic data and concerns about cardiac and other toxicities (particularly with lopinavir/ritonavir).

**HIV Life Cycle**

Understanding HIV replication is foundational for comprehending how antiretrovirals intervene. The 10-step cycle begins with free virus in the bloodstream. Step two is binding and fusion: the virus binds to a CD4 molecule and one of two coreceptors (CCR5 or CXCR4), which are abundant on immune cell surfaces, then fuses with the target cell. Step three is infection: the virus penetrates the cell and empties its contents into the cytoplasm. Reverse transcription (step four) converts single-stranded viral RNA into double-stranded DNA via reverse transcriptase. Integration (step five) combines viral DNA with the host cell's DNA through integrase. Transcription (step six) occurs when the infected cell divides and the viral DNA is transcribed into long chains of viral proteins. Assembly (step seven) brings sets of viral protein chains together. Budding (step eight) pushes the immature virus out of the cell, incorporating cell membrane and initiating protease processing of viral proteins. Step nine marks the immature virus breaking free. Maturation (step ten) completes the process as protease finishes processing viral proteins into individual functional units, creating infectious virus particles. This life cycle is targeted by different classes of antiretrovirals: nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs) block step four; non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors block the same step; integrase inhibitors block step five; protease inhibitors block step eight and ten; entry inhibitors block steps two and three.

**EMTCT Service Delivery**

EMTCT services span preconception through 18 months of age and must be embedded across the healthcare continuum: family planning, antenatal care, delivery and labour, postnatal care, EPI vaccination visits, and IMCI (Integrated Management of Neonatal and Childhood Illness) contacts. Early follow-up at 3–6 days post-delivery includes PCR and antiretroviral initiation as indicated. At six weeks, birth PCR and maternal viral load results are reviewed. At ten weeks, PCR is repeated for all previously negative infants. At six months, all exposed uninfected infants receive PCR; HIV status of unexposed children is established through maternal testing or rapid testing on the child if maternal results are unavailable. At 18 months, universal rapid HIV testing is performed for all exposed uninfected children. Throughout this period, if an infant's PCR becomes positive or indeterminate, a new confirmatory PCR sample is obtained and ART initiated without delay. This integrated approach to PMTCT, anchored by high-quality laboratory testing, risk stratification, judicious use of prophylaxis, and adherence to evolving evidence, has transformed perinatal HIV from an inevitable sentence to a manageable chronic condition.

## RDS - KINC

### Lecture Notes

#### Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) — KINC

Respiratory Distress Syndrome, formerly known as Hyaline Membrane Disease (HMD), is a life-threatening condition affecting premature infants born before 34 weeks of gestation. The disease arises from a fundamental deficiency in pulmonary surfactant, a complex lipid-protein substance essential for maintaining alveolar patency throughout the respiratory cycle. Understanding the pathophysiology, clinical presentation, and evidence-based management of RDS is crucial for neonatal care providers.

Surfactant functions as a surface-active agent that dramatically reduces surface tension at the alveolar air-liquid interface. In healthy lungs, this reduces the pressure gradient needed to inflate alveoli and prevents alveolar collapse during expiration. Premature infants have immature lungs that do not yet produce adequate quantities of surfactant, particularly in those born before 34 weeks of gestation. Without sufficient surfactant, alveoli undergo progressive collapse at the end of expiration—a process called micro-collapse. This is not complete pneumothorax but rather widespread small-unit alveolar atelectasis. The collapsed alveoli become fluid-filled, creating pathognomonic radiographic findings while simultaneously increasing the work of breathing and impairing gas exchange.

The clinical presentation of RDS typically manifests within the first hours of life and is characterized by progressive respiratory distress. Affected infants exhibit a respiratory rate exceeding 60 breaths per minute, grunting sounds during expiration (the infant's attempt to maintain positive airway pressure and prevent alveolar collapse), intercostal and subcostal recession (inward collapse of chest wall muscles as the infant works harder to generate negative intrathoracic pressure), generalized cyanosis indicating significant hypoxemia, and nasal flaring as accessory respiratory muscles are recruited. The onset and severity correlate directly with the degree of prematurity and surfactant deficiency. Infants born at 28 weeks are far more likely to develop severe RDS than those born at 32 weeks.

Chest radiography reveals a distinctive ground-glass appearance throughout both lungs, reflecting the uniform distribution of micro-collapsed alveoli filled with fluid. This appearance stems from the fact that collapsed alveolar units appear as an opacified or white field, contrasting with normal air-filled lung. The ground-glass pattern extends to the lung periphery and is bilateral and equal, a key distinguishing feature from other causes of neonatal respiratory distress. In mild RDS, the ground-glass appearance is subtle and the lungs may appear relatively normal in volume. In moderate disease, the ground-glass opacity becomes denser, and air bronchograms—the silhouette of air-filled bronchi appearing as dark lines against the white background of collapsed alveoli—become visible. Air bronchograms appear because patent airways remain air-filled while surrounding alveoli collapse; the contrast between these structures creates this characteristic finding. In severe RDS, the entire lung field appears almost uniformly white with prominent air bronchograms, and lung volumes become markedly reduced. All presentations show bilateral, equal involvement, reflecting the uniform deficiency of surfactant across both lungs.

The initiation of continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) represents the cornerstone of RDS management and should be started as soon as respiratory distress is identified by the KINC assessment criteria. CPAP works by maintaining a distending pressure throughout the respiratory cycle, mechanically opening and holding alveoli patent despite surfactant deficiency. All district hospitals must have the capacity to initiate CPAP immediately when clinically indicated, following the respiratory distress algorithm in the KINC chartbook. The specific pressure settings and oxygen concentration are titrated based on the infant's response, with typical PEEP (positive end-expiratory pressure) starting ranges between 5–7 cm H₂O and inspired oxygen adjusted to achieve acceptable oxygen saturation. Infants who fail to stabilize or improve on CPAP—remaining in severe respiratory distress, showing signs of fatigue, or developing complications—require immediate contact with the referral hospital for transfer and consideration of advanced interventions.

Exogenous surfactant therapy is a life-saving intervention that replaces the missing substance and rapidly improves oxygenation and compliance in many affected infants. Surfactant is administered via the endotracheal route directly into the lungs of mechanically ventilated or CPAP-supported infants. Following surfactant instillation, infants often show dramatic improvement in oxygen requirement and chest X-ray appearance within hours. The specific dosing regimens and repeat-dose criteria vary by surfactant product and institutional protocols, but generally initial doses range from 100–200 mg/kg of phospholipid, with repeat doses given if the infant's condition worsens or fails to improve after the initial dose. Critically, surfactant therapy requires proper training and supervision before implementation in district hospital settings. Many centers have adopted a "rescue" approach where surfactant is given only to infants failing CPAP, while others use early surfactant plus rapid extubation to CPAP (INSURE technique). Regardless of approach, the goal is to minimize duration of mechanical ventilation while ensuring adequate gas exchange.

Mechanical ventilation becomes necessary when CPAP and surfactant therapy fail to maintain adequate oxygenation and ventilation, or when the infant shows signs of respiratory fatigue or severe apnea. Ventilation criteria include persistent hypoxemia despite maximal CPAP support (typically FiO₂ >0.60–0.70 needed to maintain acceptable saturation), hypercapnia with respiratory acidosis indicating inadequate CO₂ elimination, recurrent apnea spells requiring frequent stimulation or intervention, and exhaustion of spontaneous respiratory effort. The goal of mechanical ventilation in RDS is to provide gentle, lung-protective support using low tidal volumes and permissive hypercapnia when possible, avoiding the additional lung injury that can result from aggressive ventilation strategies.

Complications of severe RDS and its treatment can be life-threatening. Pneumothorax, rupture of alveoli into the pleural space, represents a major risk in RDS due to unequal ventilation and air trapping. Affected infants present with acute deterioration, asymmetric breath sounds, and hypotension. Chest X-ray shows free air in the pleural space with mediastinal shift away from the pneumothorax, collapsed lung, and in severe cases, tension physiology. Pneumothorax is a complication particularly associated with aggressive ventilation and with MAS (which causes air-trapping via a ball-valve mechanism); it is also a risk with CPAP in certain conditions, though CPAP is still the first-line respiratory support. Secondary bacterial infection of the damaged lung is common, as the alveolar and bronchial injury makes lung tissue susceptible to colonization and infection. Antibiotic coverage with penicillin and gentamicin is standard for suspected infection in at-risk neonates. Chronic lung disease (bronchopulmonary dysplasia) can develop in infants requiring prolonged mechanical ventilation and oxygen therapy, particularly those born at extremely low birth weight.

Prevention of RDS through antenatal intervention is one of the most effective strategies in perinatal medicine. Any pregnant woman at risk for preterm delivery before 35 weeks of gestation should receive antenatal corticosteroids—specifically, the mother receives the steroids, not the infant. Maternal steroid administration (typically betamethasone or dexamethasone given as two doses 24 hours apart) crosses the placenta and induces fetal lung maturation by accelerating surfactant production in the fetal lungs. This intervention dramatically reduces the incidence and severity of RDS, as well as reducing neonatal mortality, intraventricular hemorrhage, and necrotizing enterocolitis. The benefit is greatest when delivery occurs within 7 days of steroid administration. Even a single dose of antenatal steroids confers significant protection, though the full benefit is realized after two doses. This simple, inexpensive, and remarkably effective intervention should be part of routine care for all threatened preterm deliveries below 35 weeks gestation.

Management of RDS requires coordinated, systematic assessment and support beginning immediately after birth. The KINC guidelines provide a structured approach to recognition and escalation. When respiratory distress is identified, OOSHI assessment (Observations, Oxygen, Sugar, Hypothermia, Infections) should be completed first to rule out other correctable causes. CPAP should then be initiated promptly, and the clinical response monitored. Infants should receive antenatal steroids if delivery is anticipated before 35 weeks, and appropriate temperature management, fluid support, and prevention of infection must be maintained throughout. As emphasized in KINC protocols: when in doubt—ask. Contact with the referral hospital should occur early, before an infant deteriorates irreversibly, allowing for timely transfer and access to advanced support including mechanical ventilation, surfactant therapy, and specialized neonatal intensive care.

## ROUTINE CARE AND FOLLOW UP OF THE NEWBORN

### Lecture Notes

#### Routine Care and Follow-Up of the Newborn

Immediate care after birth begins with drying the baby with a warm towel and assessing vital signs. The labour-ward assessment and classification relies on three critical questions: Is the baby breathing spontaneously? Is the heart rate more than 100 bpm? Is the baby active and moving? If the answer to any question is no, the baby requires resuscitation. The Apgar score is assessed at 1 minute and 5 minutes after birth; the 1-minute score indicates whether resuscitation is needed, while the 5-minute score indicates whether resuscitation has been successful.

Every newborn requires seven essential elements of immediate care: cord clamping, vitamin K administration, drying and warming, neonatal vitamin prophylaxis (NVP) or AZT if HIV-exposed, immunizations, and transfer to the mother for breastfeeding if the baby is well. Maternal and birth history must be documented to identify high-risk categories: prematurity or low birth weight below 2 kg, maternal diabetes, maternal tuberculosis, other maternal illnesses (such as cardiac disease with warfarin exposure), reactive serological tests for syphilis (RPR positive), Rh negative mothers, and congenital abnormalities. These categories determine admission location and level of care.

Admission and warmth management is stratified by weight and condition. All extremely low birth weight (ELBW) babies must be admitted to the high care area and placed in a servo-controlled incubator wrapped in plastic to maintain thermoregulation. Very low birth weight (VLBW) babies are admitted to high care for initial observation; if ill, they remain in a servo-controlled incubator, but once well they can use a standard incubator and transition to intermittent kangaroo mother care (KMC). Low birth weight babies under 2 kg are admitted to the neonatal unit for observation, and once well and stable can progress to intermittent KMC. Babies over 2 kg who are ill or high-risk are admitted to high care. For well babies over 2 kg, KMC is the primary warmth method.

Feeding is managed according to maturity and clinical status. Babies under 1.5 kg and all sick babies are kept nil by mouth for at least the first 24 hours. Once the acute phase stabilizes, sick babies transition to nasogastric tube feeding. When a baby is no longer receiving head box oxygen, cup feeding can be initiated. Once the baby demonstrates effective suckling, transition to breastfeeding occurs. Blood glucose is monitored three-hourly for the first 24 hours in all infants.

Continuous observation and monitoring are essential during the neonatal period. Babies receiving oxygen require continuous pulse oximetry until optimal saturation is achieved. Vital signs including heart rate, respiratory rate, temperature, colour, and activity are observed and recorded according to the baby's classification. Babies with birth weight below 1.5 kg or gestational age below 35 weeks are at high risk for apnoea and require respiratory stimulant therapy. Available medications include intravenous aminophylline, oral theophylline, or caffeine. Treatment continues until the baby reaches 1.8 kg or has been apnoea-free for at least 7 days, whichever comes first. Discharge can only occur after apnoea-free status for more than 7 days and cessation of theophylline therapy.

Oxygen therapy is indicated for respiratory rate greater than 80 breaths per minute, severe chest indrawing, grunting, or blood oxygen saturation below 88%. Antibiotic regimens are initiated for babies with unknown prolonged rupture of membranes (UPTL), premature rupture of membranes (PROM), or obvious signs of infection. The standard regimen is intravenous penicillin 100,000 U/kg twice daily combined with gentamycin 5 mg per kilogram daily. C-reactive protein (CRP) is checked after 48 hours of prophylactic treatment; if normal and the baby is well, antibiotics are stopped. If infection is confirmed, antibiotics continue for a total of 5 days.

Micronutrient supplementation begins on day 5 once feeding is well established. Multivitamin drops are given at 0.6 ml daily and must contain 400 IU of vitamin D. Babies under 1.5 kg require additional separate vitamin D supplementation of 400 IU daily. Iron supplementation in the form of ferrous lactate (Ferrodrops) starts from day 14 at a dose of 0.6 ml daily if the baby is feeding well.

Growth and development are monitored with daily weight measurements charted on the baby record, while head circumference and haemoglobin are recorded weekly. Discharge criteria for normal term babies require the mother to be well and coping, the baby to be well and feeding well (preferably breastfeeding), and if birth weight is below 2.5 kg, the mother to be practising KMC. For small or sick babies, discharge is permitted once the baby is no longer sick, has lost less than 10 per cent of birth weight, demonstrates weight gain of at least 10 grams per kilogram per day, is feeding well, the mother is well and coping with feeding, KMC is being practised, and the KMC score is adequate. Additional discharge requirements include weight of at least 1.8 kg, a KMC score exceeding 19 in breastfeeding babies, apnoea-free status for at least 7 days with no ongoing theophylline therapy, known HIV status with antiretrovirals prescribed if positive, and iron and multivitamin supplements prescribed.

Administrative requirements before discharge include documentation of immunizations given, detailed recording of birth and post-natal period information on the Road to Health card and discharge summary, scheduling of routine follow-up visit dates, and maternal counselling regarding emergency signs and when to seek care.

Follow-up guidance differs by urgency. Mothers should bring babies to primary health care (PHC) clinic for discharge from the eyes, umbilical sepsis with pus drainage and redness around the umbilicus, septic skin lesions typically presenting as pustules, and jaundice. Hospital referral is indicated for poor feeding or poor suckling (an early sign of severe illness), convulsions, rapid breathing, diarrhoea, and bleeding. Routine high-risk follow-up visits are required for babies with birth weight below 2 kg, those who experienced sepsis, necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC), or meningitis, those with encephalopathy, those with severe jaundice, those with congenital abnormalities, those with documented hypoglycaemia, and those requiring invasive positive pressure ventilation (IPPV) or continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) during admission.

Follow-up visit assessments are comprehensive and include evaluation of growth parameters, neurodevelopmental milestones and developmental progress, feeding history and current practices, eligibility for social and care dependency grants, identification of new problems and parental concerns, follow-up of outstanding investigations, retinopathy of prematurity (ROP) and audiology screening and referral, onward referrals as needed to physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, or dietetics services, and repeat HIV polymerase chain reaction (PCR) testing for infants with maternal HIV exposure.
