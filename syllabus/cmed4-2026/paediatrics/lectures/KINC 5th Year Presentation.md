### KINC — 5th Year Presentation

KINC, the KZN Initiative on Newborn Care, is a comprehensive framework developed by the KZN Department of Health for managing newborns in resource-limited settings such as district and regional hospitals. This initiative provides essential guidance for routine neonatal care while placing particular emphasis on the management of sick and small newborns, enabling clinicians to deliver high-quality care even in settings with constrained resources. The OOSHI mnemonic encodes the five core principles of newborn care: Observation, Oxygen, Sugar, Hypo/hyperthermia, and Infection. These principles form the foundation of KINC management and represent the essential domains that must be systematically addressed in every newborn. The five core principles themselves are to maintain normal body temperature, provide safe oxygen therapy, maintain normal glucose levels, ensure adequate feeds and fluids for sick and small babies, and implement robust infection prevention and control practices.

Systematic observation begins in the delivery room, where preparation must include ensuring adequate ambient temperature and having resuscitation equipment ready according to Helping Babies Breathe and Golden Minute protocols to enable prompt intervention if required. Post-delivery assessment follows a structured approach: rapid assessment immediately after birth focuses on the APGAR score and detection of any signs of distress, followed by a full postnatal ward assessment that includes head-to-toe examination, ongoing observation for signs of distress, and identification of any risk factors requiring special management. The APGAR score comprises five key components assessed at one and five minutes: colour, tone, heart rate, cry or respiratory effort, and activity level. The full assessment in the postnatal ward must include vital signs (pulse and respiratory rate) alongside SpO2 measurement at birth and at six hours post-delivery, with SpO2 saturation being the most important vital sign in guiding oxygen therapy decisions.

Oxygen management is guided by the fundamental principle that oxygen is a drug and excessive administration is harmful, requiring careful titration and continuous monitoring to maintain appropriate saturation while minimizing complications. Target oxygen saturations differ by gestational age: term neonates should maintain SpO2 above 92%, while preterm neonates should target 90 to 94% saturation. During resuscitation, term babies are started at 21% FiO2 and increased as required, whereas preterm babies begin at 30% FiO2 and are titrated upward based on response. Oxygen therapy is indicated in several clinical scenarios including severe hypothermia, respiratory failure, circulatory failure, and severe respiratory distress syndrome characterized by respiratory rate above 80 breaths per minute, severe chest indrawing or grunting, SpO2 below 90%, or central cyanosis. Multiple delivery routes are available depending on severity and setting: nasal prongs oxygen (NPO2) and face mask oxygen (FMO2) represent standard approaches, with headbox oxygen as a backup option, while CPAP is considered for severe RDS when available, and ventilation is employed with a view toward transfer to a tertiary facility.

Blood glucose monitoring follows a tiered schedule based on risk: all newborns should have glucose checked within one hour of delivery, small and sick babies require measurement every three hours for the first 24 hours and until glucose remains normal for a full 24 hours, and large-for-gestational-age infants plus infants of diabetic mothers require hourly testing for the first six hours. Additional groups requiring glucose monitoring include hypothermic babies and those who have not yet fed. Prevention of hypoglycaemia is paramount and involves feeding preterm babies immediately after birth, providing nasogastric feeds with expressed breast milk or cup feeds if sucking is inadequate, using a neonatal nasogastric line immediately for babies who cannot take oral feeds, and maintaining thermal neutrality. When blood glucose falls in the mild hypoglycaemia range of 1.4 to 2.5 mmol/litre, feed immediately and repeat glucose measurement in 15 minutes; if glucose remains low, treat as severe hypoglycaemia, but if normal, check three-hourly until sustained normoglycaemia is confirmed over 24 hours. Severe hypoglycaemia (glucose below 1.4 mmol/litre) demands urgent intervention: administer a 10% dextrose bolus of 2 millilitres per kilogram via nasogastric line, continue dextrose infusion at the recommended rate for the baby's age and gestation, and recheck glucose in 15 minutes; if glucose remains low, increase dextrose concentration or seek specialist consultation. Hyperglycaemia occurs commonly in small, sick, and septic babies and is managed by checking hydration status and correcting any deficits, switching intravenous fluids to 5% dextrose if already on infusions, and considering insulin therapy if glucose remains above 14 mmol/litre, in which case specialist advice should be sought.

Thermoregulation is critical for preventing hypothermia-related complications and begins immediately at birth by thoroughly drying the newborn and providing skin-to-skin contact if possible; if skin-to-skin is not feasible, the baby should be covered and clothed with only necessary observation areas left uncovered. Early feeding supports thermoregulation by providing metabolic fuel for heat generation. The newborn unit environment must be maintained at 24 to 25 degrees Celsius, kept free of draughts, with the wall thermometer checked at least four times daily and readings recorded to ensure consistent environmental thermal support. Temperature monitoring of the baby itself varies by weight and clinical status: infants below 1.2 kilograms with serious infection require hourly temperature measurement, babies weighing 1.2 to 1.5 kilograms require three-hourly monitoring, and stable babies above 1.5 kilograms require six-hourly monitoring.

Infection prevention and control encompasses multiple interconnected practices that must be consistently applied throughout the neonatal unit. Hand hygiene is fundamental and must be performed on entering the unit and between patients using either soap and water or alcohol-based hand sanitizer. Comprehensive IPC measures also include isolation protocols and appropriate admission management for sick neonates or those suspected of infection, consistent application of clean nursing practices, documentation of staff compliance, and proper cleaning and decontamination of all equipment between patient uses. Antibiotics warrant consideration in several risk categories: babies weighing less than 1.5 kilograms, those born in potentially infected environments such as following prolonged rupture of membranes, infants with obvious clinical signs of infection, babies born at less than 37 weeks gestation without an apparent reason for preterm delivery, and those presenting with respiratory distress. First-line empiric therapy for suspected neonatal infection consists of penicillin or ampicillin, with gentamycin added as required based on clinical judgment for gram-negative coverage. For suspected meningitis, ampicillin combined with cefotaxime at high dose is employed for broader central nervous system penetration. Initial investigations on admission include full blood count, C-reactive protein, and blood culture; follow-up investigations should be obtained after at least 48 hours. Antibiotics may be ceased if CRP is normal and blood culture is negative in a clinically stable baby, allowing discontinuation of unnecessary prolonged therapy.
