## Lecture Notes

### Normal Respiratory Rate by Age

Respiratory rate varies significantly by age in paediatric patients. Newborns have the highest normal respiratory rates at 40 - 50 breaths per minute. In infants, this decreases to 25 - 40 breaths per minute. Between 1 and 5 years of age, the normal respiratory rate is 20 - 30 breaths per minute. Children aged 5 - 10 years should have a respiratory rate of 15 - 25 breaths per minute. By age 10 years and older, the respiratory rate approaches adult values of 15 - 20 breaths per minute. These cutoffs are essential for recognizing tachypnoea and assessing respiratory status in different age groups.

### Cough Classification and Associated Conditions

The character and quality of a cough provides valuable diagnostic information in paediatric respiratory conditions. A bark cough is characteristic of subglottic obstruction and often sounds like a seal barking. Brassy cough indicates tracheal compression and typically has a metallic, hollow quality. Paroxysmal cough with a characteristic whoop is pathognomonic for pertussis and reflects the intense inspiratory effort following prolonged expiratory bursts.

### Chest Wall Deformities

Abnormalities of chest wall structure may indicate underlying systemic disease or be functionally significant. Pectus excavatum presents as a caved-in or sunken appearance of the chest and is characteristically associated with Marfan syndrome. Pectus carinatum, conversely, shows protrusion of the sternum and ribs and is associated with Noonan syndrome. A barrel chest develops secondary to chronic hyperinflation, particularly in conditions causing air trapping.

### Recognition of Respiratory Distress

Respiratory distress manifests through several clinical signs that indicate increased work of breathing. Recession—inward movement of the chest wall during inspiration—is classified by anatomical location. Tracheal tug represents recession at the level of the trachea. Supraclavicular recession occurs above the clavicles. Intercostal recession occurs between the ribs, while subcostal recession occurs below the rib cage. Nasal flaring represents use of accessory muscles of inspiration, as does abdominal breathing and head bobbing, the latter secondary to sternocleidomastoid contractions. General signs of increased work of breathing include difficulty speaking or feeding, expiratory grunting, restlessness and agitation, drowsiness, and cyanosis.

### Acute Tonsillitis

Acute tonsillitis is one of the most common infections in paediatric practice. Typical symptoms include sore throat, fever, and pain on swallowing. The condition progresses through characteristic inflammatory stages. The parenchymatous stage presents with uniformly inflamed, hyperaemic tonsils. The follicular stage develops yellow exudate within the tonsillar crypts. In the membranous stage, this exudate coalesces to form a visible membrane covering the tonsillar surface.

First-line antibiotic treatment consists of penicillin or amoxycillin for 7 - 10 days. Without appropriate treatment or despite treatment, complications may arise. Chronic tonsillitis can develop with repeated infection. Peri-tonsillar abscess, also known as Quinsy, represents purulent collection adjacent to the tonsil. Para-pharyngeal abscess develops between the pharyngeal constrictor and superior pharyngeal constrictor muscles.

Recurrent tonsillitis is defined by specific frequency criteria: more than 7 episodes per year in a single year, or more than 5 episodes per year over 2 consecutive years, or more than 3 episodes per year over 3 consecutive years. Recognition of this pattern influences management decisions regarding potential tonsillectomy.

### Epidemiology and Risk Factors for Acute Respiratory Infection

Acute respiratory infection (ARI) remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality in paediatric populations. In South Africa, ARI accounts for 20% of deaths in children under 5 years of age. Multiple factors influence the incidence of ARI. Breastfeeding status provides significant protective benefit through passive immunization and improved nutrition. Nutritional status directly correlates with immune competence and infection risk. Socio-economic status, particularly poverty and overcrowding, increases transmission. HIV status profoundly affects susceptibility to severe infection. Immunization status determines protection against vaccine-preventable causes. Parental smoking increases respiratory epithelial irritation and infection risk. Parasitic infection may impair immune function. Structural abnormalities of the airways predispose to infection. Environmental factors including rainy and cold weather correlate with increased ARI incidence.

### Microbiology of Respiratory Tract Infections

Different sites of respiratory infection have characteristic microbial causes. Rhinitis, the commonest upper respiratory tract infection, is exclusively viral in aetiology. Otitis media is caused by bacteria, particularly Streptococcus pneumoniae and Haemophilus influenzae. Tonsillitis and pharyngitis are caused by Streptococcus species. Sinusitis may be viral or bacterial, with S. pneumoniae and H. influenzae being common bacterial pathogens. Epiglottitis is caused by Haemophilus influenzae and represents a medical emergency. Laryngotracheobronchitis (croup) is typically viral, caused by Parainfluenza types 1, 2, and 3, or measles. Tracheobronchitis is exclusively viral. Bronchiolitis is caused by respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), Adenovirus, or Parainfluenza. Bacterial pneumonia is caused by Streptococcus pneumoniae, Staphylococcus aureus, Haemophilus influenzae, or Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Viral pneumonia may be caused by RSV, Adenovirus, Influenza, or COVID-19.

### Pneumonia: Antibiotic Regimens

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) treatment depends critically on age and immune status. In children under 3 months of age or in immunosuppressed children, the regimen is ampicillin/gentamycin. For children older than 3 months with uncomplicated CAP, ampicillin/amoxil is used. When aspiration risk or Staphylococcus aureus coverage is needed, augmentin (amoxicillin-clavulanate) is the choice.

Hospital-acquired pneumonia requires more broad-spectrum coverage. The second-line antibiotic regimen is piperlillin-tazobactam/amikacin, reserved for cases with normal renal function and no CNS involvement concerns. For resistant organisms, carbapenem-class antibiotics represent third-line therapy.

### Stridor and Upper Airway Obstruction

Stridor is a high-pitched breathing sound indicating partial airway obstruction. Characteristically, stridor begins during inspiration. As obstruction worsens, it may become biphasic, occurring during both inspiration and expiration. Stridor indicates primarily extrathoracic airway obstruction. Associated hoarseness suggests vocal cord involvement. Stridor that changes with head and neck position indicates supra-glottic aetiology.

Upper airway obstruction has multiple aetiologies categorized by acuity and mechanism. Acute infectious causes include laryngotracheobronchitis (croup), epiglottitis, bacterial tracheitis, retropharyngeal abscess, and paratracheal gland enlargement. Acute mechanical causes include foreign body and tumour. Acute allergic causes include angioneurotic oedema. Acute traumatic causes include intubation injury and thermal injury.

Chronic congenital causes include laryngomalacia, tracheomalacia, laryngeal cysts or clefts, and vascular rings. Chronic infectious causes include laryngeal papillomatosis and hypertrophy of tonsils and adenoids. Chronic traumatic causes include subglottic stenosis.

### Laryngomalacia

Laryngomalacia is the most common congenital abnormality affecting the larynx. The condition is characterized by flaccidity of the supra-glottic region, sometimes described as a 'floppy voice box'. Affected infants develop inspiratory stridor from birth. Notably, the stridor may disappear when the infant is in a prone position and becomes absent or softer when the infant is asleep. The condition has a favourable natural history; mild stridor improves with age and completely disappears by 1 - 2 years of age without intervention in most cases.

Diagnosis is confirmed by visualization using a flexible pharyngo-laryngoscope. Management of mild cases is supportive and expectant. However, moderate to severe laryngomalacia complicated by upper respiratory tract infection requires admission, provision of humidified oxygen, and adrenaline nebulization to reduce subglottic oedema.

### Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

Croup is the most common cause of stridor in infancy and occurs most frequently between 6 months and 2 years of age. The typical causative viruses are Parainfluenza types 1, 2, and 3, and respiratory syncytial virus (RSV). In developing countries, measles and herpes simplex virus remain common causes of severe croup. The virus causes inflammation of the subglottic larynx and proximal trachea, resulting in the characteristic seal-like barky cough and stridor.

### Wheeze and Lower Airway Obstruction

Wheeze is a musical breathing sound indicating small or large airway disease or obstruction. It is classically associated with hyperinflation of the lungs and development of a barrel-shaped chest. The wheeze is typically more prominent during expiration, particularly early in the course of obstruction. Wheeze represents partial obstruction within the intrathoracic airways.

The causes of wheezing in children are diverse. Acute wheezing is commonly caused by reactive airway disease (asthma), bronchial oedema from bronchiolitis, foreign body or aspiration, eosinophilic pneumonia, cardiac failure, cystic fibrosis, and bronchiectasis. Chronic or recurrent wheeze may indicate hypersensitivity reactions such as aspergillosis or cystic fibrosis, dynamic airway collapse from bronchomalacia, airway compression from vascular rings or enlarged lymph nodes, aspiration from gastro-oesophageal reflux or tracheo-oesophageal fistula, failure to clear secretions as in cystic fibrosis, intrinsic airway lesions such as tumours or tracheal stenosis, congestive heart failure, or parasitic infection causing Loeffler syndrome.

### Asthma Diagnosis

Asthma should be strongly considered in ALL children presenting with recurrent airway obstruction that is relieved by bronchodilators. This diagnostic principle is critical because early recognition and treatment can prevent progression and reduce morbidity. Signs suggesting asthma diagnosis include a family history of asthma in parents and clinical signs of atopy such as eczema, allergic rhinitis, or food allergy.

### Acute Viral Bronchiolitis

Acute viral bronchiolitis is the most common respiratory tract infection in infants up to 2 years of age. The peak incidence occurs at 3 - 4 months of age. Certain populations are at increased risk for severe disease, including preterm infants, those with congenital heart disease, and those with chronic lung disease (particularly those with bronchopulmonary dysplasia). Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) is the most common causative agent, though Adenovirus and Parainfluenza also cause bronchiolitis.

The pathophysiology involves viral infection of the bronchiolar epithelium. This causes mucosal oedema and desquamation of the bronchiolar epithelium, leading to small airway obstruction. The obstruction causes increased airway resistance and air trapping, particularly during expiration. This leads to hyperinflation of the distal alveoli and the characteristic radiological and clinical findings of bronchiolitis. Most cases are self-limited, resolving over 7 - 10 days, though severe cases may require hospitalization for oxygen support and monitoring.
