# 2024 IMAM (Integrated Management of Acute Malnutrition)

## Lecture Notes

### Definition and Classification of Malnutrition

Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) is defined by any of the following: Weight-for-height/length <-3 SD, Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC) <11.5 cm (for children >6 months), or bilateral pitting oedema. SAM is distinguished from Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM), which is defined as Weight-for-height/length between -2 and >-3 SD, or MUAC between 11.6 and 12.5 cm.

### Danger Signs Requiring Immediate Intervention

SAM children presenting with any of the following danger signs require urgent intervention: hypoglycaemia, hypothermia, hypovolaemia, septic shock, severe dehydration (not in shock), severe anaemia, and eye signs of vitamin A deficiency.

### Stabilisation Phase: Managing Hypoglycaemia

Low blood glucose in SAM is defined as <3 mmol/l. For asymptomatic hypoglycaemia, immediate feeding with F75 is the first-line approach. Alternatively, give sugar solution orally at 10 ml/kg, or a 50 ml bolus of 10% dextrose. After treatment, check blood glucose after 30 minutes; if normal, continue feeding and monitor every 3 hours. If blood glucose remains low, continue to check every 30 minutes until it rises above 3 mmol/l.

For symptomatic hypoglycaemia, administer IV Dextrose 10% at 2 ml/kg over 2-3 minutes, then check blood glucose after 30 minutes. Once the acute episode is managed, continue feeding and maintain 3-hourly monitoring.

### Stabilisation Phase: Managing Hypothermia

Normal axillary temperature in children is 36.5–37.4°C. Any temperature <36.5°C indicates an urgent need to warm the child. Prevention of hypothermia is paramount and includes keeping the child covered, especially the head, at all times; avoiding drafts; keeping the child dry and changing wet nappies regularly; avoiding exposure during examination and bathing; maintaining the ambient environment at 25–30°C; ensuring regular, correct feeding; and checking axillary temperature every 3–4 hours.

Active rewarming measures are employed when hypothermia is present. These include skin-to-skin contact with the mother or caregiver, wrapping the child with warm blankets, placing a heater nearby, and using external warming devices such as heaters, hot-water bottles, or bear huggers. During rewarming, monitor body temperature every 30 minutes. Body temperature generally rises at a rate of 0.5–1°C per hour. Stop rewarming once body temperature has stabilised above 36.5°C to prevent overheating.

### Stabilisation Phase: Managing Dehydration

Dehydration is assessed in three grades. No visible dehydration presents with normal general appearance. Some dehydration is indicated by sunken eyes, slow skin pinch turgor (>2 seconds), the child drinks eagerly, and irritability or restlessness. Severe dehydration shows sunken eyes, very slow skin pinch (>2 seconds), and poor drinking.

For diarrhoea without dehydration, show the mother how to give ORS using a cup and spoon with frequent small sips. Give 10 ml/kg of ORS after each loose stool until diarrhoea resolves. Continue feeding with F75 and reassess for dehydration frequently.

For diarrhoea with some or severe dehydration, give ORS at 5 ml/kg every 15 minutes (equivalent to 20 ml/kg/hour) for 4 hours, using frequent small sips. Continue to feed the child throughout. After 4 hours, reassess the level of dehydration by evaluating general condition, capillary filling time, level of consciousness, skin turgor, sunken eyes, respiratory rate, abdomen, urine output, and the number and quality of stools.

For IV fluid replacement in dehydration, use Darrow's half-strength solution with dextrose 5%. The infusion rates are weight-dependent: for children weighing 2–10 kg, give 10 ml/kg/hour; for 11–20 kg, give 8 ml/kg/hour; for 21–50 kg, give 6 ml/kg/hour. These IV fluids are given in addition to normal oral feed volumes and times.

### Stabilisation Phase: Managing Shock

Compensated shock in SAM presents with delayed capillary filling time >3 seconds, increased pulse rate (>140–160 bpm), and cool peripheries. Late or preterminal shock is characterised by decreased level of consciousness (lethargic), decreased blood pressure, and decreased pulse volume.

Resuscitate shock with a fluid bolus using 0.9% Normal Saline or Ringers lactate. The volume is 10 ml/kg, given as a bolus over 20 minutes. Monitor closely for response and signs of fluid overload. If shock is resolved after the first bolus, proceed to manage dehydration. If there is no response, repeat the fluid bolus up to 3 times. After three 10 ml/kg boluses (total 30 ml/kg) with inadequate response, transfer the patient to a high-care bed for CVP monitoring and inotropic support.

Monitor for fluid overload during IV therapy, evidenced by further tachycardia, gallop rhythm, breathing that increases even faster, puffy eyelids, and enlargement of the liver.

### Stabilisation Phase: Managing Anaemia

Very Severe Anaemia is defined as Hb <4 g/dL. Severe Anaemia is defined as Hb 4–6 g/dL with respiratory distress.

Blood transfusion is indicated using packed cells at 5–10 ml/kg, infused slowly over 4 hours. Blood transfusions should only be given in the first 24 hours if a child has very severe anaemia or severe anaemia with respiratory distress.

To prevent fluid overload during transfusion, give furosemide 1 mg/kg IV at the start and at the end of the transfusion. Watch for signs of fluid overload throughout.

### Stabilisation Phase: Managing Electrolyte Abnormalities and Oedema

All SAM children have excess total body sodium, even though plasma sodium may be low. There is also deficiency of potassium and magnesium. These electrolyte imbalances can take up to 2 weeks to correct. Oedema in SAM is partly due to these electrolyte imbalances.

The critical principle in managing oedema in SAM is: do not treat oedema with a diuretic. Giving high sodium can kill. Therefore, electrolyte correction must occur gradually through appropriate nutrition and feeding, not through diuretic therapy.

### Stabilisation Phase: Antibiotic Therapy

All SAM children receive antibiotic therapy initiated on the first day of admission. Gentamycin IV is given at 6 mg/kg once daily for 7 days.

For children with no danger signs who are alert and feeding well, Amoxicillin is given orally at 30 mg/kg per dose, 8-hourly for 5 days.

For all other children (those with danger signs or not meeting low-risk criteria), Ampicillin is given IV or IM at 50 mg/kg, 6-hourly for 7 days. Avoid IV infusions if possible; use a heparin lock to prevent fluid overload. The infusion hang time is 1 hour.

For severely ill children or those failing to improve after 48 hours on initial antibiotic therapy, use a third-generation cephalosporin such as Ceftriaxone IV or IM at 50 mg/kg per dose once daily. If meningitis is suspected, increase the dose to 80–100 mg/kg per dose.

### Stabilisation and Rehabilitation Phases: Managing Worm Infestation

Mebendazole is given during the rehabilitation phase. For children aged 1–2 years, the dose is 100 mg orally, 12-hourly for 3 days. For children older than 2 years, a single dose of 500 mg is given immediately.

### Stabilisation and Rehabilitation Phases: Micronutrient Supplementation

Folic acid: Give a stat dose of 5 mg on day 1. Do not give daily supplementation, as folic acid is already present in F75, F100, and RUTF. If the child is not receiving F75 or F100, prescribe folic acid at 2.5 mg daily.

Iron supplementation: Give only after the stabilisation phase is completed, typically during the rehabilitation phase. Iron is started once the child is gaining weight and oedema has resolved, usually after 7 days. The dosing during rehabilitation is 2 mg/kg of elemental iron per dose, 8-hourly with meals when the child is on F100. Stop iron supplementation when the child is taking the full prescribed amount of RUTF.

Vitamin A: Give a single age-specific high-dose vitamin A capsule only if the child has severe measles and/or clinical signs of vitamin A deficiency. For children without these complications who are receiving F75, F100, or RUTF, high-dose vitamin A supplementation is not required, as these therapeutic feeds contain adequate amounts.

Zinc: Children with SAM receiving Ready-To-Use therapeutic feeds (F75, F100, or RUTF) should not be given additional zinc supplements, even if they have diarrhoea, because these therapeutic feeds contain the recommended amounts of zinc for managing diarrhoea.

### Stabilisation Phase: Feeding Principles and Protocols

F75 (Starter Formula) is the initial feed during stabilisation. For children without oedema, the volume is 130 ml/kg/day, distributed over 8 feeds. For children with severe oedema, reduce the volume to 100 ml/kg/day over 8 feeds. F75 provides 100 kcal/kg/day with protein at 0.9 g/kg/day and is low-lactose.

Lactose intolerance should be diagnosed only if copious watery diarrhoea occurs promptly after milk-based feeds are begun, the diarrhoea clearly improves when milk intake is reduced or stopped, and it recurs when milk is given again. Other diagnostic features include acidic faeces (pH <5.0) and the presence of increased levels of reducing substances in the faeces. For lactose intolerance, substitute an extensively hydrolysed feed, and reintroduce milk feeds gradually during the rehabilitation phase.

During F75 feeding, monitor and record: the amounts of feed offered and any leftover, vomiting (and reoffering of vomited amounts), the frequency of watery stools, and daily body weight. During the stabilisation phase, diarrhoea should gradually diminish. In oedematous children, expect gradual weight loss as fluid is mobilised.

For exclusively breastfed infants <6 months admitted as SAM, there is likely a problem with breastfeeding (low milk supply, incorrect suck, or poor latching) or the breastmilk is insufficient to meet increased nutritional requirements. For these infants, add F75 in addition to breastmilk, regardless of HIV status. Calculate the F75 volume according to stabilisation guidelines and allow the baby to breastfeed in-between F75 feeds.

Absolute contraindications to feeding exist in two situations: while shock is being corrected, and in cases of surgical abdominal emergency. In all other situations, feeding should be initiated as early as possible.

Feeding principles protect vulnerable organs. For the heart, avoid excess fluid and give the correct calculated volume of feed. For the kidneys, avoid excess fluid and excess sodium; give the correct volume and use a low-sodium feed. For the liver, avoid excess protein and provide adequate glucose; give a low-protein feed that is sugary and offered frequently. For the gut, avoid large meals; give small amounts and feed frequently.

### Rehabilitation Phase: Transitioning to Higher-Calorie Feeds

For the first 1–2 days of the rehabilitation phase, replace the stabilising feed (F75) with the rehabilitation feed (F100 or DF100) at the same volume. The transition phase should be gradual over 2 days to avoid the risk of heart failure.

Ready-To-Use Therapeutic Food (RUTF) is an energy-dense, vitamin and mineral-enriched therapeutic food that is equivalent to F100. RUTF is a groundnut paste composed of vegetable fat, peanut butter, skimmed milk powder, lactoserum, maltodextrin, sugar, and a mineral and vitamin complex. Start RUTF once the child is tolerating oral feeds well during the rehabilitation phase. Increase the volume by 20 ml per day until some feed remains unfinished or the volume reaches 220 ml/kg.

During rehabilitation, the target for weight gain is moderate, defined as >1–2 g/kg/day, which indicates good positive weight gain.

### Discharge Criteria

For children aged 0–6 months, discharge criteria are: established breastfeeding, bilateral pitting oedema resolved, child clinically well and alert, medical complications resolved, and persistent and good weight gain of >5 g/kg/day over 5 days.

For children aged 6–59 months, discharge criteria are: appetite returned and tolerating RUTF, child clinically well and alert, bilateral pitting oedema resolved, medical complications resolved, and persistent and good weight gain of >10 g/kg/day over 5 days.

Before discharge, investigate for tuberculosis by conducting a tuberculin skin test (TST) and chest X-ray, asking about TB contacts and symptoms. If the TST is negative, repeat it just before discharge and read it within 48 hours; record findings. Ensure HIV counselling and testing of the mother or caregiver was undertaken and record findings. Ensure information on family background and socio-economic status was obtained and reviewed by a social worker.

### Follow-up After Discharge

Follow up at 2 weeks or 1 month after discharge at the Place Of Primary care Discharge (POPD). Continue dietetics review until the child is growing well. Maintain a malnutrition register and nutrition care plans with contact details and addresses of patients. Follow up with defaulting patients to prevent readmissions.

### Special Considerations

For TB management in SAM, actively investigate for tuberculosis as soon as possible, recognising that TB is difficult to diagnose and confirm. Ask about TB contacts and symptoms. Perform a Tuberculin Skin Test (TST) and chest X-ray. If the TST is negative, repeat it just before discharge. If TB is clinically likely, presumptive TB treatment is reasonable, but once begun should be completed.

For HIV management in SAM, counsel the mother or caregiver and undertake HIV testing. Once the child enters the rehabilitation phase, commence antiretroviral therapy (ART) according to provincial guidelines. HIV-infected children with SAM should be closely monitored in the first 6–8 weeks following initiation of ART.

Sensory stimulation and development promotion during rehabilitation phase includes fostering cognitive skills, language skills, motor skills, exploratory skills, and social skills. All SAM patients must be referred to occupational therapy and speech therapy in the rehabilitation phase. All SAM patients must be referred to a social worker.

Special cases requiring continued inpatient care after completion of the rehabilitation phase include children remaining due to another medical condition, those unable to eat RUTF and ward diet, and those with significant social challenges.
