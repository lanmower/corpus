# ASTHMA 2021 CONSENSUS GUIDELINE

## Lecture Notes

### Diagnosis

Asthma in children is fundamentally defined as variable expiratory airflow limitation with chronic airway inflammation. It remains largely a clinical diagnosis, supported by lung function testing in school-aged children. The diagnostic approach incorporates clinical features, bronchodilator response, and patterns of symptom occurrence.

**Clinical Features:** Children with asthma present with episodic wheeze, shortness of breath, difficult or laboured breathing, chest tightness, and reduced activity, with or without cough. These symptoms are often the primary indicators prompting investigation.

**Bronchodilator Response:** Symptoms that improve after correct use of a rapid-acting inhaled bronchodilator strongly support an asthma diagnosis, indicating variable airway obstruction responsive to beta-2 agonists.

**Wheezing Patterns and Frequency:** Wheezing that occurs frequently (more than three episodes per year) and lasts longer (more than ten days at a time) may indicate asthma. More importantly, wheeze that does not occur only during airway infections, but also in response to other triggers between infections, provides strong diagnostic support. Events that persist after age three years, with night-time worsening and association with exercise or environmental change (such as cold air exposure), further substantiate an asthma diagnosis.

**Diagnostic Approach in Children Under Six Years:** The diagnostic process for children younger than six years follows four structured steps. Step 1 involves detailed history taking, which remains the most important component. Step 2 requires excluding alternative reasons for wheezing episodes. Step 3 involves assessing airway inflammation through a therapeutic trial with inhaled corticosteroids (ICS). Step 4 seeks objective evidence of variable expiratory airflow limitation through appropriate testing.

**ICS Therapeutic Trial:** A pragmatic therapeutic trial with correctly administered low-dose inhaled corticosteroid will confirm the presence of corticosteroid-responsive airway inflammation, supporting an asthma diagnosis. Initial treatment response must be evaluated at 6-8 weeks following initiation. Additionally, presence of allergen-specific IgE (determined through skin-prick testing or ImmunoCAP) may support an asthma diagnosis by indicating atopic sensitisation.

### Differential Diagnosis

When a child presents with wheeze, multiple alternative diagnoses must be considered before concluding asthma. The differential encompasses infective, structural, and functional causes.

**Infective Causes:** Wheeze may be secondary to bronchiolitis, atypical infection, bacterial airway infection, laryngotracheobronchitis, or protracted bacterial bronchitis. These conditions often feature acute onset related to viral or bacterial respiratory infection.

**Structural Causes:** Anatomical abnormalities can produce wheeze including tracheal and bronchomalacia, tracheal webs, tracheal or bronchial stenosis, lymphadenopathy, tumours, vascular compression (double aortic arch, innominate artery compression, left pulmonary artery sling, patent ductus arteriosus ligament, or cardiac chamber and pulmonary artery enlargement), laryngeal clefts, retained foreign body, and H-type tracheo-oesophageal fistula. These typically feature fixed obstruction and asymmetric findings.

**Functional Causes:** A range of functional disorders can mimic asthma including wheezy phenotypes, primary ciliary dyskinesia, cystic fibrosis, gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD), perceived tight chest, pulmonary oedema or cardiac disease, interstitial lung disease, bronchiolitis obliterans, bronchopulmonary dysplasia, bronchiectasis, and immunodeficiency.

**Red Flag Findings:** Clinical findings suggesting an alternative diagnosis rather than asthma include digital clubbing, growth faltering, and asymmetric wheeze. These warrant further investigation before attributing symptoms to asthma alone.

### Diagnostic Testing

Objective evidence of variable expiratory airflow limitation supports asthma diagnosis. Multiple testing modalities are available depending on child age and cooperation.

**Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF) Assessment:** PEF variability with an average daily diurnal variability greater than 13%, when documented twice daily for two weeks, indicates variable expiratory airflow limitation. Excessive variation of PEF greater than 15% between outpatient visits using the same equipment, with or without airway infections, similarly indicates variable airflow limitation.

**Exercise Challenge Testing:** A positive exercise challenge test is defined as a decrease in PEF greater than 15% after reaching target heart rate (calculated as 0.8 × 220 minus age in years). This indicates variable airflow limitation in response to exercise-induced obstruction.

**Spirometry Assessment:** Normal FEV1/FVC ratio is greater than 0.9. When the FEV1/FVC ratio is decreased due to reduced FEV1, variable expiratory airflow limitation is indicated.

**Bronchodilator Reversibility:** A positive bronchodilator reversibility test is defined by an increase in FEV1 greater than 12% following rapid-acting beta-2 agonist administration. This demonstrates reversible airflow obstruction characteristic of asthma.

**Exercise Challenge with Spirometry:** A positive exercise challenge test using spirometry is defined as a decrease in FEV1 greater than 12% after reaching target heart rate (0.8 × 220 minus age in years).

**FEV1 Variation:** Excessive variation of FEV1 greater than 12% between outpatient visits using the same equipment, with or without airway infections, indicates variable airflow limitation.

### Severity Classification

Asthma severity is classified into four categories based on symptoms, night-time disturbance, peak expiratory flow, and PEF variability.

**Mild Intermittent Asthma:** Characterized by symptoms occurring twice or fewer per week, night-time symptoms two or fewer per month, PEF at least 80% of predicted, and PEF variability less than 20%.

**Mild Persistent Asthma:** Defined by symptoms occurring more than twice per week, night-time symptoms more than twice per month, PEF at least 80% of predicted, and PEF variability between 20-30%.

**Moderate Persistent Asthma:** Features daily symptoms, night-time symptoms more than once per week, PEF between 60-80% of predicted, and PEF variability greater than 30%.

**Severe Persistent Asthma:** Characterized by continual symptoms throughout the day, frequent night-time symptoms (often seven nights per week), PEF at or below 60% of predicted, and PEF variability greater than 30%.

### Treatment Framework

Asthma management follows a stepped approach, with treatment intensity adjusted based on disease severity and control. Three age-based frameworks address the developmental and pharmacological differences across the paediatric population.

**Children Under Five Years**

*Intermittent Treatment:* For children with infrequent symptoms, management consists of a short-acting beta-2 agonist (SABA) as needed and a short course of ICS (7-10 days) at the start of upper respiratory tract infection.

*Step 1 Persistent Asthma:* Daily low-dose ICS with SABA as needed for symptom relief.

*Step 2 Persistent Asthma:* Medium-dose ICS is recommended. Alternatively, in children older than four years, daily low-dose ICS/LABA combination with SABA as needed is appropriate.

*Step 3 Persistent Asthma:* Either daily medium-dose ICS/LABA combination with SABA as needed, or medium-dose ICS combined with leukotriene-receptor antagonist (LTRA).

*Step 4 Persistent Asthma:* Referral to a specialist is recommended for further assessment and management of children under six years with inadequate control on Step 3 therapy.

**Children Six to Eleven Years**

*Step 1 Persistent Asthma:* Multiple options are available: daily low-dose ICS with SABA as needed, LTRA alone, or low-dose ICS administered only when SABA is taken.

*Step 2:* Daily and as-needed low-dose ICS/LABA (formoterol) combination, or low-dose ICS/LTRA combination.

*Step 3:* Either daily and as-needed medium-dose ICS/LABA (formoterol) combination, high-dose ICS/LABA combination, or the addition of LTRA or tiotropium to existing therapy.

*Step 4:* Referral for expert assessment for anti-IgE therapy or other biologic agents, or daily and as-needed high-dose ICS/LABA combination plus LTRA.

**Children Twelve Years and Older**

The dosing regimens for adolescents align with adult recommendations. ICS doses follow age-appropriate guidelines adjusted to disease severity, with similar stepwise progression through controller combinations and biologic options for severe disease.

**2021 Guideline Update - ICS-Formoterol Approach:** In children aged six years and older with moderate to severe persistent asthma, ICS-formoterol in a single inhaler is recommended as both daily controller and reliever therapy. This represents an advancement compared with either higher-dose ICS as daily controller therapy plus SABA for quick-relief, or the same-dose ICS-LABA as daily controller therapy plus SABA for quick-relief.

**2021 Guideline Update - Respiratory Tract Infection-Triggered ICS:** In children under six years with recurrent wheezing triggered primarily by respiratory tract infections and minimal wheeze between infections, a short course (7-10 days) of daily ICS at the onset of a respiratory tract infection with as-needed SABA is recommended. In children aged six to eleven years with similar presentation, as-needed SABA and a short ICS course (7-10 days) at the start of an upper-respiratory tract infection, or daily low-dose ICS, are recommended options.

### ICS Dosing Tables

Inhaled corticosteroid dosing varies by drug, formulation, age group, and severity classification. All doses are expressed as total daily inhaled dose in micrograms (µg).

**Children Under Five Years**

- **Beclomethasone dipropionate (HFA):** 100 µg total daily
- **Budesonide (pMDI with spacer):** 200 µg total daily
- **Budesonide (nebulised):** 500 µg total daily
- **Fluticasone propionate (HFA):** 100 µg total daily

*Note on Potency:* Fluticasone propionate may be used at half the dose of budesonide equivalent due to its higher potency.

**Children Six to Eleven Years**

- **Beclomethasone dipropionate HFA:** Low 100-200 µg, Medium 200-400 µg, High >400 µg
- **Budesonide:** Low 100-200 µg, Medium 200-400 µg, High >400 µg
- **Ciclesonide:** Low 80 µg, Medium 80-160 µg, High >160 µg (Note: Approved for ≥12 years; used off-label in younger children)
- **Fluticasone propionate:** Low 100-200 µg, Medium 200-500 µg, High >500 µg
- **Mometasone furoate:** Low 110-220 µg, Medium 220-440 µg, High >440 µg
- **Triamcinolone acetonide:** Low 400-800 µg, Medium 800-1200 µg, High >1200 µg

**Adolescents Twelve Years and Older**

- **Beclomethasone dipropionate HFA:** Low 100-200 µg, Medium >200-400 µg, High >400 µg
- **Budesonide:** Low 200-400 µg, Medium >400-800 µg, High >800 µg
- **Ciclesonide:** Low 80-160 µg, Medium >160-320 µg, High >320 µg (Registered for ≥12 years)
- **Fluticasone propionate:** Low 100-250 µg, Medium >250-500 µg, High >500 µg
- **Mometasone furoate:** Low 110-220 µg, Medium >220-440 µg, High >440 µg

**Dosing Frequency and Formulation Considerations:** Budesonide is approved for once-daily dosing in patients with mild illness. When transitioning between CFC (chlorofluorocarbon) and HFA (hydrofluoroalkane) preparations, medication package inserts should be carefully reviewed for the equivalent correct dosage, as the conversion factor may not be 1:1.

**Ongoing ICS Benefit Review:** An ongoing benefit of ICS treatment should be reviewed every three months, with ICS maintained at the lowest possible dose necessary for symptom control.

### Control Assessment

Asthma control is the extent to which the effects of asthma can be reduced or eliminated by treatment. Assessment includes evaluation of symptom control (frequency of symptoms, reliever medication use, and activity limitation over the past week and month) and future risk assessment (likelihood of exacerbations, medication side-effects, and lung function loss).

**Well Controlled Asthma:** Characterised by absence of all four of the following in the past week: daytime asthma symptoms more than twice per week, night-time awakening due to asthma, reliever (SABA) use more than twice per week, and limitation of activity.

**Partly Controlled Asthma:** Defined by the presence of one to two of these features in the past week.

**Uncontrolled Asthma:** Characterised by three to four of these features present in the past week.

**Assessment Interval:** After initiation of treatment, asthma control must be assessed at every follow-up visit, no less frequently than every six months, with treatment adjusted accordingly.

**Treatment Review Timing:** Asthma control should be reviewed 4-6 weeks after starting treatment, with assessment of control and stepping up or stepping down of treatment depending on the level of control achieved.

### Devices

The selection of an appropriate inhalation device is a key principle of asthma treatment, with device choice suited to the patient's age and capability. Device technique significantly impacts treatment efficacy.

**Metered-Dose Inhaler with Spacer (MDI+Spacer):** The MDI with spacer is the most commonly used asthma delivery device. Spacers are simple holding chambers with or without a valve mechanism. Medication is suspended within the spacer and then breathed in, significantly enhancing lung deposition of the drug. A spacer is recommended for all children and adults with difficult-to-control asthma. A very effective spacer device can be constructed at low cost from a 500 mL plastic cold-drink bottle, with a hole large enough to accommodate the MDI mouthpiece cut in the bottom end to form a simple non-valved spacer.

**Dry-Powder Inhalers (DPI):** DPIs are easy to use but are only suitable for older children and adults because they require a specific inspiratory flow for actuation. Measurements of peak inspiratory flow can be performed with appropriate devices to assess suitability of DPI use in individual patients.

**Nebulisers:** Home nebulisers are not recommended for routine asthma management.

### Monitoring and Follow-Up

Systematic monitoring ensures treatment efficacy and allows timely adjustment of therapy.

**ICS Therapeutic Trial Evaluation:** When an ICS therapeutic trial is initiated to assess for corticosteroid-responsive airway inflammation, initial treatment response must be evaluated at 6-8 weeks following start.

**Ongoing Monitoring:** An ongoing benefit of ICS treatment should be reviewed every three months, with ICS maintained at the lowest possible dose necessary for symptom control. After initiation of treatment, asthma control must be assessed at every follow-up visit, no less frequently than every six months.

**Treatment Adjustment:** Review asthma control 4-6 weeks after starting treatment. Assess control and step up or step down treatment depending on the level of control achieved. This systematic approach allows optimisation of therapy while avoiding overtreatment.

### Education and Prevention

Patient and family education is integral to successful asthma management. Prevention strategies focus on both infection control and environmental trigger management.

**Viral Infection Prevention:** Asthmatic children should keep a distance from others (social distancing, approximately 1 metre) to reduce transmission of respiratory infections. Hand washing should be performed frequently for 20-30 seconds, always after coughing or sneezing.

**Mask-Wearing:** Mask-wearing to protect people from respiratory infection in public spaces is recommended by the World Health Organization (WHO) and is endorsed for asthma patients. There is no evidence to suggest that mask-wearing is deleterious to people with asthma.

**Environmental Trigger Control:** Asthmatic children should avoid environmental tobacco smoke and other indoor air pollutants. Exposure should be controlled for indoor allergens including house-dust mites, pets, cockroach, and mould. Identification and avoidance of environmental triggers is essential for optimal asthma control.

### Adherence and Comorbidities

Poor medication adherence and untreated comorbidities are significant barriers to asthma control.

**ICS Adherence in Children:** Treatment adherence to asthma therapy in the paediatric population is poor, with studies reporting that only one-third of children use ICS therapy appropriately. Understanding and addressing adherence barriers is critical.

**Barriers to Adherence:** Barriers to adherence may be intentional, driven by illness perceptions or medication beliefs, leading to patients and caregivers deliberately choosing not to follow treatment recommendations. Alternatively, barriers may be unintentional, related to family routines and socioeconomic factors affecting ability to access or administer therapy consistently.

**Associated Comorbidities:** Identification and treatment of associated comorbidities may significantly improve asthma control. Important comorbidities include rhinitis or sinusitis, obesity, gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD), and allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA). Screening and management of these conditions should be part of comprehensive asthma care.

### Management Cycle

Effective asthma management follows a structured cycle incorporating assessment, adjustment, and review phases.

**Assess Phase:** Initial and ongoing assessment encompasses diagnosis confirmation, evaluation of current symptom control, risk factor assessment (including environmental triggers and comorbidities), medication technique observation, and adherence evaluation.

**Adjust Phase:** Based on assessment findings, adjustments may include modification of controller medication type or dose, introduction of non-pharmacological strategies (environmental control, education), and treatment of identified modifiable risk factors.

**Review Phase:** Following adjustment, review focuses on medication effects and side-effects, the child's response to treatment modifications, and measurement of symptom control and lung function.

This cyclical process ensures systematic optimisation of asthma management over time.

### Eight Long-Term Management Goals

Comprehensive asthma management should address eight key long-term goals: (1) achievement of good symptom control, (2) maintenance of normal activity levels, (3) minimisation of future asthma-related mortality risk, (4) reduction in frequency of exacerbations, (5) maintenance of lung function and normal lung development, (6) minimisation of side-effects of treatment, (7) provision of a written action plan, and (8) consideration of the patient's own goals with regard to treatment.

### Special Considerations

**Distinguishing ICS Response from Natural Resolution:** In children with preschool wheeze, symptoms that resolve during ICS therapy may be due to either the natural history of the wheezing disorder or the effect of treatment. To distinguish between these possibilities, treatment should be withdrawn after initial improvement. Treatment should be restarted only if symptoms recur, thus identifying those with true asthma requiring ongoing controller therapy.

**Allergen-Specific IgE Testing:** Presence of allergen-specific IgE, determined through skin-prick testing or ImmunoCAP, may support an asthma diagnosis by indicating atopic sensitisation and helping identify specific environmental triggers for individual patients.

**Comprehensive Treatment Principles:** One of the key principles of asthma treatment is to choose an appropriate device and delivery system suited to the patient's age and capability. This ensures optimal medication deposition and efficacy while maintaining adherence through age-appropriate selection.
