# Medical Professionalism

## Introduction: Defining Professionalism

Medical professionalism describes the skills, attitudes, values and behaviours common to those undertaking the practice of medicine. According to the World Medical Association (WMA), it encompasses the maintenance of competence for a unique body of knowledge and skill set, personal integrity, altruism, adherence to ethical codes of conduct, accountability, a dedication to self-regulation, and the exercise of discretionary judgment. This definition captures the essence of what it means to practise medicine at the highest standards.

The University of Ottawa offers a complementary perspective: professionalism embodies the relationship between medicine and society as it forms the basis of patient-physician trust. It attempts to make tangible certain attitudes, behaviours, and characteristics that are desirable among the medical profession. Central to this understanding is the concept of calling—a set of values, behaviours, and relationships that underpins the trust the public has in doctors. This calling is not merely an occupational choice but a commitment to principles that extend far beyond the technical practice of medicine.

### The Foundation of Trust

Understanding professionalism begins with recognizing that it is fundamentally about trust. Patients entrust doctors with their most intimate health information and their physical safety. The public grants the medical profession special privileges and autonomy based on the assumption that practitioners will act in their patients' best interests, maintain rigorous standards, and remain accountable to society. This trust is earned through demonstrated commitment to ethical practice, competence, and continuous improvement.

## The Social Contract and Professional Role

Professionalism is the moral understanding among medical practitioners that gives reality to the social contract between medicine and society. This contract grants the medical profession a monopoly over the use of its knowledge base, the right to considerable autonomy in practice and the privilege of self-regulation. In exchange, society places particular expectations on practitioners: they must act with integrity, demonstrate competence, prioritize patient welfare, and remain accountable to those they serve.

The CanMEDS framework provides a structured understanding of how physicians fulfill this social contract. As Professionals, physicians are committed to the health and well-being of individual patients and society through ethical practice, high personal standards of behaviour, commitment to the profession, profession-led regulation, and maintenance of personal health. The Professional Role is grounded in a professional identity and is guided by codes of ethics and a commitment to clinical competence, ongoing professional development, integrity, honesty, altruism, respect for diversity, the adoption of appropriate attitudes and behaviours, and promotion of the public good.

The commitments and elements of the Professional role form the basis of the social contract between a physician and society. In return, society grants physicians the privilege of profession-led regulation, with the understanding that they are accountable to those served, to society, and to the profession itself. This accountability is not a burden but a cornerstone of medical practice—it ensures that the profession maintains its standing and trustworthiness across generations.

## HPCSA Section 27A: Core Requirements for Professional Practice

The Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA) Section 27A outlines eight fundamental requirements that all health practitioners must meet. These requirements provide clear, enforceable standards that embody the broader principles of professionalism.

### Requirement (a): Acting in the Best Interests of Patients

A practitioner shall at all times act in the best interests of his or her patients. This foundational requirement places patient welfare at the center of every clinical decision. It requires practitioners to advocate for their patients, prioritize their needs, and ensure that personal, financial, or institutional considerations do not compromise patient care. In paediatrics, this imperative is particularly pronounced, as children are vulnerable patients who depend entirely on their healthcare providers to champion their welfare.

### Requirement (b): Respecting Confidentiality, Privacy, Choices, and Dignity

A practitioner shall respect patient confidentiality, privacy, choices and dignity. Confidentiality is a cornerstone of the therapeutic relationship. Patients must feel secure that the sensitive information they share with their practitioners will be protected. Beyond confidentiality, practitioners must respect patients' autonomy, honour their choices regarding treatment, and treat them with the dignity that every person deserves. This respect extends to all aspects of practice, including how information is stored, communicated, and used.

### Requirement (c): Maintaining High Standards of Personal Conduct and Integrity

A practitioner shall maintain the highest standards of personal conduct and integrity. Professional integrity means being truthful, keeping promises, avoiding conflicts of interest, and conducting oneself honourably in all circumstances. Integrity is not situational—it applies whether a practitioner is in the clinic, hospital, community, or beyond. It encompasses financial dealings, interactions with colleagues, and personal conduct that affects professional standing.

### Requirement (d): Providing Informed Consent and Information

A practitioner shall provide adequate information about the patient's diagnosis, treatment options and alternatives, costs associated with each such alternative and any other pertinent information to enable the patient to exercise a choice in terms of treatment and informed decision-making pertaining to his or her health and that of others. Informed consent is a fundamental right and a legal requirement. Practitioners must communicate in language that patients understand, ensure comprehension, and allow time for questions. In paediatrics, this extends to families and guardians who make decisions on behalf of children, with age-appropriate involvement of the child according to their capacity.

### Requirement (e): Maintaining Professional Knowledge and Skills

A practitioner shall keep his or her professional knowledge and skills up to date. Medical science advances continuously, and practitioners have an ethical obligation to remain current. This requirement demands engagement in continuing professional development, evidence-based medicine, and reflection on practice. Failure to maintain competence puts patients at risk and violates the trust society has placed in the profession.

### Requirement (f): Maintaining Effective Communication

A practitioner shall maintain proper and effective communication with his or her patients and other professionals. Clear communication prevents errors, builds trust, and ensures collaborative care. Whether communicating diagnoses, explaining treatment plans, discussing risks, or liaising with colleagues, practitioners must be clear, compassionate, and culturally sensitive. In paediatrics, communication skills are essential when working with children and families from diverse backgrounds.

### Requirement (g): Obtaining Informed Consent

Except in an emergency, a practitioner shall obtain informed consent from a patient or, in the event that the patient is unable to provide consent for treatment, from his or her next of kin. This requirement protects patient autonomy and prevents non-consensual treatment. In emergencies, implied consent applies, but practitioners must explain their actions afterward. For children, guardians provide consent, though older children may provide assent when developmentally appropriate.

### Requirement (h): Maintaining Accurate Patient Records

A practitioner shall keep accurate patient records. Comprehensive, accurate documentation is essential for continuity of care, legal protection, and quality improvement. Records should be legible, timely, and contain sufficient detail to allow another practitioner to understand the patient's history, findings, and management. Good record-keeping reflects accountability and professional standards.

## The CanMEDS Framework: Competence and Commitment

Professional competence extends beyond technical skill. According to CanMEDS, professional competence is the habitual and judicious use of communication, knowledge, technical skills, clinical reasoning, emotions, values, and reflection in daily practice for the benefit of the individual and community being served. This definition integrates the cognitive, technical, emotional, and ethical dimensions of medical practice.

The CanMEDS Professional role is structured around four major commitments: to patients, to society, to the profession, and to self. Each commitment encompasses specific competencies and responsibilities that physicians must develop and maintain throughout their careers.

### Commitment to Patients

The CanMEDS Commitment to Patients encompasses altruism, bioethical principles and theories, commitment to excellence in clinical practice and mastery of the discipline, compassion and caring, confidentiality and its limits, integrity and honesty, moral and ethical behaviour, professional boundaries, and respect for diversity. This commitment reflects the primacy of the patient-physician relationship. Altruism is central—practitioners must consistently place patient welfare ahead of personal gain. Bioethical knowledge provides the framework for navigating complex clinical dilemmas, while compassion and caring humanize the practice of medicine.

Professional boundaries are particularly important. Physicians must maintain appropriate relationships with patients, avoiding dual relationships and conflicts of interest that could compromise objectivity or exploit patients' vulnerability. Understanding confidentiality and its limits—including mandatory reporting, child protection, and public health concerns—is essential for ethical practice.

### Commitment to Society

The CanMEDS Commitment to Society includes commitment to the promotion of the public good in health care, social accountability, and understanding the social contract in health care. Physicians are not isolated practitioners but members of a healthcare system serving entire populations. This commitment requires advocating for health equity, understanding social determinants of health, and contributing to population health initiatives. In paediatrics, practitioners must consider the broader social context—poverty, education, family stability—that affects child health and development.

### Commitment to Profession

The CanMEDS Commitment to Profession encompasses accountability to professional regulatory authorities, codes of ethics, commitment to patient safety and quality improvement, commitment to professional standards, awareness of conflicts of interest (personal, financial, administrative), knowledge of medico-legal frameworks governing practice, and responsibility to the profession including obligations of peer assessment, mentorship, collegiality, and support.

This commitment recognizes that physicians are stewards of the profession. They must support colleagues, mentor junior practitioners, engage in honest peer feedback, and contribute to maintaining professional standards. Understanding medico-legal frameworks and regulatory requirements protects both patients and practitioners. Conflict of interest management—whether personal, financial, or administrative—ensures that decisions are made in patients' best interests rather than practitioners' interests.

### Commitment to Self

The CanMEDS Commitment to Self includes applied capacity for self-regulation—assessing and monitoring of one's thoughts, behaviours, emotions and attention for optimal performance and well-being—commitment to disclosure of error and/or adverse events and their impact, mindful and reflective approach to practice, professional identity and career development and transitions, resilience for sustainable practice, and responsibility to self including personal care in order to serve others.

Professional sustainability requires self-care. Practitioners cannot serve others effectively if they are burnt out, physically unwell, or emotionally compromised. Self-regulation involves honest self-assessment, seeking feedback, recognizing limitations, and pursuing support when needed. The capacity to disclose errors and learn from them is essential for patient safety and professional growth. Building professional identity and resilience helps practitioners navigate the challenges inherent in medical practice over a lifetime.

## Professional Attributes and Identity

Professional identity is more than a credential or job title—it is a deep integration of values, knowledge, skills, and behaviours into one's sense of self. Professionals develop this identity through education, mentorship, experience, and reflection. In medical education, students and trainees gradually internalize the norms, values, and ethical commitments of the profession, developing what might be called a "healer-professional model."

Professionalism requires the integration of clinical competence with ethical commitment, emotional intelligence, and social awareness. It is about becoming a certain kind of person—one who can be trusted with vulnerable patients, who prioritizes their welfare, who maintains integrity under pressure, and who contributes to the profession's reputation and standing. This transformation is neither quick nor automatic; it requires conscious effort, regular reflection, and ongoing engagement with professional values.

### Professional Boundaries

Professional boundaries establish appropriate limits on practitioner-patient relationships. These boundaries protect both patients and practitioners. They prevent exploitation, maintain therapeutic effectiveness, and ensure that the relationship remains focused on the patient's needs. Inappropriate relationships—whether romantic, financial, or otherwise—violate professional boundaries and undermine trust.

## Unprofessional Behaviour: Understanding Violations

Understanding professionalism also requires recognizing what it is not. Unprofessional behaviour encompasses a range of actions that violate the trust placed in practitioners and the standards of the profession.

### Unprofessional Behaviour Related to Patient Care

Unprofessional conduct in patient care includes criminal behaviour or conviction, behaviour that suggests a disregard for wellbeing of patients or members of the public and/or their dignity and rights, failing to honour clinical commitments, treating patients when not fit to do so, and agreeing to undertake a procedure for which the person lacks the necessary training, expertise or competence. These violations directly harm patients and breach the fundamental duty of beneficence. Treating patients while impaired—whether by illness, fatigue, substance use, or impaired judgment—puts patients at risk. Similarly, undertaking procedures beyond one's competence violates the duty to act in patients' best interests.

### Unprofessional Interpersonal Behaviour

Unprofessional interpersonal behaviour includes being rude, abusive or disrespectful, showing a disregard for the time and effort of those relying on them (such as consistently bad timekeeping), making gratuitous criticism of colleagues or others, and engaging in inappropriate relationships with patients or employees. These behaviours damage the professional environment, undermine teamwork, and erode trust. Disrespect toward colleagues, staff, or patients violates the principle of dignity and creates toxic workplaces that compromise patient safety.

### Unprofessional Behaviour Related to Standards and Conduct

Practitioners who accept unsatisfactory clinical standards, demonstrate an irresponsible or apathetic attitude, show laziness, sloppiness or lack of attention to detail, resist feedback, fail to maintain continuing professional development, or engage in dishonest business/financial dealings are failing in their professional obligations. These behaviours suggest that practitioners have lost sight of their commitment to excellence and accountability.

### Unprofessional Behaviour Undermining Public Trust

The most serious category involves actions that directly undermine public confidence in the profession. This includes putting one's own financial or personal interests above patient care, any abuse of the doctor-patient relationship, anything that undermines public confidence in the profession, and anything that undermines the reputation or standing of the profession. These violations strike at the heart of the social contract and erode the profession's legitimacy.

## Professional Practice Areas: Contemporary Considerations

Modern medical practice encompasses many areas where professionalism must be actively considered and applied.

### Digital Presence and Online Conduct

Social media presence and online persona, as well as photographs and online content, have become significant professional concerns. What practitioners share publicly affects their professional standing and the profession's reputation. Practitioners must consider how their online presence reflects on their professionalism and be mindful of confidentiality even in informal digital spaces.

### Clinical Education and Student Development

Student learning and clerking of patients requires particular professionalism. Students must be supervised adequately, their competence must be assessed honestly, and patients must be informed about and consent to student involvement. Professionalism in education means mentoring trainees, providing constructive feedback, and modeling professional behaviour.

### Pharmaceutical and Commercial Relationships

Interaction with pharmaceutical companies requires transparency and careful management of conflicts of interest. While physicians benefit from industry research and innovation, they must maintain independence in prescribing and clinical decisions. Financial relationships must be disclosed, and clinical judgment must never be compromised by industry interests.

### Evidence-Based Practice and Clinical Decision-Making

Evidence-based medicine reflects professionalism—using the best available evidence to guide practice, recognizing uncertainty, and remaining open to new evidence. This approach respects patients by providing scientifically justified care while acknowledging the complexity of individual clinical situations.

### Legal and Regulatory Compliance

Knowledge of rules, regulations and laws governing medical practice is essential. Practitioners must understand their legal obligations, scope of practice, and regulatory requirements. This knowledge protects both patients and practitioners.

### Business and Self-Interest Management

Awareness of business and self-interest considerations helps practitioners recognize potential conflicts. Whether establishing a private practice, negotiating contracts, or making clinical decisions with financial implications, practitioners must ensure that business considerations do not compromise patient care or professional integrity.

### Healthcare Economics

Understanding costs of medical care and the allocation of healthcare resources reflects mature professionalism. Practitioners should practice cost-effective medicine, be aware of expensive interventions, and consider resource allocation in clinical decisions—all while maintaining patient welfare as the primary concern.

### Conflict Resolution

Professional skill in conflict resolution—whether with colleagues, patients, or administrative bodies—prevents disputes from escalating and maintains professional relationships. This includes the capacity to acknowledge when one is wrong and to take responsibility for errors.

### Personal Life Boundaries

Finally, practitioners must establish boundaries between their personal lives and professional obligations. While maintaining personal health and relationships is essential, practitioners must be available and reliable in their professional commitments. The integration of personal and professional life is a lifelong challenge requiring ongoing reflection and adjustment.

## Conclusion: The Living Practice of Professionalism

Professionalism is not a state that is achieved and then maintained passively. Rather, it is a living practice—a continuous commitment to ethical behaviour, competence, accountability, and service. It is built on the foundation of trust between individual practitioners and their patients, and between the profession collectively and society.

For paediatricians and all physicians, professionalism means remembering that every patient encounter is an opportunity to affirm the values and commitments that define the profession. It means acting with integrity even when no one is watching, maintaining competence even when practice is busy, respecting patient autonomy even when time is short, and remaining accountable to those we serve.

The WMA definition, the HPCSA Section 27A requirements, the CanMEDS framework, and the concept of calling all point toward a unified vision: medicine is a profession of service, characterized by specialized knowledge, ethical commitment, self-regulation, and accountability. Practitioners who embrace this vision contribute not only to individual patient wellbeing but to the profession's standing and the public's trust.

As the field of paediatrics evolves and new challenges emerge, the fundamental principles of professionalism remain constant. They provide a moral compass that guides decision-making, a framework for reflecting on practice, and a commitment to excellence that defines what it means to be a physician.
