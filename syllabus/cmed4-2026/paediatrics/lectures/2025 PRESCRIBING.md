## Lecture Notes

### Paediatric Physiology and Dosing Principles

Children are not simply dosed as small adults in paediatric prescribing due to fundamental physiological differences. Children differ due to immaturity of liver and kidney, which results in low clearance and less efficient drug metabolism. Additionally, children have a higher percentage body water, which creates a larger apparent volume of distribution for water-soluble drugs. These key differences necessitate special consideration when determining appropriate drug dosages for the paediatric population.

Two methods are used for dose calculation in paediatrics: Weight and Body Surface Area. Each method has distinct advantages depending on the drug being administered and the clinical context.

### Routes of Drug Administration

Paediatric prescribing must account for multiple routes of drug administration available to clinicians: Oral, Intravenous, Parenteral, Rectal, Topical, Inhaled, and Other routes. However, three child-specific issues must be considered when selecting administration routes: Compliance, Palatability and smell, and Pain. These factors significantly impact successful drug delivery and patient adherence to therapy in the paediatric setting.

### Drug Scheduling and Regulatory Framework

The South African regulatory system classifies medicines into distinct schedules based on their safety profile and dispensing requirements. Schedule 0 medicines are designated for Shop (unrestricted trade), allowing open purchase without pharmacy involvement. Schedule 1 and 2 medicines are dispensed at OTC pharmacy (over-the-counter) locations. These OTC medicines can be dispensed with or without prescription and can be repeated without formal prescription restrictions.

Prescription requirements escalate for Schedule 3 and above medicines, where a Prescription is required for dispensing. For Schedules 3, 4, and 5 prescriptions, the maximum repeat period is 6 months maximum. Schedule 6 substances carry a unique restriction: NO repeats allowed because of habit forming nature, potential of abuse, and risk of adverse effects. This strict regulation ensures safety and prevents misuse of potentially dangerous drugs.

Section 21 authorization in South Africa allows SAHPRA to authorise medicines not registered in South Africa for medicinal purposes, providing a pathway for access to unregistered but potentially beneficial drugs when no suitable registered alternatives exist.

### Clinical Trial Phases and Drug Development

The clinical development pathway for new medicines progresses through defined phases. Phase 0 of clinical trials involves Microdosing in humans to study pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics, providing initial human safety and metabolism data with minimal drug exposure. Phase 1 of clinical trials focuses on Safety, tolerability, side effects, and dose-ranging studies in healthy volunteers (small group), establishing the drug's basic safety profile and appropriate dosing range.

Phase 2 of clinical trials assesses Safety with dosing studies (component A) and Efficacy studies in a larger group (component B), beginning to evaluate whether the drug actually works for its intended indication while continuing to monitor safety. Phase 3 of clinical trials involves Randomized controlled trials in larger groups to assess efficacy in comparison to gold standard and side effects, providing robust evidence of the drug's effectiveness relative to existing treatments.

Phase 4 of clinical trials consists of Post-marketing surveillance for safety monitoring, continuing to track the drug's safety profile in real-world use after regulatory approval and market introduction.

### Reference Books and Professional Resources

Paediatric prescribing relies on authoritative reference materials. Frank Shann is the author of the reference book 'Drug Doses' used in paediatric prescribing, a widely recognised resource. The three main reference books recommended for paediatric prescribing in South Africa are: Drug Doses by Frank Shann, Essential Medicines List and Standard Treatment Guidelines (2023), and South African Medicines Formulary. These references provide evidence-based guidance and dosing information essential for safe paediatric medication prescribing.

### Prescription Writing Requirements

Proper prescription documentation is critical for patient safety and legal compliance. Patient information that must be included on a prescription chart comprises Name, identification number, diagnosis, ward, allergies, and weight. The patient's weight is particularly important in paediatrics given the weight-based dosing methods frequently employed.

Drug information must be included on a prescription with specific requirements: Name of drug (DO NOT use trade names), DO NOT use abbreviations, Dose, Frequency, Duration, and Route. Using generic drug names rather than trade names ensures clarity and avoids confusion across different formulations and manufacturers. Prescriber information is required on a prescription, including Name, qualifications, HPCSA number, and date of prescription. These prescriber identifiers ensure accountability and allow verification of prescriber credentials.

Only generic drug names should be used to ensure clarity and avoid confusion when writing prescriptions. This practice prevents medication errors that could result from unfamiliar brand names and ensures that any appropriately qualified healthcare provider can dispense the medication.

### Drug-Specific Contraindications and Off-Label Use

Certain drugs carry important contraindications in the paediatric population. Ciprofloxacin is contraindicated in children under 18 years, reflecting concerns about potential effects on developing bone and cartilage. Aspirin can cause Reye's Syndrome in children and is therefore avoided in paediatric fever management and viral illness treatment.

Off-label drug use represents a common clinical reality in paediatrics. Sildenafil usually has no approved paediatric dose, demonstrating that sometimes drugs are used off-label which are not yet approved for paediatric use. Clinicians must exercise careful clinical judgment and ensure informed consent when considering off-label medication use in children, weighing the potential benefits against unknown or unproven risks in the paediatric population.
