# DEHYDRATION

## Lecture Notes

### Definition & Initial Assessment

Dehydration in paediatric emergency care is a state of fluid loss from body tissues requiring assessment and fluid replacement in children with diarrhoea and other causes of fluid loss. The initial assessment approach screens systematically for shock versus non-shock dehydration, as this distinction fundamentally determines the urgency and type of intervention. The clinician must first assess for the presence of shock (abnormal Circulation, Coma, or Convulsions) before proceeding to classify the degree of dehydration in children presenting with diarrhoeal illness.

### ETAT Assessment Sequence

The ETAT framework provides the structured assessment sequence for all paediatric emergency presentations. The Priority Signs 3TPR-MOB — Tiny baby, Temperature, Trauma, Pallor, Poisoning, Pain (severe), Respiratory, Restless, Referral (urgent), Malnutrition, Oedema, and Burns — guide rapid initial screening. Critically, severe dehydration assessment does not occur at this initial screening stage. Rather, assessment for severe dehydration happens only after completion of Circulation/Coma/Convulsions evaluation. This sequence ensures that life-threatening shock is identified and acted upon before the more detailed dehydration classification is undertaken.

### Classification of Severe Dehydration

Diarrhoeal illness with fluid loss is classified into three categories: non-severe, severe, and shock. A child with diarrhoea is classified as having severe dehydration when two or more of the three key clinical signs are present, provided the child is not in shock. These three cardinal signs are: lethargy or altered consciousness, sunken eyes, and a skin pinch that goes back very slowly — taking longer than 2 seconds to return to normal. The presence of any one sign alone, or the absence of diarrhoea, does not establish the diagnosis. Additionally, the assessment must identify whether the child has severe malnutrition, defined by the presence of both visible severe wasting and bilateral pitting oedema of both feet. This distinction is crucial because severely malnourished children require fundamentally different fluid management strategies than well-nourished children.

### Shock Assessment & Management

Shock in a child with severe dehydration presents with characteristic signs: cold hands, a capillary refill time of 3 seconds or more, and a weak or fast pulse. When shock is detected, fluid resuscitation becomes the immediate priority. The initial management steps are to stop any active bleeding, provide oxygen, keep the child warm, and obtain either peripheral IV or intraosseous (IO) access. At the moment of vascular access, collect DEFG samples — the mnemonic reminding clinicians to collect samples for Don't Ever Forget Glucose measurement plus septic workup cultures. The IV or IO cannula must be fixed securely and the extremity immobilised if applicable. IV antibiotics should be considered if sepsis is suspected. Once these initial steps are complete, proceed to fluid bolus administration.

During fluid bolus administration, continuous monitoring is essential. The clinician must check the pulse and breathing rate frequently and watch vigilantly for signs of congestive cardiac failure (CCF), which may be precipitated by rapid fluid administration. Hypoglycaemia must be actively managed: if the child's glucose is less than 3 mmol/L, administer 5 mL/kg of 10% dextrose intravenously immediately. If improvement is not evident or if the child deteriorates during shock management, reassess for ongoing shock, consider administering fluid faster, seek senior advice, check glucose levels, assess for and treat severe malnutrition if present, and consider antibiotics if sepsis is suspected.

### Malnutrition Modifications

Children with severe malnutrition have precarious metabolic and circulatory status far more than well-nourished children, making their fluid and sodium handling distinctly different. Standard fluid rates can precipitate cardiac failure in this population. The fundamental modification is the rehydration rate: whereas a well-nourished child with severe dehydration (but no shock) receives 10 mL/kg/hr over 4 hours, a severely malnourished child receives the same volume per kilogram — 10 mL/kg/hr — but extended over 8 hours. This slower, lower-volume regimen gives the precarious cardiovascular system time to accommodate the fluid without triggering circulatory failure. Additionally, glucose status must be monitored and managed rigorously in malnourished children, as hypoglycaemia is a major risk in this vulnerable population. All severely malnourished children require active glucose assessment and management as part of their rehydration protocol.

### Plan A: Oral Rehydration Therapy

Plan A attempts rehydration via the oral route, making use of oral rehydration solution (ORS). The dosing is 5 mL/kg administered via nasogastric tube or oral intake every 15 minutes. This regimen is continued for 2 hours, after which the child is reassessed for clinical improvement. If the child is improving after this 2-hour period, oral rehydration is continued for another 2 hours, extending the trial to 4 hours total before considering escalation to intravenous therapy. If the child vomits everything and shows no improvement after the initial 2 hours, Plan A has failed and the clinician should proceed to Plan B (intravenous fluids). The nasogastric tube is a valuable tool in this context, providing non-invasive fluid access when IV access is difficult or when a child cannot drink sufficient volumes orally. ORS is delivered as a continuous infusion or as 5 mL/kg every 15 minutes — never as a single 20 mL/kg bolus per hour, which overwhelms the GI tract and commonly triggers vomiting.

### Plan B: IV Fluid Therapy

When oral rehydration with Plan A has failed — either because the child vomits everything or because no improvement occurs after 2 hours — intravenous fluid therapy is initiated. For a child with severe dehydration but no severe malnutrition, give IV dextrose-saline at 10 mL/kg/hr for 4 hours. The child must be reassessed every 1–2 hours during this infusion. As soon as the child is able to drink, begin offering oral ORS simultaneously; the intravenous rate is then progressively slowed while oral intake is gradually increased. This overlapping transition is safer than abruptly stopping IV fluids. Once dehydration has been fully corrected and the IV rate is low, proceed to the maintenance phase.

For a child without severe malnutrition who is not improving on the standard 10 mL/kg/hr regimen, rapid rehydration at 20 mL/kg/hr over 4 hours may be considered. This higher rate is used only in well-nourished children and is never appropriate for malnourished children. In contrast, a severely malnourished child receives 10 mL/kg/hr over 8 hours — the same infusion rate as for well-nourished children, but extended over twice the duration to allow safe fluid accommodation.

### Plan C & Alternative Access Routes

When peripheral intravenous access cannot be established, two alternative routes are available. Intraosseous access allows infusion of dextrose-saline at 10 mL/kg/hr for 4 hours, using the same fluid and rate as peripheral IV. If intraosseous access is also unavailable or unsuccessful, nasogastric fluid administration becomes the next option. For NG delivery, give ORS at 20 mL/kg/hr for 6 hours. This is best delivered as a continuous infusion, which is preferred over intermittent bolus dosing for better tolerance. Importantly, the 20 mL/kg/hr dose must never be given as a single 20 mL/kg bolus per hour; divided dosing as a continuous drip prevents gastric overdistension and vomiting.

### Fluid Rates & Weight Banding

Calculating fluid requirements in paediatric emergency care requires speed and accuracy at the bedside. The standard approach uses the child's actual weight in kilograms to determine millilitres per hour, but weight banding provides a practical shortcut that reduces calculation errors. Weight banding involves estimating the child's weight to the nearest practical band (e.g. 5 kg, 10 kg, 15 kg) and using that rounded figure to look up doses and infusion rates. This approach significantly decreases the likelihood of tenfold errors when calculations are performed rapidly during an emergency.

Concrete examples illustrate how weight banding works in practice. A child weighing 7.3 kg is placed in the 5–10 kg weight band; the clinician uses 10 kg (or sometimes 8 kg as an intermediate) for calculations. A child weighing 3.1 kg falls in the infant band and calculations are based on 3–5 kg. In each case, the weight band is used consistently throughout the fluid calculation to derive the hourly rate, and the same weight band is used to double-check drip rates.

### Drip Rate Calculations

Converting millilitres per hour to drops per minute requires knowing the calibration of the IV giving set in use. Most hospital drip sets are either 60 drops/mL or 15 drops/mL. Understanding the relationship between volume and drop rate is essential for safe manual infusion control.

For a 60 drops/mL dropper: volume per hour in millilitres equals drops per minute. An infusion of 100 mL/hr runs at exactly 100 drops per minute. An infusion of 146 mL/hr runs at 146 drops per minute.

For a 15 drops/mL dropper: divide the volume in millilitres per hour by 4. An infusion of 100 mL/hr runs at 100 ÷ 4 = 25 drops per minute. An infusion of 146 mL/hr runs at 146 ÷ 4 ≈ 36.5 drops per minute.

Practical case examples demonstrate application. A 7.3 kg child (weight band 10 kg) requiring Plan A oral rehydration receives ORS 5 mL/kg per 15 minutes: 5 × 7.3 ≈ 36.5 mL, rounded to 40 mL per 15 minutes using the weight band. If this child later requires Plan B IV therapy at 20 mL/kg/hr for 4 hours: 20 × 7.3 = 146 mL/hr (weight band 160 mL/hr). Using a 60 drops/mL dropper, set the rate at 146–160 drops per minute. Using a 15 drops/mL dropper, set the rate at 34–40 drops per minute.

An 11 kg child with severe dehydration and severe malnutrition receives Plan B at the slower malnutrition rate: 10 mL/kg/hr × 8 hours = 110 mL/hr (weight band 80 mL/hr). Using a 60 drops/mL dropper, set at 80 drops per minute. Using a 15 drops/mL dropper, set at 20 drops per minute. The duration is 8 hours, not 4 hours, due to malnutrition.

A 3.1 kg infant (weight band 3–5 kg) requiring rapid infusion at 20 mL/kg/hr for 4 hours: 20 × 3.1 = 62 mL/hr (weight band 60 mL/hr). Using a 60 drops/mL dropper, set at 60–62 drops per minute. Using a 15 drops/mL dropper, set at 15 drops per minute. A critical safety consideration applies to tiny infants: ensure that no more than 100 mL can run through suddenly, as rapid over-infusion is catastrophic in this population.

A 10 kg child who cannot tolerate IV access and requires nasogastric infusion receives ORS at 20 mL/kg/hr for 6 hours: 20 × 10 = 200 mL/hr (weight band 240 mL/hr). Using a 60 drops/mL dropper, set at 200–240 drops per minute. Using a 15 drops/mL dropper, set at 50–60 drops per minute.

### Equipment Safety & Manual Infusion

Manual infusion controllers and roller clamps present significant safety hazards that must be understood and mitigated. Roller controllers, once adjusted to a desired rate, tend to slow progressively over time due to friction changes and fluid viscosity variations. Dial-flow systems are affected by the height of the drip bag above the infusion site: a higher bag creates greater hydrostatic pressure, driving fluid faster than the dial setting indicates; a lower bag reduces pressure and slows flow below the dial setting. The position of the limb and the degree of vascular resistance at the cannula site further alter the actual delivered rate; occlusion or increased resistance slows flow, while movement or relaxation may speed it up.

These variables make manual control unreliable. A clinician cannot reliably set a roller controller and walk away — the rate will change unpredictably. Manual systems provide no audible or visual alarm if the flow goes too fast or too slow; the clinician must maintain constant bedside vigilance. Most importantly, if a roller clamp becomes dislodged or slips, catastrophic infusion of a sudden large volume can occur, particularly dangerous in infants. The solution is twofold: (1) use electronic infusion pumps wherever possible, as they deliver the set rate regardless of drip height, limb position, or vascular resistance, preventing overdose; and (2) if manual control must be used, continuously observe the drip chamber, protect the roller or dial from accidental displacement, and be prepared to intervene immediately if unexpected changes occur.

### Hypoglycaemia Management

Hypoglycaemia is a serious and common complication in dehydrated children, particularly those with severe malnutrition. During shock management or fluid bolus administration, if the child's capillary glucose is less than 3 mmol/L, immediate treatment is required. Administer 10% dextrose at 5 mL/kg intravenously. This is the specific concentration and dose — not other dextrose concentrations, and not higher volumes. The glucose check should be repeated after 10–15 minutes to confirm resolution of hypoglycaemia.

### Monitoring During Rehydration

Throughout rehydration, continuous clinical monitoring ensures safety and detects complications early. While fluid bolus is being administered (in shock cases), measure pulse and breathing rate frequently — at least every 15–30 minutes. Observe vigilantly for signs of congestive cardiac failure, particularly in children receiving rapid fluid infusion or in those with severe malnutrition. The appearance of respiratory distress, rales on auscultation, hepatomegaly, or peripheral oedema signals CCF and warrants slower infusion rates and possible diuretic therapy.

Reassess the child's clinical status every 1–2 hours during IV rehydration. Look for improvement in lethargy, restoration of sunken eyes, and normalisation of skin turgor. If improvement is occurring as expected, continue the planned regimen. If the child is not improving or is deteriorating, stop and reassess: check for persistent or recurrent shock, measure glucose, verify the IV rate is running as prescribed, assess weight change, and consider antibiotics if sepsis is suspected. When improvement is evident and the child begins to tolerate oral intake, begin transitioning to the next phase.

### Transition from IV to Oral

The transition from intravenous to oral fluids should not be abrupt. As the child's dehydration improves and oral intake becomes tolerated, gradually slow the IV infusion rate while simultaneously increasing oral ORS intake. This overlapping approach allows continuous hydration support while the child adapts to full oral intake. Once the child is drinking well and maintaining hydration, the IV rate can be discontinued and oral maintenance fluids become the sole source of fluid replacement.

### Maintenance Phase & Observation

Once severe dehydration has been fully corrected and the child is stable on low-rate IV fluids (or if oral rehydration succeeds), proceed to the maintenance phase. At this stage, give preventive ORS at 10 mL/kg orally after each stool. This is not a fixed schedule but rather a stool-based regimen: each time the child passes stool, offer 10 mL/kg of ORS to replace ongoing losses and prevent recurrent dehydration. The child must be observed for a minimum of 6 hours post-hydration to ensure that dehydration is maintained at resolution and that no complications have developed. During this observation period, continue monitoring vital signs, reassess clinical signs of hydration, and ensure adequate oral intake. Once the child has been stable for the full observation period with maintained hydration and no complications, discharge planning with family education about continued ORS use, feeding, and return precautions can begin.
