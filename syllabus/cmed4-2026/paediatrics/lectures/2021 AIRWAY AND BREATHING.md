## Lecture Notes

### Assessment Frameworks

The approach to paediatric emergencies relies on two primary assessment frameworks that guide initial evaluation and management. The ABCCCD framework systematizes the assessment in order of priority: A stands for Airway, B for Breathing, C for Circulation, followed by a second C for Coma, another C for Convulsion, and finally D for Dehydration. This hierarchical approach ensures that life-threatening airway and breathing problems are addressed before other conditions.

The 3TPR framework focuses on specific assessment factors, particularly in infants and young children. The three T's represent Tiny baby assessment, Temperature, and Trauma, while the three P's stand for Pallor, Poisoning, and Pain. This framework helps clinicians consider age-specific vulnerabilities and environmental factors that may contribute to the child's condition.

### Breathing Assessment

Determining whether a child is breathing requires a systematic approach using the Look-Listen-Feel technique. Looking involves observing the chest to see if it is moving or if the child is active, talking, or crying—signs of adequate ventilation. Listening means paying attention to breath sounds from the nose or mouth. Feeling involves placing your hand at the child's nose or mouth to detect breath movement. This three-pronged assessment provides rapid, reliable confirmation of breathing status.

A child in respiratory distress will show multiple warning signs that require immediate attention. These include increased work of breathing, visible fatigue and distress, fast breathing measured by an elevated respiratory rate, and difficulty with normal activities such as talking, eating, or breastfeeding. Physical examination may reveal severe chest wall indrawing, where the chest wall retracts with each breath. Auditory signs include grunting—a short noise on expiration that indicates low oxygenation—and head nodding that occurs with each respiration, particularly in severely distressed infants.

Abnormal respiratory sounds carry specific clinical meanings. Stridor is a high-pitched, squeaky noise heard during both the inspiratory and expiratory phases of breathing, indicating upper airway obstruction from airway narrowing. Grunting, beyond the context of distress, generally indicates low oxygenation and requires urgent intervention. Wheeze indicates obstruction of the lower airway. Stertor or snoring sounds represent obstruction at the level of the oropharynx and tongue, distinct from the upper airway obstruction that produces stridor.

Central cyanosis—bluish or purplish discoloration of the tongue, inside the mouth, and skin—results from abnormally low oxygen levels in the blood. This must be distinguished from peripheral cyanosis and can occur even in severe anaemia. Cyanosis is assessed by examining the mouth and tongue. An oxygen saturation of less than 92% indicates dangerously low oxygen levels and constitutes an emergency requiring immediate intervention.

### Airway Obstruction and Signs

Several conditions can obstruct or partially obstruct the airway, each producing characteristic signs. Gurgling sounds indicate pooled secretions or blood in the airway that requires suctioning. Stertor or snoring suggests soft palate obstruction. Stridor indicates upper airway obstruction due to airway narrowing and requires investigation for the underlying cause.

Croup is the most common infectious cause of upper airway obstruction in children and produces the characteristic stridor sound. Foreign body aspiration represents another important cause of airway obstruction, with coins and peanuts being among the most frequently encountered objects. Foreign body aspiration is a genuine emergency because the child faces the threat of complete airway obstruction and the aspiration can lead to obstructive respiratory failure, central respiratory failure, or tiredness with subsequent pneumonia. A thorough history should be obtained to assess the risk of foreign body aspiration. Epiglottitis, though less common than croup, can rapidly cause severe upper airway obstruction and must always be considered in the differential diagnosis.

### Head Positioning for Airway Opening

The position of the child's head during airway management differs significantly between age groups due to anatomical differences. Infants have small-diameter airways and a soft, compressible windpipe that is easily obstructed. In infants, the head should be maintained in a neutral position with the nose pointing upward. Excessive backward head tilt must be avoided because it compresses and narrows the infant's windpipe, worsening obstruction. In contrast, older children can safely tolerate the head tilt-chin lift maneuver used in adults, where gentle pressure is applied to the forehead to produce a slight backward tilt of the head.

Before any head positioning or manipulation, always check for signs of head and neck trauma or history of falls that might have damaged the cervical spine. If trauma is suspected, alternative airway-opening techniques must be employed to avoid further injury. After performing any airway maneuver, the senior practitioner must verify its effectiveness using the Look-Listen-Feel assessment rather than assuming success from a single attempt.

### Airway Opening Maneuvers

The head tilt-chin lift maneuver remains a standard airway-opening technique for patients without suspected cervical spine injury. This maneuver begins with assessment for trauma and proceeds with gentle head extension and chin elevation to open the airway.

The jaw thrust maneuver without head tilt is the safe technique when cervical spine injury is possible. The procedure involves placing both thumbs on the child's forehead while positioning 2 to 3 fingers under the angle of the jaw on both sides. The jaw is then lifted upward to displace the tongue away from the posterior pharynx. Importantly, the thumbs serve to keep the head in place and ensure the neck is not moved during the maneuver. This technique is safe for use in all ages and must be mastered by anyone managing pediatric airways in trauma settings.

### Cervical Spine Management

Any child with suspected cervical spine injury requires careful attention to neck stabilization and alignment during all procedures. Assessment should include questioning about head or neck trauma or any falls that could have damaged the cervical spine, combined with visual inspection for bruises or other signs of trauma. Neck stabilization begins with manual support and progresses to more formal immobilization.

The log roll technique represents a critical procedure for children with suspected cervical spine injury. A senior person assumes responsibility and positions themselves at the top end of the child. This senior practitioner holds the child's head in order to keep the neck straight and in line with the body by placing fingers under the angle of the mandible with palms over the parietal regions. Two to three assistants carefully roll the child's body in line with the head and neck following the senior person's clear instructions. A further person examines the back for injuries. Notably, per rectal examination is NOT performed in children during this process.

Immobilization of the cervical spine involves multiple methods used individually or in combination. Manual support involves constant hands-on stabilization of the head and neck. Blocks, sandbags, rolled towels, or one-litre bags of intravenous fluid are placed on each side of the head to prevent movement. An appropriately sized rigid neck collar must be applied with the child lying on their back. A critical safety point must be emphasized: the child's head should NEVER be taped to the bed or trolley, as this creates a risk of vomiting and aspiration if the child develops nausea.

### Airway Equipment

The oropharyngeal airway is a simple artificial airway device that moves the tongue away from the back of the throat and opens the upper airway. It is curved to fit the contours of the throat and must be long enough to reach the airways leading to the lungs. Oropharyngeal airways are typically only tolerated in children with a low level of consciousness. However, they may provoke vomiting, and if the child develops a gag reflex or begins to retch, the airway must be removed immediately as this indicates the child is regaining consciousness and will not tolerate the device.

The Guedel airway is a specific type of oropharyngeal airway that comes in seven sizes: 000, 00, 0, 1, 2, 3, and 4, each marked with a different colour for easy identification. Sizing is critical and is measured from the midpoint of the child's incisors to the angle of the mandible. An oversized Guedel airway can depress the epiglottis and obstruct the airway, while an undersized airway may allow the tongue to obstruct the airway. Insertion technique differs by age: in infants, the airway is inserted right side up using a tongue depressor to prevent tongue obstruction. In older children, the device can be inserted like an anaesthetic would insert it, with care to ensure the curved tip points upward without being obstructed by the tongue, then rotated 180 degrees to pass under the hard palate. In all children, if a gag reflex develops or the child begins to retch, the airway must be removed immediately.

The Yankaer suction tip is a firm plastic suction device with a large opening surrounded by a bulbous head. This design allows effective suctioning of secretions without damaging surrounding tissue. It provides superior control compared to softer flexible suction catheters and is longer and more effective at removing secretions from the airway. The Yankaer tip's design prevents plugging of the suction line and makes it the preferred device for oral suctioning in emergency situations.

### Ambu Bag Use and Manual Ventilation

The Ambu bag is a hand-held device that delivers positive pressure ventilation to children requiring respiratory support. It consists of a squeezable bag with a one-way valve system and a face mask attachment. When the bag is compressed, the valve opens and forces air through the mask or artificial airway into the lungs. When the bag is released, the valve closes and exhalation occurs passively through an exhalation port. With exhalation, the bag refills with oxygen or room air, preparing for the next compression cycle.

Before using an Ambu bag, five essential checks must be performed. First, verify that the bag is connected to an oxygen source. Second, confirm that it has a functioning blow-off valve to prevent excessive pressure build-up. Third, ensure that the bag and mask fit the child appropriately. Fourth, check for any leaks in the system. Fifth, verify that the ventilation rate is appropriate—typically 2 seconds per breath for adequate inflation and exhalation time.

The C-grip represents the correct hand positioning technique for holding the mask in place during manual ventilation with an Ambu bag. Different mask types may be used depending on the child's age and size, but proper grip technique is essential regardless of mask type to ensure an adequate seal and prevent air leak.

Several common errors can prevent effective ventilation even when the basic setup is correct. Soft tissue compression under the chin can obstruct the airway—this error may occur even when the head position is appropriate, the reservoir is present, oxygen is attached, and the mask fits well. Addressing this requires careful attention to hand positioning. Other causes of failed ventilation include inappropriate head or jaw position, mouth closure, insufficient pressure application, mucus or blood obstructing the airway, presence of a foreign body, or a poor seal between mask and face. If the chest does not rise during ventilation, systematically address each of these potential causes.

The ideal ventilation rate when manually ventilating a child with an Ambu bag is 2 seconds per breath, allowing adequate time for both chest inflation and passive exhalation.

### Abnormal Respiratory Sounds

Stridor is a high-pitched, squeaky noise heard during both the inspiratory and expiratory phases of breathing. It is caused by upper airway obstruction or narrowing. Common causes of stridor include croup (the most frequent infectious cause), foreign body aspiration (less common than croup), and epiglottitis. This sound must be distinguished from stertor, which is the snoring sound produced by obstruction at the level of the oropharynx and tongue rather than the upper airway.

Grunting is a short noise heard during exhalation that generally indicates low oxygenation in the child. It is a sign of respiratory distress requiring prompt assessment and intervention.

Wheeze indicates lower airway obstruction and requires evaluation for causes such as bronchospasm or aspiration.

### Breathing Management Principles

The management of breathing problems in paediatric emergencies follows three main principles: manage the airway, give oxygen, and keep the child warm. These principles work together to optimize oxygenation and ventilation while maintaining normothermia.

When administering oxygen during resuscitation for respiratory or airway problems, always give the highest concentration of oxygen possible until you are satisfied with the child's breathing and oxygenation. This aggressive approach to oxygenation during emergencies contrasts with chronic oxygen management and is justified by the immediate threat to life.

### Oxygen Sources

Two primary oxygen sources are available in emergency settings, each with distinct advantages and limitations. Oxygen cylinders are easy to use and require only a flow meter and tubing. They operate without electricity, making them reliable in any setting. However, they are relatively expensive to purchase and maintain, and ensuring a continuous supply can be logistically difficult in resource-limited settings.

Oxygen concentrators work by pumping room air through a zeolite canister to remove nitrogen and concentrate oxygen. They have a moderate initial cost, require little maintenance, and produce oxygen continuously at low ongoing cost. The critical disadvantage is their dependence on a continuous electrical supply. Oxygen concentrators produce approximately 5 L/min of oxygen, which is best suited for use with nasal prongs or nasal catheters that do not require high flow rates to achieve adequate oxygenation.

### Oxygen Delivery Methods

Four methods are recommended for oxygen delivery in paediatric emergency settings, each suited to different clinical situations and oxygen concentration requirements.

Face masks with non-rebreather reservoir bags deliver the highest concentration of oxygen available, typically exceeding 80% when properly sealed to the face. The mask includes a one-way valve system that directs exhaled air away from the reservoir bag, preventing inhalation of room air and re-inhalation of exhaled air. This system ensures the highest possible oxygen concentration and is preferred when emergency situations demand maximum oxygenation.

Venturi face masks utilize a tube-shaped reservoir with a Venturi fitting at the end. These masks deliver variable oxygen concentrations ranging from 28 to 60% depending on the colour code of the Venturi fitting used. The specific percentage is determined by checking the colour marking on the bottom of the Venturi fitting. The percentage delivered depends critically on providing the correct flow rate of 100% oxygen to the oxygen tube fitting.

Nasal prongs consist of short tubes inserted into the child's nostrils. Correct application requires attention to right size, right way up, and right fixation to the child's face. It is essential to keep the nose clear of mucus because secretions can block the flow of oxygen. When 100% oxygen is supplied at a flow rate of 1 L/min in infants and 1 to 2 L/min in older children, nasal prongs deliver 30 to 35% oxygen concentration in the inspired air.

Nasal catheters are thin tubes made from nasogastric tubes or suction catheters, typically sized 6 or 8 French gauge (with 8 FG being preferred when using a suction catheter). The catheter is inserted into either nostril and secured in place, then connected to an oxygen source. The tip of the catheter should not be visible below the uvula. Like nasal prongs, correct application requires attention to right size, right way up, and right fixation. When 100% oxygen is supplied at a flow rate of 0.5 L/min in infants and 1 L/min in older children, nasal catheters deliver 35 to 40% oxygen concentration in the inspired air, providing slightly higher oxygenation than prongs at equivalent flow rates.

### Choking Assessment and Initial Management

The first step in managing a choking child is to determine whether the child is actually choking. Once choking is suspected, the immediate priority is to assess the effectiveness of the child's cough. This assessment has critical implications for management.

The assessment of a choking child must address two dimensions: the effectiveness of the child's cough and the child's level of consciousness. These two factors together determine the appropriate management approach. A child with an effective cough and maintained consciousness requires a very different approach than a child with an ineffective cough.

An effective cough is recognized by several positive findings. The child should be able to speak, cry, and take breaths between coughs. If the child demonstrates these abilities, the cough is effective and the child's own spontaneous cough is more effective at relieving the obstruction than any externally imposed maneuver. Therefore, no intervention should be made. Instead, the child should be observed closely and assessed continuously for any change in cough effectiveness.

Signs that the cough is becoming ineffective include the cough becoming quiet or silent, inability to cry or speak, inability to take a breath, development of cyanosis, and beginning loss of consciousness. When any of these signs appear, the situation has escalated to an emergency. Call for help immediately and begin intervention maneuvers without delay.

The principle of supporting effective cough cannot be overemphasized: spontaneous cough is more effective than any externally imposed maneuver, and interrupting an effective cough with unnecessary interventions may worsen the outcome.

A child with a history of foreign body aspiration who shows increasing respiratory distress is in immediate danger of completely obstructing the airway. In this situation, attempts to remove the foreign body must be made instantly, but care must be taken not to push the object further into the airway with the intervention.

### Choking Intervention Maneuvers

When a choking child develops an ineffective cough, action and intervention maneuvers are required immediately. The specific maneuvers differ according to the child's age, with infants requiring one approach and older children requiring another.

If a child becomes unconscious during choking maneuvers, discontinue the choking maneuvers immediately. Instead, begin ventilation with a bag and mask using high-flow oxygen, and perform cardiac compressions if there are no signs of circulation. The transition from choking management to resuscitation must be prompt and decisive.

### Infant Choking Management

Infants with choking and an ineffective cough are managed using back blows combined with chest thrusts. Throughout all maneuvers, the infant's head should be kept in a head-down position, which aids in the expulsion of the foreign body through gravity.

Back blows are directed forcefully at the back of the infant, between the shoulder blades. These blows force the diaphragm upward and create a pressure wave in the airway that can dislodge the foreign body. The sequence is to perform 5 back blows, then reassess the infant's condition before proceeding to chest thrusts.

Chest thrusts for infants utilize a 2-finger compression technique. The location is precisely 1 finger breadth ABOVE the xiphisternum (the lower end of the breastbone). The compression depth should be 1/3 of the chest diameter and must be forceful enough to cause a compression wave in the airway. The infant should be maintained in a head-down position throughout. The sequence is to perform 5 chest thrusts, then reassess before recycling the cycle of back blows and chest thrusts. Continue this alternating sequence until the foreign body is expelled or the clinical status changes.

### Older Child Choking Management

Older children with choking and an ineffective cough are managed using a combination of back blows and abdominal thrusts (the Heimlich maneuver). This differs from infant management because abdominal thrusts are not appropriate for infants.

Back blows in older children are directed forcefully at the back to force the diaphragm upward and create a compression wave in the airway. Perform 5 back blows and then reassess the child's condition.

The abdominal thrust or Heimlich maneuver involves positioning the fist above the navel and below the xiphisternum, then pulling the fist up and back with force to elevate the diaphragm and create a compression wave in the airway. The direction of pull is upward into the abdomen. Perform 5 abdominal thrusts, then reassess. This maneuver is not appropriate for infants.

The complete cycling sequence for an older child with ineffective cough involves performing 5 back blows and reassessing, then performing 5 chest thrusts or abdominal thrusts (depending on whether the child is transitioning between age categories) and reassessing, then repeating this cycle. Continue until the foreign body is expelled and the child can cough effectively, or until the child loses consciousness and resuscitation becomes necessary.

### Choking Management Algorithm

A systematic approach to the choking child encompasses the following steps: First, determine whether choking is actually likely based on history and presentation. Second, assess the effectiveness of the child's cough—is the cough effective or ineffective?—and determine the child's level of consciousness—is the child conscious or unconscious? Third, if the cough is effective, observe the child continuously without intervening; reassess frequently. Fourth, if the cough is ineffective, call for help and begin intervention maneuvers immediately. Fifth, apply age-specific maneuvers: for infants, use back blows plus chest thrusts; for older children, use back blows plus abdominal thrusts. Sixth, if the child becomes unconscious during any maneuver, discontinue the choking maneuvers immediately and begin bag-mask ventilation with high-flow oxygen plus chest compressions if no signs of circulation are present. Seventh, repeat cycles of maneuvers (5 compressions or thrusts per technique) until the object is expelled, the child can cough effectively, or resuscitation becomes necessary.

This iterative approach ensures that spontaneous cough is maximally supported while intervention is provided when needed, and that transitions to resuscitation occur rapidly if the child deteriorates.
