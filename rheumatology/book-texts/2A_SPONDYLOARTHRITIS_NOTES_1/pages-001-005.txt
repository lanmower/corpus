LECTURE NOTES – SPONDYLOARTHROPATHY 
 
Summary 
• 
Spondyloarthritis / Spondyloarthropathy (SpA) is a type of arthritis that affects the 
axial skeleton, and sometimes also the peripheral joints. 
• 
It is one of the seronegative arthritides. Also differentiating the disease from 
rheumatoid arthritis is the pattern of disease (a predilection for the spine, and 
asymmetric involvement of large, lower limb joints). 
• 
The disease can also affect the eyes and heart (extra-articular manifestations). 
• 
Prominent features are enthesitis (inflammation at the site of insertion of a tendon, 
ligament or articular capsule into bone) and dactylitis.  
• 
Symptoms typically show a good response to non-steroidal anti-inflammatory 
drugs (NSAIDs) especially early on in the disease. 
• 
SpA is more common in males and there is an association with the HLA-B27 
antigen.  
 
Subtypes of SpA: 
• 
Ankylosing spondylitis (AS) - prototype 
• 
Psoriatic arthritis (PsA) 
• 
Reactive arthritis (ReA) 
• 
Enteropathic arthritis (ESpA) 
• 
Juvenile ankylosing spondylitis (JSpA) 
 
CLASSIFICATION CRITERIA: 
The Modified New York criteria (1984) relied on radiographic evidence of sacroiliitis as well 
as clinical criteria to classify AS. Radiographic changes occur later in the disease and are 
not reversible. 
Newer criteria have been developed to classify the disease for early and established cases 
and include MRI techniques (active inflammation) as an important tool for early diagnosis of 
AS. 
 
European Spondyloarthropathy Study Group (ESSG) criteria: 
 
Inflammatory spinal pain OR synovitis (asymmetric or predominantly lower limb) 
AND 
One or more of the following variables: 
• 
Positive family history 
• 
Psoriasis 
• 
Inflammatory bowel disease 
• 
Urethritis, cervicitis or acute diarrhoea within one month before 
arthritis 
• 
Alternating buttock pain 
• 
Enthesopathy 
• 
Sacroiliitis

DEFINITION OF INFLAMMATORY BACK PAIN: 
 
Appliesto patients with chronic back pain (> 3 months): 
• 
Age of onset < 40 years 
• 
Insidious onset 
• 
Improvement with exercise 
• 
No improvement with rest 
• 
Pain at night (with improvement upon getting up) 
 
The criteria are fulfilled if at least four out of five parameters are present. 
 
HLA-B27 ANTIGEN AND THE SPONDYLOARTHROPATHIES: 
 
There is a strong association between the presence of the HLA-B27 antigen and SpA. The 
condition occurs throughout the world in proportion to the presence of HLA-B27. 
HLA-B27 can be found in 8.56% of the Caucasian population, and in 4% in people of African 
descent. 
 
AS is a rare disease, affecting 0.2% of the general population. The risk of developing AS in 
the HLA-B27 positive population is 20%. 
 
Of people with AS, >90% are HLA-B27 positive. There is no association between disease 
severity and the presence of HLA-B27. Further, the presence of the antigen does not 
distinguish between AS and other SpA subtypes as it is found in varying prevalence in all of 
the subtypes. Thus, AS remains a clinical diagnosis. The Modified New York criteria can be 
used.

ANKYLOSING SPONDYLITIS (AS): 
The Modified New York criteria for diagnosis of AS: (Definite AS if the radiological 
criterion is associated with at least one clinical criterion): 
Clinical criteria: 
• 
A history of inflammatory low back pain 
• 
Limitation of motion of the lumbar spine in the sagittal and frontal planes 
• 
Limitation of chest expansion relative to normal values correlated for age and sex 
 
Radiological criterion: 
• 
Sacroiliitis grade ≥2 bilaterally or grade 3-4 unilaterally 
 
TREATMENT OF AS: 
Spinal disease: Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are first-
line treatment of axial disease. 
Failure to respond to NSAIDs within four weeks – anti-tumour necrosis factor alpha 
inhibitors (anti-TNFi). 
 
Peripheral arthritis: Sulphasalazine or methotrexate can be used to treat 
peripheral arthritis. 
Non-responders will require anti-TNFi. 
 
PSORIATIC ARTHRITIS (PsA): 
Psoriatic arthritis is a seronegative arthropathy associated with psoriasis. 
 
The pattern of disease and joint involvement is different from rheumatoid arthritis (RA): 
• 
Inflammatory arthritis involving the distal interphalangeal (DIP) joints 
• 
Asymmetric arthritis of small and large joints 
• 
Prominent dactylitis,enthesitis 
• 
Nail changes – nail pitting, ridging, dactylitis 
• 
Negative rheumatoid arthritis 
• 
Axial involvement 
 
Radiological findings also differ from rheumatoid arthritis in the following ways: 
• 
Erosive arthritis without osteopaenia 
• 
Sacroiliitis, often asymptomatic, can be unilateral or bilateral 
• 
Presence of syndesmophytes 
• 
Paravertebral ossification 
• 
Enthesopathy 
 
Five patterns of arthritis have been found in PsA. These are not mutually exclusive 
categories and patients may have features of more than one pattern, and the disease can 
evolve over time. The five patterns that are described in PsA are: 
• 
Spondylitis, axial involvement 
• 
DIP involvement (typically spared in RA) 
• 
Oligoarticular, asymmetrical joint involvement 
• 
Polyarticular, symmetrical involvement (so-called ‘rheumatoid pattern’) 
• 
Arthritis mutilans (severe destructive, erosive disease) 
 
TREATMENT OF PsA: 
Spinal disease: NSAIDs – first-line treatment of axial disease 
Anti-TNFi for axial disease and for peripheral arthritis that has not responded to 
conventional agents 
 
Peripheral disease: Sulphasalazine, methotrexate (methotrexate has added benefit for 
skin disease) 
Newer biologic agents specifically target cytokines identified as key in the pathogenesis of 
PsA (interleukin-12/23 and interleukin-17).

REACTIVE ARTHRITIS (ReA): 
 
Reactive arthritis is an immunologically-mediated response to a distant pathogen. There 
is NO infection in the joint itself. 
Common organisms that can trigger reactive arthritis include Chlamydia 
trachomatis, Yersinia enterolitica, Salmonella spp., Salmonella spp., Campylobacter 
spp. 
Some patients may present with a triad of symptoms (urethritis, conjunctivitis, 
arthritis). This is not a separate subtype of ReA but rather just a peculiar presentation. The 
eponymous term ‘Reiter’s syndrome’ has been applied to this presentation in the past. 
 
EXTRA-ARTICULAR MANIFESTATIONS OF ReA: 
Keratoderma blennorrhagicum: papulo-squamous rash most commonly affecting the palms 
and soles 
 
Nail dystrophy: can occur with ReA, further highlighting the clinical overlap of some features 
with psoriatic arthritis 
 
Circinate balanitis: presents as shallow ulcers on the glans or shaft of the penis and is 
plaque-like and hyperkeratotic 
 
TREATMENT OF ReA: 
NSAIDs – first-line therapy for synovitis and enthesitis 
Intra-articular corticosteroids can be used for mono-arthritis 
 
As this is not a septic arthritis, treatment with antibiotics is not generally recommended. 
If an acute infection is present (e.g. current diarrhoea, urethritis) then this should be treated. 
The exception to this is in patients with proven chlamydia-induced ReA, where 
prolonged treatment with tetracyclines has been shown to reduce the rate of 
relapses 
 
Those patients who have recurrent or persistent arthritis may require treatment with disease- 
modifying anti-rheumatic drugs such as methotrexate and sulphasalazine.

ENTEROPATHIC ARTHRITIS (ESpA): 
Enteropathic SpA is an inflammatory arthritis that accompanies the inflammatory bowel 
diseases (IBD) (Crohn’s disease [CD] and ulcerative colitis [UC]). 
 
Arthritis occurs in 10-22% of patients with IBD, with a higher prevalence in CD than in 
UC. The arthritis may precede the gastrointestinal symptoms by many years, and these 
patients may be regarded as undifferentiated SpA until the IBD declares itself. 
The arthritis is typically pauci-articular and asymmetric, and may occur in a migratory 
pattern in some patients. 
 
Similar to the other subtypes of SpA, ESpA has a predilection for the lower limb 
joints. Dactylitis and enthesitis are also prominent features. 
The activity of the peripheral arthritis generally correlates well with the degree of active 
bowel inflammation, particularly in UC. 
 
EXTRA-ARTICULAR MANIFESTATIONS OF ESpA: 
Pyoderma gangrenosum: Chronic, recurrent skin inflammation presenting initially as small 
red blisters that can coalesce to form large painful ulcers of varying depths. The legs are most 
commonly affected, but other parts of the skin or mucous membranes may be involved. 
 
Anterior uveitis: Inflammation of the iris and adjacent tissue; if untreated it can cause 
permanent damage and loss of vision 
 
Erythema nodosum: Erythematous, tender subcutaneous nodules, most commonly located 
on the extensor aspect of the legs 
Localised inflammation and necrosis of subcutaneous adipose tissue 
 
TREATMENT OF ESpA: 
NSAIDs are again first-line therapy, although these are to be used with caution as they 
may worsen IBD. 
Peripheral arthritis can be treated with suphasalazine or methotrexate with or 
without steroids. 
Both the bowel disease and the arthritis respond well to anti-TNF. Several different 
anti- TNFi agents are available on the market, with good effect on both aspects of the 
disease, except for etanercept which may have good effect on the joint disease but has no 
effect on the IBD. Any of the other agents are thus preferred for ESpA.
