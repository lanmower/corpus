## Lecture Notes

### Understanding Shock in Paediatric Patients

Shock represents a critical emergency state defined by impaired tissue perfusion and inadequate oxygen delivery. In paediatric patients, shock is identified through three cardinal clinical signs that guide rapid assessment and intervention. These signs are the cornerstone of circulation assessment and must be recognised immediately. Cold hands indicate peripheral vasoconstriction and reduced perfusion. Capillary refill time (CRT) of ≥3 seconds demonstrates sluggish circulation, measured by blanching the skin and counting the time for colour return. A weak and fast pulse reflects the body's compensatory response to low blood pressure. When all three signs are present together, the child is in shock and requires emergency intervention. When only one or two of these signs are present, the child is classified as having impaired circulation and requires priority attention but may allow slightly more time for careful evaluation.

The causes of shock in paediatric settings are diverse but follow predictable physiological patterns. Diarrhoea remains the most common cause globally, leading to acute fluid loss from the circulation. Blood loss from trauma or active bleeding can rapidly deplete circulating volume. Capillary leak—where fluid escapes from the vasculature into the interstitial space—occurs in sepsis and severe malnutrition. Septic shock results from infection-triggered inflammation that compromises cardiac output and vascular tone. Understanding the underlying cause helps guide management, though the initial resuscitation approach remains consistent across all shock types.

### Initial Management of Shock

Upon recognition of shock, the first priority is to stop any ongoing bleeding by applying direct pressure, elevating affected limbs, or arranging urgent haemostasis. Oxygen delivery must be maximised immediately, typically through high-flow oxygen by mask or bag-and-mask ventilation if the child is in extremis. Warmth is critical because hypothermia impairs cardiac function and metabolic processes; remove wet clothing and apply blankets or heat lamps. Obtain intravenous or intraosseous (IO) access urgently—this is not a moment to attempt multiple peripheral veins when IO access is available. At the time of access establishment, take blood for emergency laboratory investigations; the mnemonic DEFG reminds clinicians to check specific parameters during bolus administration. Consider antibiotics immediately if septic shock is suspected, with ceftriaxone being the empiric agent of choice.

### Fluid Resuscitation: Sequential Bolus Protocols

The primary therapeutic intervention for shock is fluid bolus resuscitation, which restores circulating volume and re-establishes perfusion. The bolus volume and infusion rate depend critically on whether the child has severe acute malnutrition, which fundamentally alters cardiac and renal physiology.

For children without severe acute malnutrition, fluid resuscitation follows an aggressive protocol. The first bolus consists of 10 ml/kg administered rapidly over 15 minutes using balanced crystalloid solutions—either normal saline or Ringers lactate. After this bolus is completed, reassess the child. If shock signs persist, administer a second rapid bolus of 10 ml/kg over 15 minutes. Reassess again. If shock remains unresolved, give a third rapid bolus of 10 ml/kg over 15 minutes. At this point, beware—three boluses of 10 ml/kg each represent approximately 30 percent of total body weight, which approximates to about half the child's circulating volume. Continuing beyond this point without escalation to advanced support carries substantial risk of fluid overload and organ dysfunction.

For children with severe acute malnutrition, the pathophysiology is fundamentally different. These children have profound depletion of lean body mass, including cardiac and skeletal muscle. They have reduced cardiac reserve and impaired renal function that limits their ability to handle rapid fluid shifts. The first bolus is 10 ml/kg of normal saline or Ringers lactate, but infused cautiously over 30 minutes rather than 15. If shock persists after reassessment, give a second bolus of 10 ml/kg over 30 minutes. If shock still remains, the third bolus is 10 ml/kg over 60 minutes—double the time of the second bolus. This stepped increase in infusion time reflects the progressive risk of fluid overload in already-compromised physiology. The choice of fluids—normal saline or Ringers lactate—remains the same, as these balanced crystalloids are appropriate for resuscitation in both groups.

### Reassessment During Fluid Therapy

Reassessment is not optional; it is mandatory and must occur frequently. After each bolus is completed, check for resolution of shock signs. Look for warm hands, capillary refill return to ≤2 seconds, and a stronger, more regular pulse. Simultaneously, assess for emerging signs of heart failure, which signals fluid overload. These signs include an increasing pulse rate (tachycardia that worsens despite fluid), rapid respirations (tachypnoea), hepatomegaly (enlarged liver edge palpable more than 2 centimeters below the costal margin), gallop rhythm (an audible triple rhythm on auscultation), and crepitations at the lung bases (fine crackling sounds suggesting pulmonary oedema). Also note the child's consciousness level—improved alertness and responsiveness are positive signs, while persistent lethargy suggests ongoing shock. Do not leave the shocked child unattended while fluids are infusing.

Reassessment may reveal one of three outcomes. The child's condition improves, with resolution of shock signs and no signs of heart failure. In this case, immediately stop the bolus administration and transition to maintenance fluids. The child shows no improvement, remaining shocked despite completed boluses. In this situation, proceed to the next bolus in the sequence. The child develops signs of heart failure during bolus administration—this is the critical complication. Stop the bolus immediately; do not give additional crystalloid.

### Management After Shock Resolution or Heart Failure

When shock improves after fluid bolus administration, maintenance fluid therapy replaces ongoing losses and provides basal requirements. If the child has no clinical evidence of dehydration, use half-daily dextrose or dextrose-saline (½ DD) at 4 ml/kg/hr. This conservative rate avoids excessive fluid in a child who was recently in shock. If the child has clinical evidence of dehydration in addition to shock, calculate the rehydration requirement as percentage deficit multiplied by 10 multiplied by body weight in kilograms, then add this to the daily maintenance requirement. Change the child to oral feeds or breastfeeding as soon as shock has resolved and the child is able to tolerate enteral intake.

If heart failure develops during fluid resuscitation, the immediate action is to stop all bolus fluids. Reduce the infusion rate dramatically to ½ DD at 4 ml/hr—roughly 10 times slower than bolus administration. Seek help from senior clinicians or specialists immediately. Simultaneously, give glucose and antibiotics. Consider more advanced respiratory and cardiovascular support: intubation to secure the airway and reduce work of breathing, mechanical ventilation to rest the respiratory muscles and allow better oxygenation, and inotropes to support cardiac output when fluid alone cannot restore perfusion.

### The Third Bolus Threshold and Escalation

The third bolus represents a critical threshold beyond which standard shock management must evolve. After administering three boluses of 10 ml/kg each, the child has received 30 ml/kg total, representing approximately half the circulating volume. If the child is still shocked at this point, it indicates either refractory shock (shock not responding to standard resuscitation) or ongoing losses. Continuing with additional boluses without escalating care is inappropriate. Instead, seek help from senior clinicians or specialists. Give glucose and antibiotics as part of comprehensive shock management. Consider intubation for airway protection and ventilation for respiratory support. Initiate inotrope infusions to augment cardiac output chemically when fluid administration alone is insufficient.

### Glucose Management and Metabolic Complications

Shocked children are at high risk of severe hypoglycaemia, which worsens outcomes and must be identified and treated during the bolus infusion. Check capillary or laboratory glucose at the time of IV or IO access while the first bolus is being given. If glucose is below 3 mmol/L, administer 5 ml/kg of 10 percent dextrose intravenously. This should be given as a bolus, not as a slow infusion, to restore blood glucose rapidly. Re-check glucose after treatment and repeat the dextrose dose if hypoglycaemia persists.

### Blood Transfusion in Shock

Blood transfusion is indicated in shocked children when anaemia is severe enough to compromise oxygen-carrying capacity further. Transfuse fresh blood when haemoglobin is below 5 g/dL. Fresh blood (not older stored blood) is preferred in shock because it has better functional properties and avoids the complications of stored blood. This typically occurs as part of sequential resuscitation in children with blood loss and profound anaemia.

### Intraosseous Line: Definition, Anatomy, and Technique

An intraosseous (IO) line is a sturdy needle placed through cortical bone into the medullary cavity, allowing emergency infusion of life-saving fluids and medications. It is indicated in very sick children, particularly those in shock with difficult or impossible vascular access in emergency situations. When peripheral and central venous access are compromised, IO access may be lifesaving by enabling immediate delivery of resuscitation fluids and medications that cannot wait for line placement.

The preferred anatomical site is the tibia 1 cm below and medial to the tibial tuberosity (the bony prominence below the knee). This location is easy to identify, has thin cortical bone, and is distant from important neurovascular structures and the epiphysis. An 18-gauge pink short lumbar puncture (LP) needle is the standard equipment. The technique requires meticulous sterile preparation. Clean the insertion site with iodine solution. Stabilise the proximal tibia firmly with one hand to prevent movement. Insert the needle at a 90-degree angle (perpendicular to the bone surface) and use a twisting and drilling motion to penetrate the cortex. You will feel a distinct 'give' as the needle passes through cortical bone into the medullary cavity—this is the key sensation confirming penetration. Obtain flashback of marrow (usually blood-stained fluid) into the needle hub, then aspirate gently to confirm placement. Secure the needle firmly and immobilise the extremity to prevent dislodgement.

### Contraindications to Intraosseous Insertion

Several conditions contraindicate IO insertion at a planned site. Do not insert an IO line if there is an acute or recent fracture of the target bone, as the needle will not achieve secure placement and may damage bone fragments. Previous significant orthopaedic procedures at the site, such as an intramedullary nail or plate fixation, contraindicate IO insertion. Overlying skin infection at the insertion site is an absolute contraindication because the needle will track the infection into bone. Previous IO insertion at the same site, even if successful previously, is contraindicated to avoid multiple bone penetrations. Conditions causing abnormal bone quality, such as osteoporosis or osteogenesis imperfecta, are relative contraindications because the needle may traverse the bone entirely without secure placement.

### Acute Complications of Intraosseous Insertion

Acute complications can occur at the time of insertion or immediately afterward. Failure to enter the bone marrow occurs when the needle does not penetrate far enough or deviates through cortex. Infused fluid then extravasates (leaks) into surrounding soft tissues, creating subcutaneous swelling and potentially necrosis. Through-and-through penetration means the needle passes completely through both cortices of the bone, emerging on the other side; this prevents reliable infusion and risks injury to posterior structures. Bleeding into the medullary cavity or from perforation of marrow vessels can cause compartment syndrome if sufficient blood accumulates in the fascial compartment. Skin sloughing can occur if the needle is inserted at too large an angle to the skin surface, causing ischaemia of the overlying dermis.

### Delayed Complications of Intraosseous Insertion

Delayed complications develop hours to days after IO insertion. Osteomyelitis (bone marrow infection) is a serious but relatively rare complication, occurring in less than 2-3 percent of cases and usually only if the line is left in place for extended periods or if sepsis was already present. Physeal (growth plate) injury can occur if the needle passes through or damages the growth plate, potentially causing growth disturbance or limb length discrepancy, though modern IO techniques below the tibial tuberosity usually avoid the physis. Local infection at the insertion site can develop if sterile technique was breached. Compartment syndrome can develop from bleeding or swelling in the closed fascial space. Fat embolism, where marrow fat and bone particles enter the venous circulation, is rare but can occur and cause respiratory or cerebral complications.

### Confirmation of Successful IO Placement

Successful intraosseous placement is confirmed by several findings. You will feel the distinct 'give' as you push through cortex—this tactile sensation is reliable. Obtain flashback of marrow into the needle hub (often appearing as blood-tinged fluid). Confirm your ability to aspirate fluid or blood from the medullary cavity—resistance to aspiration suggests failure of placement. All three findings together indicate successful placement; if any is absent, placement has failed and the needle should be repositioned or removed.

### Special Anatomical Considerations

The physeal (growth) plate lies between the epiphysis and metaphysis and is vulnerable to damage. The cortical bone must be penetrated completely to access the medullary cavity, but penetration through both cortices is a complication. The medullary cavity contains abundant haematopoietic marrow and fat, which are the target tissues for the needle's final position. Growth disturbances from physeal injury are potential long-term consequences of unsuccessful or traumatic IO insertion.

### Malnutrition as a Critical Modifier of Shock Management

Severe acute malnutrition profoundly alters the physiology of shock and resuscitation. Malnourished children have depleted cardiac muscle mass and reduced myocardial contractility. Their kidneys have diminished function and cannot excrete excess fluid appropriately. As a result, rapid fluid administration risks pulmonary oedema, hepatomegaly, and acute cardiac decompensation. The principle is to give the same total volume (10 ml/kg per bolus) but over extended time periods. This allows gradual restoration of perfusion without overwhelming cardiac and renal function. The time differences are critical: non-malnourished children receive boluses over 15 minutes, but malnourished children receive the first and second boluses over 30 minutes and the third over 60 minutes. The difference between these groups highlights how malnutrition status is not merely an underlying condition but an active modifier of resuscitation tactics.

### Monitoring Parameters During Fluid Therapy

Ongoing monitoring during bolus administration tracks progress and early warning signs. Pulse rate should decrease and become stronger as perfusion improves, though it may initially rise if the child is compensating for hypovolaemia. Respiratory rate should decrease as tissue hypoxia resolves. Watch continuously for signs of congestive cardiac failure: the appearance of hepatomegaly, development of crackles on lung auscultation, or emergence of a gallop rhythm indicate fluid overload. Consciousness level should improve as the brain receives better perfusion. All children receiving fluid boluses must be observed continuously—do not leave them unattended.

### Practical Approach to Reassessment

At each reassessment point, use a systematic approach. Check for resolution of the three shock signs: are the hands now warm, is capillary refill ≤2 seconds, is the pulse stronger and more regular? Check for signs of heart failure that would demand immediate cessation of bolus therapy: increasing pulse rate unrelated to fever or anxiety, rapid respirations worsening despite oxygenation, newly palpable hepatomegaly, or new crackles on auscultation. Assess consciousness, which should improve with restoration of cerebral perfusion. This systematic reassessment after each bolus determines the next step: continue to maintenance fluids if shock resolves, give another bolus if shock persists, or stop fluids immediately if heart failure emerges.
