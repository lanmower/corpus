# EPILEPSY

## Lecture Notes

### Definitions and Epidemiology

A seizure, or convulsion, is a paroxysmal, time-limited change in motor activity and behavior that results from abnormal electrical activity in the brain. This fundamental definition underpins our understanding of epilepsy, which is a chronic brain disorder characterized by an increased predisposition to seizures, along with neurobiologic, cognitive, psychological, and social consequences. Importantly, epilepsy is considered to be present when two or more unprovoked seizures occur at an interval greater than 24 hours apart.

Seizures are remarkably common in the pediatric population, occurring in approximately 10% of children in the pediatric age group. However, a crucial epidemiological point to remember is that less than one-third of seizures in children are actually caused by epilepsy. This distinction is vital because it means the majority of children presenting with seizures may not have epilepsy itself.

There are multiple provoking factors that can trigger seizures in children, including high fever, infection, syncope, head trauma, hypoxia, toxins, cardiac arrhythmia, and various metabolic abnormalities. Understanding these factors is essential for appropriate management and prevention of future events.

### Etiology and Causes of Seizures

The causes of seizures in children are diverse and multifactorial. Infection represents a major category, including meningitis and encephalitis. Developmental and structural abnormalities of the brain, trauma, and intracranial hemorrhage are significant structural causes. Hypoxia-related seizures can occur in the perinatal and early childhood period. Metabolic abnormalities constitute a broad category of causes, including hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, hypoglycemia, hyper- and hyponatremia, kernicterus in the newborn period, and both amino acid and organic acid disorders. Drug withdrawal, particularly in infants exposed to sedative or opioid medications in utero, is another important cause to consider.

### Seizure Classification

Seizures are classified into two major categories: partial (focal) and generalized seizures. Partial seizures are further subdivided based on the presence or absence of consciousness impairment. Simple partial seizures occur with consciousness retained, and may present with motor manifestations such as focal jerking, sensory symptoms, autonomic phenomena (such as palpitations or pupillary changes), or psychic manifestations (such as fear or hallucinations). Complex partial seizures involve impairment of consciousness, which may present as a simple partial seizure followed by impaired consciousness, or with consciousness impaired from the onset. Partial seizures with secondary generalization represent a partial seizure that progresses to become a generalized seizure.

Generalized seizures include a variety of types: absence seizures (both typical and atypical forms), generalized tonic-clonic seizures, tonic seizures, clonic seizures, myoclonic seizures, and atonic seizures. These seizures involve both cerebral hemispheres from the onset and typically result in bilateral motor manifestations or loss of consciousness.

In newborns, seizures may take subtle forms due to the relatively undeveloped cerebral cortex. Manifestations include facial grimacing, nystagmus, intermittent apneic episodes, myoclonus, sudden loss of muscle tone, eye blinking, chewing movements, and lip smacking. These subtle presentations can easily be missed if the examiner is not specifically attuned to recognizing neonatal seizures.

### First-Time Seizure Evaluation

When a child presents with a first-time seizure, a systematic approach is essential. Initial assessment begins with the primary survey: assessment of airway, breathing, and circulation (A, B, C), supplemented by measurement of temperature, blood pressure, and blood glucose. It is critical to check for life-threatening causes during this phase, including meningitis, systemic sepsis, accidental or non-accidental head trauma, and ingestion of drugs or toxins.

A detailed history from an eyewitness is invaluable and should include a specific description of events. It is helpful to ask the eyewitness to imitate the movements observed. The history should cover what the child was doing before the episode, what happened before the seizure began, what occurred during the seizure, and what happened after. Inquire about an aura or behavior preceding the seizure, such as epigastric discomfort, pain, or fear. Specific details about the seizure itself are important: posture, whether tongue biting occurred, whether urinary incontinence happened. The post-ictal state should be characterized—whether the child slept afterward or experienced headache. Also determine the state of consciousness during the episode, the duration of the seizure, the timing (whether it occurred while awake, asleep, or on awakening), and any triggering or precipitating factors.

Physical examination should focus on searching for organic causes and complications. Measure height, weight, and head circumference. Perform a thorough general examination looking for dysmorphic features or neurocutaneous lesions such as those seen in neurofibromatosis or tuberous sclerosis. Conduct a comprehensive neurological examination including fundoscopic examination to assess the retina.

Investigations for a first-time seizure are substantial and include blood work: U&E (urea and electrolytes), creatinine and minerals, glucose, HIV status, full blood count, blood culture and/or urine culture, and toxicology screening. Lumbar puncture should be performed unless contraindicated, and a CT scan of the head should be obtained.

Regarding antimicrobial therapy, always consider the possibility of infection. Antimicrobials should be given to all children less than 18 months with fever, to all children less than 6 months regardless of fever, and to all children greater than 18 months who are unwell or have a persistently altered mental state. When a metabolic disorder is suspected—particularly in children younger than 18 months presenting with myoclonic seizures and delayed development—this should be specifically investigated. If lumbar puncture is contraindicated, an urgent CT scan should be performed. The approach to aborting a seizure follows the same protocol as status epilepticus management.

### Seizure Mimics and Differential Diagnosis

Several conditions can mimic seizures and must be distinguished from true seizures. Benign sleep myoclonus is a condition in which jerking movements occur exclusively while asleep. Sandifer syndrome presents with dystonia and stiffness of the head and neck, often associated with cerebral palsy. Shuddering attacks are movements seen in toddlers, characterized by distal flapping movements that occur when the child becomes excited. Reflex anoxic seizures result from noxious events that lead to vagally mediated bradycardia and asystole, resulting in brief collapse. Cyanotic breath holding is a form of vasovagal syncope. Pseudoseizures are psychogenic in origin. Recognizing these mimics prevents unnecessary treatment with antiepileptic drugs.

### Epilepsy Classification and Syndromes

When classifying a child with epilepsy, three key classification questions should be asked: First, what is the cause of the epilepsy—is it idiopathic, symptomatic, or cryptogenic? Second, where in the brain is affected—are the seizures generalized or focal in origin? Third, which seizure types are present—are they generalized or focal seizures? An example of correct epilepsy classification would be "idiopathic generalized epilepsy with tonic-clonic seizures" or "remote symptomatic epilepsy with focal motor seizures secondary to previous TB meningitis."

Several specific epilepsy syndromes deserve particular attention in pediatric practice. West Syndrome, also known as infantile spasm, usually starts in infancy and presents with brief jerks of head and trunk flexion lasting 1 to 2 seconds, with the limbs flexing or extending, usually followed by crying. EEG shows a characteristic pattern called hypsarrhythmia. Delayed treatment has worse neurological outcome, so urgent referral is essential.

Childhood Absence Epilepsy accounts for 5 to 10% of all epilepsy in children. A distinctive feature of this syndrome is that seizures are precipitated by hyperventilation, which can be used diagnostically.

Rolandic Epilepsy occurs in children aged 3 to 13 years. Seizures start during or soon after sleep with facial twitching or grimacing while the child remains conscious, but seizures can then secondarily generalize with a tonic-clonic seizure. Sleep EEG shows bilateral centrotemporal spikes, which are characteristic.

Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy is an important syndrome occurring in the age range of 10 to 16 years.

### Antiepileptic Drug Therapy: General Principles

The decision to start anticonvulsant therapy should be made after two or more seizures have occurred and after careful consideration of the diagnosis. Before deciding to treat, the risk of side effects must always be weighed against the benefits of seizure control. Once the decision to treat is made, it is reassuring that 80% of children with epilepsy can be controlled with one drug, making monotherapy the initial approach.

During treatment, ongoing monitoring is crucial. Maintain a seizure diary to track frequency and nature of breakthrough seizures. Perform thorough neurodevelopmental assessment to identify any developmental delay that might be related to seizures or drug effects. Assess growth and nutritional status, as many antiepileptic drugs can affect these parameters.

An important long-term consideration is that most antiepileptic drugs increase the risk of osteoporosis. The "START LOW, GO SLOW" principle is fundamental to prescribing anticonvulsant drugs: start with low doses and increase gradually to minimize side effects. Educating the patient and family is essential. Provide a patient information leaflet, address practicalities such as schooling and lunch-time doses, plan for high-risk activities such as swimming, develop a seizure management plan with the caregiver and child, emphasize treatment adherence and the importance of using a seizure diary, and warn about common and serious side effects of antiepileptic drugs.

Holistic management extends beyond pharmacotherapy. Address associated behavioral problems and learning disability by utilizing educational psychology and occupational therapy services. Connect families with local support groups, such as Epilepsy South Africa.

### Sodium Valproate

Sodium valproate is a first-line agent for generalized epilepsy. However, it is contraindicated in childhood absence epilepsy and juvenile myoclonic epilepsy, despite these conditions being generalized epilepsies. Sodium valproate should be avoided if possible in patients with hepatic impairment, inborn errors of metabolism, or those receiving concomitant anti-retroviral therapy. An important drug interaction is that sodium valproate raises the blood level of AZT and also interacts with EFV (efavirenz).

The starting dose of sodium valproate is 10 mg/kg/day, divided into 3 doses for syrup preparations or 2 divided doses for tablets. The therapeutic dose range is 25 to 40 mg/kg/day. Side effects of sodium valproate include increased appetite and weight gain, tremor, ataxia, hepatotoxicity, thrombocytopenia, and teratogenicity with risk of neural tube defects in offspring.

### Carbamazepine

Carbamazepine is a first-line agent for focal seizures and for some forms of generalized epilepsy. It has significant drug interactions: certain antibiotics such as erythromycin interact with carbamazepine, as do anti-retroviral drugs. The starting dose of carbamazepine is 5 mg/kg/day, divided into 2 doses for both syrup and tablet preparations. The therapeutic dose range is 10 to 20 mg/kg/day. Side effects include ataxia, drowsiness, confusion, agitation, rashes, diplopia, thrombocytopenia, and the rare but serious Stevens-Johnson syndrome.

### Status Epilepticus Management

The cardinal principle in managing status epilepticus is to STOP THE SEIZURE EARLY. The longer a seizure lasts, the more difficult it becomes to control, and the greater the risk of complications including permanent neurological damage, systemic complications, and mortality.

**STEP 1: Initial Resuscitation.** Ensure that the patient has a patent airway. Administer 100% oxygen by non-rebreather face mask and monitor oxygen saturation. Assess cardiovascular status, attach appropriate monitors, and obtain intravenous access. Check blood glucose immediately. If glucose is low, administer 5 ml/kg of 10% dextrose intravenously and start an infusion of 5% dextrose with 0.9% saline.

**STEP 2: Abort the Seizure.** If the seizure has not spontaneously stopped within 5 minutes, proceed to benzodiazepine administration. Three benzodiazepines are appropriate choices, and one should be selected:

Lorazepam is given at a dose of 0.1 mg/kg intravenously or intraosseously over a few minutes, with a maximum dose of 4 mg.

Alternatively, midazolam may be used at a dose of 0.2 to 0.3 mg/kg over a few minutes, with a maximum dose of 7.5 mg.

Diazepam is another option, given at 0.3 mg/kg intravenously or intraosseously over a few minutes, with a maximum dose of 10 mg.

If intravenous access is not available, alternative routes include rectal diazepam at 0.5 mg/kg with a maximum dose of 20 mg, buccal midazolam at 0.5 mg/kg, or lorazepam given intramuscularly at 0.1 mg/kg.

**STEP 3: Benzodiazepine Repeat.** If seizures do not stop after 10 minutes of the initial benzodiazepine dose, or if they reappear within 1 hour after stopping, repeat the benzodiazepine exactly as given in STEP 2.

**STEP 4a: Phenytoin.** If seizures continue after 10 minutes of benzodiazepine therapy, administer phenytoin at a dose of 20 mg/kg in 5 ml/kg of normal saline, given intravenously over 30 minutes. However, if the patient is already on oral phenytoin at baseline, omit this step and proceed directly to STEP 4b. During phenytoin infusion, careful monitoring is essential: watch closely for cardiac arrhythmias, hypotension, and bradycardia. If any of these complications occur, stop the infusion immediately and contact the pediatric intensive care unit.

**STEP 4b: Phenobarbitone.** If seizures do not stop after 10 minutes of phenytoin infusion, or if the patient was already on oral phenytoin at baseline (requiring bypass of STEP 4a), administer phenobarbitone. Phenobarbitone is the drug of choice in children younger than 2 years with status epilepticus. The dose is 20 mg/kg given intravenously or intraosseously over 5 to 10 minutes.

**STEP 5: Phenobarbitone Repeat.** If the child remains convulsing 30 minutes after starting either phenobarbitone or phenytoin, repeat phenobarbitone at a reduced dose of 10 mg/kg intravenously or intraosseously over 5 to 10 minutes. If the child is still convulsing after a further 10 minutes, repeat phenobarbitone again at 10 mg/kg intravenously or intraosseously over 5 to 10 minutes. After this final repeat dose, if seizures have not been controlled, telephone the pediatric intensive care unit immediately for further management of what has now become refractory status epilepticus.

The timing of progression through these steps is critical. Benzodiazepine repeat is triggered if seizures do not stop after 10 minutes of the initial dose or if they reappear within 1 hour. Progression from benzodiazepines to phenytoin is triggered if seizures continue after 10 minutes of benzodiazepine therapy. Progression from phenytoin to phenobarbitone is triggered if seizures do not stop after 10 minutes of phenytoin infusion. Finally, pediatric intensive care unit referral is indicated for continued seizures after STEP 5, representing a status epilepticus episode lasting more than 30 minutes from initiation of treatment.
