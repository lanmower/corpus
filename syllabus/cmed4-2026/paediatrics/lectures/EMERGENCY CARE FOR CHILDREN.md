# EMERGENCY CARE FOR CHILDREN

## Lecture Notes

### Overview of Emergency Care

Emergency care for children comprises four main components working in sequence: first, triage to find the children who need emergency care; second, provision of emergency care through resuscitation and life support; third, provision of post-resuscitation care; and fourth, stabilization and transport. Triage is not a diagnostic process but rather a critical sorting mechanism designed to quickly identify life-threatening problems and prioritize which children need immediate intervention. The goal of triage is to be quick and accurate in finding life-threatening emergencies.

Child mortality in South Africa remains a significant public health concern. Current estimates show high variation depending on the source: the United Nations estimates 69 per 1000 births, the South African Actuarial Society estimates 76 per 1000 births, while the Millennium Development Goal (MDG) 4 target aims for 20 per 1000 live births. In Area 2 data spanning 2005 to the present, almost one third of children die within the first 24 hours of admission (total deaths recorded: 4564), underscoring the importance of rapid identification and treatment of critical conditions in the emergency setting.

### Triage Systems: ETAT and 3TPR-MOB

The ETAT system provides a structured framework for emergency triage, with each letter representing a distinct phase: E for Emergency, T for Triage, A for Assessment, and T for Treatment. This systematic approach ensures that emergency identification, sorting, evaluation, and intervention occur in a logical sequence.

The 3TPR-MOB system is a complementary mnemonic that helps identify priority signs during triage. The "3T" group includes Tiny baby (defined as less than 3 months old), Temperature (greater than 38 degrees Celsius, assessed as "hot to touch"), and Trauma. The "3P" group covers Pallor (severe pallor assessed by comparing the child's palm with the mother's palm, indicating severe anaemia), Poisoning, and Pain (severe). The "3R" group captures Respiratory distress (assessed by clinical judgment and experience), Restlessness (the child is continuously irritable, crying, and inconsolable—corresponding to V on the AVPU scale), and Referral (urgent). Finally, MOB identifies Malnutrition, Oedema, and Burns as additional priority concerns.

### Assessment Frameworks: ABCC-C-D and AVPU

The ABCC-C-D assessment system provides a comprehensive checklist for identifying emergency conditions in pediatric patients. It evaluates:
- **A (Airway)**: Patency and protection of the airway
- **B (Breathing)**: Respiratory effort and adequacy
- **C (Circulation)**: Perfusion status and cardiac output
- **C (Coma)**: Level of consciousness
- **C (Convulsion)**: Presence of seizure activity
- **D (Dehydration)**: Fluid status and signs of hypovolemia

The AVPU scale assesses consciousness level with four categories: A (Alert), V (responds to Voice), P (responds to Pain), and U (Unresponsive). Coma is defined as a state where the child responds only to pain (P) or is unresponsive (U). Recognition of these levels is critical for determining urgency of intervention.

### Vital Signs and Clinical Red Flags

Capillary refill time is a key indicator of peripheral perfusion and shock status. Normal capillary refill is less than 2 seconds; capillary refill greater than 2 seconds indicates poor circulation and shock. Additional signs of poor circulation include cold hands, weak and fast pulse, and the combination of these signs suggests significant hemodynamic compromise.

Temperature greater than 38 degrees Celsius is considered a priority sign and warrants close observation for serious infection, particularly in very young infants. Severe respiratory distress is characterized by fast breathing, severe chest-indrawing, grunting, difficulty feeding, and signs of tiredness—features that demand immediate emergency intervention. Airway obstruction presents with distinctive signs including stridor, snoring, grunting, or noisy breathing with secretions, any of which requires urgent airway management.

Severe dehydration is identified by three cardinal signs: lethargy or unconsciousness, sunken eyes, and skin pinch that goes back very slowly (skin turgor loss). These signs indicate significant fluid loss and potential shock. Any child with ANY sign of ABCC-C-D abnormality should immediately receive emergency treatment without delay.

### Priority Signs and Triage Categories

Triage personnel must distinguish between emergency signs and priority signs, as this distinction determines urgency of care and queue placement. Emergency signs represent immediate life threats; priority signs indicate high-risk conditions that require close monitoring and prompt evaluation but may not require immediate resuscitation.

The triage categories themselves are simple but critical: **E (Emergency)** means the child must be taken immediately to the emergency treatment area for resuscitation and life support; **P (Priority)** means the child should be placed at the front of the queue for rapid evaluation; and **Q (Queue)** means the child has no emergency or priority signs and can wait their turn for routine care.

Assessment of the respiratory priority sign requires clinical judgment and experience—it identifies children with concerning respiratory patterns but not the severe chest-indrawing that would constitute an emergency sign. The restlessness priority sign (assessed on AVPU as V—responds to Voice) represents a child who is continuously irritable, crying, and inconsolable, a behavioral indicator of significant systemic illness.

Severe pallor is assessed by direct comparison of the child's palm with the mother's palm; if the child's palm is markedly paler, this suggests severe anaemia warranting priority assessment. Similarly, trauma in any form should elevate a child to priority triage even if other signs are absent. Urgent referral from another facility should always result in priority triage classification.

### Final Pathways to Death

Understanding the mechanisms by which children die in emergency settings helps guide assessment and intervention. Six major final pathways to death are recognized:

**Respiratory Obstruction**: Caused by anatomical blockage of the airway, common examples include croup, asthma, and bronchiolitis. These conditions prevent air from reaching the lungs and require urgent airway management.

**Respiratory Depression**: Occurs when the brain fails to drive adequate breathing efforts, seen in post-ictal state (immediately after seizures), meningitis, and poisoning. These children may have patent airways but insufficient ventilation.

**Alveolar or Chest Wall Failure**: Occurs when the lungs themselves or the chest wall cannot expand adequately to enable gas exchange. Examples include pulmonary contusion, flail chest, and diaphragmatic hernia (either traumatic or congenital).

**Fluid Loss**: Results from hypovolemia due to loss of intravascular volume, commonly from gastroenteritis with severe diarrhea or vomiting, and from internal hemorrhage associated with femoral or pelvic fractures.

**Fluid Maldistribution**: Occurs when fluids leave the intravascular space despite normal total body water, as seen in meningococcal shock (with endotoxin-induced capillary leak) and anaphylaxis.

**Heart Failure**: Occurs when the heart cannot maintain adequate cardiac output, either from direct myocardial toxicity (as with local anaesthetic toxicity) or from structural abnormalities (congenital heart disease).

### Malnutrition Assessment

Two distinct patterns of protein-energy malnutrition warrant urgent identification and management in the emergency setting.

**Kwashiorkor** presents with bilateral foot oedema, which may be accompanied by hair and skin changes. The oedema involves both feet and indicates a particular form of protein deficiency despite caloric intake. Recognition of these changes is critical because the child may not appear severely wasted despite serious nutritional compromise.

**Marasmus** represents severe wasting and is readily visible when rapidly inspecting specific body areas: the arms, legs, and chest should be examined for signs of severe wasting. Unlike kwashiorkor, marasmus presents with visible muscle and fat loss and a "skin and bones" appearance. Both conditions warrant priority triage and nutritional rehabilitation.

### ETAT Workflow and Implementation

Triage should occur as soon as the child arrives at the facility, even before reception and administrative processes. Triage can be performed at any location where a child might present: the outpatients queue, the emergency room, the ward, or the emergency treatment area itself.

Importantly, ALL STAFF are trained and empowered to perform triage—not just nurses and doctors, but also clerks, gatekeepers, and cleaners. This universal responsibility ensures that no critically ill child is missed due to administrative barriers.

The ETAT workflow follows a systematic process: First, look for emergency signs and move any child with these signs immediately to the emergency treatment area, treating any signs found while there. Second, look for priority signs in the remaining children. Third, place all priority patients at the front of the queue so they are evaluated promptly. Fourth, move on to the next patient in the queue. This four-step workflow ensures that emergency cases are identified and treated first, followed by priority cases, while non-urgent cases proceed through normal queue order.

### Emergency Treatment Area Resources

The emergency treatment area must be properly equipped to provide immediate life support. At minimum, it requires a dedicated room or trolley with immediate access to drugs and medical supplies necessary for emergency resuscitation and life support. This dedicated space ensures that critically ill children receive treatment without delay and that equipment and medications are available when needed.

### Scenario-Based Learning: E/P/Q Classification Examples

Applying triage classification to realistic clinical scenarios helps consolidate learning and develop pattern recognition skills necessary for rapid decision-making in the emergency setting.

**Scenario 1**: A 3-year-old girl presents hot to touch but with cold hands, capillary refill of 1.5 seconds, alert, with 4 loose stools per day and skin pinch taking 3 seconds to return. **Classification: P (Priority)**. This child shows signs of dehydration (prolonged skin turgor) with possible early shock (cold hands), though capillary refill remains normal. Priority triage allows prompt fluid resuscitation before frank shock develops.

**Scenario 2**: A 4-year-old boy had a convulsion 1 hour ago, now presents with fast breathing, no cyanosis or severe distress, is very hot, and responds appropriately to questions but has no diarrhea or vomiting. **Classification: P (Priority)**. Recent febrile seizure with fever and altered consciousness warrants priority triage due to high risk of recurrent seizure and underlying serious infection.

**Scenario 3**: A 1-year-old had seizures both at home and outside the clinic, is now unconscious with wet noisy breathing, drooling, and cyanosis. **Classification: E (Emergency)**. This child meets multiple emergency criteria: coma (unresponsive), airway compromise (drooling and wet breathing), cyanosis, and ongoing or recent seizure activity. Immediate emergency treatment is essential.

**Scenario 4**: A 2-year-old male is actively convulsing at presentation. **Classification: E (Emergency)**. Active seizure is an absolute emergency requiring immediate seizure management and airway protection.

**Scenario 5**: A 9-month-old girl presents choking on a bead from an old necklace. **Classification: E (Emergency)**. Foreign body aspiration is an immediate airway emergency requiring rapid intervention to prevent complete obstruction and hypoxia.

**Scenario 6**: A 3-year-old boy presents pale and floppy, with difficulty breathing, 3-day cough history, weight 14 kg, fast breathing with severe chest-indrawing, patent airway, but alert. **Classification: E (Emergency)**. Severe respiratory distress (severe chest-indrawing) combined with signs of shock (pallor, decreased responsiveness/"floppy") suggest severe pneumonia with sepsis, requiring immediate emergency care.

**Scenario 7**: A 4-month-old baby presents with fever, rapid breathing, refusing breastfeeds, 2 episodes of vomiting and diarrhea, weight 5 kg, cold hands, capillary refill 6 seconds (>2s), fast weak femoral pulse, but no chest-indrawing or abnormal lung sounds. **Classification: E (Emergency)**. This infant is in shock (cold hands, markedly prolonged capillary refill of 6 seconds, weak rapid pulse) with signs of sepsis (fever, vomiting, diarrhea). This represents septic shock in a young infant, requiring immediate resuscitation.

**Scenario 8**: An 18-month-old boy has been unwell for 2 days with abdominal pain and fast breathing, weight 11 kg, fine airway sounds, no chest-indrawing, no diarrhea. **Classification: Q (Queue)**. While fever and tachypnea are present, the child shows no signs of severe distress, clear airway, and no respiratory compromise. This child can safely wait in queue for routine evaluation.

**Scenario 9**: A 10-week-old baby will not suckle, is crying a lot, very hot, weight 3.5 kg, with normal airway, breathing, and circulation, no diarrhea or dehydration signs. **Classification: P (Priority)**. This tiny baby (less than 3 months old) with fever and irritability represents high risk of serious occult infection (meningitis, bacteremia, urinary tract infection) and warrants priority assessment despite appearing well otherwise.

**Scenario 10**: A 14-month-old girl has been sleeping all day and is irritable when awoken, rouses only to loud voice and arm shaking (V on AVPU—responds to voice only), has warm hands but is pale, no diarrhea or vomiting, but shows deep breathing with lower chest-wall indrawing. **Classification: P (Priority)**. This child demonstrates altered consciousness (responds to voice only = V on AVPU, approaching coma), respiratory distress with chest-indrawing, and signs of shock (pallor), suggesting possible sepsis with early septic shock.

**Scenario 11**: A 1-year-old girl has a 2-day history of diarrhea and vomiting, weight 6.5 kg, is restless and irritable, airway and breathing are normal, hands are warm, AVPU is V (responds to voice), skin pinch takes 4 seconds to return, sunken eyes are present, and severe malnutrition is noted. **Classification: P (Priority)**. This child demonstrates severe dehydration (sunken eyes, prolonged skin turgor) combined with severe malnutrition. The combination of dehydration and malnutrition increases risk significantly and warrants priority assessment.

**Scenario 12**: A 4-year-old boy presents with sore ear, is well-appearing and playful. **Classification: Q (Queue)**. No emergency or priority signs are present; this is a routine, non-urgent presentation.

**Scenario 13**: A 6-year-old boy presents with chest pain, breathing is normal, child is not shocked, and is conversant. **Classification: Q (Queue)**. Despite the concern raised by chest pain complaint, the absence of emergency or priority signs and a well-appearing child allow routine queue placement.

**Scenario 14**: A 7-week-old boy presents with fever for 1 day, is looking around appropriately, breathing is normal, with no signs of shock or dehydration. **Classification: P (Priority)**. This tiny baby (less than 3 months old) with fever warrants priority triage despite appearing well, due to the high risk of serious infection at this age.

**Scenario 15**: A 7-year-old girl with asthma presents with mild wheeze, is bright and alert. **Classification: Q (Queue)**. Mild wheeze with no severe respiratory distress and an alert, well-appearing child indicates routine, non-emergency asthma exacerbation.

**Scenario 16**: An 18-month-old girl presents with cough and runny nose, has a barking cough and stridor, and is agitated. **Classification: E (Emergency)**. Stridor with the characteristic barking cough of croup, combined with agitation indicating respiratory compromise, requires emergency airway management.

**Scenario 17**: A 2-year-old girl will not walk (1-day history), is crying but consolable, breathing is normal, and she is not shocked. **Classification: Q (Queue)**. No emergency or priority signs are evident; consolability and normal vital function allow routine queue placement.

This comprehensive overview provides the foundation for safe, systematic triage and emergency care of pediatric patients, enabling rapid identification of life-threatening conditions and appropriate prioritization of care resources.
