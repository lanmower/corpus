# 2024 CYANOSIS

## Lecture Notes

### Definition and Pathophysiology

Cyanosis is a bluish discolouration of the skin and mucous membranes that results from two primary mechanisms. The first occurs when the concentration of deoxygenated haemoglobin is excessive in blood. The second mechanism involves increased concentration of abnormal haemoglobin derivatives, such as methaemoglobinemia or sulphaemoglobinemia, in the superficial blood vessels. Understanding these fundamental pathways is essential for recognising and managing cyanotic presentations in clinical practice.

The underlying pathophysiology of central cyanosis involves five key mechanisms. The first is hypoventilation of the alveoli, where inadequate air exchange leads to insufficient oxygenation. Second, diffusion impairment occurs when barriers prevent oxygen from crossing the alveolar-capillary membrane. Third, ventilation-perfusion mismatch develops when areas of the lung are ventilated but not perfused, or vice versa. Fourth, right-to-left shunting at intracardiac or intrapulmonary vessels allows deoxygenated blood to bypass gas exchange entirely. Fifth, haemoglobinopathy that limits oxygen transport, such as methaemoglobin accumulation, impairs the blood's ability to carry oxygen regardless of how much is available.

### Central Cyanosis

Central cyanosis presents as bluish discolouration of the highly vascularised areas, specifically the lips, tongue, and mucous membranes. Unlike peripheral cyanosis, which is caused by poor circulation, central cyanosis reflects a fundamental problem with oxygenation. The arterial oxygen saturation (SO₂) is reduced in central cyanosis, while cardiac output and temperature typically remain normal. This pattern helps distinguish central from peripheral causes and guides investigation toward cardiopulmonary pathology.

The differential diagnosis of central cyanosis is systematically approached using the PALMS mnemonic. P represents polycythaemia, an increase in red blood cell mass that can present with cyanosis due to high concentrations of deoxygenated haemoglobin. A stands for altitude, where reduced atmospheric oxygen pressure leads to lower arterial saturation. L encompasses lung disease, the most common category. M includes methaemaglobin and metabolic disorders such as hypoglycaemia. S represents shunt, referring to intracardiac or intrapulmonary right-to-left shunting of blood.

Pulmonary causes of central cyanosis subdivide into three categories. Alveolar hypoventilation results from central nervous system depression caused by asphyxia, sedation, or intracranial pathology. Neuromuscular diseases such as Guillain-Barré syndrome or Duchenne muscular dystrophy impair respiratory muscle function. Airway obstruction, exemplified by choanal stenosis, prevents adequate air entry to the lungs. Ventilation-perfusion mismatch occurs from both extrinsic compression of the lungs—including pneumothorax, pleural effusion, and thoracic dystrophies—and intrinsic compression from conditions like pneumonia, atelectasis, hyaline membrane disease, and asthma. Diffusion impairment, the third pulmonary mechanism, develops in pulmonary oedema and bronchopulmonary dysplasia, where the alveolar-capillary interface is thickened or fluid-filled.

Cardiac causes of central cyanosis involve structural heart defects. Decreased pulmonary blood flow occurs in tetralogy of Fallot and tricuspid atresia, both conditions in which blood bypasses the lungs due to right-to-left shunting. Increased pulmonary blood flow with systemic desaturation occurs in transposition of the great vessels, total anomalous pulmonary venous return, and truncus arteriosus, where mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood occurs. Severe heart failure can present with cyanosis, particularly in coarctation of the aorta and hypoplastic left heart syndrome, where global perfusion is severely compromised.

Haemoglobinopathy as a cause of cyanosis includes methaemoglobinaemia, which may be congenital or secondary to toxin exposure, and sulphaemoglobinaemia, which is secondary to toxin exposure. These conditions reduce the oxygen-carrying capacity of blood or prevent oxygen binding to haemoglobin, resulting in tissue hypoxia and cyanotic appearance despite potentially normal oxygen saturation readings on standard pulse oximetry.

### Peripheral Cyanosis

Peripheral cyanosis, in contrast to central cyanosis, involves bluish discolouration of the distal extremities only, sparing the lips, tongue, and mucous membranes. This localised presentation arises from increased extraction of oxygen from sluggish movement of blood through the capillaries. The systemic arterial oxygen saturation remains normal, indicating that the lung and heart are functioning adequately to oxygenate blood; rather, peripheral tissues are extracting more oxygen due to poor perfusion. Common mechanisms include peripheral vasoconstriction and polycythaemia.

The causes of peripheral cyanosis are remembered by the COLD mnemonic. C stands for cold exposure, where peripheral vasoconstriction reduces blood flow to extremities. O represents obstruction to blood flow, whether from thrombosis, embolism, or external compression. L encompasses left ventricular failure and shock states, where cardiac output is severely reduced. D stands for decreased cardiac output from any cause, including arrhythmias or severe systemic disease.

### Distinguishing Central from Peripheral Cyanosis

Clinical discrimination between central and peripheral cyanosis is crucial for narrowing the differential diagnosis. The area of involvement differs markedly: central cyanosis is generalised, while peripheral cyanosis is localised to the distal extremities. Tongue involvement distinguishes central (involved) from peripheral (not involved) cyanosis. Temperature is warm in central cyanosis but cold in peripheral cyanosis. Clubbing, a chronic sign of hypoxaemia, is usually present in central cyanosis but absent in peripheral cyanosis.

The response to oxygen supplementation differs significantly. In central cyanosis due to pulmonary causes, oxygen supplementation produces improvement in the cyanotic appearance as oxygenation is restored. In peripheral cyanosis, oxygen supplementation produces no improvement because the problem is circulatory, not oxygenation. Warming the affected parts improves peripheral cyanosis by reversing peripheral vasoconstriction, whereas warming does not improve central cyanosis. The underlying mechanism differs: central cyanosis reflects reduction in oxygen saturation, while peripheral cyanosis reflects reduction in blood flow. Capillary refill time, measured by pressing the skin and observing how quickly colour returns, is less than 2 seconds in central cyanosis but greater than 2 seconds in peripheral cyanosis.

### Factors Affecting Detection of Cyanosis

The clinical recognition of cyanosis is influenced by several patient and environmental factors. Haemoglobin concentration plays a critical role; cyanosis becomes more apparent at high levels of saturation because more deoxygenated haemoglobin is present in absolute terms. Conversely, fetal haemoglobin makes cyanosis more apparent at low levels of saturation. Skin pigmentation significantly affects the visibility of cyanosis; in patients with darker skin pigmentation, cyanosis is less apparent due to increased melanin masking the subtle colour change. These factors mean that absence of visible cyanosis does not exclude significant hypoxaemia, and that cyanosis may appear with relatively modest degrees of desaturation in some patients.

### Investigation of Cyanosis

A systematic approach to investigating cyanosis begins with a saturation monitor (pulse oximetry) to quantify oxygen saturation, though recognising its limitations in methaemoglobinaemia. Haemoglobin or haematocrit measurement is essential because cyanosis depends on absolute deoxygenated haemoglobin concentration, not saturation percentage. Arterial blood gas analysis provides definitive assessment of oxygenation, ventilation, and acid-base status, distinguishing hypoxaemic from non-hypoxaemic causes. The hyperoxia test is a pivotal diagnostic tool: the child breathes 100% oxygen, and if saturation rises significantly (typically to above 90-95%), a pulmonary cause is likely; if saturation remains low despite supplemental oxygen, a cardiac shunt or methaemoglobinaemia is suggested. Septic screen including blood cultures and inflammatory markers helps exclude infection as an underlying cause. Electrocardiogram and echocardiography assess cardiac structure and function, identifying congenital heart defects or cardiac dysfunction. Haemoglobin electrophoresis is performed when haemoglobinopathy is suspected, confirming diagnoses such as methaemoglobinaemia or sulphaemoglobinaemia.

### Management of Cyanosis

Treatment of cyanosis is pathophysiologically directed toward the underlying cause. The first principle is to treat the primary condition causing hypoxaemia or poor perfusion. Simultaneously, efforts must be made to optimise oxygenation through oxygen supplementation, airway management, or ventilatory support as indicated. Symptomatic management addresses comfort and prevents complications of chronic hypoxaemia, such as polycythaemia or clubbing progression. In acute presentations, rapid assessment and stabilisation takes precedence, while in chronic conditions, long-term management focuses on preventing complications and optimising quality of life.
