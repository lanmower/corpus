# ETAT - Dehydration

## Lecture Notes

### ETAT Integration and Triage Placement

The ETAT (Emergency Triage, Assessment and Treatment) algorithm provides a systematic approach to identify and manage dehydration in children. The framework uses the 3TPR-MOB priority assessment system: Tiny Baby, Temperature, Trauma, Pallor, Poisoning, Pain, Respiratory Distress, Restlessness, Referral Urgency, Malnutrition, Oedema, and Burns. When a child presents with diarrhoea, dehydration assessment occurs after evaluation of emergency signs via the Circulation/Coma/Convulsions (CCC) pathway. For children without diarrhoea, the triage proceeds directly to 3TPR-MOB priority assessment.

Emergency signs requiring immediate intervention include hands that are not warm, capillary refill time greater than 2 seconds, weak or rapid pulse, coma, and active convulsions. These signs supersede all other assessments and trigger immediate management of shock and stabilization before dehydration-specific therapy.

### Clinical Assessment of Severe Dehydration

Assessment for severe dehydration in children with diarrhoea occurs after the CCC (Circulation/Coma/Convulsions) assessment is completed. The three key clinical signs that indicate severe dehydration are: lethargy, sunken eyes, and a skin pinch that takes longer than 2 seconds to return to normal. The skin pinch assessment specifically requires that the skin fold, when pinched, remains elevated rather than quickly returning to normal position.

Concurrent assessment for severe malnutrition is essential during the dehydration evaluation. Signs that indicate severe malnutrition assessment is needed include visible severe wasting and oedema of both feet. Children with severe malnutrition present a unique clinical challenge because they have a precarious metabolic and circulatory status that significantly alters the approach to rehydration.

Clinical decision-making in severe dehydration management requires consideration of multiple domains: differentiation between shock and severe dehydration, selection of fluid type, determination of administration route (oral versus intravenous), and understanding the distinction between diarrhoeal loss and plasma equivalent fluid replacement. Blood glucose must be checked in all children with severe malnutrition and dehydration, as hypoglycaemia below 3 mmol/L is a serious complication requiring immediate treatment with 5 mL/kg of 10% dextrose.

### Oral Rehydration (Plan A)

Oral rehydration with oral rehydration solution (ORS) is the preferred initial fluid route for mild to moderate dehydration because it is cheaper, safer, and more physiologic than intravenous therapy when the child is able to tolerate oral intake. ORS should be given as soon as possible after assessment of dehydration.

The ORS dosing regimen for mild-moderate dehydration (Plan A) is 5 mL/kg administered every 15 minutes. After each diarrhoeal stool, an additional 10 mL/kg of ORS is given. Reassessment of the child's hydration status occurs after 1-2 hours of ORS therapy. If the child is rehydrated after this interval, maintenance Plan A fluids are continued. If oral rehydration fails—indicated by persistent vomiting, inability to tolerate oral intake, or inadequate clinical response—the child is escalated to intravenous or nasogastric fluid therapy.

### Intravenous Fluid Therapy

When intravenous access is obtained, the fluid composition used for severe dehydration treatment is 1/2 Dextrose Saline (1/2DS) with 5% dextrose in water. The infusion rate and duration depend on whether severe malnutrition is present.

**For severe dehydration without malnutrition:** IV fluid is administered at 20 mL/kg/hour for 4 hours. Reassessment for effectiveness and for signs of shock occurs within 1-2 hours of starting therapy. If shock persists after initial fluid administration, the rate may be increased and senior advice should be sought. Antibiotics should be considered based on clinical presentation, and blood glucose should be checked.

**For severe dehydration WITH severe malnutrition:** The infusion rate is much slower and longer: 10 mL/kg/hour for 8 hours. This slower approach is critical because rapid rehydration in severely malnourished children can precipitate metabolic collapse from sudden fluid and electrolyte shifts, leading to cardiovascular instability. Sodium handling requires careful management in these children, as their requirements differ from non-malnourished children. Blood glucose must be monitored closely and hypoglycaemia (below 3 mmol/L) treated immediately with 5 mL/kg of 10% dextrose intravenously.

### Alternative Routes: Nasogastric and Intraosseous

When intravenous access cannot be obtained, two alternative routes are available.

**Nasogastric (NG) fluid therapy:** ORS is delivered via nasogastric tube at 20 mL/kg/hour for 6 hours. Alternatively, continuous infusion or administration of 5 mL/kg every 15 minutes may be preferred in some settings. NG infusion is indicated when IV access has failed and when oral or ORS is not tolerated or is failing due to persistent vomiting or failed oral trial. Weight-based example: A 3.1 kg child requiring NG therapy receives 20 mL/kg/hour = 20 × 3.1 = 62 mL/hour (weight band 60 mL/hour). A 10 kg child receives 20 × 10 = 200 mL/hour (weight band 240 mL/hour).

**Intraosseous (IO) fluids:** When neither IV nor NG access is feasible, intraosseous infusion at 10 mL/kg/hour for 4 hours is an alternative route for delivering rehydration fluids.

### Drip Rate Calculations and Weight-Based Examples

Accurate calculation of drip rates is essential for safe fluid delivery, particularly when electronic infusion devices are unavailable and manual roller controllers must be used.

**Conversion formulas for manual drip control:**
- For a 60 drops/mL dropper: Drops per minute = mL per hour
- For a 15 drops/mL dropper: Drops per minute = mL per hour divided by 4

**Weight-based clinical examples:**

*ORS administration (5 mL/kg/15 minutes):* A 7.3 kg child with severe dehydration requires 5 × 7.3 = 36.5 mL/15 minutes. Using a weight band, this rounds to 40 mL/15 minutes for practical administration.

*IV therapy without malnutrition (20 mL/kg/hour × 4 hours):* An 11 kg child receives 20 × 11 = 220 mL/hour, which rounds to a weight band of 240 mL/hour. Using a 60 drops/mL dropper, this equals 240 drops/min. Using a 15 drops/mL dropper, this equals 240 ÷ 4 = 60 drops/min.

*IV therapy with malnutrition (10 mL/kg/hour × 8 hours):* An 11 kg child receives 10 × 11 = 110 mL/hour, rounded to a weight band of 80 mL/hour. Using a 60 drops/mL dropper, this equals 80 drops/min. Using a 15 drops/mL dropper, this equals 80 ÷ 4 = 20 drops/min.

*Nasogastric fluid delivery (20 mL/kg/hour × 6 hours):* A 10 kg child receives 20 × 10 = 200 mL/hour, rounded to a weight band of 240 mL/hour. Using a 60 drops/mL dropper, this equals 200-240 drops/min. Using a 15 drops/mL dropper, this equals 50-60 drops/min.

### Manual Drip Controllers: Limitations and Safety Considerations

Manual roller controllers have several inherent limitations that affect their reliability and safety for fluid delivery, particularly in resource-constrained settings.

**Mechanical limitations:** Manually set roller clamps tend to slow down progressively over time, requiring frequent monitoring and adjustment to maintain the target drip rate. The flow rate depends critically on the height of the IV bag above the infusion site—greater height increases the rate, and changes in bag height necessitate controller readjustment.

**Positional factors:** The position of the child's limb at the infusion site can affect flow by occluding vessels or altering vascular resistance, changing the infusion rate unpredictably. Vigilant physical inspection and regular rate checks are required throughout infusion.

**Safety hazards of unattended use:** Manual drip controllers cannot reliably be set and left unattended. They lack alarms for too-fast or too-slow flow, creating risk of catastrophic rapid bolus delivery or inadequate rehydration from under-infusion. A maximum bolus of less than 40 mL/kg must be enforced to prevent injury.

**Preparation safety for small children:** For children weighing less than 5 kg, 200 mL vacolitres must be prepared with caution. Before hanging, 100 mL should be removed from the vacollitre to limit the maximum possible bolus in case of accidental free-fall of fluid.

**Preferred delivery systems:** Rather than relying on manual roller controllers alone, burettes or sachet systems are the preferred choice for safer, more controlled rehydration, particularly in young children.

### Electronic Infusion Pumps and Equipment Standards

Electronic infusion pumps provide substantially greater safety than manual controllers. They allow reliable setting of flow in millilitres per hour, provide audible and visual alarms for flow deviation (too fast or too slow), and permit safe unattended infusion when properly programmed and monitored.

**Institutional priority:** Institutions should prioritize ensuring an adequate supply of electronic drip minders or syringe pumps with proper maintenance schedules. Reliance solely on manual controllers for paediatric rehydration represents a significant patient safety risk.

### Training and Skill Development

Competency in rehydration management requires practice with rate calculations and conversion formulas. **Oral rate drills** involve calculating ORS delivery rates—for example, millilitres per 15-minute interval—to ensure accurate dosing in children managed without intravenous access. **Nasogastric rate drills** similarly involve practice calculations for NG fluid delivery, converting millilitres per hour to drops per minute using the appropriate dropper calibration (60 or 15 drops/mL) for 20 mL/kg/hour regimens.
