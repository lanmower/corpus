# ETAT - Coma and Convulsions

## Lecture Notes

### Learning Objectives

The ETAT framework for managing children in coma and convulsions establishes four core competencies: (1) assess consciousness using the AVPU score to systematically evaluate mental status; (2) identify the child in coma by recognizing which AVPU states indicate loss of consciousness; (3) identify the child having convulsions by recognizing the characteristic clinical features; and (4) treat both coma and convulsions using evidence-based emergency protocols. These objectives guide the entire approach to these life-threatening presentations.

### Emergency Triage Signs (ETAT Framework)

ETAT identifies six life-threatening signs requiring immediate assessment and intervention. The mnemonic A-B-C-D emphasizes the priority sequence: **Airway** integrity must be established first, followed by **Breathing** adequacy, then **Circulation** status. Beyond these fundamentals, three additional emergency signs demand immediate recognition: **Coma**, **Convulsion**, and **Dehydration (severe)**. Any child presenting with one or more of these signs requires urgent triage and rapid treatment. The ETAT framework provides a standardized language that enables clinicians to quickly communicate severity and prioritize limited resources.

### Assessing Consciousness: The AVPU Score

The AVPU scale provides a rapid, reproducible method for assessing consciousness in the emergency setting. **Alert (A)** indicates the child is awake and aware, able to interact appropriately with their environment. **Voice response (V)** means the child does not spontaneously interact but will respond when called to by name or when verbal stimuli are applied. **Pain response (P)** indicates the child only responds to pain stimulation, such as pressure on a fingernail bed or sternal rub—no response occurs to voice alone. **Unresponsive (U)** describes the child who does not respond to voice and is also unresponsive to pain, either through shaking or painful stimuli.

Critically, **AVPU scores of P and U indicate coma**. A child scoring P (responding only to pain) or U (unresponsive) is unconscious and requires urgent airway management, investigation, and treatment. The P and U states represent deepening levels of impaired consciousness that place the child at severe risk for aspiration, brain swelling, and death.

### Clinical Presentation of Convulsions

A convulsion is characterized by a distinctive and unmistakable cluster of signs. The cardinal feature is a **sudden loss of consciousness** that occurs simultaneously with uncontrolled jerky movements of the limbs and/or face. Additional features may include stiffening (tonic contraction) of the arms and legs, involuntary twitching of the face, hands, or feet, abnormal eye movements (typically divergent or rolling), and brief episodes of apnoea during the tonic phase. The sudden onset, loss of consciousness, and involuntary motor activity together make convulsions clinically distinctive from other causes of altered behavior or movement.

**Status epilepticus** is defined as a seizure lasting longer than 5 minutes and represents a medical emergency. Prolonged seizures place the child at severe risk for multiple complications: brain swelling (cerebral edema), irreversible brain damage from neuronal cell death, aspiration of gastric contents with subsequent airway obstruction or aspiration pneumonia, hypoxia from respiratory compromise, and death. Any seizure lasting more than 5 minutes must be treated urgently with anticonvulsant medication.

### Management of the Child in Coma: Airway and Breathing

The first and most critical step in managing a comatose child is to establish and maintain a patent airway while ensuring adequate ventilation. Begin by **positioning the child appropriately**. For infants, use a **neutral head position**—avoid hyperextension of the head, which can paradoxically obstruct the infant airway. For older children, the **sniffing position** (mild extension of the head at the atlanto-occipital joint) optimizes airway diameter and breathing ease.

In any child where cervical spine injury is suspected (trauma history, mechanism of injury), **protect the cervical spine** during positioning maneuvers. Use the **jaw thrust** technique to open the airway without extending the neck—this forward displacement of the mandible lifts the base of the tongue away from the posterior pharynx. Gently **suction the oropharynx** to remove secretions, blood, or gastric contents that could obstruct breathing. **Administer oxygen via a well-fitting face mask** to achieve the highest oxygen concentration possible and prevent hypoxia during the critical early period.

Once immediate airway patency is assured, place the unconscious child in the **recovery position**—lying on their side with the lower arm supporting the head, upper leg flexed, and mouth slightly open. This position drains fluid from the mouth, prevents the tongue from falling back and obstructing the airway, and greatly reduces the risk of aspiration of vomitus should the child vomit.

### Management of the Child in Coma: Checking and Treating Hypoglycaemia

Immediately after securing the airway and breathing, **check blood glucose using Dextrostix** (rapid bedside glucose testing). Hypoglycaemia is defined as blood glucose **< 3 mmol/litre (< 55 mg/dl)** and is a common and treatable cause of altered consciousness and coma in children. The principle "**D-E-F-G: Don't Ever Forget Glucose**" emphasizes that missing hypoglycaemia can result in permanent brain damage while its treatment is simple, safe, and effective.

If the child is hypoglycaemic, **immediately administer IV glucose at a dose of 5 ml/kg of 10% glucose solution given as a rapid IV injection**. This rapidly raises blood glucose and may dramatically improve consciousness. Never delay treating hypoglycaemia while awaiting other investigations. Hypoglycaemia is the only metabolic emergency for which treatment should precede complete diagnosis.

### Management of Convulsions: The Three-Step Approach

The management of a convulsing child follows a systematic three-step approach. **Step 1: Manage the airway and breathing.** Position the child safely, suction gently to clear the airway, and provide oxygen via face mask. **Critically, do not place anything into the mouth**—this includes oral airways, tongue depressors, or fingers. Such objects can obstruct the airway, cause aspiration, or cause dental trauma. The child will not swallow their tongue; protection of the airway is accomplished through positioning and gentle suctioning, not by force-feeding objects into the mouth.

**Step 2: Check blood glucose immediately.** Use Dextrostix to measure blood glucose. If the glucose is < 3 mmol/l, **administer IV glucose at 5 ml/kg of 10% solution** rapidly by IV injection. Hypoglycaemia is a common cause of or contributor to convulsions in children, particularly those who are malnourished or have prolonged illnesses.

**Step 3: Stop the seizure with anticonvulsant medication.** Once airway, breathing, and glucose have been addressed, administer an anticonvulsant drug to terminate the seizure activity.

### First-Line Anticonvulsants

**Lorazepam** is the first choice anticonvulsant, a short-acting benzodiazepine given by the **IV route**. If IV access cannot be obtained, lorazepam may be given by **IM (intramuscular) or buccal routes**.

**Midazolam** is the second choice if lorazepam is unavailable. It has the advantage of multiple routes: **IV, IM, or buccal**. The buccal and IM routes are particularly valuable in settings where IV access is difficult or delayed.

**Diazepam** is the last choice of the benzodiazepine group, used only when both lorazepam and midazolam are unavailable. Diazepam may be given by **IV injection or rectal (PR) administration only**. Importantly, diazepam cannot be given by IM or buccal routes, which limits its utility in difficult IV access situations.

### Second-Line Anticonvulsants: When Benzodiazepines Fail

If the seizure continues despite appropriate benzodiazepine administration, **phenobarbitone is the drug of next choice**. This is a long-acting barbiturate that provides sustained seizure control but takes longer to achieve peak effect than benzodiazepines.

**Phenytoin is an alternative** if phenobarbitone is unavailable. Phenytoin must be given as an **IV infusion over 30 minutes** to allow for safe metabolism and to reduce the risk of cardiac arrhythmias. If during the infusion the child develops an arrhythmia, bradycardia, or hypotension, **immediately stop the phenytoin infusion**. Once the child is clinically settled, the infusion may be resumed at a slower rate, **infusing over 1 hour instead of 30 minutes**.

### Special Considerations in Neonatal Convulsions

Neonates (infants less than 4 weeks of age) have different anticonvulsant requirements than older children. **Phenobarbitone is the first-choice anticonvulsant for neonates** with convulsions, not the benzodiazepines used for older children.

Benzodiazepines carry a significant risk in neonates: they may cause respiratory depression and apnoea (temporary cessation of breathing), particularly in sick infants. **Benzodiazepines should not be used in neonates without ventilatory support**—that is, neonates must be intubated and on mechanical ventilation before benzodiazepines are administered. In neonatal convulsions where IV access is not immediately available, phenobarbitone remains the safest first-line agent.

### Vascular Access and Continued Seizure Activity

During convulsion management, establishing reliable vascular access is essential for administering medications and drawing blood for investigations. An IV line should be placed as soon as possible. If the child continues to convulse despite initial anticonvulsant therapy and IV access has not been achieved, an **intraosseous (IO) line should be placed** through the proximal tibia or humerus. This provides rapid access to the central circulation, allowing delivery of medications and fluids even when peripheral veins are unavailable or difficult to access. The IO line permits both blood drawing for laboratory investigations and administration of glucose and additional anticonvulsant medications.

### Post-Seizure Management and Monitoring

Once the seizure has stopped, the work is not complete. **Recheck A-B-C** (airway, breathing, circulation) to ensure these remain adequate, as post-seizure drowsiness and residual medication effects can compromise respiratory efforts. **Monitor closely for further seizures**—children who have one seizure in an acute illness are at high risk of recurrence.

**Treat for infection**, particularly meningitis, which is one of the most common causes of convulsions in children. A lumbar puncture to obtain cerebrospinal fluid for analysis and culture should be performed urgently (after ensuring the child is stable and after excluding contraindications such as signs of increased intracranial pressure or focal neurological signs).

**Recheck blood glucose** to ensure hypoglycaemia is not recurring or persisting, which would increase the risk of further seizures or brain damage. If glucose remains low, administer IV glucose again. **Provide comprehensive after-care** including monitoring, treatment of the underlying cause, nutritional support, and careful observation for complications.

### Summary of Management Priorities

The key to successful management of coma and convulsions in children is systematic application of ETAT principles. After establishing the ABC (Airway, Breathing, Circulation), immediately assess consciousness using AVPU and identify whether coma or convulsion is present. Remember **D-E-F-G: Don't Ever Forget Glucose**—check blood glucose with Dextrostix and treat hypoglycaemia immediately with 5 ml/kg of 10% IV glucose solution. For convulsions, administer first-line benzodiazepines (lorazepam, then midazolam, then diazepam) via the most accessible route. Know the second-line agents (phenobarbitone and phenytoin) for convulsions that do not respond to benzodiazepines. Remember that neonates require phenobarbitone as first-line therapy, not benzodiazepines. After the seizure stops, recheck A-B-C and glucose, monitor for recurrence, investigate for infection, and provide supportive care. This systematic approach saves lives.
