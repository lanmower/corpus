# THE BLUE CHILD 2021

## Lecture Notes

### Definition and Pathophysiology of Cyanosis

Cyanosis is a bluish discoloration, especially of the skin and mucous membranes, due to excessive concentration of deoxyhemoglobin in the blood. It represents one of the most clinically important signs in pediatric practice, particularly in neonates and infants presenting with respiratory or cardiac compromise. Understanding cyanosis requires knowledge of the normal physiology of oxygen transport and the pathologic mechanisms that disrupt it.

Two fundamental mechanisms result in cyanosis. The first is systemic arterial oxygen desaturation, which occurs when insufficient oxygen is delivered to the arterial blood due to pulmonary or cardiac pathology. The second mechanism is increased oxygen extraction by the tissues, resulting in elevated levels of deoxyhemoglobin in the venous circulation and capillary beds. Recognition of which mechanism predominates is essential for accurate diagnosis and appropriate management.

### Hemoglobin Biochemistry and Thresholds

Normal hemoglobin exists in two main chemical forms. Reduced hemoglobin, symbolized HbFe+2, is hemoglobin unbound to oxygen and is responsible for the characteristic bluish coloration associated with cyanosis. A critical threshold exists for clinically apparent central cyanosis: 5 g/dL of reduced (deoxygenated) hemoglobin must be present in the capillaries, which translates to approximately 3.4 g/dL in arterial blood. This threshold is important because it is absolute and does not correlate with oxygen saturation or hemoglobin concentration alone; a severely anemic infant may be profoundly hypoxic without appearing cyanotic, while a polycythemic infant with relatively normal oxygenation may appear cyanotic.

Methemoglobin, symbolized HbFe+3, is the oxidized form of hemoglobin that imparts an intense bluish tinge to the skin. Normally, about 2% of hemoglobin is in the form of methemoglobin; this small proportion is maintained by reducing enzymes present in red blood cells. When this balance is disrupted and methemoglobin accumulates, cyanosis can occur even with normal arterial oxygen saturation, making differentiation from other causes of cyanosis diagnostically important.

### Central Versus Peripheral Cyanosis

Central cyanosis represents bluish discoloration of the skin and mucous membranes in core areas, particularly the lips and tongue, due to systemic arterial oxygen desaturation. When central cyanosis is present, it indicates a primary defect in oxygenation at the systemic level. The mucous membranes are involved because they receive poorly oxygenated blood.

Peripheral cyanosis, by contrast, appears as bluish discoloration only of the extremities or fingers and is caused by increased oxygen extraction from sluggish blood movement through the capillary beds. In peripheral cyanosis, the systemic arterial oxygen saturation may be entirely normal; the mechanism is increased oxygen extraction resulting in a wide arteriovenous oxygen difference and accumulated deoxygenated blood on the venous side of the capillary beds. This distinction has significant diagnostic implications, as peripheral cyanosis indicates problems with tissue perfusion or local blood flow rather than primary pulmonary or cardiac oxygenation failure.

### Systemic Causes of Central Cyanosis

The etiologies of central cyanosis are organized by physiologic system. Respiratory system causes include pneumonia, bronchiolitis, bronchospasm (asthma), pulmonary hypertension, pulmonary embolism, and hypoventilation. These conditions result in inadequate ventilation, diffusion impairment, or ventilation-perfusion mismatch, leading to arterial oxygen desaturation.

Cardiovascular causes of central cyanosis include cyanotic heart disease, congenital heart disease, heart failure, and valvar heart disease. Right-to-left shunting allows deoxygenated blood to bypass the lungs and enter the systemic circulation directly, causing profound cyanosis. Non-shunting cardiac lesions such as severe heart failure may cause cyanosis through secondary pulmonary edema and ventilation-perfusion mismatch.

Central nervous system causes produce cyanosis through hypoventilation and include intracranial hemorrhage, drug overdose (particularly heroin), tonic-clonic seizures, and other seizure disorders. These conditions impair the neural drive to respiration, reducing minute ventilation and causing hypoxia.

Blood disorders causing central cyanosis include methemoglobinemia, polycythemia, hemoglobinopathies, and CO poisoning (carbon monoxide intoxication). Methemoglobinemia is of particular clinical importance and is discussed in detail below.

Environmental and other causes of central cyanosis include high altitude greater than 2400 m, where reduced atmospheric oxygen pressure decreases alveolar and arterial oxygen tension; hypothermia, which reduces metabolic oxygen demand but may impair central nervous system function; and obstructive sleep apnea, which causes recurrent hypoxic episodes.

### Methemoglobinemia

Methemoglobinemia deserves special emphasis as a cause of cyanosis that can be rapidly life-threatening and requires specific treatment. Several drugs are known to induce methemoglobinemia by oxidizing the iron in hemoglobin from the ferrous (Fe+2) to ferric (Fe+3) state. Common culprits include nitrite and nitrate compounds such as nitroglycerin, topical anesthetics including benzocaine (particularly when used for pharyngeal anesthesia), and dapsone (used in certain infections and dermatologic conditions).

When methemoglobinemia develops, the classic presentation is cyanosis that does not respond to oxygen therapy. The diagnosis should be suspected in any cyanotic patient who does not improve with supplemental oxygen. The specific treatment involves reducing agents such as methylene blue or ascorbic acid, depending on the clinical severity and the presence of glucose-6-phosphatase deficiency.

### Mechanisms of Peripheral Cyanosis

Peripheral cyanosis occurs in the setting of normal systemic arterial oxygen saturation but is characterized by increased oxygen extraction from tissues. This creates a wide arteriovenous oxygen difference, with increased deoxygenated hemoglobin accumulating on the venous side of the capillary beds.

Causes of peripheral cyanosis due to reduced cardiac output include heart failure and hypovolemia, both of which reduce blood flow to the periphery and increase peripheral tissue oxygen extraction. Vascular causes include arterial obstruction from peripheral vascular disease or Raynaud phenomenon, and venous obstruction from deep vein thrombosis. Non-cardiac causes include cold exposure, which causes peripheral vasoconstriction and increased local oxygen extraction, and sepsis, in which severe illness and possible septic shock reduce peripheral perfusion.

### Respiratory Gas Exchange: The Foundation

To understand cyanosis fully, one must recall the five steps of respiratory gas exchange. These are: ventilation, the exchange of gas between the atmosphere and the alveoli; diffusion across the alveolar-capillary membrane, where oxygen enters the blood and carbon dioxide is eliminated; transport of gases in the blood by hemoglobin and dissolved oxygen; diffusion from the capillaries to the cells at the tissue level; and finally, the use of oxygen and production of carbon dioxide within the cells. Disruption at any of these five steps can lead to hypoxia and potential cyanosis.

### Diagnostic Approach to Cyanosis

The clinical approach to cyanosis follows a systematic methodology. History and clinical assessment form the foundation, including the age of onset, triggers, and associated symptoms. Physical examination includes auscultation for cardiac murmurs, palpation of femoral artery pulses (to assess for coarctation or ductal-dependent systemic flow), measurement of blood pressure in all four limbs, the oxygen test (administration of 100% oxygen to assess response), and chest imaging for cardiomegaly.

The hypoxia test, or "hyperoxia test," is particularly valuable. In infants with central cyanosis from pulmonary disease, administering 100% oxygen typically results in improvement of saturation above 90-95%, as the lungs are capable of oxygenating blood when adequate oxygen is supplied. In contrast, infants with cyanotic heart disease remain desaturated or hypoxemic even when given 100% oxygen, because deoxygenated blood bypasses the lungs via right-to-left shunting. This simple test is one of the most powerful diagnostic tools available at the bedside.

Pulse oximetry provides rapid, non-invasive assessment of peripheral oxygen saturation and is based on pulsatile absorbance. Deoxygenated blood absorbs more red light, while oxygenated blood absorbs more infrared light. Two wavelengths of light are passed through an arterial bed, and the ratio of infrared and red light transmitted to the photodetector is determined and converted to a saturation percentage. It is essential to recognize that pulse oximetry measures saturation, not content, and may be unreliable in peripheral cyanosis where perfusion is poor.

Chest X-ray is a key investigation. The cardiothoracic ratio (CTR) is calculated by measuring the widest diameter of the heart and dividing by the widest internal diameter of the thorax. A cardiothoracic ratio greater than 50% on a PA (posteroanterior) view indicates cardiomegaly and suggests a cardiac etiology for cyanosis. The X-ray also assesses pulmonary parenchyma for evidence of pneumonia, pulmonary edema, or other lung disease.

Additional investigations for suspected congenital heart disease include blood gas analysis (to assess degree of hypoxia and acid-base status), lactate (elevated in shock states), CRP (C-reactive protein, to assess for infection), and FBC (full blood count, to assess hemoglobin and white blood cell count).

### Clinical Features: Distinguishing Pulmonary from Cardiac Etiologies

The clinical presentation helps differentiate the primary cause of cyanosis. Features suggesting congenital heart disease in a cyanotic infant include cyanosis with minimal or no respiratory distress (the infant appears "blue but comfortable"), presence of cardiorespiratory failure, the infant remaining desaturated or hypoxemic even when given 100% oxygen, and notably, the absence of a murmur (many serious cyanotic lesions produce no murmur).

In contrast, features suggesting pulmonary disease in a cyanotic infant include cyanosis accompanied by respiratory distress (the infant is "blue and uncomfortable"), with tachypnea and rib retractions evident. Critically, the infant's saturations improve significantly on 100% oxygen, and there is clinical evidence of respiratory failure. The presence of respiratory distress is a major distinguishing feature.

A mixed presentation may occur with respiratory distress and cyanosis that can be confused with infectious etiologies such as sepsis, pneumonia, meconium aspiration, or respiratory distress syndrome (RDS). In these cases, detailed history, examination for cardiac signs, and the hyperoxia test are crucial to differentiate.

### Management of Pulmonary Cyanosis

The initial management for suspected pulmonary disease is straightforward: supply oxygen via appropriate means (nasal prongs for mild hypoxia, face mask for moderate hypoxia, or CPAP/mechanical ventilation for severe cases) and start antibiotics if infection is suspected. This approach addresses both the hypoxia and potential infectious etiologies.

### Tetralogy of Fallot: A Case Example

Tetralogy of Fallot illustrates the clinical presentation of a significant cyanotic heart lesion. A typical case involves a 3-month-old infant with fast breathing, cyanosis since birth, a loud murmur audible on auscultation, warm periphery (indicating adequate cardiac output), spell-like attacks characterized by acute episodes of respiratory distress and hypercyanosis, and notably, normal growth and development. The warm periphery and maintained growth indicate the infant is compensating well despite the anatomic defect.

Diagnostic confirmation of Tetralogy of Fallot involves history and physical examination, the oxygen test (demonstrating minimal improvement with supplemental oxygen), chest X-ray (showing characteristic "boot-shaped" heart), blood gas analysis (documenting the degree of hypoxia), lactate (typically normal unless there is associated shock), CRP (to rule out infection), and FBC (to assess hemoglobin, as polycythemia often develops in response to chronic hypoxia).

### Emergency Management of Cyanotic Heart Disease: The Prostaglandin E1 Protocol

A critical clinical adage guides emergency management: "In doubt, give prostin AND antibiotics. This is a lifesaving intervention." This phrase encapsulates the importance of not delaying treatment while awaiting definitive diagnosis in a cyanotic neonate who may have ductal-dependent congenital heart disease.

The significance of maintaining ductal patency in cyanotic heart disease cannot be overstated. Opening and maintaining ductal patency is the only medical intervention that can restore adequate systemic cardiac output in ductal-dependent lesions, which are those in which either systemic or pulmonary blood flow depends on right-to-left shunting through the patent ductus arteriosus. Examples include critical aortic stenosis (ductal-dependent systemic output) and critical pulmonary stenosis or pulmonary atresia (ductal-dependent pulmonary output).

Prostaglandin E1 (PGE1) is the pharmacologic agent used to maintain ductal patency. The dosing is 0.05 to 0.1 mcg/kg/min, infused intravenously. This infusion is started emergently, immediately upon suspicion of ductal-dependent heart disease, and continued until a cardiac echocardiogram can be obtained to define the anatomy and guide further management. The therapeutic window is narrow; delayed administration can result in closure of the ductus arteriosus and sudden severe deterioration, while prolonged use of PGE1 risks side effects such as apnea and systemic hypotension.

PGE1 should be considered emergently in neonates presenting in shock within the first 2 weeks of life with suspected ductal-dependent systemic output. The clinical scenario is a term or near-term infant who appears well at birth but deteriorates during the first days to weeks of life with signs of shock, poor perfusion, metabolic acidosis, and cyanosis. Rapid diagnosis and initiation of PGE1 therapy can be lifesaving, providing time for echocardiographic diagnosis and definitive surgical intervention.

### Conclusion

The blue child represents a spectrum of pathophysiology ranging from simple hypoxia from pulmonary disease to complex cardiac anatomy with ductal dependence. Mastery of the definitions, mechanisms, diagnostic approach, and management algorithms outlined in this lecture provides the framework for safe and effective care of these critically ill infants.
