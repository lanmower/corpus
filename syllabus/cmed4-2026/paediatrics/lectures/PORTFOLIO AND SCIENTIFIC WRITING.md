# PORTFOLIO AND SCIENTIFIC WRITING

## Lecture Notes

### Learning Objectives

The paediatric portfolio is designed to integrate bedside learning with textbook learning, emphasizing the application of theory to real clinical problems encountered in practice. Your primary objectives in this assignment are fourfold:

First, you will integrate bedside learning with book learning by focusing your learning on specific questions relating to real clinical problems. This means moving beyond passive consumption of information to active engagement with patient care scenarios and using them as the foundation for deeper understanding.

Second, you will improve the quality of your clinical interviewing and examination skills through careful documentation and reflection on your encounters with patients. Each case you document should represent an opportunity to refine your communication and physical examination techniques.

Third, you will develop your clinical reasoning skills, particularly in planning appropriate investigation and treatment strategies and formulating follow-up plans based on clinical findings and diagnostic reasoning.

Fourth, you will engage in reflection on the multiple dimensions of illness affecting your patient, considering not only the biological aspects but also the psychological, social, and ethical dimensions of clinical care.

### Portfolio Structure and Requirements

The portfolio should contain information about patients you have clerked and managed during your ward rotation. Your selection of cases is important: choose patients who were either clerked on intake or newly admitted to the ward, as these cases will showcase your full engagement with the clinical process. You are required to document your patient records on standard A4 notepads, maintaining handwritten clerking notes that serve as the original case notes. These must be of high quality and fully represent your clinical work.

Your portfolio must consist entirely of your own original work. You are required to sign a declaration regarding plagiarism, confirming that all content is your own. Importantly, you may not use your formal case presentation as a portfolio case; these submissions must be separate pieces of work.

By the end of the second week of the block, you must submit one case in the format specified by the template available on Moodle. The template is mandatory, and you must adhere to it strictly. You should use recommended textbooks for additional detail and context, and you must develop your science writing skills throughout the process.

### Formatting Rules and Technical Requirements

All portfolio submissions must comply with strict formatting specifications:

- Maximum length: 4 pages of A4-sized paper
- Font: Times New Roman
- Font size: 12 point
- Line spacing: Single spacing
- You will be penalized for not adhering to these specifications

These requirements apply uniformly to all submissions, and deviation from them may result in mark deductions.

### Submission Timeline and Deadlines

Your portfolio case is due for midblock review, with submission scheduled for 8:00 AM on the second Friday of the block. This deadline is firm: all students not meeting the deadline will not be given deferred examination periods (DPs) initially. However, an appeal for a condoned DP may follow, during which the Department will specify whether they will accept your late submission.

If your submission is condoned as late, know that your marks will be capped at 50%, regardless of the quality of your work. Additionally, unsuitable submissions will be marked down and may receive a failing mark. The midblock portfolio is assessed according to departmental guidelines, and informal feedback will be provided to you. Students are encouraged to arrange a feedback session with the assessor to discuss their performance and areas for improvement.

### Marking Allocation

The portfolio is marked out of a total of 100 points, with marks distributed across six major components:

1. **History: 25 marks** - Your ability to document a detailed, relevant clinical history using appropriate templates and your own words.

2. **Examination: 25 marks** - Your recording of physical examination findings, including anthropometry and nutritional assessment with interpretation.

3. **Assessment: 25 marks** - Your problem list, differential diagnosis formulation, and clinical reasoning. This is the largest weighted component.

4. **Plan: 10 marks** - Your proposed investigations and management strategy, including interpretation of results.

5. **Follow-up and Ethics: 10 marks** - Your structured follow-up documentation and critical reflection on the case's ethical dimensions and lessons learned.

6. **Science Writing: 5 marks** - Your adherence to proper writing standards, grammar, spelling, and formatting conventions.

**Total: 100 marks**

### Clinical Case Report Sections

#### 1. History (25 marks)

A comprehensive history forms the foundation of your case report and represents one-quarter of your total mark. The history should be detailed and constructed according to BASIC templates and guidelines applicable to paediatric practice. However, templates are not fixed; you should adapt your approach based on the patient presentation. For example, neonates require the SAM (Subjective, Antenatal, Maternal) template rather than the standard BASIC framework.

Use your own words when documenting the history. Rather than copying verbatim from ward notes, synthesize information to demonstrate your understanding of the patient's problems. Focus on relevant information that reflects your clinical thought process: what information matters, and why did you pursue it?

Document the problems present at the time you saw the patient, and briefly note old or resolved problems in a "Previous Medical History" section. Avoid using long, complex sentences; instead, aim for clarity and conciseness. This approach demonstrates better clinical communication skills.

Ensure that the patient has a caregiver present during history taking when possible, as caregivers provide essential contextual and developmental information in paediatric cases. Consult Coovadia's textbook and other standard references for additional lists and frameworks, such as the Neonatal First Examination checklist.

**Example of Poor History Documentation:**
An antenatal history written as a single paragraph: "The mother said that she had been to the clinic and that the doctor had told her that she has hypertension. The doctor then put her onto medication and asked her to return in 2 weeks for another check-up. The mother said she was HIV positive and that she had started on ARVs during her pregnancy. She used to eat pears but now she no longer likes them."

**Example of Good History Documentation:**
The same information presented concisely:
- Mother: Hypertension on treatment
- HIV infected on ARVs

The second version is superior because it uses bullet points, removes irrelevant details (the pears), and presents key medical information at a glance.

#### 2. Examination (25 marks)

Physical examination findings represent another quarter of your total mark and should be documented with the same attention to detail as the history. Begin with anthropometry and nutritional assessment, providing not just the measurements but also your interpretation of what they signify for this particular patient.

Include general observations with your interpretation of their significance. Then document the general examination findings that are relevant to the presenting complaint. Proceed to systems examination, examining the relevant system first before moving to other systems. For example, if the patient presents with respiratory distress, examine the respiratory system in detail first, then complete examination of other systems to rule out contributing factors.

Document important positive findings (signs that are present) and important negative findings (expected findings that are notably absent). This demonstrates comprehensive examination and systematic thinking. Ensure that the signs you record are those you actually found during your examination of the patient. Do not include signs noted three weeks ago in previous examinations; document what you personally observed.

#### 3. Assessment (25 marks)

The assessment section carries equal weight to history and examination, reflecting the importance of clinical reasoning. Your assessment comprises two components: the problem list and the differential diagnosis.

**Problem List:**
Create a current, accurate, and thorough problem list that reflects competent and holistic care. List at least three important and relevant causes of the presenting complaint, demonstrating that you have considered multiple dimensions of the patient's illness.

**Differential Diagnosis:**
Provide at least four common differential diagnoses relevant to the presenting complaint. For each differential diagnosis, provide supporting evidence both for and against the diagnosis, drawing exclusively from the history and examination findings you have documented. Each piece of supporting evidence must be traceable to your own clinical findings.

Include a severity assessment for each differential diagnosis, considering the acuity of presentation and the potential consequences if that diagnosis were missed. This assessment demonstrates higher-order clinical reasoning and clinical judgment.

#### 4. Plan (10 marks)

Your plan section should address both investigations and management, representing 10% of your total mark.

**Investigations:**
Propose investigations that will aid in confirming your diagnosis. Include not just a list of tests but also your interpretation of the results. Ensure that all proposed investigations are relevant to your differential diagnoses; avoid ordering tests simply because they are available.

**Management:**
Outline both medical and non-medical management strategies. Your proposed management should align with accepted protocols and handbooks in paediatric practice. Be aware that in ward practice, you may observe several different treatment styles and approaches. Your role is to document evidence-based management while remaining aware of practical variations in clinical practice.

#### 5. Follow-Up and Ethics (10 marks)

Follow-up documentation should include two structured follow-up visits. Each follow-up should include both subjective and objective components, followed by your assessment and an updated plan based on the patient's progress.

Beyond clinical follow-up, use this section to reflect on the ethics and critical lessons from the case. Document what you learned from managing this patient. Consider the impact of the illness on the child, their family, and potentially on society or public health. This reflection demonstrates integration of clinical knowledge with professional values and demonstrates the personal growth that portfolio work is intended to foster.

#### 6. Science Writing (5 marks)

Scientific writing standards, worth 5% of your grade, encompass multiple dimensions:

**Writing mechanics:**
- Grammar: Ensure correct sentence structure, verb agreement, and tense consistency
- Spelling: Proofread carefully; spelling errors reflect poorly on professionalism
- Punctuation: Use punctuation correctly to enhance clarity
- Abbreviations: Use only standard medical abbreviations; define non-standard ones on first use

**Style and tone:**
- Avoid euphemisms and vague language (for example, "passed away" is less precise than "died"; similarly, be clear in your terminology)
- Avoid slang and informal expressions
- Use appropriate capitalization; avoid random capitalization
- Attend to gender-neutral language where appropriate

**Layout and formatting:**
- Organize content into clear sentences and paragraphs
- Use bullet points and lists for clarity where appropriate
- Apply Times New Roman font, size 12, with single spacing consistently throughout

Proper science writing demonstrates professionalism and enhances the clarity of your clinical reasoning for readers reviewing your work.

### Plagiarism and Originality

All work submitted in your portfolio must be original. Before submission, your case notes should be reviewed and signed off by a consultant or senior medical officer at the teaching hospital, confirming that the notes and your analysis represent your own work.

You are required to complete and sign a plagiarism declaration confirming the originality of your submission. Your submission will be registered, and this registration serves as documentation of your declaration. Plagiarism is a serious academic offense and will result in significant consequences.
