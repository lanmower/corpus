# Bleeding child

## Lecture Notes

### Haemostasis: Primary and Secondary

Haemostasis is the body's mechanism to prevent excessive bleeding and consists of two integrated processes. **Primary hemostasis** involves vascular contraction and platelet plug formation at the site of injury. The blood vessels constrict, and platelets aggregate to create an initial barrier. **Secondary hemostasis** follows, in which coagulation factors respond in a complex cascade to form fibrin strands that strengthen the platelet plug, ultimately creating a stable fibrin clot. Together, these mechanisms ensure rapid and effective blood clotting when needed.

### Components of the Hemostatic System

An abnormal hemostatic system can cause bleeding through defects in several key components: the vasculature itself, platelets (quantitative or qualitative defects), von Willebrand factor, and the coagulation pathway, which includes fibrinolysis. Understanding which component is affected is essential for diagnosis and management.

### Diagnostic Framework for Bleeding Disorders

When evaluating a child with bleeding, several key diagnostic questions must be asked. First, is there a significant bleeding disorder, and if so, is it hereditary or acquired? Second, is the problem primarily related to coagulation or platelets? Third, is there a factor deficiency or an antibody-mediated process? These questions guide investigations and management.

Several clinical clues help differentiate between inherited and acquired bleeding disorders. Always consider hemophilia in a boy with bleeding, as this X-linked condition is common. If the child is ill with systemic symptoms, an acquired disorder is more likely than a hereditary one. Petechiae are rare in factor deficiency alone, suggesting a platelet or vascular problem instead. Multiple bleeding sites are commoner in acquired bleeding disorders, whereas inherited disorders typically present with single-site bleeds. Finally, if only a single coagulation test is abnormal, an inherited cause is more likely.

### History and Physical Examination

A thorough assessment of a bleeding child requires careful history-taking covering age or neonatal status, sex, whether bleeding is from a single site or multiple sites, the type of bleeding (mucocutaneous versus soft tissue), whether the child appears well or ill, family history of bleeding disorders, current medications, and recent immunisations or viral infections. These details help establish whether the bleeding is acute or chronic, localised or systemic.

Physical examination should assess whether the child appears well or unwell, the distribution and character of bleeding (single versus multiple sites), the type of lesions (mucocutaneous, soft tissue, or joint involvement), and systemic signs including splenomegaly, organomegaly, lymphadenopathy, dysmorphism, or skeletal abnormalities. Physical findings often point to the underlying mechanism.

### Recognising Platelet, Vascular, and Coagulation Disorders by Physical Signs

Different patterns of bleeding help identify the underlying defect. **Platelet disorders** present with petechiae (which may be wet or dry), superficial ecchymosis, and mucosal bleeding, reflecting quantitative or qualitative platelet dysfunction. **Vascular disorders** cause palpable purpura, which indicates vasculitis, scurvy, or hypersensitivity reactions. **Coagulation factor deficiencies** (particularly factors VIII and IX deficiency seen in Haemophilia A and B) present with haemarthrosis (bleeding into joints), deep ecchymosis, and sometimes mucosal bleeding. Conditions that affect both platelets and coagulation factors include disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), leukaemia, and liver disease with portal hypertension (which causes splenomegaly).

### Understanding Petechiae, Purpura, and Ecchymoses

Petechiae, purpura, and ecchymoses all result from extravasation of blood from the vasculature into the skin or mucous membranes, but they differ in size. **Petechiae** are red, nonblanching maculopapular lesions caused by intradermal capillary bleeding and measure less than 2 mm in greatest diameter (pinpoint hemorrhages). **Purpura** measures 2 mm to 1 cm in diameter. **Ecchymoses** are larger bruises measuring more than 1 cm. These size distinctions are important for accurate description and documentation.

### Initial Investigations

The first-line investigations for a bleeding child are straightforward but essential: a full blood count (FBC) including differential and blood smear examination, INR/PT (measuring the extrinsic coagulation pathway), and APPT (measuring the intrinsic coagulation pathway). These tests provide rapid information about platelet numbers and basic coagulation function.

### Thrombocytopenia: Definition and Platelet Physiology

Thrombocytopenia is defined as a platelet count less than 150 x 10⁹/L. Understanding normal platelet production helps explain thrombocytopenic states. Platelets develop from hematopoietic stem cells that differentiate into megakaryoblasts and then megakaryocytes in the bone marrow under the influence of thrombopoietin. Each megakaryocyte produces approximately 4000 platelets. Platelets have a half-life of 7-10 days in circulation.

### Clinical Significance of Platelet Counts

The risk and severity of bleeding correlates with platelet count levels. A platelet count of less than 75 x 10⁹/L impairs primary hemostasis and is typically observed after major surgery or trauma. At counts less than 50 x 10⁹/L, spontaneous bleeding may occur, predominantly seen in the skin as petechiae and purpura. When the platelet count falls below 20 x 10⁹/L, noticeable hemorrhage appears in the skin and mucosa, including epistaxis and gingival bleeding. At counts less than 10 x 10⁹/L, life-threatening hemorrhage becomes possible, with risk of acute gastrointestinal hemorrhage and intracranial hemorrhage—serious CNS complications.

### Mechanisms of Thrombocytopenia

Thrombocytopenia results from three main mechanisms: increased platelet destruction (either immune or nonimmune), decreased platelet production (either congenital or acquired), or sequestration of platelets away from the circulation.

### Immune Causes of Increased Platelet Destruction

Immune-mediated platelet destruction includes several important causes. **Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)** is the most common cause of thrombocytopenia in children. **Neonatal allo-immune thrombocytopenia (NAIT)** occurs when a neonate inherits maternal alloimmunisation. Neonatal autoimmune thrombocytopenia can occur when maternal IgG crosses the placenta. Autoimmune and collagen vascular disorders, certain viral infections (including HIV), and drug-induced reactions also cause immune thrombocytopenia.

### Non-Immune Causes of Increased Platelet Destruction

Non-immune causes of platelet destruction include disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), haemolytic-uraemic syndrome (HUS), thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP), and sepsis. These conditions consume platelets through microangiopathic haemolysis or activation of the coagulation cascade.

### Decreased Platelet Production

Several categories of conditions impair platelet production in the bone marrow. **Primary bone marrow malignancies** such as leukaemia directly replace normal megakaryocytes. **Bone marrow failure states** including aplastic anaemia (acquired or genetic, such as Fanconi's anaemia), bone marrow infiltration by neuroblastoma or storage disorders, infections (HIV, parvovirus, CMV, tuberculosis), exposure to cytotoxic drugs, and radiation all reduce megakaryocyte numbers. **Congenital causes of selective ineffective thrombopoiesis** include TAR syndrome, Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome (WAS), congenital amegakaryocytic thrombocytopenia (CAMT), and Bernard-Soulier syndrome. **Viral causes** of selective ineffective thrombopoiesis include EBV, CMV, and HIV.

### Platelet Sequestration and Dilution

**Hypersplenism** causes sequestration of platelets in an enlarged spleen due to infection, vascular congestion, red cell disorders such as haemolytic anaemias, or infiltrative disorders. **Kasabach-Merritt syndrome** is caused by a massive haemangioma that sequesters platelets and causes thrombocytopenia. **Dilutional thrombocytopenia** results from recent massive blood transfusion, which dilutes the patient's platelet population.

### Idiopathic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)

ITP is the most common cause of thrombocytopenia in children, with an annual incidence of 5 per 100,000 children. The typical presentation is a sudden onset of petechiae, bruising, or mucosal bleeding in a previously healthy child with otherwise unremarkable physical findings. The bleeding often follows a viral infection or recent immunisation. **Pathogenesis** involves formation of antiplatelet antibodies (autoantibodies) that bind to the platelet surface; destruction is enhanced by Fc-receptor-mediated phagocytosis in the spleen and liver. **Immunological abnormalities** including T-cell, B-cell, and cytokine abnormalities are noted in ITP, though the exact mechanism of immune dysfunction is not fully understood.

### HIV Infection and Thrombocytopenia

HIV-positive patients are at significant risk for thrombocytopenia; approximately 30% of HIV-positive patients develop immune thrombocytopenic purpura. The causes of increased platelet loss or destruction in HIV patients include ITP (30%), thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP), haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS), hypersplenism (secondary to infection or hemophagocytosis), and drugs such as interferon and saquinavir. Decreased platelet production can result from multiple drugs (co-trimoxazole, ganciclovir, fluconazole, rifabutin, clarithromycin, didanosine), infections (HIV itself, CMV, mycobacterium tuberculosis, mycobacterium avium complex, histoplasmosis), malignancies (non-Hodgkin's lymphoma, Hodgkin's disease), and nutritional deficiencies (vitamin B12 and folate).

**Management** of thrombocytopenia in HIV patients begins with initiation of highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) and treatment of any underlying infection. Offending drugs should be removed. Caution must be exercised with steroid therapy, as steroids may aggravate underlying tuberculosis, viral or fungal infections, and the underlying immune deficiency.

### Management of Immune Thrombocytopenia

Management of immune thrombocytopenia is stratified by platelet count and clinical symptoms. For a platelet count between 20–50 x 10⁹/L on an immune basis with no bleeding, **observation** is appropriate. For counts greater than 20 x 10⁹/L with no mucosal bleeding, **observe and educate** the parents about warning signs. For counts greater than 20 x 10⁹/L with mucosal bleeding, **treat** with either IVIG or steroids. For counts less than 20 x 10⁹/L or in the presence of mucosal bleeding, administer **IVIG at 1 gram/kg over 6 hours OR a short course of steroids; NO PLATELET TRANSFUSIONS** are given in immune thrombocytopenia, as transfused platelets will be rapidly destroyed.

### Epistaxis: Recognition and First Aid

Epistaxis (nosebleed) is common; 60% of the population experience at least one nosebleed in their lifetime. Correct management is important. **Actions to avoid** include placing the head between the knees (which increases intracranial pressure), having the child lie down, stuffing gauze into the nose, allowing the child to blow the nose once bleeding has stopped (which dislodges the clot), and panicking. **Correct management** involves remaining calm and keeping the patient calm, having the child sit up, leaning the head forward to allow blood to drain rather than be swallowed, pinching the soft part of the nostril firmly, and pressing toward the face for **15 minutes**, repeating if necessary.

### Neonatal Allo-Immune Thrombocytopenia (NAIT)

NAIT is thrombocytopenia in neonates born to mothers with normal platelet counts, analogous to Rh haemolytic disease of the newborn. The condition results from maternal allo-immunisation against fetal platelet antigens. Presentation occurs with profound thrombocytopenia either present at birth or developing within hours. Clinical manifestations include petechiae and bruising; visceral bleeding can also occur. The most serious complication is intracranial hemorrhage, which occurs in 8–22% of cases and can occur in utero. Fortunately, NAIT is self-limited, typically lasting 2–3 weeks before resolving spontaneously as maternal antibodies are cleared.

### Inherited Coagulation Disorders

Inherited coagulation disorders include haemophilia A (factor VIII deficiency), haemophilia B (factor IX deficiency), von Willebrand disease, and deficiencies of factors II, V, VII, X, XI, XII, or XIII. Dys-, hypo-, or afibrinogenemia (disorders of fibrinogen quantity or quality) also occur. Other rare inherited coagulation disorders exist but are beyond the scope of common paediatric practice.

### Acquired Coagulation Disorders

Acquired coagulation disorders are more common in clinical practice than inherited ones. Causes include vitamin K deficiency, liver disease (which impairs synthesis of multiple factors), disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), massive transfusion syndrome (which dilutes clotting factors), dys- or hypo-fibrinogenemia, and disorders associated with malignancy. Other rare acquired coagulation disorders also occur.

### Interpreting Coagulation Tests: PT/INR and APPT

Coagulation test results guide diagnosis and reflect the pathway involved. An **elevated PT/INR with normal APPT** indicates a defect in the extrinsic pathway and suggests vitamin K deficiency, liver disease, warfarin therapy, or factor VII deficiency. The **PT/INR tests the extrinsic pathway**. A **normal PT/INR with elevated APPT** indicates a defect in the intrinsic pathway, suggesting factor VIII deficiency (haemophilia A), factor IX deficiency (haemophilia B), factor XI or XII deficiency, or heparin therapy. The **APPT tests the intrinsic pathway**. **Both PT/INR and APPT elevated** indicates a defect in the common pathway (common to both extrinsic and intrinsic pathways) and suggests factor II, V, or X deficiency, vitamin K deficiency, liver disease, or disseminated intravascular coagulation. When **PT/INR and APPT are both normal with a normal platelet count**, another cause for the bleeding must be sought, such as platelet function abnormalities, vascular disorders, or factor XIII deficiency.

### Vitamin K and Factor Activation

Vitamin K plays a crucial role in coagulation by catalysing the γ-carboxylation of factors II, VII, IX, and X, thereby activating these factors. Vitamin K deficiency thus impairs the extrinsic pathway and common pathway.

### Haemorrhagic Disease of the Newborn (HDN)

Haemorrhagic disease of the newborn (also called vitamin K deficiency bleeding) is due to vitamin K deficiency in the newborn. It classically presents between day 2 and 3 after birth. Typical bleeding sites include the umbilicus, gastrointestinal tract, nose, skin, and sites of circumcision. The most severe complication is intracranial bleeding. **Prophylaxis** with vitamin K is recommended for all newborns prior to discharge home.

### Bleeding Time and Platelet Function Disorders

Several hemostatic disorders may present with a normal coagulation screen (normal PT/INR and APPT) and normal full blood count, making diagnosis challenging. These include platelet function abnormalities (acquired causes such as aspirin use and uremia; inherited conditions such as Glanzmann thrombasthenia), mild von Willebrand disease, factor XIII deficiency, collagen disorders, vitamin C deficiency (scurvy), and mild inherited factor deficiencies. If hemostasis is abnormal despite normal screening tests, a **bleeding time test** should be performed and the patient should be **referred to a hematologist** for further evaluation.

### Procedures and Medications to Avoid

In children with bleeding disorders, certain invasive procedures carry high risk and should be avoided: intramuscular injection (IMI), lumbar puncture (LP), surgical cut-down, tooth extractions, suturing (if there are alternative options), and venepunctures via the jugular or femoral veins. **Aspirin should be avoided**, as it impairs platelet function. These precautions prevent unnecessary bleeding complications.

### Emergency Management of Bleeding

When a child presents with active bleeding, the first step is to **control the bleeding** using firm direct pressure and elevation of the affected limb. Once bleeding is controlled and investigations are underway, treatment depends on the suspected underlying cause. If a **coagulation defect is suspected**, administer fresh frozen plasma (FFP). If **thrombocytopenia is suspected**, administer platelet transfusions. The overarching management principle is **prompt replacement therapy** of the deficient component to restore haemostasis.
