## Cyanosis — The Blue Child

Cyanosis is the bluish discolouration of the skin and mucous membranes that results when the concentration of deoxygenated haemoglobin becomes excessive in blood, or when there is an increased concentration of abnormal haemoglobin derivatives such as methaemoglobin or sulphaemoglobin in superficial blood vessels. Clinically recognizing and differentiating the cause of cyanosis in a child requires understanding the underlying pathophysiology, the five primary mechanisms by which it arises, and the anatomical distinction between central and peripheral presentations.

The five pathophysiological mechanisms of cyanosis are alveolar hypoventilation, diffusion impairment, ventilation-perfusion mismatch, right-to-left shunting (whether intracardiac or intrapulmonary), and haemoglobinopathy limiting oxygen transport. Central cyanosis arises from reduction in arterial oxygen saturation and appears as bluish discolouration in highly vascularized areas including the lips, tongue, and mucous membranes, typically with warm extremities and normal cardiac output. Peripheral cyanosis, by contrast, is confined to the distal extremities and results from increased oxygen extraction from sluggish blood movement through capillaries due to peripheral vasoconstriction or polycythaemia; arterial oxygen saturation in peripheral cyanosis remains normal.

The clinical distinction between central and peripheral cyanosis is critical for diagnosis. In central cyanosis, the tongue is involved and the affected areas remain warm, whereas in peripheral cyanosis the tongue is spared and the extremities are cold. Clubbing is usually present with central cyanosis but absent in peripheral cyanosis. The capillary refill time is less than two seconds in central cyanosis but exceeds two seconds in peripheral cyanosis. Central cyanosis may improve with oxygen supplementation if a pulmonary cause is present, while peripheral cyanosis shows no improvement with oxygen; conversely, warming improves peripheral cyanosis but not central cyanosis. The underlying mechanisms differ fundamentally: central cyanosis reflects reduction in blood oxygen saturation, while peripheral cyanosis reflects reduction in blood flow.

Central causes of cyanosis are remembered by the PALMS mnemonic. P stands for polycythaemia, in which an absolute increase in red blood cell mass increases the concentration of deoxygenated haemoglobin. A represents altitude, where the lower partial pressure of oxygen at high elevation reduces arterial saturation. L stands for lung disease, encompassing multiple pulmonary causes. M represents methaemoglobin and metabolic disorders including hypoglycaemia. S stands for shunt, including both intracardiac and intrapulmonary right-to-left shunting lesions.

Pulmonary causes of cyanosis are subdivided into three categories by mechanism. Alveolar hypoventilation occurs when the alveoli fail to clear carbon dioxide and oxygenate blood adequately, caused by central nervous system depression from asphyxia, sedation, or intracranial pathology such as head injury, raised intracranial pressure, or intracranial haemorrhage; neuromuscular disease including Guillain-Barré syndrome with its ascending paralysis and respiratory muscle weakness, or Duchenne's muscular dystrophy with progressive muscle degeneration affecting the respiratory muscles; and airway obstruction such as choanal stenosis causing congenital narrowing of the posterior nasal aperture in neonates. Ventilation-perfusion mismatch arises when ventilated alveoli are not perfused or perfused alveoli are not ventilated, caused by extrinsic compression of the lungs from pneumothorax with air in the pleural space causing lung collapse, pleural effusion with fluid compressing lung tissue, or thoracic dystrophies as skeletal abnormalities reducing thoracic volume; intrinsic compression occurs with pneumonia as inflammation of the lung parenchyma blocking airways, lung atelectasis with alveolar collapse from surfactant deficiency or airway obstruction, hyaline membrane disease as infant respiratory distress syndrome in preterm neonates with surfactant deficiency causing alveolar collapse, and asthma with airways inflammation and bronchoconstriction. Diffusion impairment occurs when the alveolar-capillary membrane is thickened, caused by pulmonary oedema with fluid accumulation in the lung parenchyma and alveolar space, and bronchopulmonary dysplasia as chronic lung disease in premature infants from mechanical ventilation and supplemental oxygen exposure causing pulmonary fibrosis.

Cardiac causes of cyanosis are classified by their haemodynamic consequence. Lesions causing decreased pulmonary blood flow include tetralogy of Fallot, a cyanotic congenital heart defect characterized by right ventricular outflow obstruction permitting right-to-left shunting and typically presenting with "Tet" spells; and tricuspid atresia, with complete absence of the tricuspid valve causing severe reduction in pulmonary blood flow. Lesions causing increased pulmonary blood flow with cyanosis through mixing of systemic and pulmonary venous return include transposition of the great vessels, in which the aorta arises from the right ventricle and the pulmonary artery from the left ventricle, causing complete mixing of deoxygenated and oxygenated blood; total anomalous pulmonary venous return, where all pulmonary veins drain anomalously rather than into the left atrium; and truncus arteriosus with a single arterial trunk arising from the heart to supply systemic, pulmonary, and coronary circulations. Lesions causing cyanosis through severe heart failure include coarctation of the aorta with narrowing of the descending aorta causing systemic hypertension and progressive heart failure; and hypoplastic left heart with severe underdevelopment of left-sided cardiac structures manifesting as cyanosis and cardiac failure from birth.

Peripheral causes of cyanosis are remembered by the COLD mnemonic. C stands for cold, where exposure to cold temperature causes peripheral vasoconstriction reducing blood flow to extremities. O represents obstruction, including arterial or venous obstruction reducing peripheral perfusion. L stands for left ventricular failure and shock, both of which cause reduced cardiac output and peripheral hypoperfusion. D represents decreased cardiac output from any cause, whether from cardiac dysfunction, hypovolaemia, or severe sepsis.

Abnormal haemoglobin derivatives present a special category of cyanosis not explained by reduced oxygen saturation. Methaemoglobinaemia may be congenital, inherited as a haemoglobinopathy in which abnormal haemoglobin derivatives cannot carry oxygen effectively, manifesting with cyanosis from birth; or toxic and acquired following exposure to oxidizing agents including nitrites, sulfonamides, and aniline dyes that convert normal haemoglobin to methaemoglobin. Sulphaemoglobinaemia is a rare condition arising from exposure to specific oxidizing toxins that convert haemoglobin into an abnormal sulphur-containing derivative incapable of carrying oxygen, causing cyanosis refractory to standard interventions.

The hyperoxia test (100% oxygen challenge) differentiates pulmonary from cardiac causes of cyanosis by measuring the arterial partial pressure of oxygen (PaO₂) after oxygen administration. A PaO₂ greater than 300 mmHg indicates normal cardiopulmonary function. A PaO₂ between 150 and 300 mmHg indicates pulmonary disorders (including hypoventilation, ventilation-perfusion mismatch, and diffusion impairment), central nervous system disorders, or methaemoglobinaemia. A PaO₂ between 100 and 150 mmHg indicates persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (PPHN) or cardiac mixing lesions with increased pulmonary blood flow. A PaO₂ less than 100 mmHg indicates cardiac lesions with parallel circulation (such as transposition of the great vessels) or cardiac mixing lesions with restricted pulmonary blood flow (such as tetralogy of Fallot or tricuspid atresia), where minimal improvement occurs despite supplemental oxygen.

Arterial blood gas reference ranges provide essential baselines for interpreting blood samples in cyanotic children. Normal pH ranges from 7.35 to 7.45. Normal partial pressure of carbon dioxide (pCO₂) is 35 to 45 mmHg. Normal serum bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) is 22.0 to 30.0 mmol/L. Normal base excess is −3.0 to 3.0 mmol/L. Normal partial pressure of oxygen (pO₂) is 75 to 100 mmHg. Normal oxygen saturation (SaO₂) is 95 to 100%. Electrolyte values must also be considered: serum potassium 3.5 to 5.5 mmol/L, serum sodium 135 to 145 mmol/L, serum chloride 95 to 110 mmol/L, ionized calcium 1.12 to 1.30 mmol/L, serum glucose 3.6 to 7.7 mmol/L, and serum lactate 0.2 to 1.8 mmol/L. Abnormalities in these parameters may contribute to cyanosis or its underlying causes.

Investigation of cyanosis begins with pulse oximetry to establish baseline oxygen saturation and trends. Haemoglobin concentration or haematocrit is measured as cyanosis becomes visible at higher haemoglobin levels but may be masked in anaemia. Arterial blood gas analysis determines the degree of hypoxia and identifies acid-base disturbances. The hyperoxia test as described above discriminates between pulmonary and cardiac causes. A septic screen including blood culture and inflammatory markers is performed in acutely unwell infants to exclude infection as an underlying cause. Electrocardiography and echocardiography are essential to evaluate cardiac structure and function, identifying congenital lesions or functional abnormalities. Haemoglobin electrophoresis is performed when haemoglobinopathy such as methaemoglobinaemia or sulphaemoglobinaemia is suspected based on clinical findings and failure to improve with oxygen.

The visibility of cyanosis depends on multiple factors beyond oxygen saturation. Cyanosis becomes more apparent when haemoglobin concentration is high; anaemic children may not appear cyanotic despite severe hypoxia because the absolute quantity of deoxygenated haemoglobin remains low. Cyanosis becomes more apparent at low levels of fetal haemoglobin saturation, with implications for distinguishing cyanosis in newborns. Cyanosis is less apparent in patients with darker skin pigmentation, requiring heightened clinical vigilance and reliance on objective measures such as pulse oximetry in non-Caucasian populations.

Treatment of cyanosis depends on identifying and addressing the underlying cause. All cyanotic children require optimization of oxygenation through supplemental oxygen or ventilatory support if needed. Symptomatic management includes positioning to optimize respiratory mechanics, maintaining normothermia, and avoiding unnecessary agitation. Definitive therapy targets the primary pathology, whether pulmonary (treating infection, recruiting collapsed alveoli, relieving obstruction), cardiac (urgent prostaglandin infusion to maintain ductal patency in certain lesions, surgical or catheter-based intervention), haematological (treatment of methaemoglobinaemia with methylene blue), or metabolic (correcting hypoglycaemia or other derangements).
