# LATEST KZN COVID GUIDELINES

## Lecture Notes

### Screening and Triage

COVID-19 screening represents three fundamental changes to paediatric management. First, screening must be incorporated into general screening for tuberculosis, HIV, developmental status, and nutritional assessment. Second, routine testing of all children requiring admission is not recommended; targeted testing of symptomatic children is mandatory instead. Third, children's wards no longer require compartmentalisation into non-COVID, COVID, and PUI (persons under investigation) sections.

During triage, implement respiratory screening for fever, cough, difficulty breathing, or sore throat within the preceding 14 days. These are the key symptoms warranting COVID-19 assessment. Once a positive respiratory screen is documented, children over 2 years old and their caregiver should receive a surgical mask at the triage stage.

Patients presenting with excessive cough and sputum production require prioritised single-room placement and must wear masks. Cough hygiene must be reinforced: cover the nose and mouth with tissue or the crook of the arm when sneezing, coughing, or blowing the nose. Hand hygiene is equally critical—wash hands and keep contaminated hands away from eyes, nose, and mouth.

### Social Distancing and Isolation Measures

Children presenting with respiratory symptoms should maintain a social distancing distance of 1-2 metres and remain within their designated space. For suspected COVID-19, isolation must occur in a side ward with strict infection prevention and control (IPC) precautions.

### Testing and Case Definition

A suspected COVID-19 case is defined as any person presenting with an acute (≤14 days) respiratory tract infection or other clinical illness compatible with COVID-19. This includes individuals with cough, sore throat, shortness of breath, anosmia (loss of smell), or dysgeusia (loss of taste), with or without associated fever, weakness, myalgia, or diarrhoea. Routine SARS-CoV-2 testing for asymptomatic patients is not recommended.

Targeted testing is appropriate for children with suspected COVID-19 requiring admission who have severe pneumonia, MIS-C, or other suspected COVID-19 related disease. The diagnostic test of choice is SARS-CoV-2 PCR.

### Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children (MIS-C)

MIS-C, also known as PIMS-TS or Kawasaki-like syndrome, typically occurs after resolution of acute COVID-19 infection. The WHO case definition applies to children and adolescents aged 0-19 years.

MIS-C diagnosis requires fever for more than 3 days plus at least two of the following systemic manifestations: (1) rash or bilateral non-purulent conjunctivitis or mucо-cutaneous inflammation of the oral mucosa, hands, or feet; (2) hypotension or shock; (3) features of myocardial dysfunction, pericarditis, valvulitis, or coronary abnormalities (evidenced by echocardiography or elevated troponin); (4) evidence of coagulopathy, including abnormal PT, PTT, or elevated d-dimers; (5) acute gastrointestinal manifestations such as diarrhoea, vomiting, or abdominal pain.

Inflammatory markers must be elevated (ESR, C-reactive protein, or procalcitonin). No other obvious microbial cause for inflammation can be present, excluding bacterial sepsis and staphylococcal or streptococcal shock syndromes. Evidence of COVID-19 is required: RT-PCR, antigen test, serology positivity (when available), or likelihood based on clinical context.

### Mild Disease Management

Children with mild COVID-19 should receive symptomatic treatment at home or in an isolation facility. Caregivers must be provided with patient information pamphlets and receive guidance on suitable infection prevention and control practices. Special investigations and imaging are not routinely indicated. Routine micronutrient or vitamin supplementation has limited evidence and is not recommended.

Routine steroid use is potentially harmful in mild disease, although steroids should be considered if indicated for non-COVID-19 conditions (such as asthma or laryngotracheo-bronchitis). There is documented increased morbidity associated with steroid use in children with influenza and bronchiolitis, so caution is warranted.

### Oxygen Therapy and Severe Disease Management

Children with moderate or severe COVID-19 require admission to a children's ward isolation cubicle or side ward for supportive in-patient care. Every hospital must establish an isolation cubicle for respiratory cases.

The target oxygen saturation (SpO2) is >92%. A systematic oxygen therapy ladder guides escalation:

**Step 1**: If unable to maintain SpO2 >92% in room air, add nasal prong oxygen (NPO2) at 2 L/min.

**Step 2**: If unable to maintain SpO2 >92% on NPO2 at 2 L/min, change to a 40% face mask (pink) at 8 L/min.

**Step 3**: If unable to maintain SpO2 >92% on a 40% face mask (pink) at 8 L/min, change to a 60% face mask (orange) at 10 L/min.

**Step 4**: If unable to maintain SpO2 >92% on a 60% face mask at 10 L/min, change to a non-rebreather face mask at 15 L/min and contact the local regional hospital/PICU.

**Step 5**: If unable to maintain SpO2 >92% on a non-rebreather face mask at 15 L/min, consider high-flow humidified nasal cannula (HFNC) subject to appropriate facility availability and contact the local regional hospital/PICU.

### Antimicrobial Therapy

For suspected bacterial co-infection, first-line antibiotic options are: Amoxicillin 45 mg/kg per dose, 12 hourly orally for 5 days, OR Ampicillin 50 mg/kg per dose intravenously 6 hourly. Gentamicin 6 mg/kg intravenously daily for 5-10 days may be added if indicated.

### Corticosteroid Therapy

Steroids should only be used in consultation with the local paediatrician or regional hospital. They are indicated in children with confirmed COVID-19 who require nasal prong oxygen (NPO2).

**Dexamethasone**: 0.15 mg/kg daily intravenously for 10 days.

**Hydrocortisone**: 1-2 mg/kg per dose intravenously 6 hourly for 10 days.

**Prednisone**: 1 mg/kg daily orally for 10 days.

The paediatric department of the local regional hospital must be consulted before prescribing steroids.

### Antiviral Therapy and Respiratory Support

Remdesivir is not recommended for use in children with COVID-19 until more information becomes available regarding its efficacy and safety at this age.

If nebulisers are necessary, use a metered-dose inhaler (MDI) with a spacer as an alternative delivery method.

### Diagnostic Investigations

Complete blood count (FBC) findings may show normal or reduced neutrophil, lymphocyte, and platelet counts, often with reduced neutrophils or lymphocytes and reversal of the neutrophil:lymphocyte ratio (age-dependent).

C-reactive protein (CRP) is normal or elevated in severe disease.

Procalcitonin (PCT) is typically normal or low; elevation suggests bacterial co-infection.

Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) may be elevated in severe disease.

Coagulation profile abnormalities include prolonged INR/APTT, and d-dimers are increased in severe disease.

Chest X-ray typically demonstrates bilateral patchy nodular or speckled ground glass opacities, although other radiological patterns have been described.

### Infection Control and Personal Protective Equipment

Equipment decontamination requires a 1000 parts per million (ppm) dilution of detergent or disinfectant solution.

For routine care of COVID-19 patients, healthcare workers should wear surgical masks, aprons, and +/- visors. For aerosol-generating procedures, N95 masks and visors are required. Staff require a mask for routine care but an N95 mask, apron, and shield for aerosol-generating procedures.

### Referral Criteria and Consultation

Consult the regional hospital's paediatric department when: (1) the child requires ≥40% oxygen to maintain SpO2 above 92%; (2) the child's condition destabilises (requiring immediate consultation); (3) the child meets criteria for MIS-C.

The KZN Provincial Paediatric COVID-19 Hotline is available at 0800 006603.

### Discharge and De-isolation Criteria

Children can be discharged from hospital once they no longer require supplementary oxygen. Before discharge, ensure holistic care and review the immunisation status, nutritional status, HIV risk, and tuberculosis risk of the child.

De-isolation timelines are: Children with mild COVID-19 can be de-isolated 7 days from symptom onset. Children with moderate or severe COVID-19 can be de-isolated 7 days after they achieve clinical stability (cessation of oxygen).

### Epidemiological Context

At the time of guideline publication, the dominant SARS-CoV-2 variants circulating were sub-variants of Omicron (B.1.1.529).
