# RSV 2023 ALERT FOR HEALTHCARE WORKERS

## Lecture Notes

### Epidemiology

Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) is the most common cause of bronchiolitis and lower respiratory tract infection (LRTI) among young children. During the 2023 southern hemisphere season, which commenced in week 6 (the week starting 6 February 2023), RSV detection rates among children aged less than 5 years were at moderate levels and continued to rise steeply throughout the season. The 2023 detection rate was rising more sharply than the mean circulation observed between 2014-2019, although it remained within the historical range of what had been seen in the past.

In contrast, the northern hemisphere experienced a significant RSV 2022-2023 season from November to February, during which there were reports of increased severe RSV-associated illness and hospitalisation, including in children greater than 1 year of age—a departure from the typical pattern of RSV disease predominantly affecting younger infants.

### Transmission

RSV is highly contagious with transmission occurring mainly by respiratory droplets. This mode of transmission is central to infection control strategies and explains why isolation measures are critical during periods of high RSV circulation.

### Clinical Presentation

Infants aged less than 6 months are at particular risk of severe RSV disease, which may manifest with hypoxia, severe respiratory distress (characterised by tachypnoea, nasal flaring, or lower chest retractions), inability to feed, or apnoea requiring hospitalisation. The physical examination findings of severe respiratory distress are tachypnoea, nasal flaring, and lower chest retractions.

Very young infants may present atypically, with only irritability, decreased activity, and breathing difficulties as the primary symptoms, making clinical recognition challenging in this age group. It is important to note that whilst the majority of infants with RSV-associated bronchiolitis do not require hospitalisation, certain children—particularly those with underlying risk factors—are at significant risk of severe disease requiring hospital admission.

### Risk Groups

Multiple risk factors increase the likelihood of severe RSV-associated disease. These include prematurity, congenital heart disease, chronic lung disease of prematurity, neurological disease, age less than 6 months, immunodeficiency, and lack of breastfeeding. Healthcare workers should maintain a high index of suspicion for severe disease when these risk factors are present.

### Prevention

Infection control measures are paramount in reducing RSV transmission. Isolation of children with influenza-like symptoms—such as not attending crèches or schools for a few days—is an effective prevention strategy given RSV's high contagiousness via respiratory droplets. Teaching children and caregivers responsible for infants to practice sneeze and cough hygiene is a recommended prevention measure that can be implemented across healthcare and community settings.

Prophylactic antibiotic use is not recommended for children with upper respiratory tract infections, as RSV is a viral infection and routine antibiotics provide no benefit.

### Palivizumab

Palivizumab is a monoclonal antibody administered monthly throughout the RSV season to infants and children at high risk of severe RSV disease. Clinical evidence has demonstrated that palivizumab is effective for prevention of severe RSV disease in high-risk infants and children. However, practical limitations exist: the therapy is associated with high costs and requires monthly intramuscular injections throughout the entire RSV season, which may limit access and adherence in some settings.

### Surveillance

Surveillance of RSV circulation relies on multiple data sources. Weekly reports documenting RSV circulation are available from pneumonia surveillance data, with weekly respiratory pathogen surveillance reports published by the National Institute for Communicable Diseases (NICD). The Moving Epidemic Method (MEM) is employed as a sequential analysis tool using the R Language to calculate the duration, start, and end of the annual epidemic based on data from the previous 5 years. For RSV 2023, surveillance thresholds were based on 2015-2019 data, providing a validated reference frame for detecting unusual activity.

### Seasonal Patterns

RSV and influenza have distinct seasonal patterns. RSV typically circulates first, with average season onset at the end of February (range: early February to mid-March) based on 10-year historical data excluding the 2020-2021 period. Influenza season follows later in the calendar year, providing a temporal window for differential diagnosis and resource planning. This predictable seasonal offset is crucial for healthcare planning, as RSV surges precede influenza season.

### Diagnostic Guidance

Clinicians should consider RSV in the differential diagnosis for severe respiratory illness, especially in young children. Diagnostic evaluation should include assessment of risk factors and careful evaluation for signs of severe disease such as hypoxia and respiratory distress. Given the northern hemisphere experience of increased severe disease in 2022-2023 and the anticipated surge in the 2023 southern hemisphere season, clinicians and paediatric hospitals or intensive care units should anticipate an increase in paediatric admissions and prepare by allocating adequate resources to respond effectively to the surge in RSV cases.
