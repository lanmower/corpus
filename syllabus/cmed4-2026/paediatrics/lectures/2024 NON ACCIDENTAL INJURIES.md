# 2024 NON ACCIDENTAL INJURIES

## Lecture Notes

### Historical Context and Overview

Child abuse has deep historical roots, evolving from infanticide in antiquity through abandonment in the medieval period to organized exploitation and abuse from the 13th to 19th centuries. Today, the modern paediatrician must approach suspected child abuse with systematic clinical acumen, understanding that it represents a significant cause of child morbidity and mortality across all socioeconomic strata.

### Types of Child Abuse

Child abuse encompasses a spectrum of harmful behaviors perpetrated against children. The primary categories include physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional and psychological maltreatment, medical neglect, nutritional neglect, intentional drugging, safety neglect, and organized abuse. Each form carries distinct clinical presentations and requires tailored clinical and medicolegal responses.

### Physical Abuse: Epidemiology and Risk Factors

Physical abuse is a leading cause of childhood injury and death. The epidemiology reveals critical patterns: the average age of abused children is approximately 7 years, though the average age at fatality is notably younger at 3 years. Males are disproportionately affected compared to females. The distribution of fatal cases shows that approximately one-third occur in children under 6 months, one-third between 3 months and 3 years, and one-third in children over 3 years—demonstrating that younger, more vulnerable children face the highest risk of severe injury and death.

Socioeconomic factors play a significant role, though physical abuse is not exclusive to disadvantaged families; it occurs across all social classes. Key predisposing factors include a parental history of abuse, alcoholism and drug abuse, severe psychological disorders (present in approximately 10% of abuse cases), family breakdown, social isolation, poverty, and unemployment.

Certain child characteristics increase abuse risk. Children with behavioral disorders—including Attention Deficit Disorder, Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder, Conduct Disorder, and Oppositional Defiant Disorder—are at heightened risk. Children with learning difficulties, neurodevelopmental disorders such as Autism Spectrum Disorder, speech and language delays, visual or hearing impairments, and chronic illnesses (neurological, hepatic, renal, or cardiac) are similarly vulnerable.

### Physical Abuse: Clinical Recognition and Presentation

Physical abuse is fundamentally determined by what the clinician observes. The pattern, age distribution, and anatomical location of injuries provide critical diagnostic clues. Several features should raise suspicion: delay in seeking medical attention, discordance between the reported history and the actual injury pattern, parents or caregivers seeking help from multiple different healthcare providers (inappropriate health-seeking behavior), and presentation to different health facilities at different times, including late at night.

Clinical features of blunt force trauma include haematoma, swelling, bruising, and subconjunctival hemorrhage. The clinician must examine the entire child, including the oral cavity and genitals, recording bruises by size, shape, position, color, and estimated age.

### Differential Diagnosis of Physical Abuse

Several conditions may mimic physical abuse and must be excluded through careful evaluation: birth trauma, congenital syphilis, osteogenesis imperfecta, rickets, scurvy (presenting with poor wound healing and bleeding gums), bleeding disorders (haemophilia, meningococcemia, and idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura), and skin diseases such as impetigo, chickenpox, and staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome.

### Modes of Injury in Physical Abuse

Non-accidental injury is defined as deliberate action by a perpetrator that intentionally threatens and inflicts physical harm to a child. This contrasts with accidental injury, which results from unforeseen events causing external trauma without intent to harm. Critically, there should be no discordance between the reported history and the actual injury in accidental cases.

Specific modes of non-accidental injury include blunt force trauma from objects such as fists, bats, elbows, and wooden spoons; whipping injuries from implements including hosepipes, sjamboks, sticks, twigs, and wet face cloths; and thermal burns, including glove-and-stocking distribution burns from immersion in hot water and branding injuries from hot metallic objects.

### Management of Physical Abuse: The Multidisciplinary Approach

Effective management of physical abuse requires a coordinated multidisciplinary team comprising a psychologist, social worker, medical doctor, nurse, and teachers. This integrated approach ensures comprehensive assessment of the child's physical health, psychological welfare, social circumstances, and educational needs.

### Long-Term Consequences of Physical Abuse

Survivors of physical abuse face substantial psychological morbidity. Anxiety, depression, mood disorders, and personality disorders are common sequelae. Critically, survivors of abuse are themselves at elevated risk of perpetuating the cycle of abuse in their own families.

### Sexual Abuse: Definitions and Frameworks

Two foundational definitions guide clinical and medicolegal practice. Kempe's definition characterizes sexual abuse as involvement of a child in sexual activity without consent, without understanding, and contrary to societal norms. Faller's definition emphasizes the element of power imbalance, defining sexual abuse as sexual activity involving a child in which a power differential exists.

### Sexual Abuse: Epidemiology and Perpetrators

Sexual abuse affects children across all social classes, races, and religions. Epidemiological data show a female-to-male ratio of approximately 1:3 in girls and 1:5 in boys. Age-specific peaks differ by sex: girls show increased incidence before age 6 years and again in early adolescence, while boys are most commonly abused in the late first decade.

Over 80% of abused children are victimized by someone they know, with approximately two-thirds of perpetrators being family members. The abuser is frequently the primary caregiver. Perpetrators commonly experience financial stress, substance abuse, underachievement, low self-esteem, and jealous or manipulative personality traits. A history of abuse in the perpetrator's own childhood is common. The vast majority of perpetrators (>95%) are male; perpetration patterns range from isolated incidents to systematic grooming and repeated abuse.

### Sexual Abuse: Behavioral Indicators and Presentation

Behavioral indicators provide crucial clinical clues to suspected sexual abuse. Presenting signs include poor concentration, learning difficulties, poor social skills, bullying victimization, separation anxiety, depression and generalized anxiety disorder, hyperactivity, behavioral disturbance, stealing, and inappropriate sexual behavior toward other children. These behavioral changes often reflect the child's attempt to cope with trauma and dysregulation.

Physical symptoms and signs of sexual abuse include recurrent abdominal pain, difficulty walking or sitting, painful micturition and recurrent urinary tract infections, secondary enuresis or fecal soiling, genital or penile discharge, abnormal vaginal or anal dilatation, genital or anal laceration and bruising, vaginal bleeding, and clinical or laboratory evidence of sexually transmitted infection.

### Key Red-Flag Signs and Suspicious Circumstances

Several clinical scenarios should prompt immediate consideration of sexual abuse: unexplained injuries, discrepancy between the reported history and physical findings, delay in presentation for medical evaluation, alleged self-inflicted injuries, allegations of third-party infliction without corroboration, repeated injuries across visits, sexualized behavior inappropriate to developmental stage, and detection of sexually transmitted infection in a prepubertal child.

### Differential Diagnosis of Sexual Abuse

Conditions that may simulate sexual abuse must be systematically excluded. These include bleeding disorders, straddle injuries (which require consistency between the reported mechanism and injury pattern—questioning the plausibility of mechanisms such as bicycle seat, palisade fence, concrete wall, car handbrake, shopping trolley, or broom handle contact), effects of anesthesia or conscious sedation, vulvovaginitis from non-abuse causes (worm infestation, poor hygiene, yeast infection, prolapsed anus secondary to acute gastroenteritis, dermatological conditions including lichen sclerosus et atrophicus and eczema, harsh soaps, allergic reactions, and chlorine exposure from swimming pools).

### The J88 Form: Medico-Legal Documentation

The J88 form is the standardized report on medico-legal examination by a health care professional and forms the cornerstone of forensic documentation in sexual abuse cases in the South African context. The form comprises six pages: the initial report page with demographic and administrative details; detailed description of the medico-legal examination; history relevant to the alleged sexual offense; clinical findings; gynecological examination results and clinical conclusions; and diagrams for anatomical documentation of injuries.

Meticulous completion of the J88 is essential, as it serves both clinical and legal purposes, documenting findings with precision and creating an evidence record that may be presented in court proceedings.

### The Adams Classification

The Adams Classification provides a standardized framework for categorizing genital findings in cases of suspected child sexual abuse. This classification describes the patterns of injuries observed in child sexual abuse and assists clinicians in distinguishing abuse-related findings from normal variants and other conditions.

### Examination Technique: History-Taking

Obtaining a careful history is the foundation of assessment. Clinicians must document the circumstances of the alleged injury: how it occurred, the informants providing the account, dates, times, place, sequence of events, and persons present. The child must be interviewed directly without leading questions, and explicit inquiry must be made regarding the alleged perpetrator. The child's psychological state during disclosure should be documented—whether the child was crying, calm, distressed, or withdrawn—and a mental status examination should be performed, with particular attention to signs of post-traumatic stress disorder.

Additional historical elements include birth history and pregnancy details, growth trajectory, developmental milestones, immunization status, systemic review of symptoms, comprehensive family and social history, school attendance and performance history (including grades passed, failed, or condoned), teachers' reports, and administration of validated behavioral rating scales such as the Conner's scale.

### Examination Technique: General Inspection and Physical Examination

The physical examination must be comprehensive and systematic. Bruises, the most common sign of abuse, should be recorded with precise documentation of size, shape, position, color, and estimated age. The entire body surface must be examined, including the oral cavity and genitals. In acute cases presenting within 72 hours—particularly when the child is uncooperative or perineal injury is present—examination under anesthesia should be considered to allow thorough inspection and evidence collection.

### Examination Technique: Sexual Abuse Examination

Sexual abuse examination requires a structured, skilled approach conducted in a private environment. Key elements include: involvement of a skilled medical officer experienced in child sexual abuse assessment; presence of a third person as a chaperone and witness; thorough explanation of the examination procedure to the child; collection of all samples and photographs before washing the child; performance of complete general examination; and appropriate positioning based on age (small children may be examined on a parent's lap, while older children are typically positioned supine in the lithotomy position for genital examination).

### Vaginal Examination Findings

The normal prepubertal vagina typically demonstrates a crescentic hymen, which is the most common normal finding. Complete absence of the hymen is extremely rare as a normal variant. When hymenal tears occur, they may heal leaving clefts or bumpy scar tissue at the healing margins. Findings should be carefully documented using clock-face notation to precisely record location of injuries.

A key principle guides interpretation: lack of physical evidence does not exclude abuse. Historical features—disparity between the reported history and injury pattern, delay in seeking medical evaluation—and behavioral findings—changes in behavior and recurrent injuries—must be integrated with physical findings to form a complete clinical assessment.

### Anal Examination Findings

Anal examination may reveal several findings with varying significance. Anal dilatation can result from child sexual abuse but must be distinguished from other causes including stool in the rectum, chronic constipation, Crohn's disease, recurrent rectal prolapse, and effects of anesthesia or sedative medications. Anal fissures may be associated with constipation or abuse; fissures from abuse are often multiple or show signs of acute trauma. Anal skin tags represent healed anal fissures and require investigation of the cause. Mucocutaneous changes and complete anal dilatation warrant careful assessment. Examination should be performed with the child in the lateral decubitus position, and the examiner should note the child's pubertal stage.

### Imaging and Photographic Documentation

Every physically abused child under 5 years of age requires skeletal survey consisting of anteroposterior views of the skull, chest, spine, and arms and legs. In suspected head injury, for medico-legal purposes, or when screening for previous injuries, computed tomography of the head should be performed. Retinal examination by an ophthalmologist with retinoscopy is essential to identify flame-shaped retinal hemorrhages.

In severe sexual abuse cases with physical signs, photographic documentation is critical for forensic and clinical purposes. Digital photography is acceptable provided images are printed immediately for the medical record.

### Treatment and Medical Management

Treatment protocols for child sexual abuse include post-exposure prophylaxis to prevent sexually transmitted infection, empiric antibiotics for proven or suspected infection, pregnancy testing when appropriate, and management of injuries evident on initial presentation. Treatment decisions must be individualized based on the nature of the abuse and associated injuries.

### PTSD Prevention and Management

Prevention of post-traumatic stress disorder should commence as early as possible and involves two key components. First, debriefing and disclosure support: the social worker facilitates the child's disclosure and initial processing, while a psychologist provides specialized counseling. The evidence is clear—the earlier this intervention, the better the outcome.

Treatment of established PTSD requires recognition of characteristic signs: sleep disturbance, change in appetite, separation anxiety, deterioration in school performance, general behavioral changes including hypervigilance, mood lability, and inappropriate sexualized behavior. Comprehensive psychological and psychiatric management is required.

### Child Abuse Accommodation Syndrome

Understanding the typical trajectory of disclosure following sexual abuse is essential for clinicians. The Child Abuse Accommodation Syndrome describes six progressive stages: Helplessness (recognition that the abuse is occurring), Coercion to secrecy (the perpetrator's enforcement of silence), Entrapment (the victim's increasing sense of powerlessness and continued victimization), Accommodative behavior (the victim's adaptation and coping mechanisms), Disclosure (often delayed and presented in an unconvincing manner), and Recantation (retraction of the disclosure, often due to guilt, fear, or pressure). Understanding this sequence assists clinicians in interpreting a child's disclosure pattern.

### The Multidisciplinary Team Approach

Effective management of child sexual abuse requires a coordinated multidisciplinary team including social workers (to facilitate disclosure and provide social support), psychologists (for specialized assessment and treatment), medical doctors (for medical assessment and documentation), nurses (for support and care), police officers from the Child Protection Unit (for investigation and protection), victim assistance officers (to support the child and family through the legal process), counselors (for ongoing psychological support), school authorities including teachers and principals (to monitor the child's adjustment and provide educational support), and court representatives (to facilitate legal proceedings and protection orders).

### Health Worker Roles and Professional Responsibilities

Health care professionals have critical responsibilities in cases of suspected child abuse. These include: recognition and identification of abuse; accurate documentation of the extent of clinical findings, both physical and psychological; provision of appropriate medical and psychological treatment; and timely reporting to relevant authorities including child protection services, law enforcement, and social services. Failure to report suspected abuse represents a breach of professional duty and violates mandatory reporting legislation.

### Professionals as Parents at Risk

Healthcare professionals including nurses, doctors, social workers, lawyers, police officers, and soldiers are themselves at risk of perpetrating abuse or failing to recognize it in their own children. These professionals commonly work extended hours, spend prolonged periods away from their families, and may have reduced vigilance regarding warning signs in their own children due to professional desensitization or personal denial.

### Sibling Assessment and Family Considerations

When abuse is identified in one child, approximately 20% of siblings will have experienced abuse at some stage. Assessment must therefore include all siblings, as well as other children in homes or places of safety. Referral for comprehensive assessment and intervention should occur promptly. The concept of "opening a can of worms" or "Pandora's box" reflects the reality that disclosure of one child's abuse often reveals abuse of multiple family members and necessitates complex family intervention.

### Long-Term Psychological Consequences of Sexual Abuse

Survivors of childhood sexual abuse are at elevated risk for multiple psychiatric and personality disorders, including mood disorders, personality disorders, depression, and anxiety disorders. The prognosis for survivors who also have underlying mental illness or a history of childhood sexual assault is guarded, requiring long-term specialized mental health care.

### Systemic Challenges in Management

The management of child sexual abuse faces significant obstacles. These include fragmented systems of care, lack of dedicated units specifically managing sexual assault cases, and overloaded public sector hospitals. Training deficiencies result in insufficient numbers of skilled health care workers capable of providing specialized assessment and treatment. Families face inadequate support services, leading to high rates of follow-up default. Insufficient places of safety for children complicate protective placement. Extended delays between initial patient contact and court proceedings increase distress and reduce evidence quality. Safety concerns affect health workers, caregivers, and children. Staff managing sexual assault cases experience severe burnout and insufficient institutional support. Finally, inconsistent adherence to clinical guidelines compromises care quality.

### South African Legislation Context

This lecture addresses non-accidental injuries within the South African legal and clinical framework, referencing the J88 form and other medicolegal documentation required by South African law. The National Child Protection Act and related legislation establish mandatory reporting requirements for all health care workers and provide the legal foundation for child protection interventions.

### Conclusions and Clinical Practice

Child abuse is a common and significant problem affecting children across all populations. All health care workers must develop competence in recognizing and appropriately managing cases of suspected abuse. Management must adopt a holistic approach, addressing the child's physical health, psychological welfare, family dynamics, and legal protection. The ultimate aim of effective management is to prevent secondary trauma to survivors and their families, facilitating recovery and promoting long-term wellbeing.
