# 2022 POISONING

## Lecture Notes

Poisoning in children represents a significant public health concern, ranking as the fourth biggest cause of unintentional injury after motor vehicle accidents, fires, and drowning in high and middle income countries. Understanding the epidemiology, pathophysiology, and management of poisoning is critical for pediatric practitioners. This lecture, instructed by Wayne Rajah across 44 slides, provides comprehensive coverage of poisoning epidemiology, pathophysiology, toxidromes, and evidence-based management strategies.

## Epidemiology and Mortality

The highest peak age for accidental poisoning in children is between 1 and 4 years, when children are most inquisitive and explore their environment without understanding consequences. Non-accidental poisoning peaks at 15 years of age, driven by adolescent peer pressure and risk-taking behavior despite greater awareness of consequences. Boys are more commonly affected than girls in poisoning incidents across all age groups.

Fatality rates remain low overall, with less than 2% of poisoning cases proving fatal in children. However, mortality follows a bimodal distribution, with the highest fatal poisoning rates occurring in children under 1 year and a secondary peak at 15 years of age.

## Physiologic Vulnerabilities in Children

Children face unique physiologic vulnerabilities that increase their susceptibility to toxins. Children under 4 months have increased skin and mucosal permeability to toxins compared to older children and adults, creating a window of heightened vulnerability during infancy. Decreased glycogen stores in children may contribute to metabolic complications during poisoning. Increased body surface area (BSA) relative to body mass in children leads to thermoregulatory issues that can complicate toxin-induced disease. Most critically, smaller body size means that the dose per kilogram is more toxic, amplifying the toxicity of ingested substances even at quantities that might be tolerable in adults.

## Risk Factors and Prevention

Risk factors for childhood poisoning fall into several categories. Young children are at high risk for accidental poisoning due to their behavior—they are naturally inquisitive, unable to read warning labels, and imitate adult behavior without understanding danger. Adolescents are at risk for non-accidental poisoning due to peer pressure and risk-taking behavior.

Primary prevention strategies aim to reduce exposure to poisoning agents. These include: (1) removing the poisoning agent from the environment entirely, (2) replacing a poisonous agent with a lower toxicity alternative (for example, paracetamol versus aspirin), (3) implementing legislation for child-resistant packaging, and (4) reducing toxicity by packaging substances in non-lethal concentrations or doses. Secondary prevention aims to prevent injury or illness once poisoning has occurred, utilizing poison control centres and decontamination methods to minimize harm.

## Assessment of Poisoning

When a poisoning is suspected, detailed history is essential. Key information includes: what toxic agent or medicine was involved, what amount was ingested, the route of exposure, when exposure occurred, any odours noted, whether the child was alert upon discovery, and any history of substance abuse. This history guides subsequent management and helps identify the specific toxin involved.

## Toxidromes: Recognizing Poisoning Patterns

Toxidromes are characteristic clinical syndromes that result from specific classes of toxins, allowing clinicians to recognize poisoning patterns and guide presumptive management before definitive toxin identification. Understanding toxidromes enables rapid clinical recognition and supports treatment decisions even when the exact toxin is unknown.

### Cholinergic Toxidrome

Cholinergic toxidrome results from excessive cholinergic stimulation, typically from organophosphates, carbamates, or muscarinic agents. The syndrome presents with characteristic signs reflecting overstimulation of parasympathetic activity.

### Anticholinergic Toxidrome

Anticholinergic toxidrome results from blockade of acetylcholine receptors, presenting with decreased parasympathetic activity and signs opposite to those of cholinergic poisoning.

### Salicylism

Salicylism is the toxidrome resulting from salicylate poisoning from aspirin and related compounds, characterized by metabolic derangements and neurological effects that evolve through distinct phases.

### Sedative Toxidrome

Sedative toxidrome is a clinical syndrome from CNS depressant poisoning, resulting in decreased consciousness and respiratory depression that can progress to severe respiratory failure.

### Dystonic Toxidrome

Dystonic toxidrome presents with dystonia (abnormal muscle tone and posturing) from agents like antipsychotics or metoclopramide.

### Sympathomimetic Toxidrome

Sympathomimetic toxidrome results from sympathetic nervous system overstimulation, causing hypertension, tachycardia, and agitation.

## Organophosphate Poisoning

Organophosphate poisoning results from exposure to organophosphate compounds used as pesticides, which irreversibly inhibit acetylcholinesterase leading to a cholinergic toxidrome. The severity of poisoning depends on the extent of acetylcholinesterase inhibition and the duration of exposure.

### Clinical Presentation: SLUDGE Mnemonic

The characteristic signs of cholinergic toxidrome from organophosphate poisoning are remembered by the SLUDGE mnemonic:

- **S (Salivation)**: Excessive drooling and salivary production in cholinergic poisoning
- **L (Lacrimation)**: Excessive tearing
- **U (Urination)**: Involuntary urination (urinary incontinence)
- **D (Diaphoresis)**: Profuse sweating
- **G (GIT upset)**: Gastrointestinal symptoms including nausea, diarrhea, and abdominal pain
- **E (Emesis)**: Vomiting

These signs develop as acetylcholinesterase inhibition allows acetylcholine to accumulate at synapses, overstimulating muscarinic and nicotinic receptors throughout the body.

### Management of Organophosphate Poisoning

Management of organophosphate poisoning follows the ABC approach, addressing life-threatening complications first. The three respiratory symptoms requiring immediate intervention—bradycardia, bronchorrhea (excessive bronchial secretions), and bronchospasm—are treated with atropine.

**Atropine Administration**: Atropine is given as a test dose, followed by repeat doses, and thereafter continued as a continuous infusion. The dose and rate of infusion are titrated to the clinical response, aiming to control respiratory and cardiovascular manifestations. Atropine should never be stopped abruptly because symptoms will recur immediately; instead, wean atropine gradually over 24 hours as the child stabilizes.

Seizures should be watched for during atropine treatment and treated accordingly with benzodiazepines or other anticonvulsants. The main limitation of atropine is that it has no effect on the paralysis caused by organophosphate poisoning—paralysis results from neuromuscular blockade and persists despite adequate atropinization.

**Pralidoxime (2-PAM)**: Pralidoxime is a cholinesterase reactivator used in conjunction with atropine in organophosphate poisoning. It must be given within 48 hours of exposure to be effective, as delayed administration does not reactivate the inhibited enzyme. Pralidoxime addresses the underlying acetylcholinesterase inhibition, complementing atropine's symptomatic management.

## Salicylate Poisoning

Salicylate poisoning results from aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) or other salicylate-containing compound overdose. The pathophysiology evolves through three distinct phases, each with characteristic metabolic and clinical features.

### Phase 1: Respiratory Alkalosis (0–24 hours)

Phase 1 of salicylate poisoning is characterized by hyperventilation due to direct stimulation of the respiratory center. This hyperventilation leads to respiratory alkalosis. Simultaneously, salicylates inhibit renal reabsorption of bicarbonate, causing compensatory alkaluria where potassium and bicarbonate are lost in the urine. Hypokalemia develops as potassium is wasted. Phase 1 may last as long as 12 hours and can appear deceptively mild.

### Phase 2: Paradoxic Aciduria (hours to 24 hours)

Phase 2 of salicylate poisoning is characterized by paradoxic aciduria when sufficient potassium has been lost. As hypokalemia worsens, the kidney retains hydrogen ions to conserve potassium, resulting in urine that is acidic despite systemic alkalemia. This paradox reflects the kidney's priority on electrolyte conservation over acid-base balance.

### Phase 3: Metabolic Acidosis (4–6 hours in children)

Phase 3 of salicylate poisoning, which occurs 4 to 6 hours after ingestion in children, is characterized by dehydration, hypokalaemia, and high anion gap metabolic acidosis. Salicylates uncouple oxidative phosphorylation in mitochondria, generating organic acids and excessive heat. The combination of dehydration, electrolyte loss, and metabolic acidosis creates a critical clinical state.

### Neurological and Systemic Manifestations

As salicylate toxicity progresses, neurological symptoms develop in a characteristic sequence: agitation, delirium, hallucinations, convulsions, lethargy, and stupor. Hyperthermia (elevated body temperature) occurs in severe salicylate toxicity due to the metabolic effects of uncoupling. Hypoglycemia (low blood glucose) can occur as a metabolic complication. Multiple organ involvement reflects systemic effects affecting the respiratory system (hyperventilation and pulmonary edema), central nervous system (neurological symptoms), renal system (electrolyte losses), and metabolic systems (acidosis and hypoglycemia).

## Iron Poisoning

Iron poisoning results from overdose of iron supplements or iron-containing products, which are commonly found in prenatal vitamins and iron fortified products. The toxicity is related to the elemental iron content, not the total iron compound dose.

### Mechanism and Organ System Effects

Iron is toxic in overdose because it causes direct corrosive injury to the gastrointestinal tract and generates free radicals that damage cellular structures throughout the body. Iron poisoning affects the gastrointestinal tract (the primary injury site), cardiovascular system, central nervous system, kidneys, and liver. Systemic toxicity follows absorption of iron into the bloodstream.

### Gastrointestinal Manifestations

Gastrointestinal effects of iron poisoning include vomiting (often with blood indicating mucosal damage), abdominal pain, diarrhea, and hemorrhage. Hemorrhagic gastroenteritis can develop in severe cases, with potential for hypovolemic shock.

## Paracetamol Poisoning

Paracetamol (acetaminophen) poisoning causes hepatotoxicity leading to acute liver failure if not treated promptly. Despite being a widely available over-the-counter medication, paracetamol poisoning remains one of the most important causes of drug-induced liver failure in children.

### Mechanism of Hepatotoxicity

Paracetamol toxicity occurs when glutathione stores are depleted and the toxic metabolite N-acetyl-p-benzoquinoneimine (NAPQI) accumulates, causing hepatocyte necrosis. In therapeutic doses, NAPQI is rapidly conjugated with glutathione and rendered non-toxic. In overdose, glutathione becomes depleted and NAPQI accumulates to toxic levels, binding covalently to hepatocyte proteins and causing cell death.

### N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) Antidote

N-acetylcysteine (NAC) is the primary antidote for paracetamol poisoning. NAC replenishes glutathione stores and prevents hepatotoxicity by providing sulfhydryl groups for conjugation of NAPQI. NAC is most effective when given within the first 8 to 10 hours after paracetamol ingestion, but can still provide benefit when administered up to 24 hours or beyond if hepatotoxicity has not yet developed.

### Clinical Phases of Paracetamol Poisoning

Paracetamol poisoning evolves through three phases:

- **Phase 1 (0–24 hours)**: Minimal symptoms. The child may be asymptomatic or have mild gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, vomiting). This deceptively benign phase creates danger if not recognized early.
- **Phase 2 (24–72 hours)**: Apparent improvement with hepatotoxicity developing. Gastrointestinal symptoms resolve and the child appears to recover, but hepatotoxicity is developing silently. Liver enzymes begin to rise.
- **Phase 3 (72–96 hours)**: Peak hepatotoxicity with jaundice, coagulopathy, and encephalopathy. If not treated, this phase may progress to acute liver failure and death.

### Risk Assessment: Rumack-Matthew Nomogram

Serum paracetamol concentration is plotted on the Rumack-Matthew nomogram at specific times (4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24 hours post-ingestion) to determine hepatotoxicity risk and the need for NAC treatment. The nomogram accounts for the time elapsed since ingestion, as the same serum concentration carries different risk at different times after ingestion. Levels below the risk line do not require treatment; levels on or above the line indicate hepatotoxicity risk and mandate NAC therapy.

### Supportive Care

Supportive care in paracetamol poisoning includes monitoring liver function tests to assess hepatocyte injury, coagulation studies to detect coagulopathy, and blood glucose to identify hypoglycemia. Management of coagulopathy with fresh frozen plasma (FFP) is necessary if the prothrombin time is significantly prolonged. Close monitoring for encephalopathy, a sign of acute liver failure, guides decisions about transfer to a center capable of liver transplantation if required.

## Caustic and Corrosive Agent Poisoning

Caustic and corrosive agents are chemical substances that destroy or damage organic tissue through direct chemical action. They include strong acids (sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid), strong alkalis (sodium hydroxide, potassium hydroxide), and other reactive industrial chemicals. Caustic ingestion in children is a serious emergency that can cause severe burns to the mouth, oesophagus, and stomach, potentially leading to perforation, mediastinitis, and death.

### Sites and Extent of Injury

Caustic and corrosive ingestion primarily affects the mouth, oesophagus, and stomach, with potential for full-thickness burns and perforation. Alkalis typically cause deeper penetrating burns because they saponify tissue fats and dissolve proteins, causing liquefactive necrosis that extends through tissue layers. Acids cause coagulative necrosis that may be more superficial but still severely damages tissue.

### Acute Management

The immediate management principle for caustic ingestion prioritizes preventing additional harm. Management includes: (1) stop ingestion immediately, (2) do NOT induce vomiting (vomiting causes further contact of caustic with tissues) or use activated charcoal, (3) dilute with small sips of water or milk (dilution reduces concentration and heat generation), (4) assess airway if burns involve the mouth, (5) urgent endoscopy to assess depth of injury. The goal of dilution is to reduce the chemical concentration, not to remove the substance completely.

### Endoscopy and Risk Assessment

Endoscopy is essential to determine the extent and depth of tissue damage (superficial versus deep burns), assess perforation risk, and guide decisions on steroids, antibiotics, and surgical intervention. Endoscopy should be performed urgently, typically within the first 24 hours, to establish the degree of injury and plan management. Superficial (first-degree) burns may resolve without long-term sequelae, while deep (second and third-degree) burns risk stricture formation and perforation.

### Complications

Complications of caustic ingestion include oesophageal stricture formation (developing days to weeks after the acute injury as scarring develops), gastric perforation (acute complication risking peritonitis), mediastinitis (if the esophageal perforation extends into the mediastinum), peritonitis (from gastric perforation), sepsis, and death if perforation occurs. These complications necessitate close monitoring and aggressive management.

## Hydrocarbon and Petrochemical Poisoning

Petrochemical poisoning results from ingestion or inhalation of petroleum-derived products such as petrol (gasoline), paraffin, kerosene, or other hydrocarbons. These substances are commonly found in homes for fuel, cleaning, and lamp oil purposes.

### Route and Systemic Effects

Ingestion is the most common route of petrochemical poisoning in children, often through accidental swallowing of petrol or paraffin. Systemic effects of petrochemical (hydrocarbon) poisoning include gastrointestinal irritation (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea), central nervous system depression (lethargy, ataxia, loss of consciousness), and potential aspiration pneumonitis if aspirated into the respiratory tract.

### Aspiration Pneumonitis: A Critical Complication

Aspiration pneumonitis is a serious complication occurring when petroleum products are aspirated into the lungs, causing chemical pneumonitis with cough, dyspnea (shortness of breath), and potential respiratory failure. The hydrocarbons cause direct chemical injury to the respiratory epithelium, leading to inflammation, increased permeability, and pulmonary edema.

Vomiting is particularly dangerous in petrochemical poisoning because it increases the risk of aspiration of hydrocarbons into the lungs, causing chemical pneumonitis. This is why inducing vomiting is contraindicated in hydrocarbon poisoning—the risk of aspiration pneumonitis outweighs any benefit from removing the substance from the stomach.

### Management of Hydrocarbon Ingestion

Management of petrochemical ingestion includes: (1) do NOT induce vomiting (increases aspiration risk), (2) do NOT use activated charcoal (hydrocarbons are poorly absorbed and activated charcoal is ineffective), (3) supportive care with airway protection, (4) monitor for aspiration pneumonitis, (5) provide oxygen and mechanical ventilation if respiratory symptoms develop. The key is prevention of aspiration while providing supportive care for any systemic toxicity.

### Monitoring and Observation

Monitoring after petrochemical ingestion includes observation for 6 to 12 hours for symptoms of aspiration (cough, dyspnea, wheezing), chest X-ray to detect pneumonitis (infiltrates may not appear immediately but develop over hours), and continuous pulse oximetry to detect hypoxemia. A normal initial chest X-ray does not exclude aspiration pneumonitis, as radiographic changes may develop over 6 to 12 hours after exposure.

### Prognosis

Most cases of hydrocarbon aspiration pneumonitis resolve with supportive care and time, as the body gradually clears the aspirated material and the inflammatory response resolves. However, severe cases can progress to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), multi-organ failure, and death, particularly if aspiration is large volume or if secondary bacterial infection develops.

### Prevention of Petrochemical Poisoning

Prevention includes proper storage of petrochemical products in child-resistant containers, storage away from food containers (to prevent accidental ingestion), and storage out of reach of children.

## General Approach to Poisoning Management

The general approach to any suspected poisoning in children follows a systematic framework: (1) ensure airway, breathing, circulation (ABC), (2) obtain detailed history of exposure, (3) identify the toxin (or likely toxin class), (4) assess toxidrome, (5) perform decontamination if appropriate, (6) administer antidotes if available, (7) provide supportive care, (8) monitor closely for complications.

Decontamination methods vary by route and toxin type and include: (1) dilution with water or milk for caustic agents (never induce vomiting), (2) activated charcoal for many toxins (must be given within hours of ingestion for effectiveness), (3) gastric lavage for recent ingestions (controversial and rarely used now), (4) skin decontamination by washing with water (for topical exposures), and (5) decontamination of eyes with water irrigation (for eye exposures).

## Poison Control Centers and Expert Resources

Poison control centres play a critical role in managing poisoning by providing expert consultation on the toxicity, management, and prognosis of poisonings. They offer guidance on specific antidotes, decontamination methods, and monitoring protocols tailored to the specific poison and dose involved. Consulting poison control should be routine in all suspected poisoning cases, providing access to expert knowledge that guides evidence-based management.

## Summary

Poisoning in children requires a systematic approach combining rapid recognition of toxidromes, accurate identification of the toxin when possible, and evidence-based management including decontamination, antidotes, and supportive care. Understanding the epidemiology of poisoning and the physiologic vulnerabilities of children informs both prevention strategies and acute management. The specific agents covered in this lecture—organophosphates, salicylates, iron, paracetamol, caustic agents, and hydrocarbons—represent common and serious poisonings in the pediatric population. Prompt recognition and appropriate management can prevent progression to severe toxicity and death.
