## Lecture Notes

### Neonatal Immunity

Neonatal immunity is characterized as new and immature. This fundamental limitation underlies much of neonatal susceptibility to infection. Preterm neonates are at particularly high risk for infection because they are less capable of responding to infection due to immunological deficiencies, lack of IgM and IgA antibodies, and passive antibody transfer occurs later in gestation. The combination of an underdeveloped immune system with incomplete maternal antibody protection creates a critical vulnerability window in early life.

### Definition and Pathophysiology of Neonatal Sepsis

Neonatal sepsis is defined as a clinical syndrome of bacteremia with systemic signs and symptoms of infection in the first 28 days of life. Understanding sepsis as a systemic inflammatory response to infection is essential for timely recognition and intervention.

### Classification of Neonatal Infections

Neonatal infections are classified along two important axes: timing of acquisition and route of transmission.

By timing of acquisition, infections are divided into early-onset sepsis (birth to 7 days) and late-onset sepsis (8 days to 28 days, acquired in hospital, home, or community). Early-onset infections are primarily maternally derived, while late-onset infections reflect environmental exposure.

By route of acquisition, infections are classified as congenital (antenatal acquisition) or acquired (typically occurs later). Congenital infections have four main routes of transmission: transplacental, ascending through the birth canal, intrapartum exposure, and acquisition from the birth canal during delivery.

### Risk Factors for Neonatal Infection

Multiple maternal and neonatal risk factors contribute to infection susceptibility. Maternal factors include infection (such as urinary tract infection and chorioamnionitis), prematurity, and colonization with Group B Streptococcus. Specific early risk factors are prematurity, chorioamnionitis, PROM (prolonged rupture of membranes) greater than 18 hours, and maternal colonization with Group B Streptococcus.

Late-onset risk factors include prematurity, invasive procedures, indwelling devices, ventilators, and VP shunts. Neonatal factors include immunological deficiencies, lack of IgM and IgA antibodies, skin barrier defects (particularly in preterm infants), and prolonged healthcare facility stay. Facility-related factors contributing to late-onset infection include overcrowding, poor hand-washing practices, and understaffing.

### TORCH Pathogens and Congenital Infections

TORCH infections represent the major causes of congenital infection and include Toxoplasma, Other agents (parvovirus, varicella, hepatitis), Rubella, Cytomegalovirus, Herpes, HIV, and Syphilis. These pathogens can traverse the placental barrier and cause significant fetal and neonatal morbidity and mortality. Most congenital infections are preventable and treatable, especially if early diagnosed, leading to cure and control. A critical clinical principle is that if one congenital infection is present, clinicians must look for others.

### Bacterial Pathogens in Acquired Neonatal Sepsis

Gram-positive organisms causing acquired neonatal sepsis include Group B Streptococcus (GBS), Streptococcus, Staphylococcus, and Listeria monocytogenes. Gram-negative organisms include Escherichia coli (E. coli) and Klebsiella. These organisms are typically acquired from maternal genital flora or the environment rather than transplacentally.

### Clinical Presentation of Neonatal Infections

The clinical signs of neonatal sepsis are variable and nonspecific. Temperature instability is a hallmark finding. Respiratory manifestations include respiratory distress and apnoea. Feeding problems and vomiting indicate systemic illness. Neurological signs include lethargy and refusal to suckle. Dermatological findings include pallor, petechiae, and mottling of skin. Jaundice (both physiological and pathological) may be present. Hemodynamic signs include poor perfusion. Metabolic derangement is reflected in unstable blood glucose.

HIV-exposed neonates in the nursery are mostly asymptomatic; however, they may have other neonatal problems such as prematurity, meconium aspiration syndrome, or hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy.

Syphilis presentation is particularly insidious. Infected neonates can be asymptomatic and missed if maternal status is not checked. The condition may become symptomatic later as late congenital syphilis (presentation up to 5 years of age), representing a tragic lost opportunity for early intervention. Early congenital syphilis presents with pneumonia (respiratory distress), rash, and hepatomegaly. Often one or more features are present, and clinicians must look for others including long bone X-ray findings. Late congenital syphilis includes earlier features with permanent morbidity and difficult treatment.

Key maternal factors in presenting congenital infections in the nursery are maternal history, exposure history, and disease state, which are key to recognizing congenital infections.

### Investigations for Neonatal Infection

Nonspecific investigations include Full Blood Count (FBC), platelet count assessment, and white cell count (normal 5-20). The immature to total (IT) neutrophil ratio greater than 0.2 is predictive of infection. C Reactive protein is a useful inflammatory marker.

Specific culture investigations include blood, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), urine, and superficial swabs. Serology for congenital infection is important for suspected TORCH infections.

Radiological investigations include X-rays of areas suspected to be affected.

Placental histology is important (noted as significant: NB) for diagnosis of congenital infections, particularly CMV (Cytomegalovirus), Rubella, Herpes, and Toxoplasmosis, which require specific laboratory tests and placental histology review.

### Management of Neonatal Sepsis

Management of neonatal sepsis addresses both supportive care and antimicrobial therapy. Supportive care involves fluid management, electrolyte correction, glucose correction, respiratory support, and cardiovascular support. The specific approach depends on whether the infection is local versus systemic, congenital versus nosocomial.

First-line antimicrobial therapy for suspected neonatal sepsis is penicillin with or without aminoglycoside. Treatment duration depends on the site of infection: proven bacterial septicaemia in a neonate should be treated for 7 to 10 days, while confirmed bacterial meningitis in a neonate requires 21 days of antimicrobial therapy.

### Prevention of Congenital Infections

Prevention strategies for congenital infections center on improved antenatal care and maternal screening. Syphilis testing must be performed and checked during pregnancy with clear documentation of results and treatment if needed per DOH guidelines. Prevention of Mother-To-Child Transmission (PMTCT) with ARV fast-tracking is essential for HIV-exposed neonates. TB screening during pregnancy identifies mothers with active disease. Education about respiratory and viral pathogen exposure reduces risk of acquisition during pregnancy.

The PMTCT 2020 antenatal strategy emphasizes Test, Test, Test at the 1st antenatal visit and every BANC (Basic Antenatal Care) visit, with an ideal target of 8 visits for optimal screening. Additional screening during PMTCT antenatal care includes viral load monitoring, TB screening and opportunistic infection (OI) screening, and TPR (Treponema pallidum—syphilis) testing.

The PMTCT 2020 treatment strategy uses Option B+ 2015: Start all HIV-positive pregnant women on ART as soon as possible. In labour, if tested positive, give TDF, 3TC, DLG (FDC) stat and continue lifelong next day with ART counselling and CHW (Community Health Worker) referral.

### Prevention of Acquired and Nosocomial Infections

Prevention strategies for acquired and nosocomial neonatal infections include early skin-to-skin contact with mother (Kangaroo Mother Care), exclusive breastfeeding, strict hand washing, aseptic technique during procedures, and limiting duration of ventilation with active weaning.

### South African Epidemiology and PMTCT

The impact of congenital infections is significant in South African maternal and child health. HIV is the 3rd leading cause of maternal mortality in South Africa. The infections prioritized in South African neonatal care are HIV/PMTCT, Syphilis (commonest cause of congenital infection in the SA context), TB, Hepatitis B (HBV), Malaria, Listeria monocytogenes, and COVID-19.

Congenital tuberculosis carries a high mortality burden, with 43.48% mortality (range 33.9-52.6%) reported in affected neonates.

The significance of maternal HIV testing frequency in PMTCT is critical: testing should occur at the first antenatal visit and ideally at every BANC visit. The peak mortality age of HIV-infected infants in South Africa is 2-4 months. Birth PCR (BPCR) implementation allows earlier diagnosis, enabling initiation of treatment before peak mortality. The target timeframe for ART initiation in HIV-infected neonates with reactive BPCR is within 7 days of birth, or at the 1st postnatal visit in primary health care settings.

If congenital syphilis is diagnosed, clinicians must actively seek HIV, TB, Hepatitis B, and malaria—a reminder that if one congenital infection is present, other infections must be excluded.

Special considerations apply to HIV-exposed premature neonates less than 35 weeks gestation, who require modified neonatal treatment regimens appropriate for prematurity and gestational age. Postnatal maternal care in PMTCT provides opportunities to care for the mother through postnatal check (CHW) and EPI (Expanded Programme on Immunization) visits.

Syphilis in pregnancy must be managed as a notifiable condition requiring appropriate follow-up and treatment in the postnatal period per DOH guidelines.

The DOH 4-Pillar approach to HIV prevention (Pillars 1+2) includes testing, couples counselling and partner testing, safe sex education (dual contraception), PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis), PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis), VMMC (Voluntary Medical Male Circumcision), and management of discordant couples.

### Learning Objectives and Clinical Integration

The comprehensive learning goal for neonatal congenital infections is that most congenital infections are preventable and treatable, especially if early diagnosed with good outcomes (cure and control). If one infection is present, clinicians must look for others. The primary focus infections for neonatal care in South Africa are HIV/PMTCT and Syphilis (commonest causes). TB, Hepatitis B (HBV), and Malaria require a structured clinical approach per DOH guidelines. CMV (Cytomegalovirus), Rubella, Herpes, and Toxoplasmosis require knowledge of specific diagnostic tests and placental histology.

### Reference Standards

The reference standards for neonatal infection management in South Africa are the 2020 Neonatal Guidelines and Drug Doses by Joolay and Horn (5th edition) and the National DOH PMTCT Guideline 2020.
