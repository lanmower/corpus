# COMA  COVULSION

## Lecture Notes

### ETAT Protocol and Emergency Assessment

The ETAT (Emergency Triage Assessment and Treatment) protocol provides a systematic framework for assessing and managing children presenting with coma and convulsions. The emergency triage signs follow the A-B-C-D approach: A represents Airway assessment, B represents Breathing assessment, C encompasses Circulation status along with evaluation for Coma and Convulsion, and D assesses for severe Dehydration. This hierarchical approach ensures that life-threatening conditions are identified and addressed in priority order.

### Consciousness Assessment Using the AVPU Scale

A critical skill in managing comatose children is the rapid assessment of consciousness using the AVPU scale. This simple but powerful tool classifies consciousness into four levels. A (Alert) indicates the child is fully awake and responsive. V (responding to Voice) indicates the child responds when spoken to. P (responding only to Pain) indicates the child responds only to painful stimuli. U (Unresponsive) describes a child who is unresponsive to both voice and pain. Importantly, coma is clinically defined as either P or U on the AVPU scale—that is, any child responding only to pain or who is completely unresponsive is considered to be in coma and requires immediate intervention.

### Recognition of Convulsions and Clinical Signs

The clinical presentation of a convulsing child is characteristic and recognizable. The first sign that may indicate convulsions is a sudden loss of consciousness. This is typically accompanied by involuntary movements: uncontrolled jerky movements of the limbs and face, stiffening of the arms and legs, or twitching of the face, hands, and feet. Abnormal eye movements frequently accompany motor manifestations. A key respiratory sign is the occurrence of brief apnoeas (temporary cessation of breathing) during the seizure. Recognition of these signs is essential for rapid diagnosis and treatment initiation.

### Airway and Breathing Management in Coma and Convulsions

The first step in treating any child with coma or active convulsions is to manage the airway and breathing. Proper positioning is fundamental: infants should be placed in the neutral position, while older children are positioned in the sniffing position to optimize airway patency. If trauma is suspected, the jaw thrust maneuver should be used to open the airway without flexing the cervical spine, protecting against potential spinal injury.

Gentle suctioning is performed if needed to clear secretions, but excessive or vigorous suctioning must be avoided as it can cause additional harm. Critically, nothing should ever be placed into the mouth of a convulsing child—not even a bite block or airway adjunct—as this risks aspiration and airway obstruction. Once the airway is secured and positioning optimized, supplemental oxygen should be administered via face mask to ensure adequate oxygenation.

### Hypoglycaemia Screening and Treatment

A crucial part of the emergency assessment of any child with coma or convulsions is checking blood glucose. This is so important that the mnemonic D-E-F-G is used: Don't Ever Forget Glucose. Hypoglycaemia is defined as a blood glucose level below 3 mmol/L (equivalent to 55 mg/dL). Once IV access is established and blood samples are drawn for emergency investigations, the glucose level should be checked immediately using point-of-care testing such as Dextrostix.

If blood glucose is confirmed to be low (< 3 mmol/L or 55 mg/dL), or if Dextrostix testing is not available, IV glucose must be administered promptly. The treatment dose is 5 ml/kg of 10% glucose solution, given rapidly by intravenous injection. After seizure control is achieved, blood glucose must be rechecked to ensure that hypoglycaemia has not recurred, as recurrent hypoglycaemia can precipitate further seizures.

### Seizure Complications and Status Epilepticus

Prolonged seizure activity carries serious consequences. Status epilepticus is defined as a seizure lasting more than 5 minutes, and it represents a medical emergency. Prolonged seizures cause significant complications: brain swelling (cerebral edema), brain damage from neuronal cell death, aspiration of vomitus with risk of choking and airway compromise, and ultimately death if not rapidly treated. The urgency of rapid seizure termination cannot be overstated, as neurological damage begins within minutes of seizure onset.

### First-Line Anticonvulsant Therapy: Benzodiazepines

Once airway and breathing are secured and hypoglycaemia has been excluded or treated, the focus shifts to stopping the seizure using anticonvulsant medications. Benzodiazepines are the first-line class of drugs used for seizure control in acute convulsions. Lorazepam is the first-choice benzodiazepine for acute seizure management. If Lorazepam is unavailable, Midazolam is the second choice. If neither Lorazepam nor Midazolam is available, Diazepam is the third choice.

The preferred route for benzodiazepine administration is intravenous (IV), which provides the most rapid onset of action. However, when IV access cannot be immediately obtained, alternative routes are available: both Lorazepam and Midazolam can be administered via intramuscular (IMI) or buccal routes. Diazepam, if IV access is not available, is given by the rectal (PR) route.

A critical safety consideration applies to neonates (infants less than 4 weeks old): benzodiazepines should be avoided without concurrent ventilatory support because of the high risk of apnoea (respiratory depression) in this age group.

### Second-Line Anticonvulsant Therapy

If seizures continue despite benzodiazepine administration, the next drug of choice is Phenobarbitone (phenobarbital). Phenobarbitone is also the preferred first-line anticonvulsant for neonates (children less than 4 weeks old) when benzodiazepines cannot be used due to ventilatory constraints.

If IV Phenobarbitone is not available, Phenytoin may be used as an alternative. However, Phenytoin has specific administration requirements: it must be given as an infusion over a minimum of 30 minutes. During Phenytoin infusion, cardiac monitoring is essential. If any cardiac complications occur—such as arrhythmia, bradycardia, or hypotension—the infusion must be stopped immediately. Once the child's cardiac status has stabilized, the infusion can be resumed at a slower rate, infusing over 1 hour instead of 30 minutes.

### Post-Seizure Management and Aftercare

After a seizure has been controlled, comprehensive post-seizure assessment and management are essential. The post-seizure assessment begins by rechecking the A-B-C parameters: Airway patency, Breathing adequacy, and Circulation status. The child must be continuously monitored for any further seizure activity, as recurrent seizures are common in certain conditions.

The child should be placed in the recovery position (on the side) to prevent aspiration of any gastric contents. Blood glucose must be rechecked to ensure hypoglycaemia has not recurred. Emergency laboratory investigations that were drawn at the time of IV access should be reviewed to identify potential underlying causes, particularly infection. If infection is suspected or confirmed, appropriate antimicrobial therapy should be initiated—antibiotics for bacterial infections or antimalarials for malaria-related seizures. Ongoing monitoring and supportive care are continued based on the identified cause and the child's clinical response.

### Learning Objectives Summary

The core competencies for managing coma and convulsions in children are: (1) the ability to assess consciousness accurately using the AVPU coma score, (2) the ability to identify the child in coma (P or U on AVPU), (3) the ability to recognize and identify a child having convulsions, and (4) the ability to provide appropriate emergency treatment for a child presenting with either coma or convulsions. Mastery of the ETAT protocol and the systematic approach outlined above ensures optimal outcomes for these critically ill children.
