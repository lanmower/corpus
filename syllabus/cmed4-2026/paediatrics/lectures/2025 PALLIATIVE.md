## Lecture Notes

### Definition and Scope of Palliative Care

Palliative care represents a fundamental shift in medical approach—from cure-focused intervention to comprehensive care addressing suffering. According to the International Association for Hospice and Palliative Care (IAHPC), palliative care is the active holistic care of individuals across all ages with serious health-related suffering due to severe illness, and especially of those near the end of life. It aims to improve the quality of life of patients, their families and their caregivers.

In the paediatric context, this definition becomes more intimate and family-centred. Palliative care for children is the active total care of the child's body, mind and spirit, and also involves giving support to the family. This holistic approach acknowledges that paediatric palliative care extends beyond the patient to encompass the entire family unit as an integrated component of care delivery.

### Timing and Approach to Paediatric Palliative Care

A crucial distinction in paediatric palliative care lies in its timing. Contrary to the historical view that palliative care begins only at end-of-life, paediatric palliative care begins when illness is diagnosed, and continues regardless of whether or not a child receives treatment directed at the disease. This approach allows for continuity of care whether the trajectory involves curative attempts, disease modification, or purely comfort-focused measures.

The effective delivery of paediatric palliative care requires a broad multidisciplinary approach that includes the family and makes use of available community resources. Importantly, it can be successfully implemented even if resources are limited. This principle is especially relevant in resource-constrained settings, where creative adaptation of available services—whether in hospitals or communities—enables meaningful palliative support.

### Settings for Delivery

Paediatric palliative care is not confined to institutional settings. Care may be provided in tertiary care facilities, in community health centres, and even in the child's own home. This flexibility allows families to choose environments that best suit their preferences, values, and practical circumstances, supporting the goal of maximizing quality of life across diverse contexts.

### Core Provider Responsibilities

Health providers delivering paediatric palliative care must evaluate and alleviate a child's physical, psychological and social distress. This three-dimensional understanding of distress ensures that symptom management addresses not only bodily discomfort but also emotional suffering and social disadvantage. The comprehensive assessment of these domains forms the foundation for individualised, responsive care planning.

### Classification of Conditions Requiring Palliative Care

Understanding the trajectory and prognosis of childhood illnesses is central to appropriate palliative care planning. Two key classification schemes help clarify the context:

**Life-Limiting versus Life-Threatening Conditions:**

A life-limiting condition is defined as a condition for which there is no reasonable hope of cure and from which children will die. These are conditions with an inherently progressive course toward death.

In contrast, a life-threatening condition is defined as a condition for which curative treatment may be feasible but can fail. Life-threatening conditions carry the possibility of cure but also risk of treatment failure and death.

This distinction is important because it affects both the emphasis of medical intervention and the psychological preparation needed by families. A child with a life-threatening condition may still pursue curative measures while simultaneously preparing for potential palliative focus. A child with a life-limiting condition requires palliative care as the primary framework, though some disease-directed treatments may still be offered for comfort or quality of life extension.

### The ACT Classification Framework

The Association for Children's Palliative Care (ACT) has developed a four-category classification that provides more granularity in understanding which children benefit from palliative care services. This framework is widely used across paediatric settings to identify children requiring specialist palliative input and to understand the complexity of their care needs.

**ACT Category 1: Life-Threatening Conditions with Possible Cure**

ACT Category 1 comprises life-threatening conditions where curative treatment may be possible. Examples include Acute lymphoblastic leukaemia, other curable cancers, pulmonary TB, and organ failure when transplant is available. Children in this category may have intensive curative treatment as their primary focus, but they benefit from integrated palliative care that manages side effects, prepares families for possible treatment failure, and provides support should curative efforts not succeed.

**ACT Category 2: Prolonged Survival with Intensive Treatment**

ACT Category 2 encompasses conditions where premature death is inevitable, but children have long periods of wellness because of treatments. Examples include Cystic fibrosis, Duchenne's muscular dystrophy, HIV/AIDS on HAART, and renal failure where dialysis is available. Children with Category 2 conditions often enjoy extended periods of relatively good health between episodes of acute illness. Palliative care focuses on optimizing quality during wellness periods, managing complications, and preparing for inevitable progression.

**ACT Category 3: Progressive Conditions Requiring Exclusive Palliative Treatment**

ACT Category 3 includes progressive conditions where treatment is exclusively palliative. Examples are Batten disease, Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA), Mucopolysaccharidoses, inoperable heart disease, organ failure without transplant, Adrenoleukodystrophy (ALD), Trisomy 13 and 18, renal failure without dialysis, and HIV without HAART. For these conditions, curative treatment is not available, and palliative care forms the entire therapeutic framework. The focus is on maximizing comfort, maintaining dignity, and supporting families through progressive decline.

**ACT Category 4: Irreversible but Non-Progressive Conditions**

ACT Category 4 encompasses irreversible but non-progressive conditions with high risk of unpredictable life-threatening events. Examples include multiple disabilities following brain or spinal cord injury, complex healthcare needs, severe cerebral palsy, Foetal Alcohol Syndrome, birth asphyxia, Down's Syndrome, and Sickle Cell Anaemia. Children in this category have fixed neurological or physical impairments but may live for many years. Palliative care addresses quality of life, manages episodic crises, and prevents complications. Unlike Category 3 conditions, progression is not the defining feature—rather, the unpredictability of life-threatening events and the complexity of ongoing care needs drive palliative input.

### The GAPS Framework: Core Domains of Paediatric Palliative Care

A comprehensive mnemonic framework guides the essential elements of paediatric palliative care: GAPS—Goals of care, Advance Care Planning, Psychosocial support, and Symptom Control. These four pillars ensure that all major dimensions of care are systematically addressed.

### Goals of Care: Defining Care Trajectories

Goals of Care conversations establish what the care team and family are trying to achieve. These conversations must occur early and revisited regularly, as circumstances and family understanding evolve.

The three intervention levels that structure Goals of Care discussions are Cure, Rehabilitation, and Comfort. At any point in a child's illness, care may emphasise one or more of these levels. Some children may move from pursuing cure, to rehabilitation during periods of stability, to comfort as disease progresses. Others may hold multiple goals simultaneously—for example, seeking cure while ensuring comfort if cure is not achieved.

Key questions that should be considered in Goals of Care conversations include: What are you trying to achieve? (Cure? Rehab? Comfort?) and How aggressive should we be? (ICU, blood transfusions, antibiotics?). These questions help families articulate their values and priorities, and help clinicians align their interventions with family wishes.

### Advance Care Planning: Anticipating the Future

Advance Care Planning is the process of discussing and documenting what matters most to a child and family, and what care they want as illness progresses. This includes several key components:

First, Advance Care Planning must revisit and clarify goals of care, ensuring they remain relevant to the child's current situation. Second, it specifies place of care—where the child and family wish care to be provided during different phases of illness and at the end of life. Third, it involves scenario sketching and 'what if' situations, helping families envision and prepare emotionally for possible futures rather than being shocked by them. Fourth, it addresses resuscitation decisions, including DNACPR (Do Not Attempt Cardio-Pulmonary Resuscitation) and what it means in practice for that particular child. Finally, it identifies medications required—both those to continue and those to discontinue, and what medications should be available if symptoms worsen.

### Psychosocial Support: Addressing the Full Burden of Illness

Paediatric illness affects the child and family across emotional, spiritual, social, and economic dimensions. Psychosocial support issues that must be addressed in paediatric palliative care include:

Emotional issues such as fear and anxiety for both mother and child require skilled, compassionate assessment and intervention. Spiritual issues—including guilt and meaning—should be explored using structured approaches such as the FICA tool (Faith, Importance, Community, Address). Education access must be preserved, allowing children to continue schooling if they wish, or receive age-appropriate education adapted to their abilities. Play access and ability are essential to childhood—even seriously ill children benefit from play and age-appropriate activities that foster normalcy and joy. Financial issues such as housing and food security often emerge; families may require social work intervention to access financial support or practical resources. Bereavement support extends care beyond the child's death, providing structured grief support for surviving family members. Legacy planning allows children and families to create enduring memories and meaningful documents for the future.

### Symptom Control: Managing Physical Suffering

The physical symptoms that most commonly require active management in paediatric palliative care are Pain, dyspnoea, cough, nausea and vomiting, thirst, and fatigue. Effective symptom control is foundational to quality of life and dignity. Each symptom requires individualised assessment, often drawing on pharmacological and non-pharmacological approaches. Pain assessment and management are particularly critical in paediatrics, as children may have difficulty expressing pain and myths about opioid use can delay necessary treatment. Dyspnoea and cough significantly affect comfort and sense of security. Nausea and vomiting can be debilitating and affect nutrition and quality of life. Thirst, often overlooked, can cause significant discomfort as illness advances. Fatigue, while seemingly less dramatic than acute symptoms, profoundly affects a child's capacity to engage with family, school, and activities that give life meaning.

### Additional Considerations

Osteogenesis imperfecta is a condition characterised by brittle bones, and may be encountered in paediatric practice with its own palliative care considerations, particularly around pain management, prevention of fractures, and mobility support.
