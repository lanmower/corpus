## Lecture Notes

### Definition and Characteristics of Childhood

A child is legally defined as a person below the age of 18 years. Childhood is not merely a chronological phase but a complex state characterized by growth, development, vulnerability, and dependency. These characteristics are shaped by the inherent abilities of the individual child, the family setting in which they develop, and the broader social context surrounding them. Understanding childhood in this multidimensional way is fundamental to appreciating why children require special protections and distinct legal frameworks from those applicable to adults.

### Basic Needs of Children

Children have fundamental needs that must be met to ensure their survival, health, and development. These basic needs encompass food, water, shelter, and protection from harm. Beyond these physiological requirements, children require access to health care services and a supportive family environment that provides love and stimulation. Critically, children must be protected from harmful situations, including involvement in armed conflict. The recognition of these comprehensive needs forms the foundation for all child rights legislation.

### United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child

The United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC) represents a watershed moment in international child protection. Developed in 1989 and implemented in 1990, the CRC recognizes the unique status, needs, and rights of children. The preamble of the Convention explicitly states that children need special care and protection, and it defines a child as any person under 18 years of age. South Africa ratified the CRC in 1995, committing itself to the principles and obligations outlined in the Convention.

#### General Principles of the CRC

The CRC is built upon four general principles that guide all its provisions. First is the right to survival and development, ensuring that children have access to the resources necessary to live and grow. Second is the right to equality and non-discrimination, protecting all children from prejudice based on any characteristic. Third is the duty to promote the best interests of children, requiring that children's welfare be a paramount consideration in all matters affecting them. Fourth is the right to be listened to and taken seriously, also framed as participation, recognizing that children have voices worth hearing in decisions that affect their lives.

#### Civil and Political Rights of Children

The CRC guarantees children a range of civil and political rights essential to their dignity and autonomy. These include the right to life, survival, and development; the right to equality and non-discrimination; and the right to dignity. Children have the right to birth registration, a name, and nationality, establishing their legal identity. The Convention protects the right to participation and the right to information, enabling children to engage with the decisions and information that affect them. Children are guaranteed freedom of expression and privacy and confidentiality. The CRC protects the right to physical and psychological integrity, the right to access courts, and the right to protection from abuse and exploitation.

#### Socio-economic Rights of Children

The CRC recognizes that children's wellbeing depends on access to social and economic resources. Children have the right to life, survival, and development, along with the right to an adequate standard of living. The Convention guarantees children the right to health and healthcare services, the right to social security including social assistance, and the right to education. Children have the right to water and sanitation, to housing and shelter, and to adequate food and nutrition. The CRC also protects the right to a clean, unpolluted environment and the right to protection from neglect, recognizing the interconnectedness of environmental and child welfare issues.

#### Child-Specific Rights

Beyond the rights shared with adults, the CRC identifies rights that are particular to children due to their developmental stage and vulnerability. These include the right to rest, leisure, and play, which recognize that childhood is not solely preparation for adulthood. Children have the right to family, parental, or alternative care, and the right to special protection within the criminal justice system when they come into contact with the law. Unaccompanied foreign children receive special protections. The CRC emphasizes that children's best interests must be of paramount importance in matters concerning them, and children have the right to be heard and taken seriously. Importantly, children have the right to guidance that respects their evolving capacities—meaning that the level of autonomy granted to a child should grow as they mature. Finally, the CRC protects children from being used in armed conflict.

### African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child

The African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child was adopted in 1996 by the Organization of African Unity (OAU) to address the specific contexts and challenges of children in Africa. South Africa ratified the African Charter in 2000, becoming one of 38 states that have committed to its provisions. The purpose of the African Charter is to extend the principles of the CRC to include issues specific to children's rights in the African context, acknowledging that African children face particular vulnerabilities and require targeted protections grounded in continental values and realities.

### South African Constitution and Bill of Rights

The South African Constitution provides explicit protections for the rights of all people, including children. Section 12(2)(c) of the Constitution guarantees everyone—including children—the right to bodily and psychological integrity, which includes the right not to be subjected to medical or scientific experiments without their informed consent. This section forms the legal foundation for medical consent principles in South African law. Section 27(1)(a) ensures that everybody has the right to have access to health care services, establishing healthcare as a fundamental right rather than a privilege. Section 28(2) places paramount importance on the best interests of the child, stating that a child's best interests are of paramount importance in every matter concerning the child.

The South African Bill of Rights in Section 28 enumerates specific rights for every child. These include the right to family care or equivalent care arrangements, access to basic nutrition, shelter, and health and social services. Every child has the right to protection from harm and exploitation, the right not to work (protecting them from child labor), the right not to be detained except as a last resort, the right to legal representation, and the right not to participate in armed conflict. These provisions reflect South Africa's commitment to treating children not merely as future citizens but as rights-bearing persons deserving of protection and provision in the present.

### Age Thresholds in South African Law

South African legislation establishes specific age thresholds at which children acquire new legal capacities and responsibilities. The age of majority is 18 years, the point at which a person becomes a fully autonomous adult in law. However, the law recognizes that children mature at different rates and grants certain capacities at earlier ages depending on the context.

A child can obtain an identity document at 16 years, as provided in the Identification Act section 15. Under the South African Schools Act section 3(1), a child may leave school at 15 years upon completion of Grade 9. The law permits children to work legally from 15 years of age, as specified in the Basic Conditions of Employment Act sections 43 and 55(6)(b), though employment of children is subject to strict protections regarding the nature and hours of work. The right to choose domicile—that is, to establish one's place of legal residence—is granted at 18 years under the Domicile Act section 1.

Sexual consent thresholds reflect the law's concern with protecting children from sexual abuse. A child is deemed incapable of consenting to sexual activity until 12 years of age; from 12 years, the law recognizes a capacity to consent but subjects such consent to heightened scrutiny. At 16 years, the law presumes a child is capable of consenting to sexual activity, as provided in the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act sections 15 and 16 read with section 1(1)(b) and section 56(2).

Medical consent capacity is recognized at 12 years with sufficient maturity. A child can donate blood at 14 years, as provided in the Human Tissue Act section 8(aa). The termination of pregnancy can be accessed at any age under the Choice on Termination of Pregnancy Act sections 5(2) and 5(3), reflecting the principle that reproductive autonomy is not contingent on adult status.

### Medical Consent Rules in South African Law

South African medical law recognizes that consent capacity is age-dependent and maturity-dependent, varying by the nature of the medical intervention. For surgical treatment, children above 12 years who demonstrate sufficient maturity and capacity can consent to their own surgical care. Children below 12 years, or those above 12 years who lack sufficient maturity and capacity, require the assistance of a parent in addition to their own consent or assent to surgical treatment.

Access to contraceptives and condoms operates under different principles. Regardless of a child's age, maturity, or formal legal capacity, a child can access contraceptives, but this access requires medical advice and examination to ensure the choice is informed and medically appropriate.

For HIV testing, children above 12 years who demonstrate sufficient maturity and capacity can consent to testing. Children below 12 years are deemed to have sufficient maturity but not capacity, meaning they can participate in the decision-making process, but parental involvement is required for the test to proceed. Importantly, for all children regardless of age, pre- and post-test counseling is mandatory, ensuring that the child understands the implications of testing and receives support with the results.

Male circumcision presents a distinct legal framework. Boys above 16 years can undergo circumcision regardless of their individual maturity or capacity to consent; the age threshold alone grants this right. Importantly, every male child retains the right to refuse circumcision, ensuring that this medical procedure is not imposed without some form of assent.

For general medical treatment, the law distinguishes between minors above and below 18 years. For medical treatment of children above 18 years (effectively for 18-year-old minors still under parental authority in some contexts), the law requires both the consent of the parent and the consent or assent of the minor. For children below 18 years receiving general medical treatment, those with sufficient maturity but not formal capacity can be treated on the basis of their wishes, provided the health professional advises the minor to consult with their parent and respects the minor's decision where it appears sound.

### Health Care Principles

Health professionals working with children must be guided by key principles that ensure child-centered, rights-respecting care. First, practitioners must recognize vulnerability in newborns, children, and adults, acknowledging that vulnerability does not diminish human dignity but rather indicates a need for specific protections and support. Health care for children should be holistic, encouraging play and stimulation as vital components of healing and development, not incidental to medical care. Appropriate support must be ensured for each child based on their developmental stage and individual needs.

Health professionals must create child-friendly environments that reduce fear and promote engagement with healthcare. This involves age-appropriate communication, comfort measures, and a physical environment designed for children. Participation should be actively fostered, ensuring that children receive information suited to their understanding, that their assent or consent is obtained, and that their voices influence decisions about their care. Finally, health professionals must facilitate access to suitable services beyond the medical encounter, including assisting with vital registration and ensuring that families know about and can access child grants and support programs.

### Social Assistance and Child Grants

The Social Assistance Act in South Africa establishes the framework for government grants that provide crucial support to vulnerable children and families. The Act controls the distribution of grants and targets assistance to the poor; consequently, most grants available to families with children have a means test to ensure resources are directed where need is greatest. Multiple types of grants are available, including the Child Support Grant, Foster Care Grant, Care Dependency Grant, Temporary Grant-in-Aid, and Social Relief of Distress.

#### Child Support Grant

The Child Support Grant serves the purpose of poverty alleviation by providing cash support to caregivers of poor children. To be eligible for the Child Support Grant, the child must be less than 18 years of age and the caregiver must be older than 18 years. The income limits for the Child Support Grant are single parent earnings below R 48,000 per annum or married couple earnings below R 96,000 per annum. The benefits of the Child Support Grant include annual revision of the grant amount and the ability to claim all biological children in the family, with a maximum of six non-biological children per caregiver.

To apply for the Child Support Grant, applicants must provide proof of income, marriage or divorce certificates, and proof of guardianship. Applications are made through the local Department of Welfare office. The grant terminates when the child turns 18 years of age, upon the death of the child or primary caretaker, or if the primary caretaker ceases to care for the child.

#### Foster Care Grant

The Foster Care Grant is designed to ensure appropriate alternative care for children not cared for by their biological parents. Eligibility requires that both the applicant and child be residents of South Africa, that a court order indicates foster care status, and that the foster parent be a South African citizen, permanent resident, or refugee. Additionally, the child must remain in the care of the foster parent or parents. The Foster Care Grant provides R1040 per month to support the costs of caring for a foster child.

#### Care Dependency Grant

The Care Dependency Grant supports parents whose ability to work is limited because they are caring for a child with severe disability. The child must be under 18 years of age. The applicant must be a South African citizen, permanent resident, or refugee, and must be resident in South Africa. The income limits for the Care Dependency Grant are single parent earnings below R202,800 per annum or family earnings below R405,600 per annum. The Care Dependency Grant provides R1860 per month to support the care of children with severe disabilities.

### Children's Act

The Children's Act represents a comprehensive legislative response to the needs of children in South Africa. The aims of the Children's Act are threefold: to support families in enabling them to ensure their children's well-being, to prevent abuse and neglect of children, and to ensure that children in need of care and protection receive appropriate care. The Act is particularly important for health professionals, who should view it as a one-stop reference for multiple child-related issues. The Children's Act provides guidance on child consent to medical treatment and surgical operations, HIV testing and disclosure, consent when a child lacks legal capacity, the age at which a child can access contraceptives, mandatory reporting requirements in cases of abuse and neglect, and definitions of what constitutes a child in need of care and protection. The Act also clarifies the legal definition and responsibilities of caregivers, providing health professionals with clear terms to guide their interactions with families.

### Progressive Realisation and the Reasonableness Test

Child rights are increasingly understood through the lens of progressive realisation, a concept embedded in international human rights law. Progressive realisation imposes an obligation on the state to move as expeditiously and effectively as possible toward the full realization of children's rights. This is not an absolute obligation to achieve all rights immediately, but rather an obligation to make continuous progress. Plans for progressive realisation must include clear goals, specific timeframes, identification of responsible parties, and allocation of sufficient funds to support implementation. The state's efforts are assessed through the Reasonableness Test, which evaluates whether laws, policies, and programs are reasonably designed and implemented to advance children's rights.

The Reasonableness Test comprises several key components. First, conceptualisation requires that the design of a law, policy, or program must be such that it can reasonably be expected to support the realisation of the right. Second, the approach must be comprehensive, coherent, and coordinated across different government departments and levels of government. Third, appropriate financial and human resource allocation is essential; good intentions unsupported by resources cannot satisfy the test. Fourth, reasonable implementation means that the program is actually executed as designed, and that barriers to access are identified and addressed. Fifth, transparency and public knowledge are required; people cannot access rights they do not know exist. Finally, the approach must be balanced and flexible, capable of meeting short-term, urgent needs while working toward medium-term and long-term sustainable solutions. The Reasonableness Test provides a framework for holding states accountable for their progress toward children's rights, moving beyond mere rhetoric to tangible, measured advancement in children's lives.
