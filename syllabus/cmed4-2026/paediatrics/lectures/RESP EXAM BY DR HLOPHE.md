# RESP EXAM BY DR HLOPHE

## Lecture Notes

### Introduction and Communication

Before beginning your examination of a paediatric patient, establish rapport with both the child and their parents or guardians. Introduce yourself clearly, stating your name and your role. This foundational step is essential for building trust, particularly with anxious children. Following your introduction, confirm key identifying information: ask for and verify the child's full name and date of birth. These details are not merely administrative—they ensure patient safety and help you identify any developmental landmarks or age-related norms against which you'll interpret your findings.

### General Appearance and Initial Observation

A systematic general observation begins the moment you set eyes on your patient. Assess the child's activity level and alertness: does the child appear engaged and interactive, or quiet and listless? Lethargy may signal serious illness. Next, observe the child's weight distribution and overall nutritional status, noting whether they appear a healthy weight for their stated age and height. 

Look for cyanosis, a bluish discolouration of the skin that can arise from two distinct mechanisms: poor peripheral circulation due to hypovolaemia with peripheral vasoconstriction, or inadequate oxygenation of blood such as occurs with right-to-left cardiac shunting. Pallor—paleness of the skin—suggests either underlying anaemia from blood dyscrasia or chronic disease, or poor perfusion from conditions like congestive cardiac failure. 

Pay particular attention to syndromic features: assess the child's stature (tall or short) and look for distinctive facial features that may point to underlying genetic conditions. This systematic observation guides your subsequent focused examination.

### Respiratory Symptoms: Cough and Voice Changes

The character of a cough provides valuable diagnostic clues. A cough accompanied by wheeze suggests asthma or viral-induced wheeze, particularly in young children where viral wheeze is common. A productive cough—one where sputum is being cleared—indicates lower respiratory tract infection. A characteristic barking or seal-like cough points toward croup or laryngomalacia. In contrast, a dry, non-productive cough is typical of allergies or tuberculosis. Voice changes warrant equal attention. Hoarseness indicates laryngitis, while a distinctive "hot potato" quality to the voice (as if speaking with a hot object in the mouth) suggests peritonsillar abscess.

Stridor, a high-pitched breathing sound reflecting turbulent airflow through narrowed upper airways, comes in two varieties. Acute stridor is seen with croup, foreign body aspiration, bacterial tracheitis, and epiglottitis—conditions requiring prompt recognition. Chronic stridor suggests laryngomalacia or subglottic stenosis, conditions that may be developmental or acquired. Understanding these distinctions helps you triage urgency appropriately.

### Hands and Pulse Assessment

Examination of the hands reveals numerous signs relevant to respiratory and cardiac pathology. Pallor in the hands specifically suggests poor peripheral perfusion, a hallmark of congestive heart failure. Cyanosis of the hands indicates underlying hypoxaemia. Hand tremor, particularly if fine and rapid, suggests recent beta-2 agonist use such as salbutamol in an asthmatic child. The presence of eczema, particularly on the hands, indicates atopy and significantly increases the likelihood of asthma and allergic rhinitis in the child.

Finger clubbing—a distinctive soft tissue swelling of the terminal phalanx—is a sign of chronic lung disease. The three conditions most commonly associated with clubbing in children are bronchiectasis, cystic fibrosis, and primary ciliary dyskinesia. To assess for clubbing, use Schamroth's window: ask the child to place the tips of their index fingers back-to-back, nails facing each other. In healthy individuals, a small diamond-shaped space is visible between the nails. When clubbing develops, this characteristic window is lost. In younger children who cannot cooperate with this test, simply inspect the fingers for telltale soft tissue swelling of the terminal phalanx.

Pulse assessment varies by age. In older children and adolescents, palpate the radial pulse using the tips of your index and middle fingers to assess both rate and rhythm. Measure for a full 60 seconds to ensure accuracy, as heart rate irregularities may be intermittent. In infants and very young babies, however, assess the femoral pulse instead—it is more reliable and easier to locate than the radial pulse in this age group.

### Face, Eyes, Ears, and Nose

Examine the face systematically. Nasal flaring—outward movement of the nostrils with each breath—indicates increased work of breathing and should prompt you to look for other signs of respiratory distress. Micrognathia, an undersized jaw, is seen in genetic syndromes including Pierre Robin, Noonan, and Marfan syndromes, and can predispose to airway obstruction.

The eyes offer diagnostic clues. Inspect the conjunctiva by gently pulling down the lower eyelid. Conjunctival pallor suggests underlying anaemia. Hearing loss should raise your suspicion for primary ciliary dyskinesia, a systemic disorder affecting mucociliary clearance.

Nasal findings are equally informative. A deviated nasal septum may contribute to breathing difficulties or impaired airway patency. Nasal polyps are associated with atopy and with cystic fibrosis and warrant investigation in the appropriate clinical context.

### Mouth and Upper Airways

Central cyanosis—bluish discolouration specifically of the lips and/or tongue—indicates hypoxaemia and requires urgent evaluation. The causes include persistent pulmonary hypertension, bronchospasm from asthma, and lower respiratory tract infection. Central cyanosis differs from peripheral cyanosis and demands immediate attention.

Examine the palate by visual inspection of its full extent, looking for any cleft. Tonsillar hypertrophy indicates a history of recurrent tonsillitis and raises concern for possible airway obstruction, particularly if severe.

### Chest Wall and Respiratory Rate

The chest wall itself provides important structural and dynamic information. Pectus excavatum—a distinctive caved-in or sunken appearance of the chest—is associated with Marfan syndrome. Pectus cariniatum, in contrast, shows protrusion of the sternum and ribs, and is associated with Noonan syndrome. Asymmetry of chest wall movement suggests underlying pneumothorax or consolidation and warrants further investigation.

Harrison's sulcus, seen as indrawing or grooving of the chest wall, results from long-term diaphragmatic tug in children with poorly controlled asthma. Barrel chest, or hyper-expansion of the thorax, can be associated with both acute and chronic asthma and with chronic respiratory obstruction.

Respiratory rate must be age-appropriate. Tachypnoea is a sensitive sign of respiratory compromise in children. The normal ranges by age are as follows:

- **Newborns**: 40–60 breaths per minute
- **1 week to 3 months**: 30–50 breaths per minute
- **3 months to 2 years**: 30–40 breaths per minute
- **2 to 10 years**: 14–24 breaths per minute
- **Over 10 years**: 12–20 breaths per minute

### Work of Breathing and Cardiac Assessment

Increased work of breathing manifests through multiple signs that the experienced clinician recognises together. Watch for difficulty speaking in full sentences or difficulty feeding—both suggest the child is devoting significant energy to breathing. Expiratory grunting, a sound made as the child exhales against a partially closed glottis, represents an attempt to maintain positive end-expiratory pressure and keep alveoli open; its presence signals increased work of breathing.

Recession—inward indrawing of the chest wall during inspiration—occurs at multiple levels. Look for tracheal tug (visible pulling at the trachea in the neck), supraclavicular recession (above the collarbones), intercostal recession (between the ribs), and subcostal recession (below the rib cage). Accessory muscle use includes nasal flaring, abdominal breathing (using the belly muscles rather than the diaphragm), and head bobbing with respiration—all indicate the child is struggling to breathe.

The apex beat, where you can palpate the heartbeat at the chest wall, is normally located in the 5th intercostal space in the midclavicular line. Displacement of the apex beat reflects underlying cardiac or pulmonary pathology. Leftward displacement occurs with cardiomegaly (an enlarged heart), right pleural effusion, or right tension pneumothorax. Rightward displacement is seen with dextrocardia (the heart on the right side of the chest), diaphragmatic hernia, left pleural effusion, or left tension pneumothorax. The pattern of displacement helps localise pathology.

### Chest Expansion and Percussion

To assess chest expansion, place your hands on the chest wall just below the nipples, wrap your fingers around the sides of the thorax, and bring your thumbs together in the midline without touching the skin. During inspiration, the thumbs should move symmetrically upward and outward; during expiration, they should move downward and inward. Reduced movement of one thumb indicates reduced chest expansion on that side, suggesting lung collapse, pneumonia, or restrictive lung disease.

Tracheal deviation, assessed by palpating the trachea at the sternal notch, indicates displacement from midline. In pneumothorax and large pleural effusions, the trachea deviates away from the pathology—that is, toward the opposite side. Conversely, in lobar collapse or following pneumonectomy, the trachea deviates toward the pathology on the affected side. This distinction aids in differential diagnosis.

Percussion is a skill requiring careful technique. Position your non-dominant hand on the chest wall with the middle finger firmly pressed against the thorax. Using a brisk wrist swing, strike the middle phalanx of that middle finger with the middle finger of your dominant hand. Immediately remove the striking finger to avoid muffling the resulting percussion note. 

Percuss systematically across the chest: the supraclavicular region (lung apices), infraclavicular region, the chest wall at 3–4 locations bilaterally, and the axilla. Always compare findings side-to-side to detect asymmetry.

A resonant percussion note is the normal finding in healthy lung. Dullness to percussion indicates increased tissue density and may reflect cardiac dullness, consolidation, tumour, or lobar collapse. Stony dullness—a particularly flat, lifeless percussion note—is pathognomonic for pleural effusion, where fluid in the pleural space dampens vibrations. Hyper-resonance, conversely, indicates decreased tissue density and strongly suggests pneumothorax, where air in the pleural space enhances resonance.

### Auscultation: Breath Sounds and Added Sounds

Normal breath sounds heard on auscultation are vesicular in quality—a soft, rustling sound reflecting air moving through small airways and alveoli. Bronchial breath sounds, by contrast, are harsh and high-pitched, similar to what you hear when auscultating directly over the trachea. Bronchial breath sounds are characterised by equal duration of inspiration and expiration with a pause between phases. When you hear bronchial breath sounds over the periphery of the lung, they indicate consolidation—the lung tissue is filled with fluid or pus rather than air, so sound transmits directly without the normal filtering effect of air-filled alveoli.

Quiet or diminished breath sounds indicate reduced air entry and suggest either pleural effusion or pneumothorax, where air or fluid outside the lung prevents normal air movement.

Added sounds heard during auscultation carry specific meanings. Wheeze is a continuous, coarse, whistling sound produced in the respiratory airways during breathing. Wheeze indicates obstruction to airflow and is classically associated with asthma and bronchiectasis. Stridor, as mentioned earlier, is a high-pitched sound reflecting turbulent airflow through narrowed upper airways; it can be acute (croup, foreign body, bacterial tracheitis, epiglottitis) or chronic (foreign body, subglottic stenosis).

Coarse crackles are discontinuous, brief, popping lung sounds. Unlike wheeze, which is continuous, crackles sound like the crackling of Velcro or pop-pop-popping. Coarse crackles indicate the presence of fluid or secretions in larger airways and are heard in pneumonia, bronchiectasis, and pulmonary oedema.

### Vocal Resonance

Vocal resonance assessment complements auscultation of breath sounds. Ask the child to repeat a word or number consistently—"99" is commonly used—while you listen over different areas of the chest wall with your stethoscope. Compare the same location on both sides of the chest to detect asymmetry.

The volume at which the child's speech is transmitted to your stethoscope depends on the acoustic properties of the lung tissue beneath. Increased tissue density—as occurs with consolidation, tumour, or lobar collapse—transmits voice sounds more readily, resulting in increased vocal resonance. Conversely, the presence of fluid or air outside the lung (pleural effusion or pneumothorax) acts as a barrier, reducing the transmission of voice sounds and resulting in decreased vocal resonance. These findings help you localise and characterise pathology alongside your other examination findings.

### Lymph Node Assessment

Lymph node examination requires proper technique to be informative. Position yourself behind the child and use both hands to palpate the neck. This allows you to assess for asymmetry in size, consistency, and mobility of lymph nodes between the two sides—asymmetry may be more significant than absolute size.

Use the pads of your second, third, and fourth fingers (not the fingertips) to palpate. A critical safety consideration applies: do not compromise cerebral blood flow by pressing too hard or for too long on the neck vessels.

You should systematically assess the following lymph node chains: submental (beneath the chin), submandibular (under the jaw), pre-auricular (in front of the ears), post-auricular (behind the ears), superficial cervical, deep cervical, posterior cervical, and supraclavicular (above the collarbones). Enlargement of these nodes may reflect infection, inflammation, or other pathology and should be correlated with the clinical presentation.

### Oedema

Oedema—abnormal accumulation of fluid in tissues causing swelling—can be a sign of significant systemic disease. Begin your assessment by asking the parents whether they have noticed that the child looks puffy or swollen. This observation from those who see the child daily is invaluable.

On inspection, examine the limbs, the sacral area (the lower back), and the face for signs of oedema. The distribution and location of oedema depend on the child's age and mobility status. In mobile children, gravity pulls fluid downward, so dependent oedema appears in the lower legs and feet. In infants and non-mobile children, oedema may be more prominent in the sacral area or face. The presence of oedema warrants investigation into its cause: it may indicate renal disease, cardiac disease with poor perfusion, or other systemic conditions affecting fluid balance.

---

This comprehensive examination, conducted systematically and interpreted in clinical context, forms the foundation of paediatric respiratory assessment and guides appropriate investigation and management.
