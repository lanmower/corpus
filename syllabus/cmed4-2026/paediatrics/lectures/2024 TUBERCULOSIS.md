# 2024 TUBERCULOSIS

## Lecture Notes

### Epidemiology and WHO Strategy

Tuberculosis remains a significant global health burden, particularly in children. The WHO End TB Strategy (2016-2035) sets ambitious targets: a 90% reduction in TB deaths and an 80% reduction in TB incidence by 2035. In paediatric populations, TB incidence is approximately half that observed in adults, yet the disease carries particular severity in young children. The progression from infection to active disease is age-dependent: 50% of infants under 12 months progress to active TB disease after primary infection, and 10-20% of infants develop complicated TB including miliary TB and TB meningitis. These statistics underscore the critical importance of early detection, preventive therapy, and appropriate management in the paediatric age group.

### Definitions and Classification

Understanding TB requires clarity on key terminology. **Adherence** is defined as the extent to which a person's behaviour corresponds with agreed recommendations from a healthcare worker for taking medication, following a diet and/or making lifestyle changes. **Adolescents** are defined by WHO as persons aged 15 to 19 years.

An **index patient** is a person with infectious pulmonary TB who is newly diagnosed. **Latent TB infection** represents a state of immune response to stimulation by Mycobacterium tuberculosis antigens, indicated by a positive tuberculin skin test (TST) or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA) test, with no evidence of active TB disease. In contrast, **TB disease** is caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis and can either be bacteriologically confirmed or clinically diagnosed.

Contact classification is crucial for management decisions. **Recent TB exposure** refers to contact with a person diagnosed with pulmonary TB in the last 12 months, which may be from the same household or close contacts outside the household (care providers, colleagues, teachers, family members or friends). **Significant TB exposure** occurs when there is known exposure to a person with pulmonary TB who shared the same enclosed space for one or more nights or for frequent or extended daytime periods during the three months before the index patient started their TB treatment. A **TB contact** is defined as all people (family members and other individuals regardless of age and HIV status) who have had a significant TB exposure.

**TB exposed infants** are infants born to mothers diagnosed with TB before or after the baby was born, or infants with other documented close TB exposure (e.g., another caregiver, family member, daycare provider).

Children weighing >25 kg (typically eight to ten years of age) can be treated with adult doses of TB medications, marking an important transition point in dosing considerations.

### Pathogenesis and Immunopathology

Mycobacterium tuberculosis is a slow-growing, rod-shaped bacterium with bacilli ranging in length from 1-10 micrometers. Children under 10 years of age rarely develop lung cavities and are therefore less likely to transmit TB to others, distinguishing the paediatric epidemiology from adult disease.

Key factors determining TB disease progression in children include the age of the child (particularly those <5 years), the time since exposure or infection (approximately 1 year), and the immune status of the child. These factors interact to determine whether primary TB infection resolves, remains latent, or progresses to active disease.

**Mycobacterial Cell Wall Components and Immune Evasion:**

The cell wall of MTB contains several highly pathogenic components. Heat shock proteins are highly immunogenic and trigger strong immune responses. Cord factor, a glycolipid, directly induces granuloma formation. LAM (lipoarabinomannan) represents 15% of the bacterial mass and profoundly affects the host immune response. LAM inhibits macrophage activation by interferon gamma and induces macrophages to secrete IL-10 (which suppresses TB-induced T cell proliferation) and TNF-alpha (causing fever, weight loss, and tissue damage). This multifaceted immune evasion strategy explains why TB infection can persist despite immune activation.

**The Ghon Complex and Primary TB Infection:**

Following initial infection, the Ghon focus develops as the initial inflammatory reaction site in the lung parenchyma where TB organisms deposit. This occurs within 2-3 weeks of infection and may subsequently resolve and calcify. The **Ghon complex** consists of the Ghon focus in the lung parenchyma together with enlarged regional hilar lymph nodes, representing a controlled and retained non-proliferating state of Mycobacterium.

Primary TB infection in children has three possible outcomes: (1) clearance by host immunity (primary infection resolves), (2) suppression into an inactive form (latent TB) with a calcified scar visible on X-ray, or (3) spread and progression to active TB disease.

**Granuloma Formation and TB Progression:**

**CD4 Th cells** secrete interferon gamma, causing activation of macrophages into true killer cells, formation of reactive nitrogen intermediates, and epithelioid granuloma formation in clearance of mycobacterium. **CD8+ T suppressor cells** kill macrophages that are infected, leading to caseating granuloma formation as a delayed hypersensitivity reaction.

Two types of TB granulomas represent different pathological states. A **proliferative TB granuloma** demonstrates effective killing of Mycobacterium due to proliferating lymphocytes, plasma cells, an outer rim of fibrosis, and a low bacillary load. In contrast, an **exudative TB granuloma** (also called caseating granuloma or fibrocaseous granuloma) exhibits a higher bacillary load, caseating necrosis, and progression of infection if not treated.

**Fibrocaseous TB** represents a pathological state in which hydrolytic enzymes dissolve MTB in larger lesions surrounded by a fibrous capsule. These fibrocaseous nodules contain viable mycobacterium and pose a lifelong risk for reactivation.

While granulomas are acidic, anaerobic, extracellular environments that inhibit MTB growth, they present a paradox: if they fail to contain the disease, they can cause significant tissue damage, leading to spread and further destruction. This balance between host immune containment and immunopathology is central to TB pathogenesis.

### Clinical Presentation and Symptomatology

**Constitutional Symptoms:**

TB infection typically presents with constitutional symptoms including fever and night sweats, weight loss and failure to thrive, and fatigue and anorexia. Two or more of these TB symptom criteria are highly suggestive of TB diagnosis. The persistent cough criterion specifically identifies persistent or non-remitting cough or wheeze lasting more than 2 weeks that is unresponsive to appropriate antibiotics for community-acquired pneumonia. The weight loss criterion includes documented low weight or failure to thrive persisting for the past 3 months, especially if not responding to intervention.

**Hypersensitivity Phenomena:**

Four to twelve weeks after primary TB infection, clinical hypersensitivity phenomena may occur, including TST conversion, erythema nodosum (raised red, painful macules on the anterior lower legs), phlyctenular conjunctivitis (a raised red nodule on the limbus with conjunctival injection), and Poncet's polyarthritis.

**Pulmonary TB with Airway Involvement:**

When TB involves the airways, the clinical presentation includes atelectasis of the involved segment or lung, persistent cough, enlarged regional lymph nodes, and large airway (monophonic) wheezing that responds poorly to bronchodilators. This airway involvement distinguishes a specific clinical pattern requiring careful radiological assessment.

### Tuberculin Skin Test (TST) and Immunological Testing

**TST Interpretation and Timing:**

The TST is read 48-72 hours after placement. A positive TST indicates TB infection (not necessarily TB disease) and measures hypersensitivity to tuberculin purified protein derivative (PPD).

**TST Cutoff Values by Risk Group:**

TST interpretation is highly context-dependent:
- **≥5 mm** in children <5 years old, HIV-infected children, or those with evidence of TB exposure
- **≥10 mm** in children ≥5 years of age without risk factors
- **≥15 mm** in non-BCG vaccinated children without TB risk factors

In children <5 years or HIV-infected children, a positive TST indicates recent infection and is a significant risk factor for progression to active TB disease.

**Causes of False Negative TST:**

Several conditions can result in false negative TST results: HIV infection, malnutrition, severe viral infections (measles, chickenpox), cancer, immunosuppressive drugs (steroids), severe disseminated TB, and recent TB exposure (where a 2-3 month conversion window period may result in negative tests despite recent infection).

**TST Interpretation in BCG-Vaccinated Populations:**

In endemic countries, a positive TST after BCG vaccination should be interpreted as indicative of TB infection, especially in very young and immunocompromised children. BCG vaccination can cause TST positivity, making this distinction critical.

**X-Ray Findings of Controlled TB:**

X-ray findings indicating controlled primary TB infection include a calcified scar in the lung parenchyma and/or hilar lymph nodes, representing the Ghon focus and complex.

### BCG Vaccination Protocol

**BCG Vaccine Characteristics:**

BCG vaccine is a live attenuated vaccine derived from Mycobacterium bovis. It is given to all neonates at birth via intradermal injection, except to symptomatic HIV-exposed neonates.

**BCG Protection:**

In TB-endemic countries, BCG provides children with a degree of protection against disseminated and severe forms of TB, specifically TB meningitis and miliary TB. This protection is particularly important in early childhood when the risk of severe disease is highest.

**BCG in HIV-Exposed Infants:**

Asymptomatic HIV-exposed babies should receive routine BCG vaccination at birth, but they must be monitored closely for BCG-related adverse events. However, BCG should not be given to neonates exposed to TB while screening for active disease; maternal infectiousness and drug susceptibility status should be carried out first.

### Danger Signs Requiring Hospital Referral

Children with TB presenting with certain danger signs require urgent hospital referral. These critical presentations include:
- Severe respiratory distress (TB pneumonia)
- Severe wheezing not responding to bronchodilators (severe airway compression)
- Signs or symptoms of meningism and seizures (TBM)
- Hepatosplenomegaly (disseminated TB)
- Breathlessness and peripheral edema (pericardial effusion)
- Severe pulmonary disease combined with malnutrition
- Distended abdomen with or without ascites (TB abdomen)
- Angulation of the spine or gibbous deformity (TB spine)

### TB Diagnosis in HIV-Coinfected Children

TB diagnosis becomes significantly more complex in HIV-infected children. TB disease is often indistinguishable from other HIV-related lung diseases, chronic cough, weight loss, and persistent fever are common to both conditions, and TST is frequently negative. This diagnostic challenge necessitates a lower clinical threshold for TB treatment initiation.

**Differential Diagnoses of Pulmonary TB in HIV Children:**

Conditions that must be differentiated from TB in HIV-infected children include bacterial pneumonia, viral pneumonia, fungal lung disease, Pneumocystis jiroveci pneumonia (PJP), pulmonary lymphoma, Kaposi's sarcoma, and lymphoid interstitial pneumonia (LIP). **LIP** is distinguished by clubbing, parotid enlargement, and generalized lymphadenopathy.

### Clinical Diagnosis of TB

**Diagnostic Approaches:**

Children can be clinically diagnosed with TB without bacteriological confirmation in several scenarios: chest X-ray abnormalities consistent with active TB, clinical picture alone (empiric treatment), histological and biochemical tests suggestive of TB, or a combination of the above due to the paucibacillary nature of TB in children.

**Radiological Findings:**

The most common radiological finding of TB in children is an enlarged hilar region; a lateral chest X-ray is particularly useful to detect hilar adenopathy and the characteristic "doughnut sign." Radiological findings indicating airway compression by enlarged lymph glands include the ball valve effect (segmental or lobar hyperinflation) or complete airway obstruction (lobar collapse).

**Miliary TB:**

Acute dissemination in miliary TB causes widespread fine millet-sized (1-2 mm) lesions with a characteristic "millet seed" appearance.

**Pleural Involvement:**

Pleural effusions may occur in children older than 6 years and appear on chest X-ray as unilateral or bilateral uniform white opacity with a concave upper border. Tracheal or mediastinal shift may occur away from the side of effusion.

**Normal Variants:**

Widened mediastinum secondary to thymus enlargement (the "sail sign") should not be misinterpreted as mediastinal widening due to TB lymph nodes.

### Bacteriological Diagnosis and Sputum Samples

**Sputum Collection Challenges:**

Sputum diagnosis in children is challenging. In children <8 years, sputum is usually smear negative (paucibacillary), lung cavities are rare, and adequate sputum sample collection is difficult due to the child's inability to expectorate.

**Sputum Sampling Protocol:**

At least 2 sputum samples on 2 consecutive days should be collected, as the probability of a positive sputum increases with multiple samples.

**Extrapulmonary TB Specimens:**

For extrapulmonary TB, tissue specimens and site-specific fluids or secretions should be sent for Gene Xpert (GXP) testing and TB culture.

### Diagnostic Tests: Urine LAM

**Urine LAM Detection:**

Urine LAM (lipoarabinomannan) testing detects LAM levels in urine. LAM is a heat-stable glycolipid of the outer cell wall of Mycobacterium species that is released by metabolically active bacilli and can be used to detect TB.

**Sensitivity and Specificity:**

Urine LAM sensitivity increases among children with severe immunosuppression, but specificity is lower than that of Gene Xpert. This test is particularly useful in severely immunocompromised children where traditional sputum samples may be difficult to obtain.

### Contact-Tracing Strategies

**Contact Tracing Definition:**

Contact-tracing is the strategy by which individuals in contact with a case of confirmed or suspected pulmonary TB are screened for the presence of TB infection or disease, with the aim of offering either anti-tuberculosis preventive therapy or anti-tuberculosis chemotherapy. This systematic approach is fundamental to TB control.

**Reverse Contact Tracing:**

Reverse contact-tracing is adopted when a child is the index case with active TB, and the potential source case (usually an adult or older child in the index case's home environment) is sought by symptom screening and/or chest radiography of household contacts. This approach is critical for identifying the source and preventing further household transmission.

### Extrapulmonary Tuberculosis (EPTB)

**Risk Groups and Timing:**

Children <2 years of age with an immature cellular immune response are at high risk for extrapulmonary TB. EPTB occurs in the first few months after TB infection, or it may develop years later (e.g., osteoarticular TB, renal TB) following reactivation of organisms that were subclinically disseminated during primary infection.

**TB Lymphadenitis:**

TB lymphadenitis presents as enlarged cervical lymph nodes that are typically painless (unless secondarily infected), firm, and matted. They may become fluctuant prior to spontaneous drainage and sinus formation, a condition historically termed "scrofula." TB lymphadenitis may involve sites other than the neck. Differential diagnoses for enlarged lymph nodes that can mimic TB lymphadenitis include persistent generalized lymphadenopathy (PGL), lymphoma, Kaposi sarcoma, sarcoidosis, and carcinomatous metastases.

**Serous Cavity Effusions:**

Inflammatory tuberculous effusions can occur in pleural, pericardial, and peritoneal cavities.

**TB Pleural Effusion:**

Pleural effusions typically occur in children older than 3 years, particularly in older children and adolescents. Clinical presentations include intermittent fever, unilateral chest pain, breathlessness, and rarely haemoptysis (which may occur in cavitatory disease). Physical examination reveals decreased chest movement and stony dullness on percussion. Chest X-ray findings demonstrate tracheal or mediastinal shift away from the side of effusion, and unilateral or bilateral uniform white opacity with a concave upper border.

Pleural aspirate in TB reveals an exudate with straw color, high protein content, high white cell count, and raised adenosine deaminase (ADA). Pleural biopsy can be performed for Xpert testing for further diagnostic confirmation.

**Osteoarticular TB:**

TB bone and joint disease occurs occasionally in infants but more commonly in older children. Clinical symptoms include painful limbs and joints, and limp. Notably, 50% of osteoarticular TB is spinal TB. Spinal TB (TB spine) presents with back pain lasting several weeks, and acute spinal cord compression (manifesting as lower limb weakness, bladder and bowel neurological signs) constitutes a medical emergency.

**TB Pericarditis:**

TB pericarditis presents with clinical signs of congestive heart failure (CCF) or pericardial constriction, raised jugular venous pressure (JVP), palpable pulsus paradoxus, and a pericardial friction rub.

**TB Abdominal Disease:**

Complications of TB abdominal disease include malnutrition with protein-losing enteropathy, abdominal distension (from ascites or bowel distension), peritonitis, and biliary or lymphatic obstruction due to massive compressive abdominal lymph nodes.

**Miliary TB:**

Miliary TB is a complication of primary TB in young children resulting from widespread blood-borne dissemination of TB bacilli. Physical signs include lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, fever, tachypnea, cyanosis, respiratory distress, and other signs including papular lesions on skin or choroidal tubercles in the retina. The chest X-ray appearance shows diffuse, uniformly distributed small miliary shadows with a "millet seed" appearance.

Blood investigations reveal pancytopenia on full blood count, abnormal liver function tests, and histological biopsy shows tubercles. MTB can be isolated from cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), bone marrow, and sputum in miliary TB.

**TB Meningitis:**

TB meningitis (TBM) presents with a typical history of TB contact, lethargy, headache (especially with early morning vomiting), irritability, drowsiness, convulsions, and weight loss. Physical signs include neck pain and resistance to neck flexion due to meningeal irritation (Kernig's sign), cranial nerve palsies, and altered level of consciousness.

Cerebrospinal fluid findings in TBM show raised protein, low glucose, low chloride, and predominantly lymphocytic pleocytosis. Gram stain is negative and acid-fast bacilli (AFB) are seldom found despite the TB diagnosis. Xpert (Gene Xpert) can be performed on CSF for MTB detection and rifampicin susceptibility testing.

Important diagnostic considerations: TST can be negative in TBM, and chest X-ray may be normal in children with TB meningitis. TB meningitis should always be considered in children diagnosed with meningitis who are not responding to standard bacterial meningitis treatment. Complications of TBM include hydrocephalus (obstruction to CSF flow), SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone), hemi- or quadriplegia, convulsions, deafness, blindness, and neurodevelopmental delay.

### Congenital and Vertical Transmission of TB

**Modes of Transmission:**

TB can be transmitted from mother to baby through three mechanisms: transplacental spread through the umbilical vein to the fetal liver (in utero), aspiration or ingestion of infected amniotic fluid (in utero/intrapartum), and inhalation of droplet nuclei from close contacts (mother or other family members) postpartum.

**Risk Factors for Vertical Transmission:**

Risk factors for vertical TB transmission include HIV infection in the mother, active TB in the mother, lack of prenatal care, non-adherence to anti-TB treatment, the infectiousness of the mother, and mother having multi-drug resistant TB.

**Progression Risk:**

Without TB preventive therapy or BCG vaccination, 50% of babies born to mothers with active TB disease will develop TB disease in the first year of life.

**Breastfeeding:**

Importantly, breast milk does not transmit TB. Breastfeeding is recommended to infants irrespective of the TB status of the mother, as the risk of transmission through breast milk is negligible.

**Clinical Presentation of Congenital TB:**

Congenital TB presents with low birth weight (LBW), poor feeding, poor weight gain, hepatosplenomegaly (HSM), abdominal distension, rashes, lethargy, and respiratory symptoms. TB infection is determined by TST. Active congenital TB disease can become apparent at 2-4 weeks of age.

**Investigation and Diagnosis:**

Specimens that should be collected include gastric washings for Xpert, microscopy and culture, and placenta for histopathology. TB treatment should be commenced while awaiting bacteriological confirmation as the disease progresses rapidly.

**Treatment and Management:**

The TB treatment regimen for congenital TB is the same as for all children <8 years (Regimen 3A). Response to treatment is indicated by increased appetite, weight gain, and radiological resolution.

**BCG Vaccination in TB-Exposed Neonates:**

BCG should not be given to neonates exposed to TB while screening for active disease. Maternal infectiousness and drug susceptibility status should be carried out and documented first.

### TB Prevention and Preventive Therapy (TPT)

**TB Preventive Therapy Regimens:**

Four main TPT regimens are used:
- **3HP**: Three months of weekly isoniazid plus rifapentine
- **3RH**: Three months of daily rifampicin plus isoniazid
- **6H**: Six months of daily isoniazid monotherapy
- **12H**: 12 months of daily isoniazid monotherapy

All of these regimens are indicated for TB contacts, TB-exposed infants, and children with latent TB infection to prevent progression to active TB disease.

**TPT Initiation Timing:**

TPT should be started after active TB disease has been excluded through clinical assessment, symptom screening, and radiography in TB contacts.

### TB Management Framework

**Pre-Treatment Investigations:**

Before starting TB treatment, the following investigations must be done:
- Exclude HIV infection
- Assess all co-infected children for ART (antiretroviral therapy) eligibility
- Nutritional assessment
- Psychosocial evaluation
- Chest X-ray
- TB Register completion

**Supportive Care:**

Psychosocial support should be provided to both the child and the parents or guardians. Referral for nutritional support should be considered in all children diagnosed with TB. Adequate nutrition is essential as TB is associated with malnutrition, which impairs immune function and treatment response. Adequate nutrition promotes weight gain and recovery.

**Administrative Steps:**

When a child is diagnosed with TB, complete the TB Register and make a note in the Road to Health Card. Additionally, ask about other children or adults in the household and screen them for TB as part of contact investigation.

**Management Categories:**

Four categories of paediatric TB management are recognized: (A) Uncomplicated pulmonary TB, (B) Complicated pulmonary TB, (C) Disseminated (miliary) TB, and (D) Tuberculous meningitis (TBM).

**Guideline References:**

Updated guidance for TB management in children comes from the Primary Healthcare STGs and EML 2020 edition (Section 17.4: Pulmonary tuberculosis) and the Paediatric Hospital Level STGs and EML 2017 edition (Chapter 10: Tuberculosis), as well as WHO 2014 guidance for national TB programmes, the National Guidelines on Treatment of TB infection 2023, and Southern African society for pediatric infectious diseases guidelines.

### TB Treatment Regimens and Drug Selection

**Major TB Drugs:**

The major TB drugs used in paediatric regimens are:
- **Isoniazid (H)**
- **Rifampicin (R)**
- **Rifapentine**
- **Pyrazinamide (Z)**
- **Ethambutol (E)**

**Regimen 3A:**

Regimen 3A is used for children <8 years with uncomplicated and congenital TB disease and specifies exact dosages and durations per national guidelines. Children weighing >25 kg (typically 8-10 years) can be treated with adult doses of TB medications, marking a transition to standard adult dosing.

### TB Treatment Phases and Duration

**Intensive Phase:**

The intensive phase comprises the first 2 months of anti-TB drugs with maximum bactericidal activity to eliminate the majority of viable TB organisms.

**Continuation Phase:**

The continuation phase represents the remaining treatment duration to prevent relapse and eliminate remaining bacilli. The typical duration of the continuation phase is 2-4 months, depending on TB type and response.

**Treatment Duration by Disease Type:**

- **Uncomplicated Pulmonary TB**: Typically 6 months total (2 months intensive + 4 months continuation)
- **Complicated Pulmonary TB**: 8 months total (2 months intensive + 6 months continuation)
- **Miliary TB**: 8 months treatment, with potential for a 2-month initial phase with 4 drugs depending on severity
- **TB Meningitis**: 9-12 months due to poor drug penetration into cerebrospinal fluid and the severity of disease

**Corticosteroids in TB Meningitis:**

Corticosteroids (typically dexamethasone or prednisone) are used as adjunctive therapy in TB meningitis to reduce inflammation and prevent complications.

### Monitoring During TB Treatment

**Baseline and Ongoing Investigations:**

Baseline investigations before treatment include HIV testing, assessment for ART eligibility in co-infected children, nutritional assessment, psychosocial evaluation, chest X-ray, and TB Register completion.

**Adverse Effects Monitoring:**

During TB treatment, monitor the following parameters:
- Liver function tests (for hepatotoxicity)
- Peripheral neuropathy (from isoniazid toxicity)
- Visual acuity if on ethambutol (for optic neuritis)
- Ototoxicity if on streptomycin
- Hypersensitivity reactions

**Adherence Assessment:**

Treatment adherence is assessed through regular clinic visits, pill counts, clinical response monitoring (appetite, weight gain, symptom resolution), and radiological improvement on follow-up chest X-ray.

### Treatment Outcomes

**Cured:**

Cured status is defined as completing treatment with evidence of clinical and radiological improvement, with no relapse for at least 12 months after treatment completion.

**Treatment Failure:**

Treatment failure is defined as lack of clinical improvement or radiological deterioration despite adequate treatment and adherence for at least 4 months.

**Default:**

Default is defined as loss to follow-up or interruption of treatment for more than 2 consecutive months after the start of treatment.

### Treatment Failure Management

Management of treatment failures requires a systematic approach:
1. Assess adherence to the regimen
2. Investigate for drug malabsorption
3. Re-evaluate the TB diagnosis
4. Perform drug susceptibility testing
5. Consider the possibility of resistance and modify the regimen accordingly

### Vitamin Supplementation

**Pyridoxine (Vitamin B6):**

Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) should be given to all children on TB treatment and/or antiretroviral treatment due to shared toxicities of these regimens. Vitamin B6 is specifically recommended to prevent peripheral neuropathy from isoniazid toxicity.

### Infection Control and Transmission

**Impact of Effective TB Treatment:**

Appropriate TB treatment renders a child non-infectious within 2 weeks of starting effective therapy, significantly reducing transmission risk.

**Infection Control Measures:**

The following infection control measures should be implemented during TB treatment:
- Respiratory isolation for symptomatic patients (first 2 weeks of treatment)
- Good ventilation in patient areas
- Use of masks for close contacts
- Strict adherence to treatment to achieve rapid reduction in infectivity

### HIV-TB Coinfection Management

**TB Development in ART-Treated Children:**

Development of TB in a child on antiretroviral therapy (ART) could indicate:
- Immune reconstitution (if within the first 6 months of ART initiation)
- Failure of the ART regimen
- New TB infection depending on exposure history

**TB Management While on ART:**

When TB is diagnosed in a child already on ART, TB treatment should be started without delay and ART should be continued. The ART regimen should be reviewed and may need to be changed based on TB drug interactions.

**Supplementation in TB-ART Coinfection:**

Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) should be given to all children on both TB and ARV treatment due to shared toxicities of the regimens.

**Drug Interactions Between TB and ART:**

Rifampicin induces hepatic enzymes, reducing ART levels significantly. Lopinavir/ritonavir levels are particularly affected, requiring dose adjustments. Isoniazid may accumulate with certain protease inhibitors (PIs).

**Ritonavir Dosing Adjustment:**

For each millilitre of lopinavir/ritonavir 80/20 mg/mL solution, add 0.75 mL of ritonavir 80 mg/mL solution. This additional ritonavir should be added at a dose of 0.75 times the volume of the lopinavir/ritonavir dose to compensate for enzyme induction by rifampicin.

### Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS)

**IRIS Definition:**

IRIS is observed in patients on TB treatment who start antiretroviral therapy (ART), characterized by worsening of disease after initial clinical improvement. This phenomenon reflects the immune system's recovery and mounting response to TB antigens.

**Timing and Duration:**

IRIS may occur during the first three months of ART initiation. It is generally self-limiting and typically lasts 10-40 days.

**Clinical Manifestations:**

Clinical manifestations of IRIS in TB patients include worsening disease after initial improvement, fever, increased size of lymph nodes, tuberculomas, and exacerbation of symptoms, signs, or X-ray manifestations.

**Management of IRIS:**

TB treatment and ART should be continued when IRIS is suspected. In some cases, the addition of corticosteroids might be useful to manage the inflammatory response. Patients already on ART may experience temporary exacerbations of symptoms, signs, or X-ray manifestations after beginning TB therapy, which should be differentiated from true treatment failure.

### Contact Screening Components

**TB Contact Screening:**

TB contact screening includes several key components:
- Clinical assessment for TB symptoms
- Tuberculin skin test (TST) or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA)
- Chest X-ray if symptomatic
- TB preventive therapy for eligible contacts

This comprehensive approach ensures all exposed individuals are appropriately managed.

## Summary of Key Points

### Critical Epidemiological Facts
- WHO End TB Strategy targets 90% reduction in TB deaths and 80% reduction in incidence by 2035
- TB incidence in children is approximately half that in adults
- 50% of infants progress to active TB after primary infection
- 10-20% of infants develop complicated TB (miliary or meningitis)

### Essential TST Cutoff Values
- ≥5 mm in children <5 years, HIV-infected, or with TB exposure
- ≥10 mm in children ≥5 years without risk factors
- ≥15 mm in non-BCG vaccinated without TB risk

### TPT Regimens at a Glance
- **3HP**: 3 months weekly (isoniazid + rifapentine)
- **3RH**: 3 months daily (rifampicin + isoniazid)
- **6H**: 6 months daily (isoniazid alone)
- **12H**: 12 months daily (isoniazid alone)

### TB Treatment Duration by Type
- Uncomplicated PTB: 6 months (2 intensive + 4 continuation)
- Complicated PTB: 8 months (2 intensive + 6 continuation)
- Miliary TB: 8 months (with possible 4-drug induction)
- TB Meningitis: 9-12 months (poor CSF penetration)

### EPTB Sites Requiring Special Attention
- Lymphadenitis (cervical and other sites)
- Pleural effusion (>3 years age)
- TB spine (spinal cord compression emergency)
- TB meningitis (always consider non-responders)
- Miliary TB (widespread dissemination)
- Pericarditis (CCF/constriction)
- Abdominal TB (ascites, obstruction)

### Key Management Principles
- HIV testing before treatment initiation
- Nutritional support essential
- Psychosocial support for family
- Vitamin B6 for all TB-treated children
- Infection control first 2 weeks critical
- TST can be falsely negative (HIV, malnutrition, disseminated TB)
- Breast milk safe; breastfeeding recommended
- BCG at birth except symptomatic HIV+ neonates
