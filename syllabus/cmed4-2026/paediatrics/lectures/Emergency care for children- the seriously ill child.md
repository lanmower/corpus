# Emergency care for children - the seriously ill child

## Lecture Notes

### Introduction: The Framework for Emergency Care

Emergency care for seriously ill children rests on four fundamental components. First is **triage** — the process of finding which children urgently need care. Second is **provision of emergency care** through resuscitation and life support. Third comes **post-resuscitation care**, ensuring the child remains stable. Finally comes **stabilisation and transport** to appropriate definitive care. These four pillars work together to save lives.

Triage, in particular, is not a diagnosis. Rather, it is a rapid sorting process aimed at identifying life-threatening problems requiring immediate intervention. Triage must be quick, focusing on finding danger rather than determining what disease caused that danger. Importantly, **all staff can perform triage** — nurses, doctors, clerks, gatekeepers, and cleaners. Triage should occur as soon as the child arrives, **before reception or administration**, whether in the outpatients queue, emergency room, ward, or emergency treatment area (a designated space with access to drugs and supplies).

The WHO developed the ETAT system to standardize this approach: E = Emergency, T = Triage, A = Assessment, T = Treatment.

### The ABCC-C-D Framework: Identifying Life-Threatening Problems

The ABCC-C-D framework is the cornerstone of emergency assessment, identifying six critical life-threatening problems:

- **A = Airway** — Is the airway open and clear?
- **B = Breathing** — Is the child breathing effectively?
- **C = Circulation** — Are there signs of adequate perfusion?
- **C = Coma** — What is the consciousness level?
- **C = Convulsion** — Is the child fitting?
- **D = Dehydration** — What is the fluid status?

If ANY sign of ABCC-C-D is present, emergency treatment must start immediately without delay. The presence of any of these problems mandates movement to the emergency treatment area for urgent intervention.

### Understanding the Six Final Pathways to Death

Most children die through one of six final pathways, understanding which helps guide prevention and management:

1. **Respiratory obstruction** — Examples include croup, asthma, and bronchiolitis. The airway becomes partially or completely blocked, preventing air entry.

2. **Respiratory depression** — The child's breathing effort becomes inadequate, seen in post-ictal states, meningitis, or poisoning. The brain fails to drive adequate breathing.

3. **Alveolar or chest wall failure** — The lungs cannot expand or exchange gas properly. Causes include pulmonary contusion, flail chest, and traumatic or congenital diaphragmatic hernia.

4. **Fluid loss** — Severe dehydration from conditions like gastroenteritis or major trauma (femoral or pelvic fractures) leads to circulatory collapse.

5. **Fluid maldistribution** — Shock occurs not from absolute fluid loss but from improper distribution within blood vessels. Meningococcal shock and anaphylaxis exemplify this mechanism.

6. **Heart failure** — The heart fails as a pump. Causes include local anaesthetic toxicity and congenital heart disease.

### Assessment of Airway and Breathing

To assess whether a child's airway is patent, ask these key questions: Is the child breathing? Is there noisy breathing (secretions, stridor, snoring, grunting)? Is the child blue?

**Signs of severe respiratory distress** include fast breathing (fast for age), severe chest-indrawing (see below), grunting, difficulty feeding, and visible fatigue. These signal that the child is working too hard to breathe and cannot sustain this effort indefinitely.

Three clinical signs warrant special attention:

**Stridor** is a high-pitched, musical breathing sound heard without a stethoscope, typically during inspiration. It indicates partial upper airway obstruction from conditions like croup, epiglottitis, or foreign body aspiration.

**Grunting** is a groaning sound at the end of expiration. It represents the child's attempt to maintain positive end-expiratory pressure to keep alveoli open — a sign of severe respiratory effort and hypoxaemia.

**Chest wall indrawing** (also called intercostal, substernal, or suprasternal indrawing) occurs when the chest wall draws inward during inspiration. This indicates increased work of breathing due to airway obstruction or reduced lung compliance, showing the child is struggling to move air.

### Assessment of Circulation

To assess circulation, check whether the child has **warm hands**. If not, measure **capillary refill time** by pressing on a fingernail or toe and counting how long colour takes to return. A capillary refill greater than 2 seconds indicates poor circulation and shock. Also assess whether the pulse is weak and fast.

**Compensated shock** presents with cold hands, cold feet, capillary refill greater than 2 seconds, weak pulses, and pale skin — but the child remains alert and vital organs still receive adequate perfusion. This is the body's attempt to preserve core organ function.

**Decompensated shock** shows all signs of compensated shock plus altered consciousness (responds only to Pain or is Unresponsive on the AVPU scale), severe hypotension, absent pulses, severe bradycardia, or cardiac arrest. The body's compensatory mechanisms have failed.

Vital sign changes signalling movement into shock include cold hands, capillary refill greater than 2 seconds, weak or absent pulses, and altered consciousness. Emergency conditions presenting as shock include septic shock (from infection or meningitis), hypovolaemic shock (from diarrhoea or bleeding), cardiogenic shock (from heart failure), anaphylactic shock, and conditions with fluid maldistribution.

### Assessment of Consciousness and Convulsions

The **AVPU scale** rapidly assesses consciousness:
- **A = Alert** — Eyes open, aware
- **V = Responds to Voice** — Opens eyes or responds to verbal commands
- **P = Responds to Pain** — Opens eyes or moves only to painful stimuli
- **U = Unresponsive** — No response to any stimulus

When a child responds only to Pain or is Unresponsive, this constitutes **coma** — a life-threatening emergency.

**Floppiness** (decreased muscle tone) in a seriously ill child is a grave sign indicating severe systemic illness, shock, severe infection, or neurological compromise. A floppy child requires emergency intervention.

**Convulsion detection** varies by age. Generalised convulsions (whole body shaking) are easy to see. Subtle signs, especially in neonates, may include eye flickering or deviation, hand or foot movements, mouth movements, repetitive movements, or apnoea. Active convulsion at the time of presentation is an emergency requiring immediate airway assessment and seizure management.

### Assessment of Dehydration

To assess severe dehydration in a child with diarrhoea, ask: Is the child lethargic or unconscious? Does the child have sunken eyes? Does the skin pinch go back very slowly?

The **skin pinch test** involves pinching skin (typically on the arm) and observing how long it takes to return to normal. If it takes greater than 2 seconds to return, this indicates moderate to severe dehydration. A normal skin pinch returns immediately.

Clinical findings of severe dehydration include:
- Lethargy or unconsciousness
- Sunken eyes
- Skin pinch that takes greater than 2 seconds to return
- Weak or absent pulses
- Cold hands

These signs reflect the child's profound fluid loss and circulatory compromise.

### Priority Signs: The 3TPR-MOB Mnemonic

Beyond the life-threatening signs of ABCC-C-D, children also need priority (early but not emergency) assessment if they display any of the 3TPR-MOB signs:

**3T = Tiny baby, Temperature, Trauma**
- **Tiny baby** — Any child less than 3 months of age presenting to emergency care needs priority assessment due to their special vulnerability and higher risk of serious infection.
- **Temperature** — A child who is hot to touch (temperature >38 degrees Celsius) needs priority assessment.
- **Trauma** — Any child with significant trauma warrants priority placement.

**P = Pallor, Poisoning, Pain (severe)**
- **Pallor** — Severe pallor indicates severe anaemia. Assess by comparing the child's palm with the mother's palm; if the child's palm is much paler, this is a priority sign.
- **Poisoning** — Any suspected poisoning requires priority assessment.
- **Pain (severe)** — Severe pain warrants priority care.

**R = Respiratory (NOT severe), Restless, Referral**
- **Respiratory** — Not severe respiratory distress (which would be emergency), but concerning respiratory findings requiring clinical judgment and experience.
- **Restless** — Lethargy or irritability; responding to voice only (V on AVPU); continuously irritable, crying, and inconsolable — these indicate altered consciousness or behaviour.
- **Referral** — Children requiring urgent referral to a higher facility.

**MOB = Malnutrition, Oedema, Burns**
- **Malnutrition** — Look rapidly at the child's arms, legs, and chest. **Marasmus** is severe wasting where the child appears very thin with minimal subcutaneous fat. This is a priority sign.
- **Oedema** — Bilateral foot oedema, with or without hair and skin changes suggesting **Kwashiorkor** (protein malnutrition), is a priority sign. Bilateral foot oedema can signal heart failure, kidney problems (nephrotic syndrome), liver dysfunction, or nutritional deficiencies.
- **Burns** — Any significant burn injury requires priority assessment.

### The EPQ Triage Action System

Once assessment is complete, triage decisions direct the child's pathway:

**E = Emergency** — Take the child immediately to the emergency treatment area.

**P = Priority** — Take the child to the front of the queue.

**Q = Queue** — No emergency or priority signs are present. The child is non-urgent and can wait their turn.

The key distinction between E and P: Emergency children have signs of ABCC-C-D or other life-threatening conditions requiring immediate treatment. Priority children have concerning signs (3TPR-MOB) requiring early assessment but not immediate emergency intervention.

### Clinical Scenarios: Applying the Framework

**Scenario 1** (3-year old with diarrhoea): Airway and breathing normal. Hot but cold hands. Capillary refill 1.5 seconds. Alert. Skin pinch takes 3 seconds. Assessment: Cold hands, prolonged capillary refill, and slow skin turgor are signs of moderate dehydration. Triage: **P (Priority)** — requires early assessment but not emergency-level shock.

**Scenario 2** (4-year old, recent seizure): Convulsed 1 hour ago. Fast breathing, no cyanosis, no respiratory distress. Very hot, responds quickly to questions. No diarrhoea or vomiting. Assessment: Recent seizure with fever suggests possible febrile convulsion, a priority sign. Maintained alertness and normal breathing prevent emergency classification. Triage: **P (Priority)**.

**Scenario 3** (1-year old, recurrent seizures): Seizures at home and outside clinic, now unconscious. Breathing wet and noisy, drooling. Looking blue. Assessment: Coma (unresponsive), airway obstruction signs (wet breathing, drooling), cyanosis, and recurrent convulsions — multiple emergency signs. Triage: **E (Emergency)** — immediate emergency treatment needed.

**Scenario 4** (2-year old, actively convulsing): Actively convulsing at presentation. Assessment: Active convulsion is an emergency sign requiring immediate airway assessment and seizure management. Triage: **E (Emergency)**.

**Scenario 5** (9-month girl, choking): Choking on a bead necklace, brought by nurse. Assessment: Choking represents acute airway obstruction. Triage: **E (Emergency)** — immediate airway intervention required.

**Scenario 6** (3-year old boy, 14kg, severe respiratory distress): Pale, floppy, difficulty breathing. 3-day cough. Fast breathing, heavy severe chest indrawing. Airway patent. Alert. Assessment: Severe chest indrawing, difficulty breathing, pallor, and floppiness all indicate severe respiratory distress. Triage: **E (Emergency)**.

**Scenario 7** (4-month baby, 5kg, shock): Fever, rapid breathing, refusing feeds, 2 episodes vomiting and watery diarrhoea. Cold hands. Capillary refill 6 seconds. Femoral pulse fast and weak. No chest indrawing. Assessment: Cold hands, capillary refill greater than 2 seconds, weak fast pulse, and significant fluid loss from diarrhoea/vomiting indicate severe dehydration and circulatory shock. Triage: **E (Emergency)** — immediate resuscitation needed.

**Scenario 8** (18-month boy, 11kg, fever and abdominal pain): Unwell 2 days, feverish. Abdominal pain, fast breathing. Airway fine, no chest indrawing. No diarrhoea history. Assessment: No emergency signs (ABCC-C-D absent) and no priority signs. Fever present but child is alert and breathing is fast without distress. Triage: **Q (Queue)** — non-urgent.

**Scenario 9** (10-week baby, 3.5kg, fever): Hot, crying a lot, won't suckle. Airway, breathing, circulation normal. No diarrhoea or dehydration. Assessment: Tiny baby (<3 months) with fever is a priority sign. Triage: **P (Priority)**.

**Scenario 10** (14-month girl, altered consciousness and distress): Sleeping all day. Irritable when awoken, rouses to loud voice or arm shaking. Warm hands but pale. No diarrhoea or vomiting. Deep breathing, lower chest wall indrawing. Assessment: Responds to voice only (V on AVPU), indicating altered consciousness. Chest wall indrawing indicates respiratory distress. Both are priority signs. Triage: **P (Priority)**.

**Scenario 11** (1-year girl, 6.5kg, malnutrition and dehydration): 2-day history diarrhoea and vomiting. Restless and irritable. Airway and breathing normal. Warm hands. Responds to voice. Skin pinch 4 seconds. Sunken eyes. Skin and bones appearance. Assessment: Severe malnutrition (skin and bones) and moderate-to-severe dehydration (sunken eyes, slow skin pinch) are priority signs. Triage: **P (Priority)**.

**Scenario 12** (4-year boy, sore ear): Well-appearing and playful. Assessment: No emergency or priority signs. Triage: **Q (Queue)** — non-urgent.

**Scenario 13** (6-year boy, chest pain): Breathing normally, not shocked, conversant. Assessment: Normal breathing, normal circulation, maintained alertness. No emergency or priority signs. Triage: **Q (Queue)** — non-urgent.

**Scenario 14** (7-week boy, fever): Fever 1 day. Looking around, breathing normally, no shock or dehydration. Assessment: Tiny baby (<3 months) with fever. Triage: **P (Priority)**.

**Scenario 15** (7-year girl, mild asthma): Mild wheeze, bright and alert. Assessment: No respiratory distress, mild symptoms, maintained alertness. No emergency or priority signs. Triage: **Q (Queue)** — non-urgent.

**Scenario 16** (18-month girl, croup): Cough, runny nose, barking cough, stridor, agitated. Assessment: Stridor indicates airway obstruction, an emergency sign. Triage: **E (Emergency)** — emergency airway assessment required.

**Scenario 17** (2-year girl, refusing to walk): Won't walk 1 day. Crying but consolable, breathing normally, not shocked. Assessment: Normal breathing, normal circulation, consolability (not continuously irritable). No emergency or priority signs. Triage: **Q (Queue)** — non-urgent.

### Systematic Triage Framework

The practical triage workflow follows these steps:
1. Look for Emergency signs (ABCC-C-D)
2. Move to ER and treat any signs found
3. Look for Priority signs (3TPR-MOB)
4. Place priority patients at the front of the queue
5. Move on to the next patient

This systematic approach ensures no child is missed while maintaining efficiency.

### Emergency Treatment and Resuscitation

When a child is triaged as Emergency, rapid treatment must begin. The essential actions immediately after identifying an emergency child are:

1. **Secure the airway** — Open the airway, position the child appropriately, and clear secretions.
2. **Provide oxygen and ventilation** — Deliver high-flow oxygen; use bag-mask ventilation if needed.
3. **Establish IV access and begin fluid resuscitation** — Insert IV lines and start appropriate fluids based on the clinical problem.
4. **Monitor continuously** — Use pulse oximetry, blood pressure, heart rate, and other vital signs monitoring.
5. **Initiate specific emergency care** — Administer medications (such as adrenaline for anaphylaxis, glucose for hypoglycaemia, antibiotics for sepsis).
6. **Arrange transport to higher-level care** — Stabilise and transport to a secondary or tertiary hospital for definitive care.

The emergency treatment area must be equipped and staffed immediately. Essential equipment and supplies include:
- Oxygen delivery equipment
- Airway management equipment (oral airways, bag-mask ventilation devices)
- IV catheters and resuscitation fluids
- Medications for resuscitation (adrenaline, glucose, antibiotics)
- Monitoring equipment (pulse oximeter, blood pressure cuff)
- Emergency drugs appropriate for paediatric doses

### Clinical Considerations in Dehydration and Malnutrition

**Severe dehydration** requires recognition of both loss and compensation signs. The skin pinch test remains a bedside clinical tool: pinch skin on the arm and observe the return time. A normal skin pinch returns immediately; greater than 2 seconds indicates moderate to severe dehydration. This correlates with up to 10% of body weight loss in children.

**Marasmus** (wasting) and **Kwashiorkor** (protein malnutrition with oedema) represent opposite presentations of severe malnutrition. Marasmus affects children with overall caloric deficiency and appears as severe thinness. Kwashiorkor affects children with protein deficiency and appears with bilateral foot oedema, moon facies, and hair changes. Both warrant priority assessment, though neither alone indicates emergency-level shock if circulation and airway are intact.

### Differential Diagnosis in Emergency Presentations

**Severe respiratory distress** may result from pneumonia, bronchiolitis, asthma, croup, epiglottitis, foreign body aspiration, pulmonary oedema (from heart failure), pneumothorax, or any condition causing airway obstruction or reduced lung compliance.

**Shock presentations** arise from septic shock (infection or meningitis), hypovolaemic shock (diarrhoea or bleeding), cardiogenic shock (heart failure), anaphylactic shock, or conditions with fluid maldistribution.

### Stabilisation and Transport

Once emergency treatment has begun, rapid stabilisation and transport to definitive care (secondary or tertiary hospital) is critical. Delays in transport lead to worse outcomes in seriously ill children. The goal is to initiate life-saving treatment and then transfer the child to a facility capable of definitive care without further delay.

Every clinician working with children should master the ABCC-C-D framework, the 3TPR-MOB priority signs, and the systematic triage approach.
