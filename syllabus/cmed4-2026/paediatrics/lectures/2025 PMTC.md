## Lecture Notes

### Overview: EMTCT and PMTCT

Eliminating Mother-to-Child Transmission (EMTCT) stands for "Preventing and ending mother to child transmission." Mother-to-Child Transmission (MTCT) is the transmission of HIV from an HIV-positive mother to her child during pregnancy, labour, delivery, or breastfeeding. Without any intervention, the transmission rate ranges from 15% to 45%, but effective interventions can reduce this below 5%.

EMTCT services are provided across a comprehensive time period from preconception through 18 months of age, or alternatively 6 weeks post cessation of breastfeeding if breastfeeding continues beyond 18 months. These services are integrated into multiple touchpoints including family planning visits, antenatal care (ANC), delivery, postnatal care (PNC), EPI (Expanded Programme on Immunisation) visits, and IMCI (Integrated Management of Childhood Illness) visits.

### HIV Transmission Modes

HIV can be transmitted to children through multiple routes, each with distinct epidemiological significance.

**Perinatal Transmission Modes** include three primary routes: intrauterine transmission, intrapartum transmission, and breastfeeding. Intrauterine transmission accounts for 25–40% of perinatal transmission cases, while intrapartum transmission (during labour and delivery) represents 60–75% of cases. Breastfeeding contributes 5–28% of transmission risk overall, with variation based on the type of breastfeeding (exclusive breastfeeding carries lower transmission risk than mixed breastfeeding).

**Sexual Transmission** to children may occur through heterosexual contact, homosexual contact, or sexual abuse.

**Parenteral Transmission** occurs through non-sexual blood exposure, including the sharing of contaminated needles for illicit drug use or anabolic steroid injection.

**Iatrogenic Transmission** results from re-use of non-sterilized equipment in healthcare settings.

**Blood Product Transmission** is extraordinarily rare in the US, with an estimated risk of 1 in 2,000,000 for transmission from contaminated blood products.

### Mechanisms of Mother-to-Child Transmission

**Intrauterine (In Utero) Transmission**

The majority of intrauterine transmission occurs in the third trimester. The mechanism involves breakdown of placental integrity leading to micro transfusions of viraemic maternal blood across the placenta to the fetus. Conditions that increase the risk of in utero transmission include genital tract infections and placental inflammation.

**Intrapartum Transmission**

Intrapartum transmission occurs through contact of infant mucosal membranes with HIV virus present in blood and secretions during labour and delivery. During labour contractions, microtransfusions cross the placenta from mother to fetus, contributing to transmission risk. Rupture of membranes greater than 4 hours is associated with increased risk when no antiretroviral therapy (ART) is given.

**Breastfeeding Transmission**

The risk of HIV transmission via breastfeeding varies substantially depending on the mother's infection status. In women with established HIV infection, the transmission risk is 14%. However, women who acquire HIV after delivery (primary infection/seroconversion) have a significantly higher rate of transmission at 29%. Maternal mastitis has been associated with increased breast milk HIV RNA levels and increased transmission risk via breastfeeding.

### Clinical Manifestations in Perinatal HIV

**Early Presentation**

At birth, there are no definitive clinical findings that can discriminate between infected and uninfected infants. Early complications that may occur include growth failure and CNS (central nervous system) disease.

**Progressive Signs and Symptoms**

Slow progression of clinical signs in pediatric HIV infection includes oral thrush, diffuse lymphadenopathy, hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, and recurrent diarrhea. Life-threatening opportunistic infections such as PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia) typically emerge at 4–6 months of life in untreated perinatal HIV.

Obscure clinical presentations may manifest as thrombocytopenia, encephalopathy, nephrotic syndrome, heart failure, or failure to thrive (FTT).

**Prognosis**

With modern antiretroviral therapy (HAART), the prognosis for perinatal HIV has dramatically improved. More than 95% of children with perinatal HIV infection are living at 16 years of age when on appropriate treatment.

### Laboratory Testing for HIV Diagnosis

**Limitations of Antibody-Based Testing**

HIV antibody assays (ELISA, Western Blot) are unreliable in infants and young children due to persistence of transplacentally acquired maternal antibodies. Additionally, antibody tests may be falsely negative in the first weeks after acute infection.

**HIV DNA PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)**

HIV DNA PCR is the gold standard for early diagnosis of HIV in infants. The "D" in HIV DNA PCR stands for "Diagnosis" in neonates. Qualitative HIV-1 DNA PCR detects HIV proviral DNA within peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) and can be performed on whole blood or dried blood samples. Early HIV diagnosis is critical because infected infants have high mortality and morbidity when not diagnosed and not on treatment.

Important limitations include: HIV DNA PCR will test positive even if the patient is on ART, and it will not detect very recent infection because 2/3 of MTCT occurs perinatally. However, its sensitivity improves markedly over the first months of life:
- At birth: 55% sensitivity
- 2–4 weeks of age: >90% sensitivity
- At 1, 3, and 6 months of age: 100% sensitivity

**HIV RNA Quantitation (Viral Load)**

HIV RNA quantitation measures viral load and is essential for monitoring treatment response and assessing transmission risk.

**Other Laboratory Tests**

Additional laboratory tests for HIV diagnosis and monitoring include genotype testing (to identify mutations in resistant strains), phenotype testing (in vitro sensitivity testing), and therapeutic drug monitoring.

### EMTCT Services and Testing Algorithms

**Maternal HIV Testing**

During antenatal care, HIV testing should be performed at the first visit and at each routine basic antenatal care visit, for a total of 8 testing occasions during pregnancy. Maternal viral load testing should be done at delivery, 6 months postpartum, and 6 monthly if breastfeeding continues. The high-risk viral load threshold is defined as VL > 50 copies/ml.

**Maternal Adherence Support**

Since 2019, ART initiation guidance has included explicit recommendations for adherence counselling.

**HIV Counselling and Testing (HCT) Schedule During Pregnancy and Postpartum**

HIV Counselling & Testing should be performed:
- Every 3 months throughout pregnancy
- At labour/delivery
- At the 6 week EPI visit
- Every 6 months throughout breastfeeding

**Infant Testing Algorithms: Evolution of Protocols**

The infant HIV testing algorithms have evolved substantially across three major protocol updates.

**2015 Protocol**

In the 2015 protocol, HIV PCR testing in exposed infants was performed at:
- Birth
- 10 weeks
- 14 weeks (for high-risk infants only)
- 6 weeks post cessation of breastfeeding
- 18 months with rapid testing and a second rapid for confirmation

High-risk infants at birth were defined as those with maternal VL > 1000 copies/ml OR maternal ART started < 4 weeks prior to delivery.

**2019 Protocol**

The 2019 protocol introduced significant changes:
- HIV PCR at birth and 10 weeks (same as 2015)
- **Elimination of the 14-week test** (major change)
- 6-month PCR for all exposed infants (not just high-risk)
- Appropriate testing 6 weeks post cessation of breastfeeding
- Universal HIV testing at 18 months if child is exposed but uninfected
- PCR used as confirmatory test up to 2 years of age

High-risk infant definition changed to: Mother VL > 1000 copies/ml at delivery (or most recent viral load in last 12 weeks of ANC) OR mother with no VL documented in the last 12 weeks of ANC.

**2022 Protocol**

The 2022 protocol refined the high-risk definition to: Maternal VL < 50 copies/ml OR no known VL at birth.

**Screening and Confirmation Test Algorithms by Age**

- **<18 months**: Screening test is PCR, Confirmation test is PCR
- **18 months – 2 years**: Screening test is Rapid, Confirmation test is PCR
- **>2 years**: Screening test is Rapid, Confirmation test is Rapid

**Positive Test Confirmation and Initiation of Therapy**

When an infant <2 years has a positive PCR or positive rapid test result, confirmation should be performed with an HIV PCR test on a new sample. Critically, therapy should be initiated while awaiting the confirmatory result—do not delay treatment initiation.

### Maternal and Infant Antiretroviral Prophylaxis

**Infant Prophylaxis Regimens by Protocol Year**

**2015 Protocol**

In the 2015 protocol, high-risk infants received:
- AZT (zidovudine) for 6 weeks
- NVP (nevirapine) for 12 weeks

**2019 Protocol**

The 2019 protocol specified:
- High-risk infants: AZT for 6 weeks and NVP for a minimum of 12 weeks
- NVP discontinuation rules: Stop NVP if mother's VL < 1000 copies/ml OR until 1 week after she stops breastfeeding

**Management When Maternal Viral Load Elevates**

If the mother's viral load elevates during the follow-up period, high-risk prophylaxis for the exposed infant should be restarted with AZT 12-hourly (for 6 weeks) and NVP daily (minimum 12 weeks), with dosages adjusted according to the infant's weight.

**Nevirapine Administration and Monitoring**

The first dose of nevirapine should be given as soon as possible after birth. Ideally, the birth PCR test should be done before administration of infant NVP, but any delay in testing should not delay the NVP administration.

NVP dosing follows strict guidelines: only one dose per 24-hour period should be given. If the baby vomits, the dose may be repeated once. 

At every infant visit, the mother's most recent viral load result should be checked to guide ongoing management decisions.

**Response to Positive PCR While on NVP**

If infant HIV PCR is positive while on NVP prophylaxis:
- Stop NVP immediately
- Confirm with a second HIV PCR test on a new sample
- Initiate ART while awaiting the second PCR test result
- Continue breastfeeding

**Breastfeeding Recommendations**

Breastfeeding in the context of EMTCT is recommended only in context of ART recommended for 24 months or longer, in line with recommendations for the general population.

### Transmission Risk Factors and Rates

**Perinatal Transmission Risk Distribution**

The cumulative risk of perinatal HIV transmission without any intervention is 13–39%, distributed as follows:
- Intrauterine: 5%
- Peripartum: 13–18%
- Breastfeeding: 5–28%

The timing of transmission among those infected perinatally is:
- In utero: 25–40% of cases
- Intrapartum: 60–75% of cases

**Maternal Risk Factors**

Multiple maternal factors influence perinatal HIV transmission:
- HIV-1 RNA levels (viral load)
- Low CD4 lymphocyte count
- Other infections: Hepatitis C, CMV, bacterial vaginosis
- Maternal drug use
- Lack of AZT during pregnancy
- Cigarette smoking

**Obstetrical Risk Factors**

Obstetrical factors that increase transmission include:
- Length of ruptured membranes/chorioamnionitis
- Vaginal delivery (compared to caesarean delivery)
- Invasive procedures (such as scalp electrodes)
- Being the first-born of twins

**Infant Risk Factors**

Prematurity is a significant infant factor increasing transmission risk.

**Viral Load and Transmission Rates**

The relationship between maternal viral load and perinatal transmission rate is well-established from clinical data:

- **VL <400 copies/ml**: 0% transmission (confidence interval 0–11.0%, 32 cases)
- **VL 400–3,500 copies/ml**: 8.6% transmission (confidence interval 2.0–11.2%, 70 cases)
- **VL 3,500–10,000 copies/ml**: 19.4% transmission (confidence interval 10.3–28.5%, 72 cases)
- **VL 10,000–30,000 copies/ml**: 18.8% transmission (confidence interval 10.3–27.4%, 80 cases)
- **VL >30,000 copies/ml**: 34.8% transmission (confidence interval 29.5–40.1%, 115 cases)

Transmission can potentially occur at all levels of maternal viral load. Risk of transmission increases with increasing maternal viral load. However, maternal plasma and cervico-vaginal secretion viral loads do not always correlate, which may explain some variation in observed transmission despite similar plasma viral loads.

**Breastfeeding-Specific Transmission Rates**

As noted above, transmission via breastfeeding is 5–28% overall, with important variation. Women who seroconvert after delivery have a higher rate of transmission than women infected prior to delivery. The range reflects the variation between exclusive breastfeeding (lower risk) and mixed breastfeeding (higher risk).

### Antiretroviral Safety in Infants

**General Considerations**

There is limited data on most ARVs in infants, particularly when given in combination. This requires careful clinical judgment and monitoring.

**Zidovudine (ZDV/AZT)**

ZDV is generally safe in infants but may cause transient anemia. When combined with 3TC, there may be increased hematologic toxicity.

**Nevirapine (NVP)**

Safety concerns with NVP in infants include rare cases of severe rash, hepatotoxicity, and the potential for resistance to develop in infants who become HIV infected.

**Protease Inhibitors**

Protease inhibitors are not recommended for neonates due to lack of pharmacokinetic (PK) data for most agents and possible cardiac and other toxicity (particularly with lopinavir/ritonavir [LPV/r]).

### Summary and Key Clinical Takeaways

The prevention of mother-to-child transmission requires integrated, comprehensive services spanning from preconception through 18 months postpartum (or 6 weeks after breastfeeding cessation). Early diagnosis through HIV DNA PCR testing is critical, with sensitivity improving from 55% at birth to 100% by 1 month. Maternal viral load is the cornerstone driver of transmission risk, with transmission occurring at all viral load levels but escalating dramatically above 1,000 copies/ml. Maternal adherence to ART, with target viral load suppression below 50 copies/ml, is the key intervention preventing transmission. Infant prophylaxis with AZT and NVP, optimally timed and dosed according to weight, provides critical additional protection. Regular monitoring of maternal viral load at each visit guides ongoing prophylaxis decisions for exposed infants. The evolution of testing algorithms from 2015 through 2022 reflects accumulating evidence on optimal timing and methods for diagnosis. With these comprehensive interventions, transmission rates can be reduced from 15–45% to below 5%, and with undetectable maternal viral loads, transmission becomes extremely rare.
